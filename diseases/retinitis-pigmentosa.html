What Is Retinitis Pigmentosa?

Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) is a group of rare, inherited eye diseases that cause progressive degeneration of the photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) in the retina. The retina is the light-sensitive tissue lining the back of the eye responsible for converting visual images into nerve signals sent to the brain. In RP, the photoreceptors gradually die, leading to progressive vision loss that typically begins with night blindness and peripheral (side) vision loss, eventually progressing to tunnel vision and, in some cases, near-total blindness.

The term "retinitis pigmentosa" is somewhat misleading—it's not an inflammatory condition ("retinitis" suggests inflammation), but rather a genetic dystrophy. The "pigmentosa" refers to the characteristic appearance of dark pigment deposits ("bone spicules") visible in the retina during examination. RP affects approximately 1 in 4,000 people worldwide, with symptoms typically appearing in childhood or adolescence, though onset and progression vary widely among individuals and depending on the specific genetic mutation involved.

Key Facts About Retinitis Pigmentosa

  • Inherited disease: Caused by genetic mutations affecting photoreceptor function and survival.
  • Progressive vision loss: Worsens over time; rate varies widely.
  • Night blindness first: Difficulty seeing in dim light typically earliest symptom.
  • Tunnel vision: Loss of peripheral vision; central vision often preserved longer.
  • Bone spicule pigment: Characteristic pigment deposits visible on retinal exam.
  • No cure (yet): Treatments can slow progression but cannot currently restore lost vision (gene therapy emerging).
  • Variable prognosis: Some retain useful vision throughout life; others become legally blind by midlife.
  • Multiple inheritance patterns: Autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, or X-linked.

Understanding RP is critical for early diagnosis, genetic counseling, access to low vision services, and participating in emerging clinical trials.

Genetics and Inheritance

Genetic Basis

  • RP is caused by mutations in over 100 different genes
  • These genes are involved in photoreceptor structure, function, and survival
  • Different mutations cause different rates of progression and severity

Inheritance Patterns

Autosomal Dominant RP (~30-40% of cases)

  • One copy of mutated gene (from one parent) causes disease
  • 50% chance of passing to each child
  • Both males and females equally affected
  • Often milder, later onset
  • Common genes: RHO (rhodopsin), PRPH2 (peripherin-2)

Autosomal Recessive RP (~50-60% of cases)

  • Two copies of mutated gene (one from each parent) needed
  • Parents usually carriers (unaffected)
  • 25% chance affected child if both parents carriers
  • Both males and females equally affected
  • Often earlier onset, more severe
  • Common genes: USH2A, EYS, PDE6A, PDE6B

X-Linked RP (~10-20% of cases)

  • Mutation on X chromosome
  • Primarily affects males (one X chromosome)
  • Females usually carriers with mild or no symptoms
  • Mother passes to 50% of sons (affected) and 50% of daughters (carriers)
  • Often most severe form
  • Common gene: RPGR

Simplex/Sporadic Cases (~20-40%)

  • No family history
  • New mutation or recessive inheritance with carrier parents

Genetic Testing

  • Identifies specific mutation in many cases
  • Aids prognosis, genetic counseling
  • Essential for enrollment in mutation-specific clinical trials
  • Commercially available multi-gene panels

What Causes RP?

Photoreceptor Degeneration

Mechanism of vision loss:

  • Rods affected first:
    • Rods responsible for peripheral and night vision
    • Degenerate and die off progressively
    • Causes night blindness and peripheral vision loss
  • Cones affected later:
    • Cones responsible for central, color, and detailed vision
    • Eventually degenerate in many patients
    • Causes central vision loss

Why Photoreceptors Die

Mutations affect:

  • Phototransduction (light-sensing process)
  • Photoreceptor structure and maintenance
  • Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) support of photoreceptors
  • Metabolic pathways essential for cell survival

Secondary Changes

  • Retinal blood vessels narrow (attenuated vessels)
  • Optic nerve becomes pale (atrophy)
  • Pigment deposits form ("bone spicules")
  • Retina thins

Symptoms and Disease Progression

Night Blindness (Nyctalopia)

Difficulty seeing in dim light or darkness; often first symptom, appearing in childhood/adolescence.

Loss of Peripheral Vision

Gradual narrowing of visual field; "tunnel vision"; difficulty seeing to the sides while walking.

Difficulty with Contrast

Trouble distinguishing objects from background; reading becomes harder.

Light Sensitivity (Photophobia)

Glare sensitivity, especially in bright sunlight; may develop as cones affected.

Color Vision Changes

Difficulty distinguishing colors, especially blues and greens, as cones degenerate.

Central Vision Loss (Late)

Reading and recognizing faces become difficult; occurs in advanced stages in many patients.

Typical Progression

Early Stage (Childhood/Adolescence)

  • Night blindness
  • Subtle peripheral vision loss
  • Difficulty adapting to dark
  • Tripping, bumping into objects not noticed peripherally

Middle Stage (20s-40s)

  • Pronounced tunnel vision
  • Difficulty with mobility (may use cane)
  • Trouble driving (may lose license)
  • Reading still possible for many

Advanced Stage (40s-60s and beyond)

  • Severe tunnel vision or only small central island remaining
  • Central vision may deteriorate
  • Legal blindness common (though complete blackness rare)
  • May retain light perception even if functionally blind

Rate of Progression

  • Highly variable depending on specific mutation and inheritance pattern
  • Some patients retain useful vision throughout life
  • Others lose most vision by age 40-50
  • X-linked RP often progresses fastest
  • Autosomal dominant often slowest

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Night blindness, especially in childhood?
  • Difficulty with peripheral vision?
  • Family history of vision loss or RP?
  • Onset and progression

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Visual Acuity

  • May be normal or near-normal in early stages
  • Decreases as disease progresses

Fundus Examination (Dilated Retinal Exam)

Classic findings:

  • Bone spicule pigmentation: Dark pigment deposits in mid-peripheral retina, resembling bone spicules
  • Attenuated retinal blood vessels: Narrowed, thin vessels
  • Waxy pale optic disc: Optic nerve atrophy
  • Macular changes: Later stages may show macular atrophy or cystoid macular edema

Visual Field Testing (Perimetry)

  • Quantifies peripheral vision loss
  • Shows progressive constriction over time
  • Classic "ring scotoma" (mid-peripheral blind spot) in some cases
  • Tracks disease progression

Electrophysiology Testing

Electroretinogram (ERG) - Gold Standard

  • Measures electrical responses of photoreceptors and retinal cells to light
  • Key finding: Markedly reduced or absent responses
  • Detects disease even before visible symptoms
  • Differentiates RP from other retinal diseases
  • Rod and cone function assessed separately

Imaging

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • High-resolution cross-sectional images of retina
  • Shows thinning of outer retinal layers (photoreceptors)
  • Detects cystoid macular edema (CME)
  • Monitors disease progression

Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

  • Imaging technique showing RPE health
  • Patterns of autofluorescence correlate with disease severity
  • Useful research and monitoring tool

Genetic Testing

  • Identifies specific gene mutation
  • Multi-gene panels test for hundreds of genes simultaneously
  • Confirms diagnosis, determines inheritance pattern
  • Critical for family planning and genetic counseling
  • Enables participation in gene-specific clinical trials
  • Success rate: ~50-70% (mutation identified)

Differential Diagnosis

Other conditions that can mimic RP:

  • Other inherited retinal dystrophies (e.g., cone-rod dystrophy, choroideremia)
  • Usher syndrome (RP + hearing loss)
  • Vitamin A deficiency
  • Medication toxicity (e.g., chloroquine/hydroxychloroquine)
  • Syphilis (rare cause of similar appearance)

Complications

Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)

  • Fluid accumulation in macula (central retina)
  • Occurs in ~20-30% of RP patients
  • Causes central vision blurring
  • Treatable with carbonic anhydrase inhibitors or steroids
  • Can be chronic or intermittent

Cataract

  • Posterior subcapsular cataracts common
  • May develop earlier than typical aging cataracts
  • Exacerbates vision loss
  • Cataract surgery can improve vision if significant cataract present

Myopia (Nearsightedness)

  • Associated with some RP types
  • Can progress

Glaucoma

  • Slightly increased risk
  • Monitor eye pressure regularly

Psychological Impact

  • Depression, anxiety common
  • Grief over vision loss
  • Social isolation
  • Career and lifestyle limitations

Treatment and Management

Current State of Treatment

There is currently no cure for most forms of RP, and treatments that restore lost vision are extremely limited. However, several approaches can slow progression, manage complications, and improve quality of life. Gene therapy has recently become available for one specific mutation, and research is advancing rapidly.

Vitamin A Supplementation

  • Based on clinical trial showing modest slowing of progression
  • Dose: 15,000 IU/day vitamin A palmitate
  • Slows decline: May delay progression by ~2 years on average
  • Not for everyone: Consult doctor; not recommended for some RP subtypes (e.g., Usher syndrome type 2, certain mutations)
  • Caution: Avoid in pregnancy (teratogenic at high doses)
  • Monitor liver function

DHA (Docosahexaenoic Acid) Supplementation

  • Omega-3 fatty acid
  • Some evidence of slowing progression when combined with vitamin A
  • Dose: ~200-1,200 mg/day

Treatment of Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)

  • Oral carbonic anhydrase inhibitors: Acetazolamide or methazolamide; reduce fluid
  • Topical drops: Dorzolamide
  • Steroids: Rarely used (eye injections or oral)
  • Can improve central vision if CME contributing to blur

Cataract Surgery

  • If cataract develops and impairs remaining vision
  • Standard cataract surgery
  • Can improve vision quality significantly

Low Vision Aids and Rehabilitation

Critical for maintaining independence:

  • Magnifiers: Handheld, stand, electronic video magnifiers for reading
  • Large-print materials, audiobooks
  • Screen readers, text-to-speech software
  • Mobility training: Orientation and mobility specialists teach cane use, navigation
  • Lighting: Bright task lighting, avoid glare
  • Contrast enhancement: High-contrast settings on devices

Protective Eyewear

  • UV-blocking sunglasses
  • Some animal studies suggest UV may accelerate damage
  • Reduces glare sensitivity

Regular Monitoring

  • Annual eye exams
  • Visual field testing to track progression
  • OCT for CME surveillance
  • Genetic counseling for family planning

Research and Emerging Therapies

Gene Therapy - Breakthrough Treatment

Luxturna (Voretigene Neparvovec) - FDA Approved 2017

  • First FDA-approved gene therapy for inherited retinal disease
  • For: Biallelic RPE65 mutations (rare form of RP)
  • Mechanism: Delivers working copy of RPE65 gene to retinal cells via viral vector
  • Procedure: Subretinal injection surgery
  • Results: Improved vision, light sensitivity in many patients
  • Limitation: Only for specific mutation (~1-2% of RP cases)
  • Cost: Extremely expensive (~$850,000 for both eyes)

Other Gene Therapies in Clinical Trials

  • Trials ongoing for other mutations (RPGR, RHO, PDE6B, others)
  • CRISPR gene editing being explored

Retinal Implants (Bionic Eye)

  • Argus II: Electronic retinal prosthesis; provides rudimentary vision (light/dark, shapes) in profound blindness
  • Requires surgery to implant electrode array on retina
  • Limited vision restoration but can aid navigation
  • Company discontinued product; newer systems in development

Stem Cell Therapy

  • Replacing dead photoreceptors with stem cell-derived cells
  • In early clinical trial stages
  • Promising but years away from widespread use

Optogenetics

  • Genetically modifying surviving retinal cells to become light-sensitive (replacing dead photoreceptors' function)
  • Early clinical trials
  • Partial vision restoration reported in some cases

Neuroprotective Therapies

  • Medications to slow photoreceptor death
  • Various trials ongoing (e.g., ciliary neurotrophic factor implants)

Participation in Clinical Trials

  • Genetic testing essential to match patients to mutation-specific trials
  • ClinicalTrials.gov lists ongoing studies
  • Foundation Fighting Blindness (FFB) maintains trial database

Living with Retinitis Pigmentosa

Mobility and Safety

  • Orientation and mobility training: Learn cane techniques, safe navigation
  • Home modifications: Bright lighting, remove tripping hazards, high-contrast markings
  • Guide dog: For some patients
  • GPS and smartphone apps: Navigation aids

Employment and Education

  • Vocational rehabilitation services
  • Assistive technology (screen readers, magnification software)
  • Career counseling toward accessible fields
  • Educational accommodations (extra time, audio textbooks)

Driving

  • Most eventually unable to drive due to peripheral vision loss
  • License depends on meeting visual field/acuity requirements
  • Plan for alternative transportation

Psychological Support

  • Counseling for coping with progressive vision loss
  • Support groups (Foundation Fighting Blindness, local groups)
  • Peer support from others with RP

Family Planning and Genetic Counseling

  • Discuss inheritance risk with genetic counselor
  • Carrier testing for at-risk relatives
  • Prenatal or preimplantation genetic diagnosis options

When to See a Doctor

Seek Evaluation If:

  • Night blindness, especially if developing in childhood/adolescence
  • Difficulty seeing peripherally or frequent tripping
  • Family history of RP or vision loss
  • Progressive vision changes

Early diagnosis allows for genetic testing, counseling, enrollment in trials, and planning for low vision needs.

Regular Follow-Up If Diagnosed

  • Annual comprehensive eye exams
  • Visual field testing to monitor progression
  • OCT to check for CME
  • Consider genetic testing if not done
  • Stay informed about clinical trials

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type/mutation of RP do I have?
  • Should I get genetic testing?
  • What is my prognosis?
  • Should I take vitamin A or DHA supplements?
  • Am I eligible for any clinical trials?
  • When should I access low vision services?
  • What are the implications for my children/family?

Bottom Line

Retinitis Pigmentosa is a group of inherited retinal diseases causing progressive degeneration of photoreceptor cells, leading to night blindness, tunnel vision, and potential severe vision loss. Caused by mutations in over 100 genes, RP can follow autosomal dominant, recessive, or X-linked inheritance. While there's currently no cure for most forms, vitamin A supplementation may modestly slow progression, and low vision aids significantly improve quality of life. Exciting developments are emerging: gene therapy (Luxturna) is now available for one specific RP mutation, and numerous clinical trials are underway for other mutations. Genetic testing is crucial for diagnosis, prognosis, family counseling, and trial eligibility. If you or your child experience night blindness or peripheral vision loss, seek evaluation—early diagnosis enables access to emerging therapies and support services.
