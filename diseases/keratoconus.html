What Is Keratoconus?

Keratoconus (ker-uh-toe-KOH-nus) is a progressive eye disease in which the normally round, dome-shaped cornea thins and begins to bulge into a cone-like shape. This cone shape deflects light as it enters the eye, causing progressively distorted and blurred vision. Keratoconus literally means "cone-shaped cornea."

The cornea is the clear, protective outer layer of the eye. In keratoconus, structural changes in the corneal tissue cause it to lose its strength and become progressively thinner and more irregular. As the cornea protrudes forward and becomes steeper, vision becomes increasingly distorted.

Key Facts About Keratoconus

  • Not uncommon: Affects about 1 in 2,000 people, though mild cases may be underdiagnosed.
  • Starts in teens/young adults: Typically begins during teenage years or early 20s.
  • Progressive disease: Usually worsens for 10-20 years, then stabilizes.
  • Affects both eyes: Usually bilateral, though often asymmetric (one eye worse than the other).
  • Not contagious: Cannot spread from person to person.
  • Rarely causes complete blindness: Can severely impair vision but rarely leads to total blindness.
  • Treatable: Multiple treatment options available, including new procedures to halt progression.
  • Genetic component: Often runs in families; about 10-15% have a family history.

The good news is that keratoconus progression can now be stopped with corneal crosslinking, and vision can be corrected with specialty contact lenses or, in advanced cases, corneal transplantation. Early detection and treatment are key to preserving good vision.

Understanding the Cornea

To understand keratoconus, it's important to know about normal corneal structure:

The Healthy Cornea

The cornea is the clear, dome-shaped front surface of the eye, covering the iris and pupil:

  • Shape: Smoothly curved dome, like a small ball
  • Function: Refracts (bends) light entering the eye, providing about 2/3 of the eye's focusing power
  • Clarity: Completely transparent to allow light through
  • Thickness: About 0.5mm thick centrally, slightly thicker at edges
  • Structure: Five layers, each with specific functions

Corneal Layers

  • Epithelium: Outermost protective layer
  • Bowman's layer: Protective layer; if damaged, cannot regenerate
  • Stroma: Thickest layer (90% of cornea); collagen fibers arranged in precise pattern for transparency
  • Descemet's membrane: Thin, strong layer
  • Endothelium: Single layer of cells that pump fluid out to keep cornea clear

What Changes in Keratoconus

In keratoconus, several structural changes occur:

  • Collagen degradation: Collagen fibers in stroma weaken and break down
  • Thinning: Cornea becomes progressively thinner, especially centrally or paracentrally
  • Bulging: Weakened area protrudes forward into cone shape
  • Irregular shape: Smooth curvature is lost; surface becomes irregular
  • Steepening: Cone area becomes much steeper than surrounding cornea
  • Scarring: In advanced cases, scarring may develop

How This Affects Vision

  • Irregular astigmatism: Cone shape creates complex, irregular light distortion
  • Myopia: Steepened cornea causes nearsightedness
  • Cannot be corrected with glasses: Irregular shape prevents good correction with standard lenses
  • Progressive blur: Vision worsens as cone progresses

Stages and Progression

Keratoconus is classified into stages based on severity:

Stage 1: Mild Keratoconus

  • Slight irregular astigmatism
  • Mild vision changes
  • Correctable with glasses or soft contact lenses
  • Corneal curvature: < 48 diopters
  • Usually detected in comprehensive eye exam

Stage 2: Moderate Keratoconus

  • Moderate irregular astigmatism
  • Increased near sightedness
  • Glasses less effective; usually need specialty contact lenses
  • Corneal curvature: 48-53 diopters
  • Increased corneal thinning visible

Stage 3: Severe Keratoconus

  • High irregular astigmatism
  • Marked vision impairment
  • Specialty contact lenses essential for functional vision
  • Corneal curvature: 53-55 diopters
  • Significant thinning; cone clearly visible
  • May have early scarring

Stage 4: Advanced Keratoconus

  • Extreme irregular astigmatism
  • Severe vision loss
  • Contact lenses may not fit or provide adequate vision
  • Corneal curvature: > 55 diopters
  • Severe thinning
  • Central scarring common
  • May require corneal transplant

Progression Pattern

Keratoconus progression varies widely:

  • Typical onset: Mid-to-late teens or early 20s
  • Active progression: Usually for 10-20 years
  • Stabilization: Typically stabilizes by age 30-40
  • Variable rate: Some progress slowly over decades; others rapidly over few years
  • Asymmetric: One eye often more affected than the other

Acute Corneal Hydrops

A rare but dramatic complication (2-3% of keratoconus patients):

  • Descemet's membrane ruptures
  • Aqueous humor floods into corneal stroma
  • Sudden, painful corneal swelling and vision loss
  • Appears as white, cloudy area on cornea
  • Usually heals over 2-4 months, leaving scar
  • May paradoxically flatten cone, sometimes improving vision

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of keratoconus is unknown, but several factors contribute:

Genetic Factors

  • Family history: 10-15% of patients have an affected relative
  • Inheritance pattern: Not simple inheritance; likely multiple genes involved
  • Genetic testing: Not currently available or recommended clinically
  • Having a parent with keratoconus increases risk but doesn't guarantee you'll develop it

Eye Rubbing (Strong Association)

Chronic, vigorous eye rubbing is strongly linked to keratoconus:

  • Mechanical trauma from rubbing damages collagen fibers
  • Can trigger or accelerate progression
  • Common in allergy sufferers (itchy eyes)
  • Stopping eye rubbing is crucial to prevent worsening

Associated Conditions

Allergic Diseases

  • Allergic conjunctivitis: Itching leads to eye rubbing
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema): Strong association
  • Asthma
  • Hay fever

Systemic Disorders

  • Down syndrome: Up to 30% develop keratoconus
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome: Connective tissue disorder
  • Marfan syndrome
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta
  • Leber's congenital amaurosis

Other Eye Conditions

  • Vernal keratoconjunctivitis: Severe allergic eye disease
  • Retinitis pigmentosa
  • Retinopathy of prematurity

Other Risk Factors

  • Age: Onset typically in teens/early 20s; rare after 40
  • Gender: Slightly more common in males
  • Ethnicity: More common in Middle Eastern, South Asian, and Pacific Island populations
  • Sleep apnea: Possible association (may relate to eye rubbing)

Theories on Underlying Cause

  • Enzymatic imbalance: Increased enzymes that break down collagen
  • Oxidative stress: Imbalance of free radicals and antioxidants
  • Biomechanical weakness: Inherently weak collagen crosslinks
  • Hormonal factors: May explain progression during puberty/pregnancy

Recognizing the Symptoms

Symptoms of keratoconus develop gradually and vary with disease stage:

Blurred Vision

Progressive blurring that worsens over time, initially correctable with glasses but eventually requiring specialty lenses.

Distorted Vision

Straight lines appear wavy or bent; images look distorted or "ghosted" (irregular astigmatism).

Frequent Prescription Changes

Needing new glasses or contact lenses frequently (every few months to a year) as vision changes rapidly.

Light Sensitivity

Increased sensitivity to light and glare, especially at night (photophobia).

Multiple Images

Seeing multiple ghost images of a single object (monocular polyopia), especially noticeable with point light sources.

Poor Night Vision

Halos around lights, starbursts, and difficulty driving at night.

Streaking Lights

Light sources appear to have streaks or "tails" radiating from them.

Eye Strain

Eye fatigue, headaches from straining to see clearly, especially during reading or computer work.

Progression of Symptoms

Early Stage:

  • Mild blurring and distortion
  • Slight increase in myopia or astigmatism
  • Glasses still provide good vision
  • May be mistaken for regular refractive error

Moderate Stage:

  • Increased blur and distortion
  • Glasses no longer adequate
  • Contact lens intolerance may begin
  • Light sensitivity increases
  • Daily activities affected

Advanced Stage:

  • Severe vision impairment
  • Cannot achieve good vision with standard contacts
  • Significant daily impact
  • May need to stop driving
  • Corneal scarring may develop

Signs Noticed by Others

  • Munson's sign: V-shaped indentation of lower eyelid when looking down
  • Squinting or tilting head to see better
  • Sitting very close to TV or holding reading material close

Diagnosis and Testing

Keratoconus is diagnosed through specialized eye examinations and imaging:

Comprehensive Eye Exam

  • Visual acuity: Measure vision with and without correction
  • Refraction: Determine prescription; irregular astigmatism is clue
  • Slit lamp examination: Magnified view of cornea shows thinning, Vogt's striae (stress lines), Fleischer's ring (iron deposits)
  • Retinoscopy: "Scissors reflex" is characteristic finding

Corneal Topography

The gold standard for diagnosis:

  • Creates detailed 3D map of corneal surface
  • Shows curvature, steepness, and irregularities
  • Classic pattern: inferior steepening with asymmetric bow-tie pattern
  • Can detect early keratoconus before symptoms appear
  • Used for monitoring progression
  • Essential for contact lens fitting

Corneal Pachymetry

  • Ultrasound measures corneal thickness
  • Keratoconus shows progressive thinning
  • Normal: ~540-550 microns centrally
  • Keratoconus: Often < 500 microns, progressive thinning over time

Corneal Tomography (Pentacam, Orbscan)

  • Advanced 3D imaging of entire cornea (front and back surfaces)
  • Measures elevation, thickness, curvature throughout cornea
  • Can detect very early changes
  • More sensitive than topography alone

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Cross-sectional images of cornea
  • Visualizes all corneal layers
  • Useful for detecting scarring

Diagnostic Signs

  • Vogt's striae: Fine vertical stress lines in stroma; disappear with gentle pressure
  • Fleischer's ring: Iron deposits forming ring around cone base (40-50% of cases)
  • Munson's sign: Conical protrusion of lower lid when looking down
  • Rizzuti's sign: Focused light from side creates illuminated point on nasal side
  • Corneal scarring: In advanced cases, white opacities

Screening Family Members

If you have keratoconus, first-degree relatives (parents, siblings, children) should be screened:

  • Comprehensive eye exam with corneal topography
  • Even without symptoms
  • Early detection allows preventive treatment

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on disease stage and progression rate:

Mild Keratoconus

Glasses or Soft Contact Lenses

  • May be adequate in early stages
  • Frequent prescription changes needed
  • Eventually become insufficient as irregularity increases

Moderate Keratoconus

Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Contact Lenses

The most common treatment for functional vision:

  • Rigid lens creates smooth optical surface, masking corneal irregularity
  • Tear layer fills space between lens and irregular cornea
  • Provides better vision than glasses
  • Custom-fit to each patient's unique corneal shape
  • May require frequent refitting as cone progresses
  • Can be challenging to fit comfortably

Hybrid Contact Lenses

  • Rigid center for clear vision
  • Soft skirt for comfort
  • Examples: SynergEyes, UltraHealth
  • May be more comfortable than RGPs
  • Good vision with better comfort

Scleral and Semi-Scleral Lenses

  • Large diameter lenses vault over entire cornea
  • Rest on sclera (white of eye), not cornea
  • Create fluid reservoir over cornea
  • Very comfortable for many patients
  • Excellent vision even in advanced keratoconus
  • Easier to handle than smaller RGPs
  • Increasingly popular option

Piggyback Lenses

  • Soft lens worn first, then RGP on top
  • Improves comfort of RGP
  • Protects cornea
  • More complex; requires managing two lenses

Stop Eye Rubbing!

Critical: Do NOT Rub Your Eyes

Vigorous eye rubbing can worsen keratoconus or trigger progression. If you have allergies:

  • Use antihistamine eye drops
  • Apply cold compresses for itching
  • Take oral allergy medications
  • Treat underlying allergies aggressively
  • If you must touch eyes, use gentle pressure with palm or knuckle, never fingertips

Corneal Crosslinking (CXL)

A revolutionary treatment that can halt keratoconus progression:

What Is Corneal Crosslinking?

Corneal crosslinking strengthens corneal tissue by creating new bonds (crosslinks) between collagen fibers, similar to adding support beams to a structure:

  • Riboflavin (vitamin B2) drops applied to cornea
  • UV-A light activated for specified time
  • Chemical reaction creates new collagen crosslinks
  • Cornea becomes stronger and more resistant to bulging

Goals of Treatment

  • Halt progression: Primary goal—stops keratoconus from worsening
  • Stabilization: Prevents need for transplant in most cases
  • Vision stabilization: Prevents further vision loss
  • Some flattening: May cause slight improvement in corneal shape (1-2 diopters)
  • Does NOT reverse existing damage or significantly improve vision

Who Should Have Crosslinking?

  • Progressing keratoconus: Evidence of worsening over 6-12 months
  • Younger patients with new diagnosis: High risk of progression
  • Before progression is advanced: Earlier treatment more effective
  • Minimum corneal thickness > 400 microns (to protect endothelium)
  • Usually for patients under 35-40 (when progression most likely)

The Procedure

Standard (Epithelium-Off) CXL:

  • Numbing drops applied
  • Epithelium (outer layer) removed
  • Riboflavin drops applied for 30 minutes
  • UV-A light exposure for 30 minutes (Dresden protocol)
  • Bandage contact lens placed
  • Takes about 90 minutes total
  • Outpatient procedure

Accelerated CXL:

  • Higher intensity UV light for shorter time (9-10 minutes)
  • Same strengthening effect
  • Less time in procedure

Epithelium-On (Transepithelial) CXL:

  • Epithelium left intact
  • Special riboflavin formulation penetrates through epithelium
  • Less discomfort, faster recovery
  • Possibly less effective (studies ongoing)

Recovery

  • First few days: Discomfort, light sensitivity, tearing, blurred vision
  • Epithelial healing: 3-5 days with epithelium-off; faster with epithelium-on
  • Vision: Initially worse, gradually improves over 1-3 months
  • Stabilization: Cornea continues strengthening for 6-12 months
  • Anti-inflammatory and antibiotic drops for several weeks

Effectiveness

  • Stops progression: >90% success rate
  • Slight flattening: Average 1-2 diopters reduction in cone steepness
  • Long-term stability: Effects last years; may need repeat treatment if progression recurs
  • Reduces transplant risk: Studies show crosslinking dramatically reduces future need for transplant

Risks and Side Effects

  • Temporary vision blurring (1-3 months)
  • Corneal haze (usually resolves)
  • Eye discomfort for several days
  • Infection risk (rare, < 1%)
  • Corneal scarring (rare)
  • Endothelial damage if cornea too thin (why thickness minimum exists)

Corneal Transplantation

Reserved for advanced keratoconus when other treatments fail:

When Transplant Is Needed

  • Contact lenses cannot be tolerated or don't provide adequate vision
  • Significant corneal scarring
  • Severe thinning or acute hydrops with scarring
  • Quality of life severely impacted despite optimal contact lens management

Only about 10-20% of keratoconus patients eventually need transplant, and with crosslinking, this percentage is decreasing.

Types of Corneal Transplant

Penetrating Keratoplasty (PK or PKP)

Full-thickness corneal transplant:

  • Entire thickness of central cornea removed and replaced with donor tissue
  • Traditional transplant method
  • Success rate > 95% in keratoconus
  • Slower visual recovery (6-12 months)
  • Sutures remain in place 12-18 months
  • Risk of rejection (10-20% over lifetime)

Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)

Partial-thickness transplant (increasingly preferred):

  • Only front layers replaced; patient's endothelium preserved
  • Lower rejection risk (endothelium not transplanted)
  • Comparable visual outcomes to PK
  • Technically more challenging surgery
  • Becoming standard for keratoconus transplant

Outcomes

  • Success rate: > 95% graft survival at 5 years
  • Vision: Most achieve 20/40 or better with glasses or contact lenses
  • Contact lenses: Some still need specialty lenses post-transplant for best vision
  • Keratoconus doesn't recur in transplanted tissue

Intacs (Corneal Ring Segments)

For moderate keratoconus; less commonly used now:

  • Tiny plastic arcs surgically placed in cornea
  • Flatten cone and improve symmetry
  • May improve contact lens fit
  • Reversible procedure
  • Modest vision improvement
  • Often combined with crosslinking

Living with Keratoconus

Contact Lens Management

  • Work with experienced fitter; specialty lenses require expertise
  • Expect multiple fitting sessions
  • Regular follow-ups needed
  • Lenses may need refitting as disease progresses
  • Proper lens care essential to prevent infections

Stop Eye Rubbing

  • Aggressively treat allergies
  • Use preservative-free lubricating drops for dry, itchy eyes
  • Cold compresses for itch relief
  • Be mindful and break the rubbing habit

Monitor for Progression

  • Regular eye exams (every 6-12 months, or more frequently if progressing)
  • Repeat topography to track changes
  • Report vision changes promptly
  • Consider crosslinking if progressing

Emotional Support

  • Join support groups (online or in-person)
  • Connect with others who have keratoconus
  • National Keratoconus Foundation resources
  • Counseling if anxiety or depression develops

Lifestyle Adaptations

  • Good lighting for reading and tasks
  • Magnification aids if needed
  • Anti-glare coatings on glasses
  • Avoid driving at night if difficult
  • Screen your children—early detection allows prevention

Optimistic Outlook

  • Most people with keratoconus maintain functional vision with proper treatment
  • Corneal crosslinking can stop progression
  • Modern specialty contact lenses very effective
  • Transplant success rates excellent if needed
  • Disease research active; new treatments emerging

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Care For:

  • Sudden vision loss or severe blurring
  • Sudden eye pain
  • Cloudy white area on cornea (possible acute hydrops)
  • Signs of eye infection (especially if wearing contacts)

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • Vision becoming progressively blurred or distorted
  • Frequent prescription changes (every few months)
  • Increasing difficulty with glasses or contacts
  • Severe glare or light sensitivity
  • Family history of keratoconus
  • Diagnosed with keratoconus and haven't been seen in over a year
  • Considering pregnancy (hormonal changes may accelerate progression)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What stage is my keratoconus?
  • Is my keratoconus progressing?
  • Am I a candidate for corneal crosslinking?
  • What contact lens options are best for me?
  • How often should I be monitored?
  • Should my family members be screened?
  • What can I do to slow progression?
  • What's my long-term outlook?
