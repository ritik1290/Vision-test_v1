What Is Retinoblastoma?

Retinoblastoma is a malignant cancer of the retina and is the most common intraocular (inside the eye) malignancy in children. It arises from immature retinal cells (retinoblasts) during fetal or early childhood development. Retinoblastoma typically affects children under age 5, with most cases diagnosed before age 3. It can occur in one eye (unilateral, ~60-70%) or both eyes (bilateral, ~30-40%). The hallmark sign is leukocoria—a white pupillary reflex (instead of the normal red reflex) often first noticed in flash photographs as a "white eye" or "cat's eye" appearance.

Retinoblastoma is caused by mutations in the RB1 tumor suppressor gene. About 40% of cases are hereditary (germline mutation—present in all body cells and passed to offspring), while 60% are sporadic (non-hereditary, mutation only in eye). Early detection is critical: when diagnosed and treated early (while tumor confined to eye), survival rates exceed 95% in developed countries. Treatment options include chemotherapy, laser, cryotherapy, radiation, and enucleation (eye removal), chosen based on tumor size, bilaterality, and potential to save useful vision. Children with hereditary retinoblastoma need lifelong surveillance for second cancers.

Key Facts About Retinoblastoma

  • Most common eye cancer in children
  • Typical age: <5 years, median ~18 months
  • White pupil (leukocoria): Hallmark sign—white reflex in photos.
  • Caused by RB1 gene mutation
  • 40% hereditary, 60% sporadic
  • Can be unilateral or bilateral
  • Treatment: chemotherapy, laser, cryo, radiation, enucleation
  • >95% survival if caught early

Understanding retinoblastoma emphasizes the importance of early detection through red reflex screening in infants and prompt evaluation of white pupil.

Types and Genetics

Unilateral vs Bilateral

  • Unilateral (one eye): ~60-70%; usually sporadic; diagnosed slightly later (median ~24 months)
  • Bilateral (both eyes): ~30-40%; nearly always hereditary; diagnosed earlier (median ~12-15 months)

Sporadic vs Hereditary

Sporadic Retinoblastoma (~60%)

  • RB1 mutation occurs only in retinal cells of one eye (somatic mutation)
  • Not inherited, not passed to children
  • Usually unilateral
  • No family history

Hereditary Retinoblastoma (~40%)

  • Germline RB1 mutation (present in all body cells from conception)
  • Can be inherited from parent OR new mutation
  • Usually bilateral, but can be unilateral
  • 50% chance of passing to each child
  • Higher risk of second cancers later in life (osteosarcoma, soft tissue sarcomas, melanoma)
  • Requires lifelong cancer surveillance

RB1 Gene

  • Tumor suppressor gene
  • Two "hits" needed for cancer (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis):
    • Hereditary: first hit inherited, second hit occurs in retinal cell → tumor
    • Sporadic: both hits occur randomly in same retinal cell

Recognizing the Symptoms

Leukocoria (White Pupil)

Most common sign (~60%); white or yellowish pupillary reflex; "cat's eye" appearance; often first noticed in flash photographs (one pupil red, other white).

Strabismus (Eye Misalignment)

Second most common sign (~20%); crossed eyes or wandering eye.

Red, Painful Eye

If advanced with elevated intraocular pressure (glaucoma).

Poor Vision

May be noticed by parents or during screening.

Heterochromia (Different Colored Eyes)

Rare presentation.

Often Asymptomatic

Child may have no complaints; detected by parent noticing white pupil or during screening.

Important Note

  • Parents often first detect leukocoria in flash photos
  • Any white pupil should be evaluated urgently

Diagnosis

Red Reflex Test (Screening)

  • Performed by pediatrician at well-child visits
  • Shine light in eyes; normal = red/orange reflex (light reflects off retina)
  • Abnormal (white reflex, absent reflex, asymmetric): Requires urgent ophthalmology referral

Dilated Fundus Examination Under Anesthesia (EUA) - Gold Standard

  • Child anesthetized for thorough, pain-free exam
  • Findings: White or cream-colored retinal mass(es); may have calcification
    • Can be endophytic (growing into vitreous), exophytic (growing under retina), or mixed
  • Examine both eyes carefully (bilateral disease?)

Imaging

  • Ocular ultrasound: Shows mass with calcification (highly suggestive of retinoblastoma)
  • MRI of brain/orbits:
    • Assess tumor size, optic nerve involvement, extraocular extension
    • Rule out pinealoblastoma (trilateral retinoblastoma—rare, associated with hereditary RB)
  • Note: Biopsy NOT done (risk of tumor seeding outside eye)

Genetic Testing

  • Blood test for RB1 germline mutation
  • Determines hereditary vs sporadic
  • Guides surveillance for patient and family

Systemic Staging (If Extraocular Disease Suspected)

  • CT/MRI, bone scan, bone marrow biopsy, lumbar puncture

Staging

Intraocular Retinoblastoma (Tumor Confined to Eye)

  • International Classification of Retinoblastoma (ICRB): Groups A-E
    • Group A: Small tumors; can treat focal therapy (laser, cryo)
    • Group B-D: Larger tumors; chemotherapy + focal therapy
    • Group E: Very advanced; poor potential for vision; enucleation usually needed

Extraocular Retinoblastoma (Tumor Spread Beyond Eye)

  • Regional (optic nerve, orbit)
  • Metastatic (brain, bone, bone marrow)
  • Much worse prognosis

Treatment

Goals: (1) Save life, (2) Save eye, (3) Save vision—in that priority order.

Chemotherapy (Systemically or Intra-Arterial)

  • Systemic chemotherapy: IV drugs (carboplatin, etoposide, vincristine)
    • Shrink tumor to allow eye-sparing focal therapy
    • Standard for Groups B-D
  • Intra-arterial chemotherapy (IAC): Catheter delivers chemo directly to ophthalmic artery
    • Higher local drug concentration, fewer systemic side effects
    • Increasingly used for advanced intraocular disease
  • Intravitreal chemotherapy: Injection into vitreous for vitreous seeding

Focal Therapy (For Small Tumors or After Chemo)

  • Laser photocoagulation: Destroys tumor blood supply
  • Cryotherapy: Freezes and destroys tumor
  • Used for Group A or residual tumors after chemo

Radiation Therapy (Less Common Now)

  • External beam radiation: Historically used; now avoided if possible due to risk of second cancers in hereditary RB
  • Plaque brachytherapy: Radioactive plaque sutured to sclera over tumor
    • Focal radiation; less risk than external beam

Enucleation (Eye Removal)

  • Indications: Group E eyes (no useful vision potential), unilateral advanced tumors, failure of other treatments
  • Curative if tumor confined to eye
  • Prosthetic eye fitted after healing

Bilateral Disease

  • Try to save both eyes if possible
  • May need enucleation of worse eye, treat better eye

Prognosis and Outcomes

Intraocular Retinoblastoma (Confined to Eye)

  • Survival: >95% in developed countries
  • Excellent cure rates with modern treatment

Extraocular Disease

  • Much worse prognosis; survival <50% if metastatic
  • Requires intensive chemotherapy

Vision Outcomes

  • Depends on tumor location and size
  • Macula-involving tumors → poor central vision even if tumor controlled
  • Peripheral tumors → can preserve good vision

Hereditary Retinoblastoma - Second Cancer Risk

  • Children with germline RB1 mutation have significantly increased risk of second cancers:
    • Osteosarcoma (bone cancer)
    • Soft tissue sarcomas
    • Melanoma, lung cancer (adults)
  • Risk higher if received radiation
  • Lifelong cancer surveillance required

Genetic Counseling and Surveillance

Genetic Testing

  • All retinoblastoma patients should have RB1 genetic testing
  • If germline mutation found:
    • Siblings need screening exams (under anesthesia every 2-4 months until age 3-5)
    • Future children 50% chance of inheriting mutation → need screening

Surveillance for Hereditary RB

  • Frequent eye exams until school age (higher risk of new tumors)
  • Lifelong monitoring for second cancers (annual exams, imaging as indicated)

Prenatal/Preimplantation Testing

  • Available for families with known RB1 mutation

When to See a Doctor

Urgent - See Ophthalmologist Immediately If:

  • White pupil (leukocoria) noticed in child—in person or in photos
  • New-onset strabismus (crossed eyes) in young child
  • Red, painful eye in infant/toddler

Family History

  • If family history of retinoblastoma, newborn should have exam by pediatric ophthalmologist within first weeks of life
  • Regular surveillance exams

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is the retinoblastoma unilateral or bilateral?
  • What group/stage is it?
  • Is it hereditary? Should my child have genetic testing?
  • What treatment do you recommend?
  • Can the eye be saved? Can vision be preserved?
  • What is the prognosis?
  • Do siblings need screening?
  • What is the risk of second cancers?

Bottom Line

Retinoblastoma is the most common eye cancer in children (typically <5 years old), arising from mutations in the RB1 tumor suppressor gene. About 40% are hereditary (germline mutation, often bilateral) and 60% are sporadic (non-hereditary, usually unilateral). The hallmark sign is leukocoria—a white pupillary reflex seen in flash photographs or by parents/pediatricians. Diagnosis is made by examination under anesthesia showing retinal mass with calcification, confirmed by imaging (ultrasound, MRI). When caught early (tumor confined to eye), survival exceeds 95%. Treatment depends on stage: small tumors treated with focal therapy (laser, cryo), larger tumors with chemotherapy (systemic or intra-arterial) to shrink followed by focal therapy, and advanced unilateral tumors with enucleation. Children with hereditary RB need lifelong surveillance for second cancers (osteosarcoma, sarcomas). Any white pupil in a child warrants urgent ophthalmology evaluation. Early detection saves lives and eyes.
