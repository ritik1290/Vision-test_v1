What Is Glaucoma?

Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that damage the optic nerve, which is crucial for transmitting visual information from your eye to your brain. This damage is often, but not always, caused by abnormally high pressure inside the eye (intraocular pressure or IOP). Glaucoma is one of the leading causes of blindness worldwide, particularly among older adults.

What makes glaucoma particularly dangerous is that it often develops gradually with no early warning signs or symptoms. The disease can steal your sight so gradually that you may not notice vision loss until the condition is at an advanced stage. This is why glaucoma is often called "the silent thief of sight."

Key Facts About Glaucoma

  • Second leading cause of blindness: Glaucoma is the second leading cause of blindness globally, affecting over 70 million people worldwide.
  • Irreversible damage: Vision loss from glaucoma cannot be recovered. However, with early detection and treatment, vision loss can be slowed or prevented.
  • Often symptomless: Most types of glaucoma have no early symptoms, making regular eye exams crucial.
  • Hereditary component: Having a family history of glaucoma increases your risk four to nine times.
  • Age factor: People over 60 are at increased risk, though glaucoma can occur at any age.
  • Treatable but not curable: While glaucoma cannot be cured, it can usually be controlled with treatment to prevent further vision loss.

The good news is that if caught early, glaucoma can usually be controlled, and vision loss can be minimized or prevented through medication, laser treatment, or surgery. Regular comprehensive eye exams are essential for early detection, especially if you have risk factors for the disease.

Understanding Eye Pressure and the Optic Nerve

To understand glaucoma, it's essential to know how eye pressure and the optic nerve work:

Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

Your eye is filled with fluid that maintains its shape and provides nutrients to internal structures. The front part of the eye is filled with a clear fluid called aqueous humor. This fluid is constantly produced by the ciliary body and drains out through a mesh-like drainage system called the trabecular meshwork, located in the angle where the iris meets the cornea.

When this drainage system works properly, the production and drainage of aqueous humor are balanced, maintaining a healthy eye pressure (normal range: 12-22 mmHg). However, if the drainage system becomes blocked or inefficient, fluid builds up, causing increased pressure inside the eye.

The Optic Nerve

The optic nerve is made up of more than a million nerve fibers that connect the retina to the brain. It acts like a cable transmitting images from your eye to your brain, which interprets these signals as vision. Damage to the optic nerve results in irreversible vision loss.

How Glaucoma Affects Vision

Elevated intraocular pressure can damage the optic nerve by:

  • Compressing nerve fibers
  • Reducing blood flow to the nerve
  • Causing mechanical stress on the nerve tissue

As nerve fibers are damaged and die, blind spots develop in your visual field. These blind spots typically begin in the peripheral (side) vision. If the entire nerve is destroyed, blindness results. Importantly, some people can have elevated eye pressure without developing glaucoma (ocular hypertension), while others develop glaucoma even with normal eye pressure (normal-tension glaucoma).

Types of Glaucoma

There are several types of glaucoma, each with different causes and characteristics:

1. Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

This is the most common form of glaucoma, accounting for about 90% of all cases. It's called "open-angle" because the drainage angle (where the iris meets the cornea) remains open, but the trabecular meshwork is partially blocked, causing gradual pressure buildup.

Characteristics:

  • Develops slowly and painlessly
  • No early warning signs
  • Peripheral vision is affected first
  • If untreated, progresses to central vision loss and blindness
  • Usually affects both eyes, though one may be more severely affected

2. Angle-Closure Glaucoma (Acute/Narrow-Angle Glaucoma)

This type occurs when the drainage angle becomes blocked or covered by part of the iris. This can happen suddenly (acute angle-closure glaucoma) or develop gradually (chronic angle-closure glaucoma).

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma:

  • Medical emergency requiring immediate treatment
  • Sudden, severe symptoms
  • Intense eye pain and headache
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Blurred vision and halos around lights
  • Eye redness
  • Can cause permanent vision loss if not treated quickly

Chronic Angle-Closure Glaucoma:

  • Develops slowly with few or no symptoms
  • Drainage angle gradually narrows
  • May go unnoticed until significant damage occurs

3. Normal-Tension Glaucoma (Low-Tension Glaucoma)

In this form of glaucoma, the optic nerve is damaged even though eye pressure remains within the normal range. The exact cause is unknown, but may involve poor blood flow to the optic nerve or structural weakness of the nerve.

Risk Factors:

  • Japanese ancestry
  • Family history of normal-tension glaucoma
  • History of cardiovascular disease
  • Low blood pressure

4. Secondary Glaucoma

This type results from other eye conditions or medical problems. Causes include:

  • Eye injuries or trauma
  • Eye inflammation (uveitis)
  • Advanced cataracts
  • Eye tumors
  • Diabetes (neovascular glaucoma)
  • Corticosteroid use
  • Eye surgery complications

5. Congenital/Pediatric Glaucoma

This rare form occurs in babies and young children due to incorrect or incomplete development of the eye's drainage system before birth.

Signs in infants:

  • Cloudy eyes
  • Excessive tearing
  • Sensitivity to light
  • Enlarged eyes

6. Pigmentary Glaucoma

This rare form occurs when pigment granules from the iris build up in the drainage channels, blocking fluid outflow and increasing eye pressure. It typically affects nearsighted men in their 20s to 40s.

Causes and Risk Factors

While the exact cause of glaucoma isn't always clear, several factors increase your risk:

Major Risk Factors You Cannot Control

Age

Risk increases significantly after age 60. For African Americans, the increased risk begins after age 40.

Family History

Having a parent or sibling with glaucoma increases your risk four to nine times. Certain genetic mutations have been linked to glaucoma.

Race and Ethnicity

  • African Americans: Six to eight times more likely to develop glaucoma than Caucasians, at a younger age, and with more severe vision loss
  • Hispanics/Latinos: At higher risk, especially after age 60
  • Asians: Higher risk of angle-closure glaucoma
  • Japanese: Higher risk of normal-tension glaucoma

Anatomical Factors

  • Thin central cornea
  • Optic nerve sensitivity
  • Narrow drainage angle
  • Thin or large optic nerve

Medical Conditions That Increase Risk

  • High eye pressure (though not everyone with high pressure develops glaucoma)
  • Diabetes: Increases risk of several types of glaucoma
  • High blood pressure
  • Heart disease
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Migraines
  • Sickle cell anemia

Other Risk Factors

  • Eye injuries: Trauma can cause immediate or delayed glaucoma
  • Previous eye surgery
  • Severe myopia (nearsightedness): Increases risk for open-angle glaucoma
  • Hyperopia (farsightedness): Increases risk for angle-closure glaucoma
  • Long-term corticosteroid use: Especially eye drops
  • Prolonged eye drop use: Some medications can affect eye pressure

Recognizing the Symptoms

Symptoms vary depending on the type of glaucoma:

Open-Angle Glaucoma Symptoms

Warning: Often No Early Symptoms

The most concerning aspect of open-angle glaucoma is that it typically has NO symptoms in its early stages. This is why regular eye exams are critical, especially if you have risk factors.

As the disease progresses, you may notice:

Patchy Blind Spots

Blind spots in peripheral (side) or central vision, frequently in both eyes. These spots gradually enlarge.

Tunnel Vision

In advanced stages, vision narrows to a small central area, like looking through a tunnel.

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma Symptoms

This is a medical emergency with sudden, severe symptoms:

Severe Eye Pain

Intense pain in the affected eye, often described as unbearable.

Severe Headache

Intense headache, usually on the same side as the affected eye.

Blurred Vision

Sudden blurred or hazy vision, seeing "haloes" around lights.

Red Eye

Eye appears red and inflamed.

Nausea & Vomiting

Often accompanying the severe pain and headache.

Rainbow Halos

Colored halos or rainbow-like rings around lights.

Seek Emergency Care Immediately

If you experience sudden eye pain, severe headache, blurred vision, or nausea/vomiting, seek emergency medical care immediately. Acute angle-closure glaucoma can cause permanent vision loss within hours if not treated.

Diagnosis and Testing

Comprehensive eye examination is the only way to detect glaucoma. A complete glaucoma evaluation includes several tests:

Essential Diagnostic Tests

1. Tonometry (Eye Pressure Measurement)

This test measures the pressure inside your eye (intraocular pressure or IOP). Several methods exist:

  • Applanation tonometry: The most common method, using a special device to gently flatten a portion of your cornea
  • Non-contact tonometry (air-puff test): Uses a puff of air to measure pressure
  • Electronic tonometry: A handheld device that touches the eye briefly

Normal eye pressure ranges from 12 to 22 mmHg, though some people develop glaucoma with normal pressure, and others can have high pressure without glaucoma.

2. Ophthalmoscopy (Optic Nerve Examination)

Your eye doctor examines the optic nerve for damage by looking inside your eye with a special magnifying instrument called an ophthalmoscope. Signs of glaucoma include:

  • Cupping of the optic disc (hollowing or deepening)
  • Changes in color of the optic nerve
  • Bleeding
  • Other abnormalities

3. Visual Field Test (Perimetry)

This test checks for blind spots in your peripheral and central vision. You'll look straight ahead while lights flash in your peripheral vision, and you'll indicate when you can see them. This test can detect even subtle vision changes and help monitor glaucoma progression.

4. Gonioscopy

This test examines the drainage angle of your eye where fluid drains out. A special contact lens with a mirror is placed on your eye to allow the doctor to see if the angle is open, narrow, or closed. This helps determine the type of glaucoma.

5. Pachymetry (Corneal Thickness Measurement)

This quick, painless test measures the thickness of your cornea using ultrasound. People with thinner corneas may be at higher risk for glaucoma, and corneal thickness can affect eye pressure readings.

6. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

This advanced imaging test creates detailed, cross-sectional images of the optic nerve and retina. It can detect nerve fiber damage before vision loss occurs and is useful for monitoring disease progression.

7. Optic Nerve Photography

Photos of your optic nerve provide a baseline for comparing future exams and tracking changes over time.

Screening Recommendations

The American Academy of Ophthalmology recommends comprehensive eye examinations:

  • Under 40 with risk factors: Every 2-4 years
  • Age 40-54: Every 2-4 years
  • Age 55-64: Every 1-3 years
  • Age 65 and older: Every 1-2 years
  • High-risk individuals: Annually or as recommended by your doctor

Treatment Goals and Options

The goal of glaucoma treatment is to prevent further vision loss by lowering eye pressure or improving blood flow to the optic nerve. The damage already done cannot be reversed, making early detection and treatment crucial.

Treatment Approaches

Treatment options include medications, laser therapy, and surgery. Your doctor will recommend treatment based on:

  • Type and severity of glaucoma
  • Current eye pressure
  • Rate of disease progression
  • Overall health and ability to comply with treatment
  • Potential side effects
  • Cost and insurance coverage

Treatment Is Lifelong

Glaucoma is a chronic condition requiring lifelong treatment and monitoring. Even if your eye pressure normalizes with treatment, you'll need to continue therapy and regular checkups. Stopping treatment can lead to rapid progression of vision loss.

Medications and Eye Drops

Eye drops are usually the first line of treatment for glaucoma. They work by either decreasing fluid production in the eye or improving fluid drainage. Sometimes oral medications are prescribed as well.

Types of Glaucoma Medications

Medication Class How It Works Common Side Effects
Prostaglandin Analogs
(Latanoprost, Travoprost, Bimatoprost)		 Increase fluid outflow from the eye. Usually once-daily dosing. Eye color change, eyelash growth, redness, stinging
Beta Blockers
(Timolol, Betaxolol)		 Reduce fluid production in the eye. Slowed heart rate, low blood pressure, fatigue, breathing problems
Alpha Agonists
(Brimonidine, Apraclonidine)		 Reduce fluid production and increase drainage. Dry mouth, fatigue, redness, allergic reactions
Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors
(Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide)		 Decrease fluid production. Bitter taste, frequent urination, tingling in fingers/toes
Rho Kinase Inhibitors
(Netarsudil)		 Increase fluid outflow through the trabecular meshwork. Eye redness, deposits on cornea, pain
Combination Drops Combine two medications in one drop for convenience. Combination of individual medication side effects

Tips for Using Eye Drops Correctly

  • Wash your hands before applying drops
  • Tilt your head back or lie down
  • Pull down your lower eyelid to create a pocket
  • Look up and squeeze one drop into the pocket
  • Close your eye gently for 1-2 minutes
  • Press gently on the inner corner of your eye to prevent drainage
  • Wait at least 5 minutes between different eye drops
  • Don't touch the dropper tip to anything
  • Use drops exactly as prescribed, even if you feel fine

Oral Medications

If eye drops aren't sufficient, oral carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide, methazolamide) may be prescribed. These medications are particularly useful for acute angle-closure glaucoma. Side effects can include frequent urination, tingling in fingers and toes, loss of appetite, and kidney stones with long-term use.

Laser Therapy and Surgical Options

If medications don't adequately control eye pressure or you can't tolerate them, your doctor may recommend laser treatment or surgery.

Laser Procedures

1. Laser Trabeculoplasty

Used for open-angle glaucoma, this procedure uses a laser to improve drainage through the trabecular meshwork.

  • SLT (Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty): Uses low-energy laser pulses, can be repeated
  • ALT (Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty): Older technique, less commonly used now

The procedure is painless, takes about 10-15 minutes, and can reduce or eliminate the need for eye drops. Effects may last several years but can diminish over time.

2. Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)

Used primarily for angle-closure glaucoma, the laser creates a small hole in the iris to allow fluid to flow more freely. This procedure prevents or treats acute angle-closure attacks and is often recommended preventively for narrow angles.

3. Laser Cyclophotocoagulation

Used when other treatments have failed, this laser treats the ciliary body (which produces aqueous fluid), reducing fluid production. It's typically reserved for severe cases.

Incisional Surgery

1. Trabeculectomy

The most common glaucoma surgery, trabeculectomy creates a new drainage channel for fluid to leave the eye. The surgeon makes a small flap in the sclera (white of the eye) and creates a filtration bleb (a small bubble) where fluid can collect and drain.

Success rate: About 60-80% of patients maintain good pressure control for 3-5 years. The procedure can be repeated if necessary.

2. Glaucoma Drainage Implants (Tube Shunts)

A small tube is inserted into the eye to help drain fluid. This is often used when trabeculectomy has failed or is likely to fail.

3. Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)

Newer techniques that are less invasive with faster recovery:

  • iStent: Tiny stent placed in the drainage angle
  • Trabectome: Removes part of the trabecular meshwork
  • XEN Gel Stent: Soft, gel-like tube that creates a drainage pathway
  • Kahook Dual Blade: Removes a strip of trabecular meshwork

MIGS procedures have lower risk profiles than traditional surgery but may not lower pressure as dramatically. They're often performed during cataract surgery.

Surgery for Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Acute angle-closure glaucoma is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment:

  • Immediate medications to lower eye pressure
  • Laser peripheral iridotomy as soon as inflammation subsides
  • Often preventive treatment in the other eye

Living with Glaucoma

A glaucoma diagnosis can be overwhelming, but with proper treatment and lifestyle adjustments, most people with glaucoma can maintain their vision and quality of life.

Medication Adherence

One of the biggest challenges with glaucoma is remembering to use eye drops consistently:

  • Set daily alarms or reminders
  • Link drops to daily routines (brushing teeth, meals)
  • Keep drops in a visible location
  • Use a medication tracking app
  • Ask your pharmacist about reminder services
  • If you miss a dose, use it as soon as you remember, but don't double up

Lifestyle Modifications

Exercise

Regular aerobic exercise can help lower eye pressure. However:

  • Start slowly and build up gradually
  • Avoid exercises that involve head-down positions (some yoga poses, headstands)
  • Be cautious with activities that involve breath-holding
  • Stay hydrated during exercise

Diet and Nutrition

  • Eat a healthy diet rich in green leafy vegetables
  • Omega-3 fatty acids may be beneficial (fish, flaxseed)
  • Limit caffeine intake (can temporarily raise eye pressure)
  • Avoid drinking large amounts of fluid quickly
  • Maintain a healthy weight

Eye Protection

  • Wear protective eyewear during sports and activities that risk eye injury
  • Wear sunglasses to protect from UV rays
  • Sleep with your head elevated (may help reduce pressure in some cases)

Emotional and Mental Health

Living with a chronic condition affecting your vision can be stressful:

  • Join a support group for people with glaucoma
  • Talk to a counselor if you're feeling anxious or depressed
  • Stay informed about your condition
  • Maintain social connections and activities
  • Be open with family and friends about your needs

Driving and Activities

  • Be aware of your peripheral vision limitations
  • Extra caution at intersections and when changing lanes
  • Avoid night driving if it's difficult
  • Consider driving rehabilitation training
  • Know when it may be time to limit or stop driving

Work and Daily Tasks

  • Ensure good lighting in your workspace
  • Use contrasting colors to help identify objects
  • Organize your environment to reduce fall risks
  • Consider low vision aids if needed
  • Inform your employer about any necessary accommodations

Prevention and Risk Reduction

While you can't prevent glaucoma entirely, early detection and protective measures can help preserve your vision:

Regular Eye Exams

This is the single most important preventive measure. Follow recommended screening guidelines, especially if you have risk factors.

Know Your Family History

Tell your eye doctor if glaucoma runs in your family. You may need more frequent screening.

Protect Your Eyes

  • Wear protective eyewear during sports and hazardous activities
  • Wear sunglasses with UV protection

Maintain Overall Health

  • Control diabetes and high blood pressure
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly
  • Don't smoke

Be Cautious with Medications

  • Inform doctors that you have glaucoma before taking steroids
  • Be aware that some over-the-counter eye drops may not be suitable
  • Check with your doctor before starting new medications

When to See a Doctor

Seek Emergency Care Immediately If You Experience:

  • Sudden severe eye pain
  • Sudden severe headache
  • Nausea and vomiting with eye pain
  • Sudden blurry vision or vision loss
  • Seeing halos around lights
  • Red eye with pain

These may be signs of acute angle-closure glaucoma, which requires immediate treatment to prevent permanent vision loss.

Schedule a Routine Eye Exam If:

  • You're due for a comprehensive eye exam based on your age and risk factors
  • You have a family history of glaucoma
  • You notice any gradual changes in your vision
  • You have other risk factors for glaucoma
  • You haven't had an eye exam in the past 1-2 years (if you're over 60)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of glaucoma do I have?
  • How advanced is my glaucoma?
  • What is my target eye pressure?
  • How often do I need to be monitored?
  • What are my treatment options?
  • What are the side effects of recommended treatments?
  • Will I need surgery?
  • How can I prevent progression?
  • Are there any activities I should avoid?
  • What is my prognosis?
