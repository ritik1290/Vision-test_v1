What Is Age-Related Macular Degeneration?

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD or ARMD) is a progressive eye disease that affects the macula, the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed central vision. AMD is the leading cause of severe, permanent vision loss in adults over age 60 in developed countries, affecting millions of people worldwide.

The disease damages the macula, causing a gradual or sometimes sudden loss of central vision needed for activities like reading, recognizing faces, driving, and seeing fine details. While AMD rarely causes complete blindness—peripheral (side) vision usually remains intact—the loss of central vision can significantly impact quality of life and independence.

Key Facts About AMD

  • Leading cause of vision loss: AMD is the #1 cause of blindness in people over 65 in Western countries.
  • Progressive disease: AMD typically worsens over time, though the rate of progression varies widely.
  • Central vision loss: AMD affects central vision but typically spares peripheral vision.
  • Age-related: Risk increases dramatically after age 60, though it can occur earlier.
  • Two forms: Dry AMD (90% of cases) progresses slowly; wet AMD (10%) is more severe but treatable.
  • Non-reversible: Damage from AMD cannot be reversed, but treatments can slow progression.
  • Both eyes affected: AMD usually affects both eyes, though often asymmetrically.

Early detection is crucial because treatments exist that can significantly slow disease progression, particularly for wet AMD. Understanding your risk factors and monitoring your vision regularly can help preserve your sight.

Understanding the Macula and Retina

To understand AMD, it's essential to know how the macula functions:

The Retina

The retina is a thin layer of light-sensitive tissue lining the back of your eye. It contains millions of photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) that convert light into electrical signals, which travel through the optic nerve to your brain where they're interpreted as images.

The Macula

The macula is a small, specialized area at the center of the retina, about 5mm in diameter. Despite its small size, the macula is responsible for:

  • Central vision: What you see when you look directly at something
  • Fine detail: Reading, recognizing faces, threading a needle
  • Color vision: The macula contains the highest concentration of cone cells responsible for color perception

The Fovea

At the center of the macula is the fovea, an even tinier area responsible for the sharpest vision. The fovea contains only cone cells packed very densely together.

What Happens in AMD?

In AMD, cellular debris called drusen accumulates beneath the retina, and the delicate cells of the macula become damaged and die. The retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)—a layer of cells that nourishes the retina—stops working properly. This leads to progressive deterioration of the macula and loss of central vision.

Types of AMD: Dry vs. Wet

AMD exists in two distinct forms, each with different characteristics and treatment approaches:

Dry AMD (Non-Neovascular, Atrophic AMD)

Dry AMD accounts for about 85-90% of all AMD cases. It's characterized by the presence of drusen (yellow deposits under the retina) and gradual thinning of the macula.

Characteristics of Dry AMD:

  • Slow, gradual progression over many years
  • Drusen (tiny yellow or white deposits) accumulate under the retina
  • Gradual break down of light-sensitive cells in the macula
  • Macula becomes thinner (atrophy) over time
  • Usually affects both eyes, though often asymmetrically
  • May or may not progress to wet AMD

Types of Drusen:

  • Small drusen: Normal age-related changes, minimal risk
  • Medium drusen: Moderate risk for AMD progression
  • Large drusen: Higher risk, often indicates intermediate AMD

Geographic Atrophy:

The advanced stage of dry AMD is called geographic atrophy, where patches of the retinal tissue waste away, creating well-defined areas of vision loss. This form progresses slowly but causes significant central vision loss.

Wet AMD (Neovascular, Exudative AMD)

Wet AMD accounts for only 10-15% of AMD cases but causes about 90% of severe vision loss from AMD. It's characterized by abnormal blood vessel growth beneath the retina.

Characteristics of Wet AMD:

  • Rapid progression—can cause severe vision loss in days to weeks
  • Abnormal blood vessels grow under the macula (choroidal neovascularization)
  • These blood vessels leak fluid and blood into the retina
  • Causes swelling and damage to the macula
  • Can lead to scarring if untreated
  • More treatable than dry AMD with current therapies

Development:

Wet AMD typically develops from dry AMD, though not everyone with dry AMD progresses to the wet form. About 10-15% of people with dry AMD eventually develop wet AMD in one or both eyes. Once wet AMD develops in one eye, there's a higher risk of it developing in the other eye.

Important: Early Treatment Crucial for Wet AMD

Wet AMD is a medical urgency (though not always an emergency). Early treatment with anti-VEGF injections can preserve vision and sometimes improve it. If you notice sudden changes in your vision, contact your eye doctor immediately—treatment within days can make a significant difference in outcomes.

Stages and Progression of AMD

AMD is classified into three stages based on the size and number of drusen and the presence of retinal changes:

Early AMD

  • Medium-sized drusen present
  • No vision loss or symptoms
  • Detected only through eye examination
  • Low risk of progression to advanced AMD (about 1% over 5 years)

Management: Regular monitoring, lifestyle modifications, and protective measures.

Intermediate AMD

  • Large drusen and/or pigment changes in the retina
  • May have mild vision loss but often no symptoms
  • Some people notice difficulty adapting to low light
  • Moderate risk of progression (about 18% over 5 years)

Management: AREDS2 vitamins, close monitoring, lifestyle changes, and using an Amsler grid at home.

Late/Advanced AMD

This stage involves either:

  • Geographic atrophy (dry AMD): Breakdown of retinal cells causing distinct patches of vision loss
  • Neovascular AMD (wet AMD): Abnormal blood vessel growth with leakage

Both forms cause significant central vision loss.

Management: Treatment for wet AMD with anti-VEGF injections; low vision rehabilitation; AREDS2 vitamins for the unaffected eye.

Rate of Progression

Progression varies widely:

  • Some people remain in early stages for decades
  • Others progress to advanced AMD within a few years
  • Wet AMD can progress very rapidly without treatment
  • Having AMD in one eye increases risk in the other eye

Causes and Risk Factors

AMD is a complex disease resulting from a combination of genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors:

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

Age

Age is the strongest risk factor. AMD is rare before age 55 but increases dramatically with age:

  • Age 55-64: About 2% have AMD
  • Age 65-74: About 6-7% have AMD
  • Age 75+: About 15-30% have AMD

Genetics and Family History

Genetics play a major role in AMD risk:

  • Family history increases risk 3-6 times
  • Several genes associated with AMD have been identified (including CFH, ARMS2, C3)
  • Genetic testing is available but not routinely recommended
  • Having a first-degree relative with AMD significantly increases your risk

Race and Ethnicity

  • Caucasians have the highest risk
  • Lower risk in African Americans and Hispanics
  • Intermediate risk in Asians

Gender

Women have a slightly higher risk than men, possibly due to longer life expectancy.

Modifiable Risk Factors

Smoking

Smoking is the most significant modifiable risk factor:

  • Current smokers have 2-3 times higher risk
  • Risk increases with pack-years of smoking
  • Quitting reduces risk over time, though it may take years
  • Secondhand smoke exposure may also increase risk

Cardiovascular Health

  • High blood pressure
  • High cholesterol
  • Atherosclerosis
  • History of heart disease or stroke

Obesity

Being overweight or obese, especially with excess abdominal fat, increases AMD risk and progression.

Diet and Nutrition

  • Diet high in saturated fats may increase risk
  • Low intake of antioxidants and omega-3 fatty acids may increase risk
  • Diet low in green leafy vegetables and fish associated with higher risk

Sun Exposure

Chronic exposure to UV and blue light may contribute to AMD, though evidence is mixed.

Eye Color

People with light-colored eyes (blue or green) may have slightly higher risk.

Recognizing the Symptoms

AMD symptoms develop gradually in dry AMD but can appear suddenly in wet AMD:

Early Stage Symptoms

Early AMD usually has NO symptoms. This is why regular eye exams are critical.

Intermediate and Advanced Dry AMD Symptoms

Blurred Central Vision

Gradual loss of sharpness in central vision, making it harder to see fine details.

Difficulty Reading

Need for brighter light when reading; words may appear blurred or missing.

Face Recognition Problems

Difficulty recognizing faces, even of familiar people.

Decreased Color Intensity

Colors appear less vivid or bright than before.

Dark or Empty Area

A blurry spot or blind spot in the center of your visual field.

Slow Dark Adaptation

Difficulty adjusting to low light levels or moving from bright to dim environments.

Wet AMD Symptoms

Wet AMD symptoms often appear suddenly and progress rapidly:

Visual Distortions

Straight lines appear wavy, bent, or crooked (metamorphopsia)—a hallmarkHow symptom of wet AMD.

Central Blind Spot

A dark, blurry area or "white-out" in the center of vision that appears suddenly.

Objects Appear Smaller

Objects may look smaller than they should (micropsia).

Rapid Vision Loss

Sudden and rapid decrease in central vision quality.

Amsler Grid Test

If you have AMD or are at risk, your doctor will likely give you an Amsler grid—a simple chart with a grid pattern. Test each eye daily. If you notice new distortions, wavy lines, or blank spots, contact your eye doctor immediately, as this could indicate wet AMD requiring urgent treatment.

Diagnosis and Testing

AMD is diagnosed through a comprehensive dilated eye examination and specialized imaging tests:

Comprehensive Eye Exam

1. Visual Acuity Test

Measures how well you can see at various distances using an eye chart.

2. Dilated Eye Exam

After dilating your pupils, your doctor examines the retina and macula for signs of AMD:

  • Drusen (yellow deposits)
  • Pigment changes
  • Areas of atrophy
  • Hemorrhages or fluid accumulation (wet AMD)

3. Amsler Grid Test

You look at a grid pattern and report any distortions, wavy lines, or missing areas—signs that may indicate wet AMD.

Advanced Imaging Tests

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

The gold standard for AMD diagnosis and monitoring. OCT uses light waves to create detailed cross-sectional images of the retina, showing:

  • Retinal thickness
  • Fluid accumulation
  • Drusen size and characteristics
  • Geographic atrophy
  • Subtle changes over time

OCT is painless, non-invasive, and takes only minutes.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

Used primarily when wet AMD is suspected:

  • A fluorescent dye is injected into a vein in your arm
  • As the dye travels through blood vessels in your eye, photographs are taken
  • Reveals abnormal blood vessel growth and leakage
  • Helps determine treatment approach

OCT Angiography (OCTA)

A newer, non-invasive imaging technique that shows blood flow in the retina without injecting dye.

Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

Detects changes in the retinal pigment epithelium, useful for tracking dry AMD progression.

Color Fundus Photography

Photographs of your retina create a baseline for comparison in future visits.

Treatment Options

Treatment varies significantly between dry and wet AMD:

Treatment for Dry AMD

Currently, there's no cure or FDA-approved treatment to reverse dry AMD. However, interventions can slow progression:

AREDS2 Vitamins

The Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 (AREDS2) found that a specific combination of vitamins and minerals can reduce the risk of progression to advanced AMD in people with intermediate or advanced AMD in one eye:

AREDS2 Formula:

  • Vitamin C: 500 mg
  • Vitamin E: 400 IU
  • Lutein: 10 mg
  • Zeaxanthin: 2 mg
  • Zinc: 80 mg (as zinc oxide)
  • Copper: 2 mg (as cupric oxide)

Important notes:

  • Only recommended for intermediate or advanced AMD—not early AMD
  • Slows but doesn't stop progression
  • Widely available as over-the-counter supplements labeled "AREDS2"
  • Talk to your doctor before starting, especially if you smoke (high-dose beta-carotene increases lung cancer risk in smokers)

Emerging Treatments for Geographic Atrophy

In 2023, the FDA approved the first treatments for geographic atrophy:

  • Pegcetacoplan (Syfovre): Injected into the eye every 25-60 days
  • Avacincaptad pegol (Izervay): Injected monthly

These medications slow (but don't stop) the growth of geographic atrophy. Discuss with your retina specialist if you have advanced dry AMD.

Treatment for Wet AMD

Wet AMD is much more treatable, and early intervention can preserve or even improve vision:

Anti-VEGF Injections

The primary treatment for wet AMD, anti-VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) medications stop abnormal blood vessel growth and reduce fluid leakage:

Common anti-VEGF medications:

  • Aflibercept (Eylea): Usually given every 4-8 weeks initially
  • Ranibizumab (Lucentis): Typically monthly injections
  • Brolucizumab (Beovu): May allow for longer intervals between injections
  • Bevacizumab (Avastin): Off-label use, less expensive option
  • Faricimab (Vabysmo): Newer medication allowing extended dosing intervals

What to expect:

  • Injections are given in the office setting
  • Eye is numbed with drops—procedure is usually painless
  • Takes only a few minutes
  • Initial "loading phase" of monthly injections for 3 months
  • Maintenance injections as needed, guided by OCT monitoring
  • Treatment is often lifelong

Effectiveness:

  • About 90% of patients maintain vision
  • About 30-40% experience vision improvement
  • Best results when treatment starts early

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

Less commonly used now, PDT involves:

  • Injecting a light-sensitive drug (verteporfin) into your bloodstream
  • Activating the drug with a laser applied to the abnormal blood vessels
  • Sometimes combined with anti-VEGF therapy

Laser Photocoagulation

Rarely used today, high-energy laser destroys abnormal blood vessels. It causes a permanent blind spot and is only used for specific cases away from the center of the macula.

Living with AMD

AMD can significantly impact daily life, but many strategies and tools can help maintain independence and quality of life:

Low Vision Aids and Devices

  • Magnifiers: Handheld, stand, electronic, or video magnifiers for reading
  • Large-print materials: Books, newspapers, playing cards, phone keypads
  • High-contrast items: Bold-line paper, high-contrast watches, clocks
  • Screen readers and voice-activated devices
  • Telescopic lenses: For distance viewing
  • Filters and special glasses: To reduce glare and enhance contrast
  • Smartphone apps: Text-to-speech, magnification, object identification

Home Modifications

  • Install brighter lighting throughout your home
  • Use task lighting for specific activities
  • Mark edges of stairs with brightly colored tape
  • Use contrasting colors (dark plates on light tablecloths)
  • Remove tripping hazards
  • Install grab bars in bathrooms
  • Label items with large print or raised markings

Vision Rehabilitation

Low vision specialists and occupational therapists can teach you:

  • Techniques to make the most of remaining vision
  • How to use low vision aids effectively
  • Eccentric viewing techniques (using peripheral vision for tasks)
  • Strategies for daily activities

Emotional and Psychological Support

Vision loss can lead to depression, anxiety, and social isolation:

  • Join AMD support groups (in-person or online)
  • Consider counseling or therapy
  • Stay socially active
  • Be open with family and friends about your needs
  • Contact organizations like the American Macular Degeneration Foundation

Maintaining Independence

  • Transportation: Explore senior transportation services, ride-sharing
  • Hobbies: Adapt activities you enjoy with low vision aids
  • Exercise: Stay physically active to maintain overall health
  • Technology: Learn to use assistive technology
  • Home safety: Make modifications to prevent falls

Prevention and Risk Reduction

While you can't prevent AMD entirely, lifestyle modifications can significantly reduce risk or slow progression:

Quit Smoking

This is the single most important modifiable risk factor. If you smoke, quit—and avoid secondhand smoke.

Eat a Healthy Diet

A diet rich in specific nutrients may protect against AMD:

  • Leafy greens: Spinach, kale, collard greens (high in lutein and zeaxanthin)
  • Fish: Salmon, tuna, sardines (omega-3 fatty acids)—at least twice weekly
  • Colorful fruits and vegetables: Carrots, peppers, berries, oranges
  • Nuts and legumes: Almonds, walnuts, lentils
  • Whole grains
  • Limit saturated fats and refined carbohydrates

Protect Your Eyes from UV Light

  • Wear sunglasses that block 99-100% of UV-A and UV-B rays
  • Wear a wide-brimmed hat outdoors
  • Consider blue-light filtering lenses

Maintain Cardiovascular Health

  • Control blood pressure
  • Manage cholesterol levels
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly (at least 150 minutes of moderate activity weekly)
  • Manage diabetes if present

Regular Eye Exams

  • Annual dilated eye exams after age 60
  • More frequent if you have risk factors
  • Early detection allows for timely intervention

Monitor Your Vision Daily

If you have AMD or are at risk:

  • Use an Amsler grid daily to check each eye
  • Report any changes immediately to your eye doctor
  • Don't wait for your next scheduled appointment if you notice changes

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Care If You Notice:

  • Sudden appearance of distorted vision (straight lines appearing wavy)
  • Rapid decrease in central vision
  • Sudden appearance of a blind spot in your central vision
  • Sudden increase in blurriness

These may indicate wet AMD requiring urgent treatment. Contact your eye doctor the same day—outcomes are better when treatment starts quickly.

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • You notice gradual changes in your central vision
  • Colors seem less vibrant
  • You're having more difficulty reading or recognizing faces
  • You haven't had an eye exam in over a year and you're over 60
  • You have a family history of AMD

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type and stage of AMD do I have?
  • How likely is my AMD to progress?
  • Should I take AREDS2 vitamins?
  • How often should I have follow-up exams?
  • What symptoms should prompt me to call immediately?
  • Am I eligible for any clinical trials?
  • What can I do to slow progression?
  • When should I consider low vision rehabilitation?
