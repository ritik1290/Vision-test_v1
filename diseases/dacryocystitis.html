What Is Dacryocystitis?

Dacryocystitis is an infection or inflammation of the lacrimal sac—the tear drainage sac located between the inner corner of the eye and the nose. The lacrimal sac collects tears from the eye and drains them through the nasolacrimal duct (tear duct) into the nose. When this drainage system becomes blocked, tears and bacteria accumulate in the sac, leading to infection. Dacryocystitis typically presents as painful swelling, redness, and tenderness in the area just below the inner corner of the eye (near the bridge of the nose), often accompanied by excessive tearing and discharge.

The condition can be acute (sudden-onset infection with pain, swelling, and sometimes abscess formation) or chronic (persistent low-grade inflammation with intermittent tearing and mucoid discharge). Dacryocystitis is most common in infants (from congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction) and in adults over 40 (especially postmenopausal women, from acquired duct narrowing). Treatment depends on severity and chronicity: acute cases require antibiotics and warm compresses, while chronic or recurrent cases often need surgical creation of a new drainage pathway (dacryocystorhinostomy or DCR).

Key Facts About Dacryocystitis

  • Infected tear sac: Bacterial infection of lacrimal sac from blocked drainage.
  • Painful swelling near nose: Tender, red lump below inner corner of eye.
  • Excessive tearing: Tears overflow down cheek (epiphora).
  • Caused by obstruction: Blocked nasolacrimal duct prevents tear drainage.
  • Acute vs. chronic: Acute—sudden, painful; chronic—recurrent, milder.
  • More common in infants and older women
  • Treatment: antibiotics +/- surgery: Acute managed medically; chronic/recurrent need DCR.
  • Can form abscess: May require incision and drainage.

Understanding dacryocystitis helps with early recognition and appropriate treatment to prevent complications.

Types of Dacryocystitis

Acute Dacryocystitis

  • Sudden-onset bacterial infection of lacrimal sac
  • Presentation:
    • Painful, red, swollen lump below inner canthus (corner of eye)
    • Warm to touch
    • Tenderness
    • Purulent (pus) discharge expressible from puncta (tear duct openings)
    • Excessive tearing
    • Sometimes fever
  • Can progress to abscess formation (pointing, fluctuant mass)
  • Medical emergency if preseptal/orbital cellulitis develops

Chronic Dacryocystitis

  • Persistent low-grade inflammation of lacrimal sac
  • Presentation:
    • Mild swelling near lacrimal sac (often not obvious externally)
    • Chronic excessive tearing (epiphora)
    • Mucoid or mucopurulent discharge from puncta with gentle pressure on sac
    • Intermittent acute exacerbations
  • Less dramatic than acute form
  • Annoying, affects quality of life

Congenital Dacryocystocele

  • In newborns: lacrimal sac distended with fluid from birth
  • Bluish cystic swelling below medial canthus
  • Can become infected (acute dacryocystitis in neonate)

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Cause: Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

  • Blockage prevents tear drainage from lacrimal sac into nose
  • Stagnant tears + bacteria → infection

Causes of Obstruction

In Infants

  • Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction:
    • Membrane (Hasner's valve) at distal end of duct fails to open
    • Affects ~6% of newborns
    • Usually resolves spontaneously by 12 months
    • Can lead to dacryocystitis if infected

In Adults

  • Involutional (age-related) narrowing/stenosis: Most common; duct narrows with age
  • Chronic inflammation: Recurrent infections, dacryocystitis begets more dacryocystitis
  • Nasal/sinus disease: Polyps, chronic sinusitis, deviated septum
  • Trauma: Facial fractures involving nasolacrimal duct
  • Previous nasal surgery
  • Tumors: Rare; lacrimal sac or nasal tumors obstructing duct
  • Stones (dacryoliths): Concretions in sac/duct

Risk Factors

Age and Sex

  • Infants: Congenital obstruction
  • Adults >40, especially postmenopausal women: Hormonal changes may contribute to duct narrowing

Chronic Nasal/Sinus Issues

  • Chronic rhinosinusitis, allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps

Previous Facial Trauma or Surgery

  • Disruption of normal anatomy

Chemotherapy

  • Some agents (e.g., docetaxel, 5-FU) can cause lacrimal duct stenosis

Bacterial Pathogens (Common)

  • *Staphylococcus aureus*: Most common
  • *Streptococcus pneumoniae*
  • *Haemophilus influenzae* (especially in children)
  • Anaerobes in chronic cases

Recognizing the Symptoms

Acute Dacryocystitis Symptoms

Painful Swelling

Tender, red lump below inner corner of eye, near bridge of nose.

Warmth and Redness

Area hot to touch; skin overlying sac red and inflamed.

Purulent Discharge

Pus expressible from puncta (tear duct openings) when pressing on sac.

Excessive Tearing (Epiphora)

Tears overflow down cheek; drainage blocked.

Fever (Sometimes)

Systemic signs if infection severe.

Conjunctival Redness

Eye itself may be red from irritation/infection spread.

Chronic Dacryocystitis Symptoms

  • Chronic tearing (bothersome, constant)
  • Mucoid discharge from eye
  • Mild swelling near lacrimal sac (may or may not be visible)
  • Discharge expressible from puncta with gentle pressure
  • Episodic acute exacerbations (painful swelling episodes)

Infant Presentation

  • Tearing since birth or early infancy
  • Mucoid discharge, mattering of eyelashes
  • If infected: red, swollen mass near inner corner of eye

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

History

  • Painful swelling near nose? Tearing? Discharge?
  • Previous episodes?
  • Nasal/sinus problems? Trauma? Surgery?

External Inspection

  • Swelling, redness, tenderness over lacrimal sac (medial to medial canthus, below medial canthal tendon)
  • Expressible discharge from puncta with digital pressure on sac (pathognomonic for dacryo cystitis)

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Assess puncta patency
  • Conjunctivitis, discharge

Irrigation and Probing

  • Lacrimal irrigation: flush saline through puncta/canaliculus
    • If duct blocked, fluid doesn't pass into nose; refluxes back through puncta
    • Confirms nasolacrimal duct obstruction
  • Probing: passage of probe through system (usually not done acutely due to infection)

Imaging (If Needed)

  • Dacryocystography (DCG): Contrast injected into lacrimal system, X-rays show obstruction level
  • CT scan: If abscess, orbital involvement, or mass suspected
  • Usually clinical diagnosis sufficient

Culture

  • If purulent discharge, culture to guide antibiotic choice
  • Especially if not responding to empiric antibiotics

Treatment

Acute Dacryocystitis

Medical Management (First-Line)

  • Oral antibiotics:
    • Empiric broad-spectrum covering Staph, Strep
    • Choices: Amoxicillin-clavulanate, cephalexin, clindamycin (if penicillin allergy)
    • 7-14 days
    • Adjust based on culture if done
  • Warm compresses:
    • Apply to affected area 4-6 times daily
    • Promotes drainage, reduces discomfort
  • Topical antibiotic drops:
    • If concurrent conjunctivitis
  • Avoid massaging sac: During acute infection, could spread

Abscess Formation

  • If fluctuant abscess develops:
    • Incision and drainage (I&D): Relieve pressure, drain pus
    • Continue antibiotics

Hospitalization and IV Antibiotics

  • If severe: orbital/preseptal cellulitis, high fever, immunocompromised patient

After Acute Infection Resolves

  • Address underlying obstruction (see below) to prevent recurrence

Chronic Dacryocystitis

Conservative Measures (Temporary Relief)

  • Warm compresses
  • Lacrimal sac massage (express discharge)
  • Topical antibiotics for discharge

Definitive Treatment: Surgery (Dacryocystorhinostomy, DCR)

  • Required for chronic/recurrent cases (see below)

Infants with Congenital Obstruction

  • Observation + lacrimal sac massage: Most resolve by 12 months
  • If infected (acute dacryocystitis): Oral antibiotics
  • If persistent beyond 12 months: Probing and irrigation (office or OR procedure to open membrane)

Surgical Treatment: Dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR)

Indications

  • Chronic dacryocystitis not responding to conservative management
  • Recurrent acute dacryocystitis
  • Persistent nasolacrimal duct obstruction with symptomatic tearing

Goal of DCR

  • Create new drainage pathway from lacrimal sac directly into nasal cavity, bypassing obstructed nasolacrimal duct
  • Restores tear drainage
  • Eliminates infection source

Techniques

External DCR

  • Incision on side of nose (near medial canthus)
  • Bone removed to create opening between sac and nasal cavity
  • Sac and nasal mucosa sutured together (anastomosis)
  • Silicone stent often placed temporarily to keep pathway open
  • High success rate (~90-95%)
  • Small external scar (fades over time)

Endonasal (Endoscopic) DCR

  • Performed through nostril with endoscope (no external incision)
  • Bone removed from inside nose
  • Cosmetic advantage (no scar)
  • Success rate similar (~85-90%)
  • Requires specialized equipment/training

Post-Operative Care

  • Nasal irrigation, nasal steroid sprays
  • Oral antibiotics
  • Sil icone stents removed after 4-12 weeks

Success Rate

  • Excellent (~90%+ resolution of tearing and infection)

Complications

Abscess Formation

  • Pus-filled mass
  • May point and drain spontaneously or require I&D

Preseptal Cellulitis

  • Infection spreads to eyelid tissues
  • Eyelid swelling, redness
  • Requires systemic antibiotics

Orbital Cellulitis (Rare, Serious)

  • Spread of infection behind orbital septum into orbit
  • Eye movement pain, proptosis, vision changes, fever
  • Medical emergency: IV antibiotics, possible surgical drainage

Lacrimal Sac Fistula

  • Chronic abscess can drain through skin, creating fistula
  • Requires surgical repair

Recurrent Infection

  • Without addressing obstruction, infections recur

Prevention

Treat Underlying Obstruction

  • DCR surgery for chronic obstruction prevents recurrent dacryo cystitis

Manage Nasal/Sinus Disease

  • Treat chronic sinusitis, polyps
  • Optimizes nasal anatomy

Lacrimal Massage (Infants)

  • Gentle downward massage over lacrimal sac
  • May help open congenital membrane

When to See a Doctor

See Doctor Promptly If:

  • Painful, red swelling near inner corner of eye
  • Purulent discharge from eye
  • Fever with eye/periocular swelling
  • Chronic excessive tearing (annoying, affecting quality of life)
  • Infant with persistent tearing and discharge (especially if looking infected)

Emergency Care If:

  • Extensive eyelid swell ing, eye movement pain, vision changes (possible orbital cellulitis)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have dacryocystitis?
  • Do I need antibiotics?
  • Will this keep coming back?
  • Do I need surgery (DCR)?
  • What are the risks and benefits of external vs. endoscopic DCR?
  • How can I prevent future episodes?

Bottom Line

Dacryocystitis is an infection of the lacrimal sac (tear drainage sac) caused by nasolacrimal duct obstruction, causing tears and bacteria to accumulate. It presents as a painful, red, swollen lump below the inner corner of the eye, with purulent discharge expressible from the tear duct openings and excessive tearing. Acute dacryocystitis is treated with oral antibiotics and warm compresses; abscesses may require incision and drainage. Chronic or recurrent dacryocystitis requires surgical treatment—dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR)—to create a new drainage pathway from the sac into the nose, bypassing the blocked duct. DCR has excellent success rates (90%+) in resolving tearing and preventing recurrent infections. If you develop painful swelling near the nose with tearing and discharge, see a doctor promptly for antibiotics. For chronic tearing or recurrent infections, consult an ophthalmologist about DCR surgery to definitively address the obstruction.
