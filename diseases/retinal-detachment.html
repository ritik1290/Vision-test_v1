What Is Retinal Detachment?

Retinal detachment is a serious medical emergency where the retina—the light-sensitive layer of tissue at the back of the eye—pulls away from its normal position. When the retina detaches, it is separated from the blood vessels that supply it with oxygen and nutrients. Without prompt treatment, retinal detachment can cause permanent vision loss in the affected eye.

The retina is crucial for vision, converting light into electrical signals that travel through the optic nerve to the brain, where they're interpreted as images. When detached, the retina cannot function properly, and vision becomes severely impaired or lost in the affected area.

THIS IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY

Retinal detachment requires immediate medical attention. If you experience sudden flashes of light, a shower of floaters, or a curtain or shadow in your vision, seek emergency eye care immediately. Every hour counts—treatment within 24-48 hours significantly improves the chances of preserving vision.

Key Facts About Retinal Detachment

  • Medical emergency: Requires urgent surgical treatment to preserve vision.
  • Painless: Retinal detachment itself doesn't hurt, but causes dramatic vision changes.
  • Not uncommon: Affects about 1 in 10,000 people per year.
  • Usually affects one eye: Though risk increases in the other eye if you've had it once.
  • Time-sensitive: Earlier treatment leads to better visual outcomes.
  • Preventable in some cases: Early treatment of retinal tears can prevent detachment.
  • High success rate: Modern surgery successfully reattaches the retina in 85-90% of cases.
  • Vision recovery varies: Final visual outcome depends on extent and location of detachment.

Understanding the Retina

To understand retinal detachment, it helps to know how the retina works:

The Retina

The retina is a thin layer of light-sensitive tissue lining the inner back wall of the eye, functioning like film in a camera:

  • Photoreceptors (rods and cones): Detect light and color
  • Macula: Central area responsible for sharp, detailed vision
  • Peripheral retina: Provides side vision and night vision
  • Optic nerve: Transmits visual signals to the brain

Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)

The retina normally rests against the RPE, a layer of cells that:

  • Nourishes the retina
  • Removes waste products
  • Absorbs scattered light
  • Maintains retinal attachment

The Vitreous

The vitreous is a clear, gel-like substance filling the eye's interior:

  • Helps maintain eye shape
  • Normally pressed against the retina
  • Can shrink and pull away with age (posterior vitreous detachment)
  • Changes can lead to retinal tears or holes

What Happens During Detachment

When the retina detaches:

  • Separation from the RPE cuts off blood supply
  • Photoreceptor cells begin dying without oxygen
  • Vision is lost in the detached area
  • The longer the detachment persists, the more permanent the damage
  • If the macula detaches, central vision is severely affected

Types of Retinal Detachment

There are three main types of retinal detachment:

1. Rhegmatogenous Detachment (Most Common)

Accounts for about 90-95% of all retinal detachments. "Rhegmatogenous" means "arising from a break or tear."

Mechanism:

  • A tear, hole, or break develops in the retina
  • Liquid vitreous seeps through the break behind the retina
  • Fluid accumulation separates the retina from the RPE
  • Gravity and eye movements cause the detachment to spread

Common Causes of Retinal Tears:

  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD): Age-related shrinkage of vitreous pulls on retina
  • Lattice degeneration: Thinning of peripheral retina
  • High myopia: Elongated eye stretches and thins retina
  • Eye trauma: Injury can cause immediate or delayed tears

2. Tractional Detachment

Scar tissue or fibrous membranes on the retina's surface contract and pull the retina away from the RPE. No retinal break is present.

Common Causes:

  • Proliferative diabetic retinopathy: Most common cause
  • Sickle cell retinopathy
  • Retinopathy of prematurity (in premature infants)
  • Penetrating eye injury with scarring
  • Post-surgical scarring

3. Exudative (Serous) Detachment

Fluid accumulates under the retina without a tear or break, usually due to inflammation or leaking blood vessels.

Common Causes:

  • Age-related macular degeneration (wet AMD)
  • Inflammatory conditions: Uveitis, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease
  • Choroidal tumors
  • Central serous retinopathy
  • Severe hypertension
  • Toxemia of pregnancy

Treatment Difference:

Exudative detachments are treated by addressing the underlying cause (unlike rhegmatogenous detachments which require surgery).

Causes and Risk Factors

Several factors increase the risk of retinal detachment:

Age

  • Most common in people over 40
  • Peak incidence in 60s and 70s
  • Age-related vitreous changes increase tear risk

Previous Retinal Detachment

  • If one eye has had retinal detachment, risk in other eye is 10-15%
  • Family history of retinal detachment increases risk

Myopia (Nearsightedness)

  • High myopia (>-6 diopters): 10x increased risk
  • Elongated eye stretches and thins the retina
  • More prone to lattice degeneration and tears

Eye Surgery or Injury

  • Cataract surgery: Small increased risk (0.5-1%)
  • Other eye surgeries: YAG laser capsulotomy, vitrectomy
  • Eye trauma: Blunt or penetrating injuries

Retinal Conditions

  • Lattice degeneration: Areas of thinned peripheral retina (8-10% of population)
  • Retinoschisis: Splitting of retinal layers
  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD): Can cause retinal tears

Systemic Diseases

  • Diabetes: Especially with proliferative retinopathy
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Marfan syndrome
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome

Other Risk Factors

  • Family history of retinal detachment
  • Male gender (slightly higher risk)
  • Previous retinal tear
  • Extreme nearsightedness in the family

Warning Signs and Symptoms

Retinal detachment itself is painless but produces distinctive visual symptoms:

Classic Warning Signs - Seek Help IMMEDIATELY

The following symptoms suggest possible retinal tear or detachment:

Sudden Flashes of Light

Brief, bright flashes (photopsia) in peripheral vision, especially in dim light. Indicates vitreous tugging on retina.

Shower of Floaters

Sudden appearance of many new floaters (spots, specks, cobwebs) that don't go away. May indicate retinal tear with bleeding.

Shadow or Curtain

A dark shadow, curtain, or veil spreading across your field of vision—the hallmark sign of detachment progressing.

Peripheral Vision Loss

Loss of side vision that progressively worsens, moving toward central vision if untreated.

Wavy or Blurred Vision

Central vision becomes distorted or blurry if macula is involved or about to detach.

Sudden Central Vision Loss

Severe, rapid loss of central vision indicates macula has detached—requires urgent surgery.

Important Points About Symptoms

  • No pain: Retinal detachment doesn't hurt
  • Progression: Symptoms typically worsen over hours to days
  • One eye affected: Symptoms occur in one eye only
  • Morning improvement: Vision may seem better after sleep when lying flat (fluid settles), but worsens again when upright
  • No redness or irritation: The eye looks normal from the outside

Distinguishing Retinal Detachment from Other Conditions

Condition Symptoms Key Differences
Retinal Detachment Flashes, floaters, curtain/shadow, vision loss Painless, progressive, one eye
Migraine Aura Flashing lights, zigzag patterns Both eyes, goes away in 20-30 min, may cause headache
Vitreous Detachment (PVD) Flashes, floaters No shadow/curtain, no vision loss (but can lead to tear)
Stroke Vision loss Both eyes affected in same visual field, other neurological symptoms

Don't Wait - Act Immediately!

If you experience flashes, a sudden increase in floaters, or a shadow in your vision, treat it as an emergency. Call your eye doctor immediately or go to an emergency room. Do NOT wait until morning or your next appointment. Prompt treatment can save your vision.

Diagnosis

Retinal detachment is diagnosed through a comprehensive eye examination:

Dilated Eye Examination

The gold standard for diagnosis:

  • Eye drops dilate (widen) pupils
  • Doctor examines entire retina with special lenses and lights
  • Can visualize tears, holes, and areas of detachment
  • Identifies type and extent of detachment

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Examines front of eye and vitreous
  • Checks for vitreous hemorrhage
  • Uses special lenses to view retina

Ultrasound (B-Scan)

Used when retina cannot be visualized directly due to:

  • Vitreous hemorrhage (blood obscures view)
  • Dense cataract
  • Corneal opacity

Ultrasound creates images showing retinal position and detachment configuration.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • High-resolution cross-sectional images of retina
  • Determines if macula is attached or detached
  • Guides treatment decisions

What the Doctor Assesses

  • Location and extent: Which areas are detached
  • Macula involvement: Attached (macula-on) vs. detached (macula-off)
  • Causative breaks: Number, size, and location of tears/holes
  • Vitreous traction: Forces pulling on retina
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR): Scar tissue formation
  • Other eye status: Check for risk in fellow eye

Treatment and Surgery

Retinal detachment requires surgical treatment. The specific procedure depends on the type, location, and extent of detachment:

Preventive Treatment for Retinal Tears (Before Detachment)

If caught early, retinal tears can be treated before detachment occurs:

Laser Photocoagulation

  • Laser creates burns around the tear
  • Burns form scar tissue that "welds" retina to underlying tissue
  • Office procedure, takes 10-20 minutes
  • Prevents fluid from seeping through tear
  • Success rate >90% in preventing detachment

Cryotherapy (Freezing)

  • Freezing probe applied to outer eye wall over the tear
  • Creates scar tissue similar to laser
  • Used when laser can't reach the tear
  • Office or surgical procedure

Surgical Treatment for Retinal Detachment

1. Pneumatic Retinopexy

Minimally invasive office procedure for selected cases:

Best for:

  • Single small tear in upper part of eye
  • Fresh detachments
  • No proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR)

Procedure:

  • Laser or cryo treats the tear
  • Gas bubble injected into vitreous cavity
  • Patient positions head so bubble pushes retina back against eye wall
  • Specific head positioning required for several days
  • Gas gradually dissolves over 1-2 weeks

Advantages:

  • Office-based procedure
  • Faster visual recovery
  • Less invasive

Success rate: 60-75% (lower than other methods, but can be repeated or followed by other surgery)

2. Scleral Buckle

Surgical procedure placing a silicone band around the eye:

Procedure:

  • Performed in operating room (outpatient or overnight stay)
  • Silicone band or sponge sutured to outer eye wall (sclera)
  • Indents eye wall toward retina, relieving vitreous traction
  • Laser or cryo seals the tear
  • Subretinal fluid may be drained
  • Buckle stays in place permanently

Best for:

  • Younger patients
  • Rhegmatogenous detachments with peripheral tears
  • No significant vitreous traction

Success rate: 85-90% with single surgery

3. Vitrectomy

Removal of vitreous gel and direct repair of retina:

Procedure:

  • Performed in operating room (usually outpatient)
  • Tiny instruments remove vitreous gel
  • Membrane peeling if PVR present
  • Laser or cryo seals tears
  • Gas bubble or silicone oil fills eye to hold retina in place
  • Specific head positioning required (face-down positioning often needed for 1-2 weeks)

Best for:

  • Macula-off detachments
  • Multiple or large tears
  • Posterior tears
  • Tractional detachments
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR)
  • Vitreous hemorrhage obscuring view
  • Failed previous surgery

Gas vs. Silicone Oil:

  • Gas bubble: Gradually absorbed over 2-8 weeks (SF6 or C3F8 gas); requires head positioning; cannot fly or travel to high altitudes during absorption
  • Silicone oil: Permanent unless removed with second surgery; used for complex cases; allows more mobility

Success rate: 85-90% with single surgery

Combined Procedures

Sometimes scleral buckle and vitrectomy are combined for complex detachments.

Post-Surgical Care

  • Positioning: Strict head positioning as directed (often face-down for 50 minutes/hour for 1-2 weeks)
  • Eye drops: Antibiotics and anti-inflammatory drops
  • Activity restrictions: No heavy lifting, straining, or vigorous activity for several weeks
  • No flying: Cannot fly while gas bubble present (risk of dangerous pressure increase)
  • Vision changes: Expect blurry vision while gas present; improves as gas absorbs

Recovery and Visual Outcomes

Reattachment Success Rates

  • Single surgery: 85-90% success
  • After additional surgery: >95% ultimate reattachment
  • Success depends on extent, duration, and complexity

Visual Recovery

Visual outcomes are highly variable and depend on several factors:

Factors Affecting Vision Recovery:

  • Macula involvement (most important):
    • Macula-on: Excellent visual prognosis (often 20/40 or better)
    • Macula-off < 1 week: Good prognosis (often 20/70 or better)
    • Macula-off > 1 week: Variable, often 20/200 or worse
  • Duration of detachment: Longer detachments have worse outcomes
  • Extent of detachment: More extensive = worse prognosis
  • Presence of PVR: Reduces visual recovery
  • Age: Younger patients often recover better

Recovery Timeline

  • Immediate post-op: Vision very blurry (especially with gas bubble)
  • 1-2 weeks: Gas begins absorbing, vision starts improving
  • 1-3 months: Significant improvement occurs
  • 6-12 months: Visual recovery continues; final vision stabilizes

Permanent Visual Changes

Even with successful reattachment, some permanent changes may occur:

  • Reduced visual acuity: Rarely returns to pre-detachment level if macula was detached
  • Distortion: Straight lines may appear wavy
  • Peripheral vision loss: Missing areas in visual field
  • Reduced night vision
  • Floaters: May persist or new ones develop
  • Double vision: Rarely, from scleral buckle

Possible Complications

  • Re-detachment: 10-15% risk, usually within first 2 months
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR): Scar tissue formation (5-10%)
  • Cataract formation: Common after vitrectomy (50-80% within 2 years)
  • Elevated eye pressure: Temporary or permanent glaucoma
  • Infection: Rare (< 1%) but serious
  • Macular pucker or hole: Can develop after surgery

Prevention and Risk Reduction

Regular Eye Exams

  • Annual dilated exams if high-risk (high myopia, family history, previous surgery)
  • Can detect retinal tears before detachment occurs
  • Prophylactic treatment of tears prevents 90% of detachments

Know Your Risk Factors

  • Be aware if you're high-risk (myopia, previous detachment, family history)
  • Learn warning signs and seek immediate care if they occur

Protect Your Eyes

  • Wear protective eyewear for sports and high-risk activities
  • Avoid activities with high risk of eye trauma if severely myopic

Manage Underlying Conditions

  • Diabetes: Maintain excellent blood sugar control
  • Regular screening for diabetic retinopathy
  • Timely treatment prevents tractional detachments

After Previous Detachment

  • Follow-up exams every 6-12 months for fellow eye
  • 10-15% risk of detachment in other eye
  • Prophylactic treatment of fellow eye tears or thinning sometimes recommended

Post-Surgical Precautions

If you've had retinal detachment surgery:

  • Attend all follow-up appointments
  • Report any new symptoms immediately
  • Follow activity restrictions
  • Lifelong vigilance for symptoms in both eyes

When to Seek Emergency Care

Seek IMMEDIATE Eye Care For:

  • Sudden flashes of light (especially in peripheral vision)
  • Sudden shower of floaters or increase in existing floaters
  • Shadow, curtain, or veil across your vision
  • Sudden loss of peripheral vision
  • Sudden central vision loss or blurriness
  • Any combination of above symptoms

What to do:

  • Call your ophthalmologist immediately, even after hours
  • Go to emergency room if you cannot reach your eye doctor
  • Do NOT wait until morning or next business day
  • Time is critical—every hour matters for visual outcome

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have a retinal detachment or just a tear?
  • Is my macula attached or detached?
  • What type of surgery do you recommend?
  • What are my chances of preserving vision?
  • What will my vision be like after surgery?
  • What positioning will I need to do?
  • When can I return to normal activities?
  • What are the risks of re-detachment?
  • What about my other eye?
