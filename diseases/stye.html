What Is a Hordeolum (Stye)?

A hordeolum, commonly known as a stye, is an acute, painful infection of the eyelid glands that forms a red, swollen lump or abscess near the eyelid margin. It appears as a tender, pimple-like bump, typically filled with pus, and can occur on the upper or lower eyelid. Styes are caused by bacterial infection—most commonly *Staphylococcus aureus*—of either the sebaceous glands (glands of Zeis), sweat glands (glands of Moll), or eyelash follicles at the eyelid margin.

While styes are uncomfortable and cosmetically bothersome, they are usually self-limited, resolving spontaneously within a week or two with simple home treatment (warm compresses). Most styes drain on their own, releasing pus and relieving symptoms. Occasionally, they require incision and drainage by an eye doctor. It's important to distinguish styes from chalazia (blocked meibomian glands), which are less painful and don't typically involve infection.

Key Facts About Styes

  • Acute bacterial infection: Usually *Staph aureus* infecting eyelid gland.
  • Painful red bump: Tender, swollen lump at eyelid margin; looks like pimple.
  • Self-limited: Most resolve within 1-2 weeks with warm compresses.
  • Common: Very common; can affect anyone at any age.
  • External vs. internal: External styes (most common) at lid margin; internal styes deeper in lid.
  • Warm compresses key treatment: Heat promotes drainage.
  • Do NOT squeeze: Can spread infection.
  • Different from chalazion: Stye is infected and painful; chalazion is non-infected blockage.

Understanding styes helps with proper home treatment and knowing when to seek medical care.

Types of Styes

External Hordeolum (External Stye) - Most Common

  • Infection of glands at eyelid margin:
    • Glands of Zeis: Sebaceous (oil) glands attached to eyelash follicles
    • Glands of Moll: Sweat glands near lash follicles
    • Eyelash follicle itself
  • Forms visible, red, painful bump at eyelid margin
  • Looks like pimple or abscess
  • Pus-filled "white head" often visible at center
  • Usually points outward (toward skin surface)
  • More common than internal stye

Internal Hordeolum (Internal Stye)

  • Infection of meibomian gland (large oil gland deeper in eyelid, within tarsal plate)
  • Forms deeper in eyelid
  • May not be visible externally initially
  • More painful than external stye (deeper, more pressure)
  • Pus may drain onto conjunctiva (inside of eyelid)
  • Can evolve into chalazion if infection resolves but obstruction persists

Causes and Risk Factors

Bacterial Infection

  • *Staphylococcus aureus*: ~90-95% of cases
  • Normal skin bacteria that enters gland
  • Infection causes inflammation, pus formation, abscess

Mechanism

  • Gland opening becomes obstructed (debris, dead skin cells)
  • Oil/secretions cannot drain
  • Bacteria proliferate in blocked gland
  • Infection develops → abscess forms

Risk Factors

Blepharitis (Chronic Eyelid Inflammation)

  • Most important risk factor
  • Chronic inflammation and bacterial colonization of lid margins predispose to styes
  • People with blepharitis have recurrent styes

Poor Eyelid Hygiene

  • Not removing eye makeup
  • Touching/rubbing eyes with dirty hands
  • Contaminated makeup or applicators (old mascara, eyeliner)

Ocular Rosacea

  • Skin condition causing eyelid inflammation
  • Increases stye risk

Seborrheic Dermatitis

  • Scaly, itchy skin condition
  • Affects eyelids, increases gland blockage

Diabetes

  • Increased infection risk in general

Stress, Fatigue, Hormonal Changes

  • May trigger styes in susceptible individuals

Contact Lens Wear (If Poor Hygiene)

  • Touching eyes with unclean hands

Recognizing the Symptoms

Red, Swollen Bump

Tender lump at eyelid margin; looks like pimple or small abscess.

Pain and Tenderness

Painful to touch; aching; worsens with blinking.

Eyelid Swelling

Entire eyelid may be swollen; sometimes dramatic.

Tearing

Excessive tearing from irritation.

Foreign Body Sensation

Feeling of something in eye, grittiness.

Light Sensitivity

Mild photophobia from inflammation.

Yellow/White "Head"

Pus-filled center visible at bump; may spontaneously drain.

Crusting

Dried discharge on eyelid, especially upon waking.

Typical Course

  • Day 1-2: Small, tender red spot develops on eyelid margin
  • Day 2-4: Bump enlarges, becomes more swollen and painful
  • Day 4-7: Pus-filled head appears; may spontaneously rupture and drain
  • Day 7-14: After drainage, pain and swelling subside; healing occurs

Stye vs. Chalazion

  • Stye (Hordeolum):
    • Acute onset (hours to days)
    • Painful, tender
    • Red, inflamed
    • At eyelid margin usually
    • Infection present
    • Resolves in 1-2 weeks
  • Chalazion:
    • More gradual onset (weeks)
    • Usually painless (unless large/inflamed)
    • Firm, round lump
    • Away from margin (within eyelid)
    • Blockage, not infection
    • Can persist for months
  • Sometimes internal stye can become chalazion if infection clears but blockage remains

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis (Usually Obvious)

  • Based on appearance and symptoms
  • No lab tests or imaging typically needed

Examination

  • Visible red, swollen, tender lump at eyelid margin
  • Pus may be visible
  • Assess for blepharitis (crusting, redness of lid margins)
  • Evert lid to check for internal abscess

When to Consider Other Diagnoses

  • If lump persists >6 weeks despite treatment: Consider chalazion or, rarely, eyelid tumor (sebaceous cell carcinoma can mimic recurrent chalazion/stye)
  • If extensive eyelid and periocular swelling: Consider preseptal or orbital cellulitis (more serious infection)

Treatment

Home Treatment (First-Line for Most)

Warm Compresses (Most Important)

  • How:
    • Soak clean washcloth in warm (not hot) water
    • Apply to closed affected eyelid for 10-15 minutes
    • Repeat 3-4 times per day
  • Purpose:
    • Increases blood flow, promotes healing
    • Softens blockage
    • Encourages spontaneous drainage
  • Key to resolution

Eyelid Hygiene

  • Gently clean eyelid with diluted baby shampoo or commercial eyelid cleanser
  • Remove crusts
  • Helps prevent spread of infection

Do NOT Squeeze or Pop

  • Tempting because looks like pimple
  • Do NOT squeeze: Can spread infection to surrounding tissues or into eye
  • Let drain spontaneously

Avoid Eye Makeup

  • Do not wear makeup until stye healed
  • Discard old mascara/eyeliner (may be contaminated)

Do Not Wear Contact Lenses

  • Until stye resolved
  • Avoid transferring bacteria

Medical Treatment (If Needed)

Topical Antibiotic Ointment

  • Erythromycin or bacitracin ophthalmic ointment
  • Applied to eyelid margin
  • May help prevent spread, speed resolution
  • Not always necessary for simple external stye

Oral Antibiotics

  • If:
    • Stye large or not responding to warm compresses
    • Extensive eyelid swelling (preseptal cellulitis)
    • Recurrent styes
  • Doxycycline, cephalexin, or dicloxacillin

Incision and Drainage (I&D)

  • When:
    • Stye does not drain spontaneously after 1-2 weeks
    • Large, very painful
    • Vision affected
  • Procedure:
    • Performed by ophthalmologist
    • Local anesthesia (numbing injection or drops)
    • Small incision made to drain pus
    • Quick, in-office procedure
  • Relief: Immediate pain relief after drainage

Timeline

  • Most styes resolve within 7-14 days with warm compresses
  • If no improvement after 1 week or worsening, see doctor

Complications

Spread of Infection

Preseptal Cellulitis

  • Infection spreads to eyelid tissues
  • Entire eyelid becomes swollen, red, warm
  • More serious; requires oral antibiotics

Orbital Cellulitis (Rare, Serious)

  • If infection spreads behind orbital septum (into eye socket)
  • Vision-threatening
  • Symptoms: severe swelling, pain with eye movement, decreased vision, fever
  • Medical emergency; requires IV antibiotics, hospitalization

Chalazion Formation

  • Internal stye may resolve partially, leaving persistent blockage (chalazion)
  • Firm, painless lump
  • May require steroid injection or surgical excision

Recurrence

  • Especially if underlying blepharitis not treated

Scarring

  • Rare; chronic or recurrent styes may cause lid margin scarring

Prevention

Eyelid Hygiene

  • Daily eyelid scrubs: Especially if blepharitis or prone to styes
    • Use diluted baby shampoo or commercial lid cleanser
    • Gently scrub eyelid margins
  • Removes debris, bacteria

Do Not Touch Eyes

  • Avoid rubbing eyes with hands
  • Wash hands before touching face/eyes

Makeup Hygiene

  • Remove eye makeup every night
  • Replace mascara, eyeliner every 3-6 months
  • Do not share eye makeup
  • Clean makeup brushes regularly

Contact Lens Hygiene

  • Wash hands before handling lenses
  • Follow proper cleaning/storage protocols

Treat Underlying Blepharitis

  • If recurrent styes, likely have blepharitis
  • Regular warm compresses, lid scrubs
  • Omega-3 supplements may help
  • Treat any rosacea or seborrheic dermatitis

Warm Compresses (Preventive)

  • Daily warm compresses if prone to styes
  • Keeps glands open, draining

When to See a Doctor

See Ophthalmologist If:

  • Stye not improving after 1 week of warm compresses
  • Vision affected
  • Entire eyelid severely swollen or warm (preseptal cellulitis)
  • Pain with eye movement, fever, decreased vision (orbital cellulitis—emergency)
  • Recurrent styes (underlying blepharitis needs treatment)
  • Lump persists >6 weeks (may be chalazion or need drainage)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this a stye or chalazion?
  • Do I need antibiotics?
  • Should it be drained?
  • Why do I keep getting styes?
  • Do I have blepharitis?
  • How can I prevent future styes?

Bottom Line

A hordeolum (stye) is an acute bacterial infection (usually *Staph aureus*) of an eyelid gland, forming a painful, red, swollen bump at the eyelid margin that looks like a pimple. Most styes are self-limited and resolve within 1-2 weeks with simple warm compresses (10-15 minutes, 3-4 times daily), which promote drainage. Do NOT squeeze styes—this can spread infection. If a stye doesn't improve within a week or causes significant swelling, see an eye doctor; you may need antibiotics or incision and drainage. Recurrent styes suggest underlying blepharitis—treat with daily eyelid hygiene (lid scrubs), maintain good makeup/contact lens hygiene, and avoid touching your eyes with unwashed hands to prevent future styes.
