What Is Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction?

Nasolacrimal duct obstruction (NLDO), commonly called "blocked tear duct," is a condition in which the nasolacrimal duct—the passageway that drains tears from the eye into the nose—becomes partially or completely blocked. Normally, tears are produced continuously by the lacrimal gland, spread across the eye surface with each blink, and drain through tiny openings (puncta) in the inner eyelids into small channels (canaliculi), then into the lacrimal sac, and finally through the nasolacrimal duct into the nose. When this drainage system is blocked, tears accumulate and overflow down the cheek (epiphora), causing chronic tearing, discharge, and sometimes infection.

NLDO is common at both extremes of life: in newborns (congenital NLDO affecting ~6-20% of infants) and in older adults (acquired NLDO from age-related narrowing). In infants, the obstruction is typically from a membrane at the distal end of the duct (Hasner's valve) that fails to open, and most cases resolve spontaneously by 12 months with conservative treatment (lacrimal sac massage). In adults, the duct narrows or scars from chronic inflammation, aging, trauma, or previous surgery, requiring surgical intervention (dacryocystorhinostomy or DCR) to create a new drainage pathway. Both forms cause bothersome tearing that significantly impacts quality of life, but treatments are highly effective.

Key Facts About Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

  • Blocked tear drainage: Tears can't drain into nose; overflow down cheek.
  • Common in infants: ~6-20% of newborns; membrane doesn't open.
  • Common in older adults: Duct narrows with age.
  • Excessive tearing (epiphora): Hallmark symptom; tears overflow.
  • Discharge: Mucoid or purulent if infected.
  • Infants: usually resolves spontaneously by 12 months; massage therapy helps.
  • Adults: surgical treatment (DCR) highly effective
  • Can lead to dacryocystitis (infection)

Understanding NLDO helps guide appropriate treatment based on age and underlying cause.

Types of Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

Congenital NLDO (Infants)

  • Present from birth or early infancy
  • Cause: Hasner's valve (membrane at distal end of nasolacrimal duct where it enters nose) fails to spontaneously open
  • Affects ~6-20% of newborns
  • Usually unilateral (one eye), but can be bilateral
  • ~90% resolve spontaneously by 12 months

Acquired NLDO (Adults)

  • Develops later in life
  • Causes: Age-related narrowing, chronic inflammation, trauma, tumors, systemic diseases
  • More common in women, especially postmenopausal
  • Usually does NOT resolve spontaneously; requires surgical treatment

Causes

Congenital NLDO (Infants)

  • Imperforate Hasner's valve: Membrane at duct opening into nose doesn't rupture/open
    • Normally opens before birth or shortly after
    • Persistent membrane → obstruction
  • Congenital nasolacrimal duct stenosis (narrowing)
  • Craniofacial abnormalities (rare): Altered anatomy

Acquired NLDO (Adults)

Involutional (Age-Related) Stenosis - Most Common

  • Duct narrows with aging
  • Chronic low-grade inflammation, fibrosis
  • More common in postmenopausal women (hormonal changes may contribute)

Chronic Inflammation

  • Chronic dacryocystitis (lacrimal sac infection)
  • Recurrent nasal/sinus infections
  • Chronic conjunctivitis

Nasal/Sinus Disease

  • Chronic rhinosinusitis
  • Nasal polyps
  • Deviated nasal septum
  • Allergic rhinitis

Trauma

  • Facial fractures (especially naso-orbital-ethmoid fractures)
  • Damage to nasolacrimal duct or surrounding bones

Previous Surgery

  • Nasal or sinus surgery
  • Can inadvertently damage duct

Medications

  • Chemotherapy agents: Docetaxel, 5-fluorouracil (5-FU)
    • Can cause punctal/canalicular/duct stenosis
  • Topical glaucoma drops (long-term): Rare

Tumors (Rare)

  • Lacrimal sac tumor
  • Nasal/sinus tumor compressing duct

Systemic Inflammatory Diseases

  • Wegener's granulomatosis (granulomatosis with polyangiitis)
  • Sarcoidosis

Dacryoliths (Stones)

  • Concretions in lacrimal sac/duct blocking drainage

Recognizing the Symptoms

Primary Symptom: Excessive Tearing (Epiphora)

Constant Tearing

Tears overflow down cheek; worse in cold, wind, or emotional situations; hallmark symptom.

Mucoid Discharge

Thick, sticky discharge; "goopy" eyes, especially upon waking; mattering of eyelashes.

Recurrent Eye Infections

Conjunctivitis, dacryocystitis (lacrimal sac infection) from stagnant tears.

Blurred Vision (Intermittent)

From excess tears pooling on eye surface; blink to clear temporarily.

Swelling Near Nose (If Infected)

Dacryocystitis — red, tender swelling below inner corner of eye.

Skin Maceration

Constant tearing irritates skin of cheek; redness, chapping.

Infant-Specific Signs

  • Tearing present from birth or first few weeks of life
  • One or both eyes affected
  • Crusting/mattering of eyelashes (especially morning)
  • Mucoid discharge
  • If infected: red, swollen lacrimal sac area (dacryocystocele or acute dacryocystitis)

Adult-Specific Symptoms

  • Chronic, bothersome tearing (affects reading, driving, outdoor activities)
  • Tearing worsens in cold weather, wind
  • May have intermittent acute dacryocystitis episodes (painful sac swelling)

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Chronic tearing? Since birth (infant) or recent onset (adult)?
  • Discharge?
  • Recurrent infections?
  • Previous trauma, surgery, chemotherapy?
  • Nasal/sinus symptoms?

External Examination

  • Observe tearing
  • Check for swelling over lacrimal sac (dacryocystitis)
  • Discharge expressible from puncta with gentle pressure on sac?

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Assess puncta (tear duct openings):
    • Open and patent?
    • Stenotic (narrowed)?
  • Tear meniscus height (increased with obstruction—tears pool along lid margin)
  • Check for conjunctivitis, other eye disease

Dye Disappearance Test

  • Fluorescein dye instilled in eye
  • Normal: dye disappears within 5 minutes (drains into nose)
  • Obstruction: dye persists in eye, doesn't drain
  • Simple screening test

Lacrimal Irrigation

  • Cannula inserted through punctum, saline irrigated through system
  • Normal: Fluid flows into nose, patient tastes/feels saline in throat
  • Obstruction: Fluid refluxes back through same punctum or opposite punctum; doesn't reach nose
  • Confirms obstruction and localizes level (punctal, canalicular, sac, duct)

Probing (Infants)

  • Fine probe passed through punctum/canaliculus into duct
  • Diagnostic and sometimes therapeutic (can rupture membrane)
  • Usually done as definitive treatment if needed (not just diagnostic)

Imaging (If Indicated)

  • Dacryo cystography (DCG): Contrast dye injected into system, X-rays show obstruction level
  • CT or MRI: If tumor suspected or pre-surgical planning
  • Usually not needed for straightforward cases

Treatment for Infants (Congenital NLDO)

Conservative Management (First-Line, Age 0-12 Months)

Observation

  • ~90% of congenital NLDO resolves spontaneously by 12 months as membrane opens naturally
  • Many resolve by 6 months

Lacrimal Sac Massage

  • Technique:
    • Parent applies firm downward pressure over lacrimal sac (just below inner corner of eye)
    • Massage downward toward nose
    • Perform 2-3 times daily, 5-10 strokes each time
  • Goal: Increase pressure in duct to rupture Hasner's membrane
  • May help speed resolution
  • Efficacy debated, but low-risk, commonly recommended

Topical Antibiotics (If Discharge Purulent)

  • Erythromycin or polymyxin-trimethoprim drops
  • Treats secondary bacterial conjunctivitis
  • Does NOT cure obstruction

Interventional Treatment (If Persistent >12 Months)

Probing and Irrigation

  • When: If obstruction persists beyond 12 months (some wait until 15-18 months)
  • Procedure:
    • Office procedure (younger infants) or brief general anesthesia (older/uncooperative infants)
    • Fine probe passed through punctum, canaliculus, lacrimal sac, and into nasolacrimal duct
    • Ruptures Hasner's membrane
    • Saline irrigated to confirm patency
  • Success rate: ~80-90% with single probing
  • If fails, can repeat or proceed to balloon dilation or intubation

Balloon Catheter Dilation

  • Balloon inserted and inflated in duct to dilate stenotic areas
  • Used if simple probing fails

Nasolacrimal Duct Intubation

  • Silicone tube threaded through system and left in place (weeks to months) to keep duct open
  • For recurrent or complex obstructions

Rare: DCR Surgery (If Above Fail)

  • Very rarely needed in children
  • Reserved for complex cases not responding to probing/intubation

Treatment for Adults (Acquired NLDO)

Conservative Measures (Temporary Relief Only)

  • Warm compresses
  • Lacrimal sac massage (express discharge)
  • Topical antibiotics for discharge/conjunctivitis
  • Does NOT cure obstruction in adults; symptoms recur

Definitive Treatment: Dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR) - Gold Standard

Indications

  • Symptomatic NLDO with bothersome tearing
  • Recurrent dacryocystitis
  • Quality of life significantly affected

Goal

  • Bypass obstructed nasolacrimal duct by creating new drainage pathway from lacrimal sac directly into nasal cavity

Techniques

  • External DCR:
    • Skin incision on side of nose
    • Bone removed (osteotomy) to access nasal cavity
    • Lacrimal sac and nasal mucosa sutured together (anastomosis)
    • Silicone stent often placed temporarily
    • Success rate ~90-95%
    • Small scar (fades over time)
  • Endonasal (Endoscopic) DCR:
    • Performed through nostril with endoscope (no skin incision)
    • Success rate ~85-90%
    • Cosmetic advantage (no scar)
    • Requires specialized equipment

Post-Operative Care

  • Nasal irrigation, steroid nasal sprays
  • Oral antibiotics
  • Silicone stents removed after 4-12 weeks

Results

  • Excellent—>90% resolution of tearing
  • Dramatically improves quality of life

Alternative: Conjunctivodacryocystorhinostomy (CDCR)

  • If canaliculi also obstructed (not just duct)
  • Glass (Jones) tube placed from conjunctiva to nose
  • Bypasses entire system
  • More complex; reserved for special cases

Complications

Dacryocystocele (Infants)

  • Lacrimal sac distension with fluid
  • Bluish cystic swelling below medial canthus
  • Can become infected (dacryocystitis)

Acute Dacryocystitis

  • Lacrimal sac infection from stagnant tears
  • Painful, red, swollen mass near inner corner of eye
  • Requires antibiotics, may need abscess drainage

Chronic Conjunctivitis

  • From retained discharge, bacterial overgrowth

Corneal Abrasion (Rare)

  • From chronic discharge irritation

Prognosis and Outcomes

Infants (Congenital NLDO)

  • Spontaneous resolution: ~90% by 12 months with conservative management
  • Probing (if needed): ~80-90% success rate
  • Overall: Excellent prognosis; nearly all resolve

Adults (Acquired NLDO)

  • Without surgery: Symptoms persist, quality of life affected
  • With DCR: >90% success rate; tearing resolves, dramatic improvement in quality of life
  • Recurrence uncommon if surgery performed correctly

When to See a Doctor

Infants - See Pediatrician or Pediatric Ophthalmologist If:

  • Persistent tearing in newborn/infant
  • Mucoid discharge, mattering of eyelashes
  • Red, swollen mass near inner corner of eye (dacryocystocele, dacryocystitis—urgent)
  • Tearing not resolved by 12 months (consider probing)

Adults - See Ophthalmologist If:

  • Chronic, bothersome tearing
  • Tearing interferes with daily activities (reading, driving)
  • Recurrent eye infections
  • Mucoid discharge
  • Painful swelling near nose (dacryocystitis)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I/does my baby have nasolacrimal duct obstruction?
  • Will it resolve on its own?
  • Should I do lacrimal sac massage? (infants)
  • When should we consider probing? (infants)
  • Do I need DCR surgery? (adults)
  • What are the risks and benefits of external vs. endoscopic DCR?
  • What is the success rate?

Bottom Line

Nasolacrimal duct obstruction is blockage of the tear drainage pathway, causing excessive tearing (epiphora) and mucoid discharge. In infants, it's usually from a congenital membrane (Hasner's valve) that fails to open; ~90% resolve spontaneously by 12 months with conservative management including lacrimal sac massage, but persistent cases require probing to rupture the membrane (80-90% success rate). In adults, the duct narrows from aging, chronic inflammation, trauma, or nasal disease; symptoms don't resolve spontaneously and require surgical treatment—dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR)—to create a bypass from the lacrimal sac into the nose, with excellent success rates (>90%) in relieving tearing and dramatically improving quality of life. If you or your infant has chronic tearing and discharge, see an eye doctor for evaluation. Infants can often be managed conservatively initially, while adults benefit from DCR surgery to definitively resolve this bothersome condition.
