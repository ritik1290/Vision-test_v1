What Is Astigmatism?

Astigmatism is a common refractive error caused by an irregularly shaped cornea or lens. Instead of being perfectly round like a basketball, the cornea has a more oval shape like a football, with different curvatures in different directions (meridians). This irregular shape causes light rays to focus at multiple points rather than a single point on the retina, resulting in blurred or distorted vision at all distances.

Astigmatism often occurs together with myopia (nearsightedness) or hyperopia (farsightedness). It can affect people of all ages and is typically present from birth, though it can develop or change over time.

Key Facts About Astigmatism

  • Very common: Affects approximately one in three people to some degree.
  • Usually present from birth: Most cases are congenital, though severity may change during childhood.
  • Causes distorted vision: Both near and far vision affected, unlike myopia or hyperopia alone.
  • Often runs in families: Strong genetic component.
  • Easily corrected: Glasses, toric contact lenses, or refractive surgery provide clear vision.
  • Not a disease: Simply a variation in eye shape; doesn't lead to more serious eye problems.
  • Can change over time: May increase, decrease, or remain stable throughout life.
  • Causes eye strain: Can lead to headaches and fatigue if uncorrected.

Understanding astigmatism helps explain why your vision may be blurry or distorted, and why correction requires special cylindrical lenses that differ from those used for simple myopia or hyperopia.

How Vision Works—And What Goes Wrong in Astigmatism

Normal Vision (No Astigmatism)

In a normal eye:

  • The cornea and lens are spherical (uniformly curved in all directions)
  • Light entering the eye focuses to a single point on the retina
  • Result: Sharp, clear image

Analogy: Think of a basketball—it has the same curvature no matter which way you turn it.

What Happens in Astigmatism

In astigmatism:

  • The cornea (or less commonly, the lens) is shaped more like a football—steeper in one direction (meridian) than another
  • Different meridians have different focusing powers
  • Light rays focus at multiple points instead of one
  • Image on retina is elongated or distorted
  • Result: Blurred, distorted vision at all distances

The Two Principal Meridians

The cornea has two principal meridians perpendicular to each other:

  • Steepest meridian: Has greatest curvature and strongest focusing power
  • Flattest meridian: Has least curvature and weakest focusing power
  • Difference between these creates astigmatism
  • Measured in diopters (D) of cylinder power

Measuring Astigmatism

Astigmatism prescriptions have three components:

  • Sphere (SPH): Overall nearsightedness or farsightedness
  • Cylinder (CYL): Amount of astigmatism (power difference between meridians)
  • Axis: Orientation of astigmatism (0-180 degrees)

Example prescription: -2.00 -1.50 x 090

  • -2.00 D of myopia (sphere)
  • -1.50 D of astigmatism (cylinder)
  • At 90 degrees axis (vertical orientation)

Severity Classification

  • Mild: 0.25 to 1.00 D of cylinder
  • Moderate: 1.00 to 3.00 D
  • Severe: > 3.00 D

Types of Astigmatism

By Location

Corneal Astigmatism (Most Common)

  • Irregular corneal curvature
  • Accounts for ~90% of astigmatism
  • Can be measured with keratometry or corneal topography
  • Usually congenital

Lenticular Astigmatism

  • Irregular lens shape or positioning
  • Less common
  • May develop with cataracts
  • Can change over time

By Pattern (Regularity)

Regular Astigmatism

  • Principal meridians are perpendicular (90 degrees apart)
  • Most common type
  • Easily corrected with cylindrical lenses
  • Uniform curvature along each meridian

Irregular Astigmatism

  • Meridians not perpendicular, or corneal surface very irregular
  • Often due to corneal disease, injury, or surgery
  • Difficult to correct with glasses
  • May require specialty rigid contact lenses
  • Causes: keratoconus, corneal scarring, post-LASIK complications

By Relationship to Other Refractive Errors

Simple Astigmatism

  • One meridian focuses light on retina; other focuses in front or behind
  • Simple myopic: One meridian myopic, one emmetropic
  • Simple hyperopic: One meridian hyperopic, one emmetropic

Compound Astigmatism

  • Both meridians focus light either in front of or behind retina, but by different amounts
  • Compound myopic: Both meridians myopic, but different degrees
  • Compound hyperopic: Both meridians hyperopic, different degrees
  • Most common pattern

Mixed Astigmatism

  • One meridian focuses in front of retina (myopic)
  • Other meridian focuses behind retina (hyperopic)
  • More challenging to correct

By Orientation

With-the-Rule Astigmatism

  • Vertical meridian steeper than horizontal
  • Axis near 90 degrees (vertical)
  • Most common in children and young adults
  • Cornea shaped like vertical football

Against-the-Rule Astigmatism

  • Horizontal meridian steeper than vertical
  • Axis near 180 degrees (horizontal)
  • More common in older adults
  • Cornea shaped like horizontal football
  • Can develop from "with-the-rule" as cornea changes with age

Oblique Astigmatism

  • Steepest meridian at 30-60 degrees or 120-150 degrees
  • Less common
  • Can be more visually disruptive

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Causes

Congenital (Present at Birth)

  • Most common cause: Natural variation in corneal shape
  • Present from birth or early childhood
  • Often hereditary
  • Usually regular astigmatism

Genetics

  • Strong hereditary component
  • Runs in families
  • Children with astigmatic parents more likely to have it
  • Specific genes associated with astigmatism identified

Acquired Causes

Corneal Conditions

  • Keratoconus: Progressive thinning and bulging of cornea (irregular astigmatism)
  • Corneal scarring: From injury, infection, or inflammation
  • Pterygium: Growth on cornea that distorts shape
  • Corneal dystrophies

Eye Surgery

  • Cataract surgery: Can induce or change astigmatism
  • Corneal transplant
  • LASIK or PRK complications (rare)
  • Glaucoma surgery

Eye Injury or Trauma

  • Corneal laceration or perforation
  • Blunt trauma changing corneal shape
  • Chemical burns

Eyelid Pressure

  • Chalazion or stye: Pressing on cornea can temporarily change shape
  • Ptosis (droopy eyelid): Chronic pressure
  • Rubbing eyes: May worsen astigmatism over time

Risk Factors

  • Family history: Strong predictor
  • Premature birth or low birth weight
  • Ethnicity: Higher prevalence in certain populations
  • Myopia or hyperopia: Often occur together

Changes Over Time

  • Childhood: May stabilize or change as eye grows
  • Adulthood: Usually stable
  • Aging: May shift from "with-the-rule" to "against-the-rule"
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal changes can temporarily alter corneal shape

Recognizing the Symptoms

Blurred Vision

Blurry vision at all distances—near, intermediate, and far. Objects lack sharp definition.

Distorted Vision

Images appear stretched, elongated, or wavy. Straight lines may look curved or tilted.

Eye Strain

Eyes feel tired, especially after prolonged visual tasks like reading or computer work.

Headaches

Frequent headaches, particularly frontal or around the eyes, from straining to see clearly.

Squinting

Habitual squinting to try to see more clearly (creates a pinhole effect that reduces blur).

Night Vision Difficulty

Trouble seeing at night, with halos, starbursts, or streaks around lights.

Light Sensitivity

Sensitivity to bright lights and glare, with discomfort in bright conditions.

Double Vision

Multiple ghost images of a single object (monocular diplopia), especially with lights.

How Symptoms Vary by Severity

Mild Astigmatism (< 1.00 D)

  • May have no noticeable symptoms
  • Slight blurring under certain conditions
  • Mild eye strain or headaches
  • May not require correction

Moderate Astigmatism (1.00-3.00 D)

  • Noticeable blurred or distorted vision
  • Regular eye strain and headaches
  • Difficulty with detailed tasks
  • Correction usually needed

Severe Astigmatism (> 3.00 D)

  • Significantly impaired vision
  • Constant blur and distortion
  • Severe headaches and eye fatigue
  • Daily activities affected
  • Correction essential

Symptoms in Children

Children may not complain directly. Watch for:

  • Difficulty reading or keeping place on page
  • Sitting too close to TV
  • Tilting head to see more clearly
  • Frequent squinting
  • Rubbing eyes
  • Avoiding reading or detailed tasks
  • Complaints of headaches
  • Poor school performance

When Symptoms Are Worse

  • During prolonged reading or screen time
  • In low light conditions
  • When driving at night
  • When tired
  • With detailed visual tasks

Diagnosis and Testing

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Visual Acuity Test

  • Reading eye chart at distance
  • Tests each eye separately
  • Astigmatism causes reduced acuity
  • Pattern of poor vision may suggest astigmatism

Refraction Assessment

Determines exact prescription needed:

  • Autorefractor: Automated measurement of refractive error
  • Phoropter/Refractor: Manual refinement using trial lenses
  • Cross-cylinder technique: Precisely determines cylinder power and axis
  • Retinoscopy: Objective measurement using light reflex

Keratometry

  • Measures corneal curvature in different meridians
  • Identifies amount and axis of corneal astigmatism
  • Essential for contact lens fitting
  • Standard part of comprehensive exam

Corneal Topography

  • Creates detailed 3D map of corneal surface
  • Shows curvature variations across entire cornea
  • Essential for irregular astigmatism
  • Used pre-operatively for LASIK
  • Diagnoses conditions like keratoconus

Aberrometry (Wavefront Analysis)

  • Measures higher-order aberrations beyond standard refractive error
  • Provides detailed optical quality assessment
  • Used for custom wavefront-guided LASIK

Additional Tests

  • Slit lamp examination: Checks for corneal abnormalities or scarring
  • Dilated fundus exam: Rules out retinal problems
  • Cycloplegic refraction: In children, to relax accommodation

Distinguishing Regular from Irregular Astigmatism

Feature Regular Astigmatism Irregular Astigmatism
Meridians Perpendicular (90° apart) Not perpendicular or multiple
Corneal topography Bow-tie pattern Irregular pattern
Caused by Congenital, normal variation Disease, injury, surgery
Corrected by Glasses or soft toric lenses Often requires rigid contact lenses
Visual quality with correction Excellent May remain suboptimal

Treatment and Correction Options

Eyeglasses

First-line treatment for most people:

Cylindrical Lenses

  • Special lenses with different powers in different meridians
  • Compensate for unequal corneal curvature
  • Prescription includes sphere, cylinder, and axis
  • Provide clear, comfortable vision for regular astigmatism

Lens Options:

  • High-index materials: Thinner, lighter lenses for high astigmatism
  • Aspheric designs: Reduce distortion and improve cosmetics
  • Anti-reflective coating: Reduces glare, important for night driving
  • Polycarbonate: Impact-resistant for children and sports

Adaptation Period:

  • New glasses may feel strange initially, especially if first correction or if axis changed
  • May notice slight distortion in peripheral vision
  • Usually adapt within a few days to 2 weeks
  • If persistent discomfort, prescription may need adjustment

Contact Lenses

Toric Soft Contact Lenses

  • Designed specifically for astigmatism
  • Different powers in different meridians, like cylindrical glasses
  • Weighted or designed to maintain proper orientation on eye
  • Available in daily, bi-weekly, and monthly disposable options
  • Effective for regular astigmatism up to ~2.75-3.00 D
  • May rotate slightly on eye, causing variable vision

Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Contact Lenses

  • Create smooth optical surface over irregular cornea
  • Tear layer fills in corneal irregularities
  • Excellent for high or irregular astigmatism
  • Sharper vision than soft lenses
  • More durable, better oxygen transmission
  • Initial discomfort; adaptation takes longer

Hybrid Contact Lenses

  • Rigid center for clear vision
  • Soft skirt for comfort
  • Combines benefits of RGP and soft lenses
  • Good for moderate to high astigmatism

Scleral Lenses

  • Large diameter lenses vault over cornea
  • Excellent for irregular astigmatism
  • Very comfortable
  • Create fluid reservoir over cornea

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K)

  • Rigid lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape cornea
  • Clear vision during day without correction
  • Effective for mild to moderate myopia with astigmatism
  • Reversible
  • Good for children, athletes

Refractive Surgery

Permanent correction for adults with stable prescriptions:

LASIK (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)

Most popular option:

  • Laser reshapes cornea to correct astigmatism
  • Flattens steeper meridian or steepens flatter meridian
  • Quick recovery, minimal discomfort
  • Can correct astigmatism with myopia or hyperopia simultaneously
  • Effective for up to ~6 D of astigmatism
  • Wavefront-guided custom LASIK for better results

PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy)

  • Similar to LASIK but no flap created
  • Longer recovery period
  • Good for thin corneas
  • Same long-term results as LASIK

Toric Intraocular Lenses (IOLs)

  • During cataract surgery, toric IOL corrects astigmatism
  • Also available as phakic IOL (lens implant without cataract removal)
  • Permanent correction
  • Good for high astigmatism
  • Must be properly aligned for optimal results

Limbal Relaxing Incisions (LRI)

  • Small incisions in corneal periphery
  • Flatten steep meridian
  • Often combined with cataract surgery
  • Effective for low to moderate astigmatism (< 3 D)

Astigmatic Keratotomy (AK)

  • Older technique, less commonly used now
  • Incisions made to flatten steep meridian
  • Less predictable than laser surgery

Candidacy Requirements

  • Age 18+ (21+ for some procedures)
  • Stable prescription for at least 1 year
  • Healthy eyes (no active disease)
  • Adequate corneal thickness
  • Realistic expectations
  • Regular astigmatism (irregular may not be suitable)

Considerations

  • Astigmatism correction may be less predictable than sphere correction
  • Axis alignment critical for optimal results
  • May need enhancement procedure
  • Doesn't prevent presbyopia
  • Possible side effects: dry eyes, glare, halos

Living with Astigmatism

Adaptation to Correction

  • New glasses: May feel strange for a few days, especially peripheral vision
  • Axis changes: Even small axis changes can require adaptation
  • Full-time wear: Helps adapt faster than part-time use
  • Contact lenses: Toric lenses may take longer to adapt to than spherical

When to Update Prescription

  • Vision no longer clear with current correction
  • Increased squinting or eye strain
  • Headaches returning
  • Every 1-2 years even if vision seems fine (astigmatism can change)

Lifestyle Considerations

  • Night driving: Anti-reflective coating helps reduce halos and glare
  • Computer work: Ensure proper correction and good ergonomics
  • Sports: Contact lenses or sports glasses often preferred
  • Swimming: Remove contacts; use prescription goggles if needed

Special Situations

Pregnancy

  • Hormonal changes can temporarily alter corneal shape
  • Astigmatism may increase or decrease
  • Usually returns to baseline after delivery
  • Delay refractive surgery until stable postpartum

Children

  • Regular eye exams to monitor changes
  • Ensure glasses fit properly and child wears them consistently
  • Watch for amblyopia risk with high or asymmetric astigmatism

Good News

  • Astigmatism doesn't worsen eye health or lead to eye disease
  • Modern correction options provide excellent vision
  • Usually stable in adults
  • Many people live full, active lives with well-corrected astigmatism

When to See a Doctor

Schedule an Eye Exam If:

  • Blurred or distorted vision at any distance
  • Frequent squinting to see clearly
  • Eye strain or discomfort
  • Headaches, especially after visual tasks
  • Difficulty with night vision or driving at night
  • Straight lines appear curved or wavy
  • Current glasses or contacts no longer provide clear vision

Children Should Be Seen If:

  • Difficulty reading or keeping place on page
  • Sitting very close to TV
  • Squinting frequently
  • Tilting head to see better
  • Complaints of blurry vision or headaches
  • Avoiding reading or detailed activities
  • Poor school performance possibly related to vision

Regular Screening Schedule

  • Children: First exam at 6 months, then age 3, before kindergarten, then annually
  • Adults under 40: Every 2 years (annually if wearing correction)
  • Adults 40-64: Every 1-2 years
  • Adults 65+: Annually
  • Between exams: If vision changes or symptoms develop

Questions to Ask Your Eye Doctor

  • How much astigmatism do I have, and in which axis?
  • Is it regular or irregular?
  • Has my astigmatism changed since my last exam?
  • Do I need to wear correction full-time?
  • What are my contact lens options?
  • Am I a candidate for LASIK or other refractive surgery?
  • Is there an underlying cause I should be concerned about?
  • How often should I have follow-up exams?
