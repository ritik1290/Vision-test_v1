What Is Keratitis?

Keratitis is inflammation of the corneaâ€”the clear, dome-shaped front surface of the eye that covers the iris and pupil. This condition can result from infection (bacterial, viral, fungal, or parasitic) or non-infectious causes (injury, dry eyes, exposure). Keratitis ranges from mild irritation to serious, sight-threatening infection that can cause corneal scarring and permanent vision loss if not treated promptly.

The cornea is essential for clear vision, providing about two-thirds of the eye's focusing power. Any damage or scarring to this transparent structure can significantly impair vision. Because the cornea lacks blood vessels and relies on tears and the aqueous humor for nourishment, it can be vulnerable to infection, especially when its protective barriers are compromised.

Key Facts About Keratitis

  • Serious condition: Can threaten vision; requires prompt medical attention.
  • Common in contact lens wearers: Improper lens care is a major risk factor.
  • Multiple causes: Bacterial, viral, fungal, parasitic infections, or non-infectious inflammation.
  • Symptoms: Eye pain, redness, light sensitivity, blurred vision, discharge.
  • Medical emergency: Infectious keratitis needs immediate treatment to prevent scarring.
  • Preventable: Proper contact lens hygiene and eye safety reduce risk significantly.
  • Can cause permanent damage: Corneal scarring may require transplant.
  • Early treatment crucial: Prompt care prevents complications and preserves vision.

Understanding keratitis, recognizing its symptoms, and seeking immediate care are critical for protecting your vision and preventing serious complications.

Understanding the Cornea

Corneal Structure and Function

The cornea is a remarkable structure with five distinct layers:

1. Epithelium (Outer Layer)

  • Protective barrier against bacteria, dust, and water
  • Smooth surface for clear vision
  • Highly regenerative; heals quickly when damaged
  • Contains nerve endings (very sensitive to pain)
  • Most keratitis starts here

2. Bowman's Layer

  • Thin protective layer beneath epithelium
  • Does not regenerate if damaged
  • Scarring here affects vision permanently

3. Stroma (Middle Layer - 90% of corneal thickness)

  • Made of organized collagen fibers and water
  • Provides strength and transparency
  • Deep infections here are more serious
  • Scarring impairs vision significantly

4. Descemet's Membrane

  • Thin basement membrane
  • Protects against infection and injury

5. Endothelium (Inner Layer)

  • Single layer of cells
  • Pumps excess fluid out of stroma (keeps cornea clear)
  • Does not regenerate; damage causes corneal swelling

Why the Cornea Is Vulnerable

  • No blood vessels: Limited immune defense; relies on tears for protection
  • Exposed surface: Direct contact with environment and potential pathogens
  • Contact lens wear: Can create microabrasions and reduce oxygen
  • Dry eyes: Reduced tear protection
  • Trauma: Breaks protective epithelial barrier

What Happens in Keratitis

  • Protective epithelial barrier is compromised
  • Microorganisms or inflammatory triggers invade corneal tissue
  • Inflammatory response causes swelling, cell infiltration
  • White blood cells accumulate (visible as corneal infiltrate or ulcer)
  • Without treatment, infection deepens, scarring occurs
  • Severe cases: corneal perforation, vision loss

Types of Keratitis

Infectious Keratitis

Bacterial Keratitis

Most common type of infectious keratitis:

  • Common organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Risk factors: Contact lens wear (especially overnight), corneal trauma, dry eyes
  • Presentation: Rapid onset, severe pain, white/yellow corneal infiltrate
  • Pseudomonas: Particularly aggressive in contact lens wearers; can perforate cornea in days
  • Treatment: Intensive antibiotic eye drops (initially hourly)
  • Prognosis: Good if treated early; can cause significant scarring if delayed

Viral Keratitis

Caused by viruses, most commonly herpes:

  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV):
    • Most common viral cause
    • Causes dendritic (branching) ulcers - pathognomonic pattern
    • Can affect epithelium (epithelial keratitis) or deeper layers (stromal keratitis)
    • Recurrent; virus remains dormant in nerves
    • Triggers: stress, illness, UV exposure, immunosuppression
    • Treatment: Antiviral drops or oral medication
  • Varicella-zoster virus (shingles):
    • Causes zoster keratitis
    • Associated with facial shingles rash
    • Can have long-term complications
  • Adenovirus:
    • Causes epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (EKC)
    • Highly contagious
    • Usually self-limited

Fungal Keratitis

Less common but serious:

  • Common organisms: Fusarium, Aspergillus, Candida
  • Risk factors:
    • Agricultural work or plant material trauma
    • Contact lens wear (especially contaminated solutions)
    • Chronic steroid eye drop use
    • Immunosuppression
    • Tropical climates
  • Presentation: Slow onset, feathery or dry-appearing infiltrate, satellite lesions
  • Treatment: Prolonged antifungal therapy (weeks to months)
  • Prognosis: More difficult to treat; higher risk of complications

Acanthamoeba Keratitis

Parasitic infection, rare but devastating:

  • Organism: Acanthamoeba - free-living amoeba in water and soil
  • Risk factors:
    • Contact lens wear with tap water exposure (swimming, showering, homemade solutions)
    • Contaminated lens cases
    • Corneal trauma with soil/water exposure
  • Presentation:
    • Severe pain out of proportion to clinical findings (classic feature)
    • Ring-shaped stromal infiltrate (late finding)
    • Progressive over weeks
  • Diagnosis: Often delayed; requires high suspicion
  • Treatment:
    • Extremely difficult; requires prolonged therapy (months to over a year)
    • Combination of antiseptic drops (PHMB, chlorhexidine, propamidine)
    • High rate of corneal scarring
    • May require corneal transplant

Non-Infectious Keratitis

Exposure Keratitis

  • From incomplete eyelid closure (lagophthalmos)
  • Causes: Bell's palsy, thyroid eye disease, scarring, unconsciousness
  • Cornea dries out, becomes vulnerable to infection
  • Treatment: Artificial tears, ointment, taping lid shut, moisture chambers

Photokeratitis (UV Keratitis)

  • From excessive UV exposure
  • Causes: Welding without protection, snow (snow blindness), tanning beds
  • Symptoms develop 6-12 hours after exposure
  • Severe pain, tearing, light sensitivity
  • Usually self-limited; heals in 24-48 hours

Neurotrophic Keratitis

  • From loss of corneal sensation (nerve damage)
  • Causes: Diabetes, herpes zoster, surgery, chronic contact lens wear
  • Reduced blink reflex and poor healing
  • Can develop persistent epithelial defects and ulcers

Autoimmune Keratitis

  • Associated with systemic autoimmune diseases
  • Peripheral ulcerative keratitis in rheumatoid arthritis, Wegener's granulomatosis
  • Requires treatment of underlying condition

Causes and Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors

Contact Lens Wear (Most Common)

  • Overwear: Sleeping in lenses reduces oxygen to cornea
  • Poor hygiene: Not washing hands, reusing solution, contaminated cases
  • Water exposure: Swimming, showering, or rinsing lenses with tap water
  • Extended wear lenses: Higher infection risk than daily wear
  • Old or damaged lenses: Increased bacterial adherence
  • Contact lens-related keratitis is a medical emergency

Corneal Injury/Trauma

  • Scratches from fingernails, tree branches, paper
  • Foreign body (metal, wood, plant material)
  • Chemical exposure
  • Any break in epithelium creates entry point for infection

Dry Eye Syndrome

  • Reduced tear production or quality
  • Less protection against infection
  • Chronic inflammation and epithelial damage

Contaminated Eye Drops or Solutions

  • Using contaminated bottles
  • Homemade saline solutions
  • Touching dropper tip to eye or surfaces

Eyelid Problems

  • Incomplete closure (exposure keratitis)
  • Ectropion (lid turns outward) or entropion (lid turns inward)
  • Trichiasis (inturned eyelashes)

Immunosuppression

  • HIV/AIDS
  • Chemotherapy
  • Organ transplant medications
  • Chronic steroid use
  • Diabetes

Previous Herpes Simplex Eye Infection

  • Virus remains dormant; can reactivate
  • Recurrence common with stress, illness, UV exposure

Environmental/Occupational Factors

  • Agricultural work (plant material trauma - fungal risk)
  • Welding (UV exposure)
  • Dusty or dirty environments
  • Swimming in contaminated water

Recognizing the Symptoms

Eye Pain

Moderate to severe eye pain, often sharp or stabbing. Pain may worsen with blinking or eye movement.

Redness

Red, bloodshot eye from inflammation and dilated blood vessels around the cornea.

Light Sensitivity

Severe photophobia - bright light causes significant discomfort and squinting.

Blurred Vision

Vision becomes blurry or hazy from corneal swelling, infiltrate, or discharge.

Excessive Tearing

Watery eyes and increased tear production as response to irritation.

Discharge

Mucus or pus discharge, especially in bacterial infections. May crust eyelids shut.

Foreign Body Sensation

Feeling like sand or grit in the eye, constant irritation and urge to rub.

Difficulty Opening Eye

Eye may be difficult to open due to pain, swelling, or lid spasm.

Specific Patterns by Type

Bacterial Keratitis

  • Rapid onset (hours to days)
  • Severe pain, redness, discharge
  • White or yellowish spot on cornea
  • Vision rapidly deteriorating

Viral (HSV) Keratitis

  • Moderate pain and redness
  • Light sensitivity
  • Tearing
  • May have prior history of cold sores
  • Recurrent episodes

Fungal Keratitis

  • Gradual onset (days to weeks)
  • Progressive pain and redness
  • May have history of plant material injury
  • Slower progression than bacterial

Acanthamoeba Keratitis

  • Disproportionate pain - hallmark symptom
  • Pain severe relative to appearance
  • Gradual onset over weeks
  • Contact lens wearer with water exposure
  • Progressive vision loss

Seek Immediate Care If:

  • You wear contact lenses and develop eye pain, redness, or vision changes
  • Eye pain after injury or foreign body
  • Sudden vision loss
  • Severe eye pain and light sensitivity
  • White spot visible on cornea

Keratitis is a medical emergency. Same-day evaluation by an ophthalmologist is essential.

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

History

  • Contact lens wear and hygiene practices
  • Recent trauma or foreign body
  • Exposure to soil, plants, or contaminated water
  • History of herpes or recurrent eye infections
  • Systemic diseases or immunosuppression
  • Symptom onset and progression

Visual Acuity

  • Measures current vision
  • May be significantly reduced

Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy

Detailed magnified examination:

  • Corneal epithelium: Defects, ulceration, dendritic pattern (HSV)
  • Corneal stroma: Infiltrate (white blood cell accumulation), edema, depth of involvement
  • Size and location: Important for prognosis
  • Anterior chamber: Inflammation, hypopyon (pus layer)
  • Fluorescein staining: Reveals epithelial defects (stain green under blue light)

Diagnostic Testing

Corneal Scraping and Culture

  • For moderate to severe or atypical cases
  • Scrape corneal ulcer base with sterile instrument
  • Obtain samples for culture and sensitivity
  • Bacterial culture: Identifies organism, guides antibiotic choice
  • Fungal culture: May take weeks to grow
  • Acanthamoeba culture: Specialized media
  • Gram stain, KOH prep for immediate microscopy

Confocal Microscopy

  • Non-invasive imaging of cornea at cellular level
  • Can detect Acanthamoeba cysts
  • Useful for fungal keratitis

PCR Testing

  • Molecular testing for herpes simplex, varicella-zoster
  • Faster and more sensitive than culture

Classification

Doctor assesses:

  • Type: Bacterial, viral, fungal, parasitic, or non-infectious
  • Severity: Size, depth, location of infiltrate
  • Central vs. peripheral ulcer (central worse for vision)
  • Complications: Perforation risk, descemetocele

Treatment

Treatment depends on the cause and severity:

Bacterial Keratitis

Antibiotic Eye Drops (Fortified Antibiotics)

  • Initial treatment: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (before culture results)
  • Common regimens:
    • Fluoroquinolone (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin, levofloxacin)
    • Or combination: Cefazolin + tobramycin or gentamicin
  • Frequency: Initially every 30-60 minutes around the clock (intensive therapy)
  • Duration: Gradually taper as infection improves; total 2-6 weeks
  • Adjustment: Based on culture results and clinical response
  • May require hospital admission for hourly drops in severe cases

Cycloplegic Drops

  • Dilate pupil, reduce pain from ciliary spasm
  • Prevent synechiae (iris adhesions)
  • Examples: Cyclopentolate, atropine

Viral (HSV) Keratitis

  • Antiviral drops: Ganciclovir gel, trifluridine (discontinued in US, available elsewhere)
  • Oral antivirals: Acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir
  • Duration: 1-3 weeks for epithelial disease
  • Stromal keratitis: May require topical steroids (with antiviral cover)
  • Prophylaxis: Long-term oral antivirals for recurrent cases
  • NEVER use steroids alone in active HSV without antiviral (worsens infection)

Fungal Keratitis

  • Topical antifungal drops:
    • Natamycin (Natacyn) - first-line for filamentous fungi
    • Amphotericin B (compounded)
    • Voriconazole (compounded)
  • Frequency: Initially hourly
  • Duration: Weeks to months (typically 6-12 weeks minimum)
  • Oral antifungals: Voriconazole, fluconazole for deep or severe infections
  • Monitoring: Very close follow-up; slow response
  • Surgery: May require corneal transplant if medical therapy fails

Acanthamoeba Keratitis

  • Antiseptic drops (combination):
    • PHMB (polyhexamethylene biguanide) 0.02%
    • Chlorhexidine 0.02%
    • Propamidine isethionate (Brolene)
  • Frequency: Every hour initially
  • Duration: Many months to over a year
  • Adjuncts: Oral voriconazole or miltefosine sometimes added
  • Challenge: Cysts are very resistant; treatment prolonged and often incomplete
  • Prognosis: High rate of corneal scarring; often requires transplant

Supportive Care

  • Pain management: Oral pain medications (never use topical anesthetics at home)
  • Stop contact lens wear immediately
  • Patching: Generally avoided (reduces oxygen, may worsen infection)
  • Lubrication: Preservative-free artificial tears

Surgical Treatment

  • Reserved for cases failing medical therapy or with complications:
  • Penetrating keratoplasty (corneal transplant):
    • For vision rehabilitation after healing
    • Sometimes therapeutic (to remove infected tissue)
  • Amniotic membrane transplant: For persistent epithelial defects
  • Corneal cross-linking: Experimental for some infections

Contact Lens Safety and Prevention

Proper contact lens care is critical for preventing keratitis:

Essential Hygiene Practices

  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water before handling lenses
  • Never use tap water with contact lenses (rinse, store, or make homemade solution)
  • Use only fresh solution - never "top off" old solution
  • Rub and rinse lenses even if using "no-rub" solution
  • Replace lens case every 3 months or per manufacturer recommendation
  • Clean and air-dry case daily (upside-down on clean tissue)

Wear Schedule

  • Never sleep in lenses unless specifically approved for extended wear
  • Even with extended-wear lenses: Risk of infection is higher
  • Follow replacement schedule: Daily, bi-weekly, or monthly as prescribed
  • Don't overwear: Remove lenses if eyes feel irritated

Activities to Avoid

  • Swimming in lenses: Remove before swimming or use daily disposables and discard immediately after
  • Showering in lenses: Increases Acanthamoeba risk
  • Hot tubs: High contamination risk

When to Remove Lenses Immediately

  • Any eye pain, redness, or irritation
  • Blurred vision
  • Light sensitivity
  • Excessive tearing or discharge
  • Contact your eye doctor same day

Consider Daily Disposable Lenses

  • Lowest risk of infection
  • No cleaning or storage required
  • Fresh, sterile lens each day
  • Ideal for occasional wear or active lifestyles

Potential Complications

Corneal Scarring

  • Most common complication
  • Results from deep stromal involvement or delayed treatment
  • Permanent cloudiness of cornea
  • Impairs vision, especially if central
  • May require corneal transplant for vision rehabilitation

Corneal Ulcer Perforation

  • Full-thickness hole in cornea
  • Medical emergency
  • Can occur with aggressive infections (especially Pseudomonas)
  • Requires urgent surgical repair

Descemetocele

  • Ulcer extends through stroma, only Descemet's membrane remains
  • Very thin, at high risk of perforation
  • Requires close monitoring, possible surgical intervention

Endophthalmitis

  • Infection spreads inside eye
  • Vision-threatening
  • Requires intravitreal antibiotics

Vision Loss

  • From central corneal scarring
  • May be permanent
  • Severity depends on location, depth, and promptness of treatment

Glaucoma

  • From inflammatory debris blocking drainage
  • From chronic steroid use

Recurrent Erosions

  • Especially after traumatic epithelial defects
  • Epithelium doesn't adhere properly
  • Painful episodes upon waking

Prevention

Contact Lens Wearers

  • Follow all hygiene recommendations above
  • Never sleep in lenses unless approved by doctor
  • No water exposure
  • Regular eye exams
  • Remove lenses immediately if any symptoms develop

Eye Protection

  • Safety glasses/goggles: For work or activities with eye injury risk
  • UV protection: Sunglasses for welding, snow, bright sun
  • Sports: Protective eyewear for basketball, racquet sports, etc.

General Eye Care

  • Don't touch or rub eyes with unwashed hands
  • Treat dry eyes: Artificial tears, address underlying causes
  • Properly remove eye makeup each night
  • Don't share eye makeup, towels, or washcloths
  • Discard old or contaminated eye drops

For Herpes Simplex Keratitis

  • Long-term oral antiviral suppression if frequent recurrences
  • Manage triggers: stress reduction, UV protection
  • Seek care at first sign of recurrence

Agricultural Workers

  • Eye protection when working with plants
  • Immediate eye irrigation if foreign body or plant material injury
  • Prompt medical evaluation for any corneal injury

When to See a Doctor

Seek IMMEDIATE Eye Care For:

  • Contact lens wearer with eye pain, redness, light sensitivity, or blurred vision
  • Eye pain after injury or foreign body
  • Sudden vision loss or significant blurring
  • Severe eye pain and redness
  • White or cloudy spot visible on cornea
  • Discharge with pain and redness
  • Light sensitivity preventing eye opening

Keratitis is an emergency requiring same-day evaluation by an ophthalmologist. Call ahead if possible, but do not delay seeking care.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of keratitis do I have?
  • What caused it?
  • How severe is the infection/inflammation?
  • What is my treatment plan?
  • How often do I need to use the drops?
  • When should I expect improvement?
  • What signs of worsening should I watch for?
  • When can I resume contact lens wear (if applicable)?
  • Will this cause permanent scarring or vision loss?
  • How often do I need follow-up visits?
  • Could this recur?
