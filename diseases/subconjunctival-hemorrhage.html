What Is a Subconjunctival Hemorrhage?

A subconjunctival hemorrhage is a bright red patch or spot on the white part of the eye (sclera) caused by a small blood vessel breaking beneath the conjunctiva—the clear, thin membrane covering the sclera. When a tiny blood vessel ruptures, blood spreads between the conjunctiva and sclera, creating a dramatic-looking but usually harmless red area. The appearance can be alarming, as the blood is bright red and highly visible, but subconjunctival hemorrhages are typically painless and do not affect vision.

This common condition can occur spontaneously or be triggered by activities that increase blood pressure in the head, such as coughing, sneezing, straining, heavy lifting, or vomiting. It's similar to a bruise on the skin, except it occurs on the eye. Most subconjunctival hemorrhages resolve on their own without treatment within 1-2 weeks as the blood is reabsorbed. While they look dramatic, they're usually benign and require no treatment.

Key Facts About Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

  • Bright red blood spot: Dramatic appearance; blood clearly visible on white of eye.
  • Usually harmless: Benign condition; does not affect vision or damage eye.
  • Painless: No pain; may have mild irritation or fullness sensation.
  • Common causes: Coughing, sneezing, straining, rubbing eyes, trauma.
  • Spontaneous in many cases: Often occurs without obvious cause.
  • Self-resolving: Clears on its own in 1-2 weeks; no treatment needed.
  • More common with age: Blood vessels become fragile; also with blood thinners.
  • Recurrence possible: Can happen repeatedly, especially if on blood thinners.

Understanding subconjunctival hemorrhage helps reduce anxiety—it looks worse than it is.

Causes and Risk Factors

Common Triggers

Sudden Increase in Pressure

  • Coughing or sneezing: Forceful, especially with respiratory infection
  • Straining: Constipation, heavy lifting, childbirth
  • Vomiting: Forceful or prolonged
  • Pressure spike causes small vessel to rupture

Eye Trauma

  • Rubbing eyes vigorously
  • Foreign body in eye
  • Blunt trauma (hit to eye or head)
  • Eye surgery or procedures

Spontaneous (No Obvious Cause)

  • Very common—many occur without identifiable trigger
  • May happen during sleep
  • Wake up and notice blood spot

Risk Factors

Blood-Thinning Medications

  • Anticoagulants: Warfarin, heparin, rivaroxaban, apixaban
  • Antiplatelet agents: Aspirin, clopidogrel
  • Increase bleeding tendency; higher risk of subconjunctival hemorrhage
  • Hemorrhages may be larger, take longer to resolve

High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

  • Chronic hypertension weakens blood vessels
  • Increases rupture risk
  • Recurrent hemorrhages may warrant BP check

Diabetes

  • Diabetic blood vessel changes

Blood Clotting Disorders

  • Thrombocytopenia (low platelets)
  • Hemophilia or other bleeding disorders
  • Rare cause

Age

  • More common in elderly
  • Conjunctival blood vessels become fragile

Contact Lens Wear

  • Trauma from insertion/removal

Eye Infections

  • Conjunctivitis can cause small hemorrhages

Appearance and Symptoms

Visual Appearance

  • Bright red patch: Blood clearly visible against white sclera
  • Size variable: Small spot to covering large area of white of eye (can cover entire visible sclera)
  • Well-defined borders: Blood doesn't cross onto cornea (clear front part of eye)
  • Flat: Not raised; eye surface smooth
  • Changes over time:
    • Initially bright red
    • Fades to orange, yellow, then disappears (like bruise)

Symptoms (Usually Minimal)

No Pain

  • Painless in vast majority
  • If painful, consider other diagnosis (corneal abrasion, iritis)

Normal Vision

  • Vision completely unaffected
  • Blood is beneath clear conjunctiva, not in front of pupil/cornea

Mild Symptoms (If Any)

  • Slight scratchy or fullness sensation
  • Awareness of "something different"
  • Foreign body sensation (mild)

No Discharge

  • No pus or mucus (unlike conjunctivitis)

What It's NOT

  • Not bleeding from inside eye: Blood is superficial, beneath conjunctiva
  • Not dangerous: Does not damage vision
  • Not progressive: Doesn't spread; existing blood reabsorbs

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

  • Based on appearance
  • Bright red blood beneath conjunctiva diagnostic
  • No special tests needed

Examination

  • Visual inspection sufficient
  • Check vision (should be normal)
  • Ask about trauma, symptoms, medications

When to Investigate Further

  • Recurrent hemorrhages: Check blood pressure, consider bleeding disorder workup
  • Associated with trauma: Ensure no other eye injury (hyphema, ruptured globe)
  • Bilateral (both eyes): Rare; may suggest bleeding disorder or severe trauma
  • Spontaneous, recurrent, + easy bruising elsewhere: Consider hematology referral

Treatment

No Specific Treatment Needed

  • Subconjunctival hemorrhage resolves spontaneously
  • No medication speeds resolution
  • Blood reabsorbed naturally over 1-2 weeks

Supportive Measures

Artificial Tears

  • If mild irritation or dryness
  • Lubricating drops for comfort
  • No impact on hemorrhage resolution

Avoid Further Trauma

  • Don't rub eyes
  • Protect eye from injury

Monitor Blood Pressure

  • If hypertensive or recurrent hemorrhages
  • Ensure BP controlled

Continue Blood Thinners (Unless Instructed Otherwise)

  • Do NOT stop aspirin, warfarin, etc. without consulting prescribing doctor
  • Subconjunctival hemorrhage does not require stopping anticoagulation
  • Benefits of blood thinners (preventing stroke, heart attack) outweigh cosmetic concern

What NOT to Do

  • No eye drops will make it go away faster
  • Don't use steroid drops (not indicated, won't help)
  • Don't apply heat or cold (ineffective)

Resolution Timeline

Typical Course

  • Days 1-3: Hemorrhage bright red; may look larger as blood spreads
  • Days 4-7: Blood begins to fade; edges become lighter
  • Days 7-10: Color changes to orange, then yellow (like bruise healing)
  • Days 10-14: Nearly or completely resolved

Factors Affecting Resolution Time

  • Size: Larger hemorrhages take longer
  • Blood thinners: May prolong resolution slightly
  • Age: May take longer in elderly

Expected Outcome

  • Complete resolution with no residual effects
  • No scarring
  • Vision normal throughout
  • Eye returns to normal white appearance

When to See a Doctor

Usually No Urgent Care Needed

  • Most subconjunctival hemorrhages benign, self-resolving
  • May reassure yourself by seeing doctor, but often unnecessary

See Doctor If:

  • Eye pain (suggests other problem—corneal abrasion, iritis, glaucoma)
  • Vision changes (blurred, decreased vision—not typical of subconjunctival hemorrhage)
  • Associated with head or eye trauma (rule out more serious injury—hyphema, ruptured globe)
  • Recurrent hemorrhages (multiple episodes; check BP, bleeding disorders)
  • Bilateral (both eyes simultaneously)
  • Hemorrhage covers entire eye, very large
  • Light sensitivity, discharge (suggests infection)
  • Easy bruising elsewhere, frequent nosebleeds, gum bleeding (possible bleeding disorder)
  • Doesn't resolve in 2-3 weeks

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this a subconjunctival hemorrhage or something else?
  • Do I need any blood tests?
  • Should I check my blood pressure?
  • Can I continue my blood thinners?
  • How can I prevent this from happening again?

Bottom Line

A subconjunctival hemorrhage is a bright red blood spot on the white of the eye caused by a broken blood vessel beneath the conjunctiva. While it looks dramatic and alarming, it's usually harmless, painless, and doesn't affect vision. Common causes include coughing, sneezing, straining, eye rubbing, or trauma, though many occur spontaneously. It's more common in people on blood thinners or with high blood pressure. No treatment is needed—the blood reabsorbs naturally over 1-2 weeks, changing from red to orange to yellow (like a bruise) before disappearing completely. See a doctor if you have eye pain, vision changes, associated trauma, or recurrent hemorrhages. Otherwise, subconjunctival hemorrhage is benign and will resolve on its own with no lasting effects.
