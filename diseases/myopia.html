What Is Myopia?

Myopia, commonly known as nearsightedness, is a refractive error where distant objects appear blurry while close objects can be seen clearly. It occurs when the eye grows too long from front to back, or when the cornea (the clear front surface of the eye) is too curved. This causes light rays to focus in front of the retina instead of directly on it, resulting in blurred distance vision.

Myopia is the most common refractive error worldwide and its prevalence is increasing dramatically. While it typically begins in childhood and progresses through the teenage years, myopia can develop at any age and in varying degrees of severity.

Key Facts About Myopia

  • Extremely common: Affects about 30% of the U.S. population and up to 90% in some Asian countries.
  • Increasing prevalence: Expected to affect 50% of the world's population by 2050 (5 billion people).
  • Usually starts in childhood: Typically develops between ages 6-14 and progresses until early 20s.
  • Easily corrected: Glasses, contact lenses, or refractive surgery provide clear vision.
  • Hereditary component: Children with myopic parents are at higher risk.
  • Not just an inconvenience: High myopia increases risk of serious eye conditions like retinal detachment.
  • Can be slowed: Myopia control strategies can reduce progression in children.
  • Lifelong condition: Once myopic, always myopic, though prescription stabilizes in adulthood.

Understanding myopia, its causes, and treatment options is important for maintaining good vision and eye health. Early detection and intervention, especially in children, can help slow progression and reduce the risk of sight-threatening complications later in life.

How Vision Works—And What Goes Wrong in Myopia

Normal Vision (Emmetropia)

In a normal eye, light enters through the cornea and pupil, is focused by the lens, and converges precisely on the retina at the back of the eye. The retina converts light into electrical signals sent to the brain via the optic nerve, creating the images we see.

Key components:

  • Cornea: Provides about 2/3 of the eye's focusing power
  • Lens: Fine-tunes focus for different distances
  • Retina: Light-sensitive tissue that captures images
  • Axial length: Distance from front to back of eye (normally ~23-24mm)

What Happens in Myopia

In myopia, light focuses in front of the retina instead of on it, causing distant objects to appear blurry. This occurs due to:

1. Axial Myopia (Most Common)

  • The eyeball is too long from front to back
  • Even with normal corneal curvature and lens power, light focuses too early
  • Accounts for most myopia cases
  • Each 1mm of extra length adds about -3 diopters of myopia

2. Refractive Myopia (Less Common)

  • Cornea is too curved or lens has excessive focusing power
  • Normal axial length, but too much refractive power

Why Close Objects Are Clear

Close objects produce diverging light rays that naturally focus farther back. In myopia, these rays converge perfectly on the retina, which is why near vision remains clear without correction.

Measuring Myopia

Myopia is measured in diopters (D), indicated by a minus (-) sign:

  • Low myopia: -0.25 to -3.00 D
  • Moderate myopia: -3.00 to -6.00 D
  • High myopia: -6.00 D or greater
  • Extreme/pathological myopia: -10.00 D or greater

Types of Myopia

Simple Myopia

The most common form:

  • Low to moderate degree (usually < -6.00 D)
  • Progression typically stops in early adulthood
  • No associated eye diseases
  • Easily corrected with glasses or contacts

High Myopia (Pathological Myopia)

More severe form with increased health risks:

  • Usually -6.00 D or greater
  • May continue progressing beyond early adulthood
  • Associated with longer axial length (typically > 26mm)
  • Increased risk of eye complications (retinal detachment, myopic macular degeneration, glaucoma)
  • Affects ~2-3% of population
  • May require regular monitoring for complications

Progressive Myopia

  • Myopia that continues to worsen throughout life
  • Frequent prescription changes needed
  • May be related to underlying eye diseases
  • Requires closer monitoring

Degenerative Myopia (Malignant Myopia)

  • Rare, severe form
  • Rapidly progressive, often reaching very high levels (-15 to -25 D or more)
  • Structural changes in eye (thinning of retina and choroid)
  • High risk of vision loss from complications
  • Leading cause of legal blindness in some populations

Onset-Based Classification

Congenital Myopia

  • Present at birth
  • Often high degree
  • May be associated with other eye abnormalities

Youth-Onset Myopia

  • Develops during school years (age 6-18)
  • Most common type
  • Related to eye growth during childhood

Early Adult-Onset Myopia

  • Develops in late teens to early 20s
  • Often related to extended near work (studying, computer use)

Adult-Onset Myopia

  • Develops after age 20-25
  • Less common
  • May signal underlying health issues (diabetes, cataracts)
  • Requires medical evaluation

Pseudomyopia

  • Temporary nearsightedness from eye muscle spasm
  • Caused by prolonged near work
  • Resolves with rest or cycloplegic drops
  • Not true structural myopia

Causes and Risk Factors

Myopia results from a combination of genetic and environmental factors:

Genetic Factors

  • Parental myopia (strongest risk factor):
    • Neither parent myopic: 7-15% risk
    • One parent myopic: 25-40% risk
    • Both parents myopic: 33-60% risk
  • Multiple genes involved: Over 40 genetic loci identified
  • Ethnicity: Higher prevalence in East Asian populations

Environmental Factors (Increasingly Important)

Excessive Near Work

  • Reading, studying, computer use, smartphones
  • Extended close-up visual tasks
  • Sustained accommodation (focusing effort) may stimulate eye growth
  • Particularly impactful during childhood development

Lack of Outdoor Time (Major Factor)

Growing evidence shows outdoor time is protective:

  • Children spending < 1 hour/day outdoors: Much higher myopia rates
  • 2+ hours/day outdoors: Significantly reduced myopia risk
  • Bright light exposure: May trigger dopamine release in retina, slowing eye growth
  • Distance viewing: Relaxes focusing mechanism
  • Effect is preventive (reduces onset), not curative

Education and Socioeconomic Status

  • Higher education levels correlate with higher myopia rates
  • College graduates: ~50% myopia
  • Urban vs. rural: Urban children have higher rates
  • Likely related to indoor lifestyle and near work

Other Contributing Factors

  • Age of onset: Earlier onset often leads to higher final myopia
  • Birth factors: Premature birth, low birth weight increase risk
  • Poor lighting: May contribute (controversial)
  • Sleep: Insufficient sleep may increase risk
  • Diet: Some evidence linking certain nutrients to myopia development

Medical Conditions Associated with Myopia

  • Diabetes (fluctuating blood sugar affects lens)
  • Down syndrome
  • Marfan syndrome
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
  • Retinopathy of prematurity

Recognizing the Symptoms

Blurry Distance Vision

Distant objects appear blurry (street signs, board at school, TV from across room), while close objects are clear.

Squinting

Squinting to see distant objects more clearly (pinhole effect improves focus).

Headaches

Eye strain headaches from trying to focus on distant objects, especially after prolonged distance viewing.

Eye Strain

Tired, fatigued eyes, especially after activities requiring distance vision like driving or watching movies.

Difficulty Driving

Trouble seeing road signs, license plates, or objects at a distance, especially at night.

Excessive Blinking

Frequent blinking or rubbing eyes in attempt to clear vision.

Symptoms in Children

Children may not realize they have vision problems. Watch for:

  • Sitting close to TV or front of classroom
  • Holding books or devices very close
  • Difficulty seeing board at school (may affect grades)
  • Squinting frequently
  • Unaware of distant objects others point out
  • Lack of interest in sports or activities requiring distance vision
  • Frequent headaches
  • Excessive eye rubbing
  • Closing one eye to see better

Progression Signs

If myopia is worsening:

  • Previously clear distances becoming blurry
  • Glasses no longer providing clear vision
  • Frequent need for prescription updates (more than annually)
  • Increased squinting despite wearing glasses

Diagnosis and Eye Examination

Comprehensive Eye Exam

Visual Acuity Test

  • Reading letters on eye chart at distance
  • Measured separately for each eye
  • Results recorded as fraction (20/20, 20/40, etc.)
  • Uncorrected myopia shows reduced distance acuity

Refraction Assessment

Determines the precise lens prescription needed:

  • Autorefractor: Automated device provides starting point
  • Phoropter: Doctor flips through lenses while asking "better 1 or 2?"
  • Retinoscopy: Manual technique using light to determine prescription
  • Determines sphere (myopia/hyperopia), cylinder (astigmatism), and axis

Cycloplegic Refraction (Children)

  • Eye drops temporarily paralyze focusing muscles
  • Reveals true refractive error without accommodation
  • Especially important in children to avoid over-minusing
  • Helps distinguish true myopia from pseudomyopia

Ocular Health Examination

  • Slit lamp: Examines front structures of eye
  • Dilated fundus exam: Checks retina, optic nerve, blood vessels
  • Especially important in high myopia: Screen for retinal problems

Axial Length Measurement

  • A-scan or optical biometry measures eye length
  • Important for tracking myopia progression in children
  • Helps assess future risk

Screening Recommendations

  • Children: First exam at 6 months, then at 3 years, before starting school, then annually
  • School-age children: Annual exams to monitor progression
  • Adults: Every 1-2 years, or as recommended
  • High myopia: Annual dilated exams to screen for complications

Vision Correction Options

Eyeglasses

Most common and simplest correction method:

Advantages:

  • Safe, non-invasive
  • Easy to use
  • Can be removed when not needed
  • Protect eyes from debris and UV
  • Wide variety of styles
  • Less expensive than contacts (usually)

Lens Options:

  • Single vision: For distance only (most common for myopia)
  • High-index lenses: Thinner, lighter for high myopia
  • Aspheric lenses: Flatter, more cosmetically appealing
  • Polycarbonate: Impact-resistant, good for children/sports
  • Anti-reflective coating: Reduces glare, especially important for high myopia
  • UV protection: Important for eye health
  • Blue light blocking: May reduce digital eye strain

Considerations:

  • High myopia lenses can be thick and heavy
  • Image minification (things look smaller)
  • Peripheral distortion in very high prescriptions
  • Can fog up, get wet, or break

Contact Lenses

Provide clear vision without glasses:

Types:

  • Soft lenses: Most popular, comfortable, various replacement schedules (daily, bi-weekly, monthly)
  • Rigid gas permeable (RGP): Sharper vision, more durable, longer lifespan
  • Toric lenses: For myopia with astigmatism
  • Extended wear: Can sleep in (up to 30 days), higher infection risk

Advantages:

  • Natural field of view (no frame obstruction)
  • No image minification
  • Better for sports and active lifestyles
  • Cosmetic benefit (no glasses)
  • Better peripheral vision

Disadvantages/Risks:

  • Requires proper hygiene and care
  • Risk of eye infections if misused
  • Dry eye symptoms
  • Ongoing cost of lens replacement
  • Not suitable for everyone (dry eyes, allergies, certain corneal issues)

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K)

Specially designed rigid contact lenses worn overnight:

  • Temporarily reshapes cornea while sleeping
  • Remove in morning; enjoy clear vision all day without correction
  • Reversible; stop wearing and cornea returns to original shape
  • Can slow myopia progression in children (see Myopia Control section)
  • Effective for low to moderate myopia (up to ~-6.00 D)
  • Requires nightly wear to maintain effect

Refractive Surgery

Permanent surgical correction of myopia (and other refractive errors):

LASIK (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)

Most popular refractive surgery:

Procedure:

  • Thin flap created in cornea with laser or blade
  • Laser reshapes underlying corneal tissue
  • Flap repositioned
  • Outpatient, takes ~15 minutes per eye
  • Anesthetic eye drops; no pain during procedure

Results:

  • Vision improvement within 24 hours
  • ~96% achieve 20/20 to 20/40 vision
  • Minimal discomfort
  • Quick recovery

Good Candidates:

  • Age 18+ with stable prescription (unchanged for 1+ year)
  • Healthy eyes (no cataracts, severe dry eye, corneal disease)
  • Adequate corneal thickness
  • Low to moderate myopia (up to -10 to -12 D typically)

PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy)

  • Laser reshapes cornea after removing epithelium (no flap)
  • Slower visual recovery (weeks vs. days)
  • More discomfort initially
  • Good for thin corneas or those at risk for flap complications
  • Similar final results to LASIK

SMILE (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction)

  • Newer, minimally invasive procedure
  • No flap; small incision only
  • May cause less dry eye than LASIK
  • Effective for low to high myopia

Phakic Intraocular Lenses (IOLs)

For very high myopia unsuitable for laser:

  • Implantable lens placed inside eye (in front of or behind iris)
  • Natural lens remains
  • Reversible procedure
  • Good for myopia beyond laser surgery range (-6 to -20 D)
  • Examples: Visian ICL

Clear Lens Extraction (Refractive Lens Exchange)

  • Natural lens removed and replaced with artificial lens
  • Similar to cataract surgery
  • For very high myopia or presbyopic patients
  • Permanent solution
  • Small risk of retinal detachment

Important Considerations

  • Surgery doesn't prevent myopia-related eye diseases
  • May still need reading glasses after age 40 (presbyopia)
  • Not recommended if myopia still progressing
  • Possible side effects: dry eyes, glare, halos, under/overcorrection
  • Not covered by most insurance

Myopia Control in Children

Strategies to slow myopia progression during childhood:

Why Control Myopia Progression?

  • Reduce final myopia level
  • Lower risk of high myopia and associated complications
  • Each diopter reduction decreases risk of myopic macular degeneration by 40%
  • Better long-term eye health

Proven Myopia Control Methods

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K)

  • Overnight rigid contact lenses reshape cornea
  • Clear vision during day without correction
  • Slows progression by 30-60%
  • Effective and reversible
  • Suitable for children as young as 6-8

Atropine Eye Drops

  • Low-dose atropine (0.01-0.05%) nightly
  • Reduces progression by 30-60%
  • Minimal side effects at low doses
  • Easy to use
  • Requires ongoing use

Multifocal Soft Contact Lenses

  • Special designs with peripheral defocus
  • Examples: MiSight (FDA-approved for myopia control)
  • Slows progression by 25-50%
  • Daily disposable options available
  • Convenient for children

Myopia Control Spectacle Lenses

  • Specialized glasses with peripheral defocus (e.g., Stellest, MiyoSmart)
  • Reduces progression by 30-60%
  • Non-invasive option
  • Good for younger children or those unable to wear contacts

Lifestyle Modifications

Increase Outdoor Time

  • Goal: 2+ hours per day outdoors
  • Bright light exposure is protective
  • Most effective for prevention (delaying onset)
  • Also beneficial once myopia has started

Reduce Near Work

  • Take breaks during prolonged reading/screen time
  • 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds
  • Maintain proper reading distance (not too close)
  • Good lighting

Limit Screen Time

  • Follow AAP guidelines for age-appropriate limits
  • Balance screen time with outdoor play
  • Position screens at arm's length

When to Start Myopia Control

  • As soon as myopia is diagnosed in children
  • Especially important for early-onset myopia (before age 10)
  • Continue through teenage years until progression stops

Complications of High Myopia

High myopia (especially > -6.00 D) increases risk of serious eye problems:

Retinal Detachment

  • Risk: 10x higher in high myopia
  • Longer eye stretches and thins retina
  • Can cause permanent vision loss if not treated urgently
  • Symptoms: Floaters, flashes, curtain over vision

Myopic Macular Degeneration

  • Leading cause of vision loss in high myopia
  • Stretching damages central retina (macula)
  • Causes progressive central vision loss
  • No cure; early detection important

Glaucoma

  • 2-3x higher risk in myopes
  • Optic nerve damage from high eye pressure or other factors
  • Can cause peripheral vision loss
  • Regular screening important

Cataracts

  • Develop earlier in myopes
  • Especially posterior subcapsular cataracts
  • Treatable with surgery

Other Complications

  • Posterior vitreous detachment: Earlier onset
  • Lattice degeneration: Retinal thinning
  • Posterior staphyloma: Outpouching of eye wall
  • Increased floaters

Importance of Regular Monitoring

High myopes should have:

  • Annual dilated eye exams
  • Immediate evaluation if new symptoms (flashes, floaters, vision changes)
  • Regular screening for glaucoma
  • Monitoring of macula

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Emergency Care For:

  • Sudden increase in floaters
  • Flashes of light
  • Shadow or curtain in vision
  • Sudden vision loss

These may indicate retinal detachment—a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

Schedule an Eye Exam If:

  • You or your child has difficulty seeing distant objects
  • Squinting to see clearly
  • Headaches from eye strain
  • Difficulty seeing while driving
  • Child sitting too close to TV or holding books very close
  • Declining school performance possibly related to vision
  • Current glasses no longer providing clear vision
  • Family history of high myopia

Regular Screening Schedule

  • Children: First exam at 6 months, then 3 years, before kindergarten, then annually
  • Myopic children: Annual exams to monitor progression
  • Adults with low/moderate myopia: Every 1-2 years
  • High myopia: Annual dilated exams

Questions to Ask Your Eye Doctor

  • What is my/my child's current prescription?
  • Is the myopia progressing? How fast?
  • What myopia control options are available?
  • Am I at risk for complications?
  • How often should I have eye exams?
  • Am I a candidate for refractive surgery?
  • What symptoms should prompt immediate attention?
