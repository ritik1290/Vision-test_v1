What Is Blepharitis?

Blepharitis is a common, chronic inflammation of the eyelids, particularly affecting the eyelid margins where the eyelashes grow. It causes red, swollen, irritated eyelids with crusting, flaking, and discomfort. While blepharitis is not sight-threatening and isn't contagious, it can be uncomfortable and persistent, often requiring ongoing management.

The condition occurs when oil glands near the base of the eyelashes become clogged or inflamed, leading to irritation and inflammation. Blepharitis often occurs alongside other conditions like dry eye syndrome and rosacea, and while it can't always be cured completely, it can be effectively controlled with proper eyelid hygiene and treatment.

Key Facts About Blepharitis

  • Very common: One of the most frequent reasons people visit eye doctors.
  • Chronic condition: Often lasts for months to years; tends to come and go (flare-ups and remissions).
  • Not contagious: Cannot spread from person to person.
  • Rarely threatens vision: Uncomfortable but usually doesn't cause permanent vision loss.
  • Affects all ages: Can occur in children and adults, though more common in adults.
  • Often bilateral: Typically affects both eyes.
  • Manageable: Proper lid hygiene and treatment control symptoms effectively.
  • Associated with other conditions: Often occurs with dry eyes, rosacea, seborrheic dermatitis, or dandruff.

Understanding blepharitis and committing to a consistent eyelid hygiene routine is essential for managing this frustrating but treatable condition.

Understanding Eyelid Anatomy

Normal Eyelid Structure

The eyelids are complex structures that protect and lubricate the eyes:

Eyelid Margin

  • Front edge of the eyelid where symptoms primarily occur
  • Contains eyelash follicles
  • Houses important glands
  • Junction point between skin and inner eyelid (conjunctiva)

Meibomian Glands

  • Oil glands embedded in eyelid (about 25-40 per lid)
  • Produce oily layer (meibum) of tear film
  • Openings at eyelid margin, just behind eyelashes
  • Critical function: Prevent tear evaporation
  • Dysfunction causes majority of blepharitis cases

Eyelash Follicles

  • Base of each eyelash
  • Associated with small sebaceous glands (glands of Zeis)
  • Can become infected or inflamed

Other Glands

  • Glands of Zeis: Sebaceous glands at lash base
  • Glands of Moll: Modified sweat glands

What Goes Wrong in Blepharitis

  • Meibomian glands become blocked or dysfunctional
  • Oil secretions thicken and don't flow properly
  • Bacteria proliferate on eyelid margins
  • Inflammation develops at eyelid margin
  • Crusting, scaling, and irritation result
  • Tear film becomes unstable (leading to dry eyes)

Types of Blepharitis

Anterior Blepharitis

Affects the outside front edge of the eyelid where eyelashes attach:

Staphylococcal Blepharitis

  • Caused by Staphylococcus bacteria
  • Normal skin bacteria that overgrow
  • Hard crusts and collarettes (cylindrical deposits) around lash base
  • Can cause loss of eyelashes
  • May have ulcerations on eyelid margin

Seborrheic Blepharitis

  • Associated with seborrheic dermatitis (dandruff)
  • Greasy, oily scales on eyelids and eyebrows
  • Often accompanied by scalp dandruff or facial skin flaking
  • Less inflammation than staphylococcal type

Posterior Blepharitis (Most Common)

Affects inner edge of eyelid that contacts the eyeball:

Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)

  • Most common cause of blepharitis
  • Oil glands become clogged
  • Thickened, cloudy oil secretions
  • Gland openings blocked with inspissated (thickened) material
  • Leads to evaporative dry eye
  • Often associated with rosacea

Mixed Blepharitis

  • Combination of anterior and posterior types
  • Very common presentation
  • Requires comprehensive treatment approach

Demodex Blepharitis

  • Caused by Demodex mites (tiny parasites)
  • Demodex folliculorum and Demodex brevis
  • Normal inhabitants of skin, but can overpopulate
  • More common with age
  • Cylindrical dandruff (sleeves) around eyelash base
  • Requires specific treatments (tea tree oil-based products)

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Causes

Bacterial Overgrowth

  • Staphylococcus aureus or Staphylococcus epidermidis most common
  • Normal skin bacteria that proliferate excessively
  • Produce toxins and enzymes causing inflammation

Meibomian Gland Dysfunction

  • Glands produce abnormally thick oil
  • Gland openings become blocked
  • Oil stagnates and changes composition
  • Bacterial lipases break down oils, causing irritation

Demodex Mites

  • Microscopic mites living in eyelash follicles
  • Present in most adults; overpopulation causes problems
  • Feed on skin oils and debris
  • Cause mechanical irritation and allergic reactions

Associated Conditions

Skin Conditions

  • Rosacea: Facial redness; strongly associated with posterior blepharitis/MGD
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: Dandruff, greasy scales on scalp and face
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema)
  • Psoriasis

Other Conditions

  • Dry eye syndrome: Often coexists with and exacerbates blepharitis
  • Allergies
  • Diabetes: May increase susceptibility

Risk Factors

  • Age: More common in adults, especially over 50
  • Dandruff or oily skin
  • Rosacea
  • Contact lens wear: Can worsen symptoms
  • Eye makeup: Especially if not removed properly
  • Environmental factors: Smoke, pollution, wind
  • Poor eyelid hygiene
  • Certain medications affecting oil glands

Recognizing the Symptoms

Red, Swollen Eyelids

Eyelid margins appear red and inflamed, may be swollen or thickened.

Crusting

Crusty debris on eyelashes, especially upon waking. Eyelids may be matted shut in morning.

Burning or Stinging

Burning, stinging, or gritty sensation in the eyes, feels like sand or foreign body.

Itchy Eyelids

Persistent itching of eyelids and eyelashes.

Watery Eyes

Excessive tearing as eyes try to compensate for irritation and unstable tear film.

Dry Eyes

Paradoxically, eyes feel dry (from poor oil layer in tears causing rapid evaporation).

Light Sensitivity

Increased sensitivity to light (photophobia), especially in bright conditions.

Flaking Skin

Flaky scales or dandruff-like debris on eyelids and eyelashes.

Additional Symptoms

  • Misdirected eyelashes: Lashes growing inward (trichiasis)
  • Loss of eyelashes: Lashes fall out (madarosis)
  • Frothy tears: Foam at inner eye corners
  • Blurred vision: From unstable tear film, clears with blinking
  • Foreign body sensation: Feels like something in eye
  • Contact lens intolerance: Difficulty wearing lenses comfortably
  • Frequent styes or chalazia: Recurrent eyelid bumps

Symptom Patterns

  • Worse in morning: Crusting and matting overnight
  • Variable: Symptoms fluctuate; flare-ups and calmer periods
  • Chronic: Persist for weeks, months, or years
  • Bilateral: Usually both eyes, though severity may differ
  • Aggravated by: Smoke, wind, low humidity, prolonged screen time

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Medical History

  • Symptom description and duration
  • Previous treatments tried
  • Associated skin conditions (rosacea, dandruff, eczema)
  • Medications
  • Contact lens wear

Slit Lamp Examination

Magnified inspection of eyelids and ocular surface:

  • Eyelid margin: Redness, swelling, irregularity
  • Meibomian gland openings: Clogging, thickened secretions, gland dropout
  • Eyelash base: Crusting, scales, collarettes (cylindrical dandruff suggesting Demodex)
  • Tear film: Quality, breakup time, debris
  • Conjunctiva and cornea: Check for secondary inflammation or damage

Meibomian Gland Expression

  • Doctor applies gentle pressure to eyelid
  • Observes quality of oil secretion
  • Normal: Clear, thin oil
  • Abnormal: Thick, cloudy, toothpaste-like, or no secretion

Tear Film Assessment

  • Tear breakup time: How quickly tears evaporate
  • Schirmer test: Measures tear production
  • Osmolarity: Tear saltiness (elevated in dry eye)

Additional Testing (Sometimes)

  • Eyelash examination: Microscopy to detect Demodex mites
  • Cultures: If infection suspected or severe
  • Biopsy: Rarely, if concerned about other conditions

Classification

Doctor determines:

  • Type: Anterior, posterior, or mixed
  • Severity: Mild, moderate, or severe
  • Associated conditions: MGD, Demodex, seborrheic, staphylococcal
  • Complications: Dry eye, corneal involvement, recurrent styes

Treatment Options

Blepharitis treatment focuses on controlling symptoms and preventing flare-ups:

Eyelid Hygiene (Cornerstone of Treatment)

Daily lid hygiene is essential for all types of blepharitis:

  • Warm compresses: Soften oil and crusts (5-10 minutes, 1-2x daily)
  • Lid massage: Express thickened oils from glands
  • Lid scrubs: Remove debris, bacteria, and oils from lid margin
  • See detailed routine in next section

Medications

Antibiotic Ointments/Drops

  • For bacterial blepharitis (especially staphylococcal)
  • Examples: Erythromycin, bacitracin ointment applied to lid margin
  • Azithromycin drops (also have anti-inflammatory effect)
  • Usually short-term (2-6 weeks)

Oral Antibiotics

  • For moderate to severe posterior blepharitis/MGD, especially with rosacea
  • Doxycycline: Most common; anti-inflammatory properties, improves oil quality
  • Alternative: Azithromycin, minocycline
  • Long-term, low-dose therapy (weeks to months)
  • Contraindicated in children, pregnancy

Steroid Eye Drops

  • For severe inflammation
  • Short-term use only (side effects with prolonged use)
  • Examples: Loteprednol, fluorometholone
  • Sometimes combined with antibiotics

Anti-Demodex Treatments

  • Tea tree oil products: Cliradex, We Love Eyes, Oust Demodex
  • Applied to lid margin daily for weeks to months
  • Effective against Demodex mites
  • Ivermectin: Oral or topical (prescription)

Omega-3 Fatty Acid Supplements

  • Fish oil or flaxseed oil
  • Improve meibomian gland function and oil quality
  • Anti-inflammatory effects
  • Typical dose: 1000-2000 mg EPA+DHA daily
  • Takes weeks to months for effects

In-Office Procedures

Thermal Pulsation (LipiFlow)

  • Device applies heat and massage to inner eyelid
  • Melts and expresses blocked meibomian glands
  • Single 12-minute treatment
  • Can provide months of relief
  • For moderate to severe MGD

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)

  • Light therapy applied to skin around eyes
  • Reduces inflammation, improves gland function
  • Particularly effective for rosacea-associated blepharitis
  • Series of treatments

Microblepharoexfoliation (BlephEx)

  • Professional cleaning of eyelid margins
  • Removes biofilm, debris, bacteria
  • Performed in-office every 4-6 months

Manual Gland Expression

  • Doctor manually expresses blocked glands
  • Can provide immediate relief
  • May be uncomfortable

Treating Associated Conditions

  • Rosacea: Oral antibiotics, topical metronidazole, lifestyle modifications
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: Medicated shampoos, topical treatments
  • Dry eye: Artificial tears, cyclosporine drops, punctal plugs

Daily Eyelid Hygiene Routine

The foundation of blepharitis management—consistency is key:

Step 1: Warm Compresses (5-10 Minutes)

Purpose:

  • Soften hardened oil in meibomian glands
  • Melt waxy secretions
  • Loosen crusts and debris
  • Increase blood flow to eyelids

How-To:

  • Washcloth method: Soak clean washcloth in warm (not hot) water, wring out excess, apply to closed eyelids for 5-10 minutes. Rewarm as needed.
  • Microwaveable masks: Reusable gel masks (e.g., Bruder mask); microwave per instructions, apply to eyelids.
  • Optimal temperature: Comfortably warm (104-110°F/40-43°C)
  • Perform 1-2 times daily, especially before bed

Step 2: Eyelid Massage (1-2 Minutes)

Purpose:

  • Express softened oils from glands
  • Prevent gland blockage

How-To:

  • With clean hands, close your eyes
  • Gently massage eyelids in circular motions with fingertip
  • Or massage downward on upper lid, upward on lower lid toward lash line
  • Use gentle pressure—don't press hard on eyeball

Step 3: Eyelid Scrubs (1-2 Minutes)

Purpose:

  • Remove crusts, scales, debris
  • Reduce bacterial load
  • Keep eyelid margin clean

How-To:

  • Commercial lid scrub products: Pre-moistened pads (OCuSOFT, Systane, TheraTears Sterilid). Gently scrub along lash line and lid margin.
  • Diluted baby shampoo method: Mix few drops of baby shampoo with warm water. Dip clean cotton swab or washcloth, scrub gently along lash line. Rinse with warm water.
  • Focus on lash base: Where debris accumulates
  • Be gentle—don't scrub too vigorously
  • Treat both upper and lower lids

Daily Routine Summary

  1. Warm compress: 5-10 minutes
  2. Gentle massage: 1-2 minutes
  3. Lid scrubs: 1-2 minutes
  4. Rinse thoroughly
  5. Apply any prescribed medications

Frequency: Once daily minimum (preferably at night); twice daily during flare-ups

Additional Tips

  • Remove eye makeup completely every night
  • Replace eye makeup regularly (every 3-6 months)
  • Avoid eyeliner on inner eyelid margin
  • Keep hair and face clean (especially if dandruff present)
  • Don't share towels, washcloths, or eye makeup

Potential Complications

Stye (Hordeolum)

  • Painful, red bump on eyelid margin
  • Infected oil gland or eyelash follicle
  • More common with blepharitis
  • Usually resolves with warm compresses; sometimes needs antibiotics or drainage

Chalazion

  • Painless, firm bump on eyelid
  • Blocked meibomian gland
  • Chronic inflammation, not infection
  • May require steroid injection or surgical removal if persistent

Chronic Dry Eye

  • MGD-related blepharitis → poor oil layer → rapid tear evaporation
  • Can be severe and disabling
  • Requires ongoing management

Corneal Problems

  • Keratitis: Corneal inflammation from chronic irritation
  • Corneal ulcers: Rare but serious; from bacterial infection
  • Corneal neovascularization: Abnormal blood vessel growth

Eyelash Problems

  • Trichiasis: Misdirected lashes growing inward, scratching eye
  • Madarosis: Loss of eyelashes
  • Poliosis: Whitening of eyelashes

Conjunctivitis

  • Chronic or recurrent conjunctival inflammation
  • Secondary to lid margin infection/inflammation

Most complications are preventable with consistent lid hygiene and appropriate treatment.

Living with Blepharitis

Long-Term Management

  • Commitment required: Daily lid hygiene is ongoing, not just during flare-ups
  • Maintenance therapy: Even when symptoms improve, continue routine
  • Expect relapses: Chronic condition; flare-ups normal
  • Adjust routine as needed: Increase frequency during flare-ups

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Avoid smoke and irritants
  • Humidify indoor air, especially in winter
  • Take breaks during screen time (20-20-20 rule)
  • Stay hydrated
  • Protect eyes: Sunglasses outdoors to reduce irritation from wind/dust

Contact Lens Wear

  • Blepharitis makes lens wear more difficult
  • Consider daily disposables (less buildup)
  • Meticulous lens hygiene essential
  • May need to discontinue during flare-ups
  • Some find lenses intolerable with blepharitis

Eye Makeup

  • Remove completely every night
  • Avoid waterproof formulas (harder to remove)
  • Don't apply liner to inner lid margin (blocks glands)
  • Replace makeup every 3-6 months
  • Consider hypoallergenic products
  • Some may need to avoid eye makeup entirely during flare-ups

When to Intensify Treatment

  • Symptoms worsening
  • Flare-up developing
  • Increased stress (can trigger flare-ups)
  • Seasonal changes (winter often worse)

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Care For:

  • Sudden vision loss
  • Severe eye pain
  • Eye discharge with severe redness (possible serious infection)
  • Symptoms not improving with proper lid hygiene after 2 weeks

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • Red, irritated, itchy eyelids
  • Crusting on eyelashes, especially in morning
  • Burning or gritty sensation in eyes
  • Recurrent styes or chalazia
  • Eyes feel chronically dry
  • Eyelids are swollen
  • Difficulty wearing contact lenses
  • Home treatments not providing relief
  • Symptoms interfering with daily life

Questions to Ask Your Eye Doctor

  • What type of blepharitis do I have?
  • What's the best lid hygiene routine for me?
  • Do I need prescription medications?
  • Should I be tested for Demodex mites?
  • Could in-office procedures like LipiFlow or IPL help?
  • Do I have meibomian gland dysfunction or dry eye?
  • How often should I have follow-up visits?
  • Are there other conditions contributing to my symptoms?
