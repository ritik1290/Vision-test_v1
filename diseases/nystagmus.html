What Is Nystagmus?

Nystagmus is a condition characterized by involuntary, repetitive, rhythmic eye movements that the person cannot control. The eyes may move side-to-side (horizontal nystagmus, most common), up and down (vertical), in a circular pattern (rotary), or in combinations. These movements can be constant or triggered by certain head positions or gaze directions. Nystagmus can significantly affect vision, depth perception, balance, and coordination.

Nystagmus is divided into two main categories: congenital (infantile-onset), present from birth or early infancy, and acquired, developing later in life due to disease, injury, or medication. Congenital nystagmus is often associated with other vision problems but is generally stable, while acquired nystagmus in adults is more concerning and requires investigation for underlying neurological or vestibular (inner ear balance system) causes.

Key Facts About Nystagmus

  • Involuntary eye movements: Eyes move rhythmically without control.
  • Congenital or acquired: Present from birth/infancy vs. develops later in life.
  • Common patterns: Horizontal (side-to-side), vertical, rotary, or mixed.
  • Affects vision: Reduced visual acuity, oscillopsia (world appears to shake), depth perception issues.
  • Null point: Many have a gaze direction where nystagmus is minimal ("null point"); may turn head to use it.
  • Associated conditions: Albinism, congenital cataracts, optic nerve hypoplasia (congenital); stroke, MS, brain tumors (acquired).
  • Variable severity: Mild to severe; impacts on daily life vary.
  • Treatment limited: No cure, but optical aids, medications, and surgery can help in select cases.

Understanding nystagmus helps patients and families manage expectations and seek appropriate interventions.

Types of Nystagmus

By Onset

Congenital (Infantile) Nystagmus

  • Present from birth or develops in first few months of life
  • Prevalence: ~1 in 1,000-5,000 people
  • Causes: Often associated with conditions affecting visual development (albinism, congenital cataracts, optic nerve hypoplasia, aniridia)
  • Vision: Reduced but stable; brain adapts somewhat
  • Oscillopsia: Less common (brain suppresses perception of movement)
  • Null point common: Head turn to dampen nystagmus
  • Prognosis: Generally non-progressive

Acquired Nystagmus

  • Develops in childhood, adolescence, or adulthood
  • Causes: Neurological (stroke, MS, brain tumor), vestibular (inner ear disease), medications/toxins, trauma
  • Oscillopsia common: World appears to shake or jump; very disturbing
  • Requires investigation: Underlying cause must be identified and treated if possible
  • Prognosis: Depends on cause; some improve with treatment, others permanent

By Waveform

Pendular Nystagmus

  • Eyes oscillate smoothly back and forth with equal velocity in each direction
  • Like a pendulum
  • Common in: Congenital nystagmus, MS, brainstem lesions

Jerk Nystagmus

  • Eyes drift slowly in one direction (slow phase), then jerk back quickly (fast phase)
  • Named by fast phase direction: E.g., "right-beating nystagmus" has fast phase to the right
  • Common in: Vestibular disorders, many acquired forms

By Direction

  • Horizontal: Side-to-side; most common
  • Vertical: Up and down; often neurological cause
  • Rotary (torsional): Circular; vestibular or brainstem
  • Mixed: Combination

Special Types

Vestibular Nystagmus

  • From inner ear (vestibular system) problems
  • Often accompanied by vertigo, nausea
  • Examples: benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), vestibular neuritis, Meniere's disease

Gaze-Evoked Nystagmus

  • Appears only when looking in certain directions (e.g., to the side)
  • Minimal in primary gaze (looking straight ahead)
  • Common with medications (sedatives, anticonvulsants) or brainstem/cerebellar disease

Downbeat Nystagmus

  • Fast phase downward
  • Often worse in lateral/downward gaze
  • Causes: Cerebellar or cervicomedullary junction lesions (Chiari malformation, MS, stroke)

Upbeat Nystagmus

  • Fast phase upward
  • Causes: Brainstem or cerebellar lesions, medication toxicity

Spasmus Nutans

  • Rare; infants 6-18 months
  • Triad: nystagmus, head nodding, abnormal head position
  • Usually resolves spontaneously by age 2-3 years

Acquired Pendular Nystagmus

  • Develops in adults
  • Often associated with MS or brainstem stroke
  • Can be very disabling (oscillopsia)

Causes of Nystagmus

Congenital/Early Onset Causes

Sensory Deprivation (Impaired Vision Development)

  • Albinism: Lack of pigment affects retinal and optic nerve development; common cause
  • Congenital cataracts: Cloudy lens from birth
  • Optic nerve hypoplasia: Underdeveloped optic nerve
  • Aniridia: Absence of iris
  • Achromatopsia: Complete color blindness
  • Leber congenital amaurosis: Severe inherited retinal dystrophy
  • Severe refractive error
  • Early visual input critical for developing stable gaze; disruption causes nystagmus

Idiopathic Infantile Nystagmus (IIN)

  • No identifiable underlying eye or brain abnormality
  • Eyes structurally normal
  • Exact cause unknown; thought to be neurological development issue
  • Genetic in some families (X-linked or autosomal dominant)

Acquired Causes

Neurological Disorders

  • Stroke: Especially brainstem or cerebellum
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS): Demyelination affects eye movement control centers
  • Brain tumors: Especially posterior fossa (cerebellum, brainstem)
  • Chiari malformation: Brain tissue extends into spinal canal
  • Cerebellar degeneration: Atrophy of cerebellum
  • Head trauma
  • Wernicke encephalopathy: Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, often in alcoholism

Vestibular (Inner Ear) Disorders

  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV): Crystals in semicircular canals
  • Vestibular neuritis/labyrinthitis: Inflammation of vestibular nerve or inner ear
  • Meniere's disease: Inner ear fluid imbalance
  • Acoustic neuroma: Tumor on vestibular nerve

Medications and Toxins

  • Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine)
  • Sedatives (benzodiazepines, barbiturates)
  • Lithium
  • Alcohol intoxication
  • Aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin, toxic to inner ear)

Other Causes

  • Vitamin deficiencies: Thiamine (B1), B12
  • Metabolic disorders: Thyroid disease
  • Infections: Meningitis, encephalitis

Symptoms and Impact on Daily Life

Visible Eye Movements

Eyes move involuntarily side-to-side, up-down, or in circles; readily noticeable to observers.

Reduced Vision

Visual acuity often 20/40 to 20/200; difficult to focus clearly due to moving eyes.

Oscillopsia

World appears to shake, bounce, or oscillate; very disturbing; more common in acquired nyst agmus.

Head Tilt or Turn

Abnormal head posture to position eyes at "null point" where nystagmus is minimal.

Depth Perception Issues

Difficulty judging distances; impacts driving, sports, pouring liquids.

Balance Problems

Especially with vestibular nystagmus; difficulty walking, dizziness.

Functional Impacts

  • Reading difficulty: Text appears to move; slow reading speed
  • Driving: Many cannot obtain driver's license due to poor vision
  • Sports and recreation: Challenges with activities requiring good depth perception
  • Social/cosmetic: Self -consciousness about eye movements; may avoid eye contact
  • Fatigue: Extra effort to see clearly causes eye strain, headaches

Null Point and Head Posture

  • Many people with nystagmus find a gaze direction where nystagmus is minimal or absent ("null point")
  • Adopt head turn/tilt to keep eyes in null position
  • Improves vision but causes neck strain, abnormal appearance

Congenital vs. Acquired Symptom Differences

  • Congenital:
    • Usually no oscillopsia (brain adapts)
    • Stable vision (doesn't get worse)
    • Null point common
    • Person often unaware eyes are moving
  • Acquired:
    • Oscillopsia common (world shakes)
    • Very symptomatic and disturbing
    • May have associated vertigo, imbalance
    • Person acutely aware of problem

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Age of onset (birth vs. later)?
  • Progression (getting worse, stable)?
  • Associated symptoms (oscillopsia, vertigo, neurological symptoms)?
  • Family history of nystagmus or eye conditions?
  • Medications?
  • Alcohol/drug use?

Ophthalmic Examination

Observation of Eye Movements

  • Direction (horizontal, vertical, rotary)
  • Type (pendular vs. jerk)
  • Frequency and amplitude
  • Presence of null point
  • Gaze-evoked vs. constant

Visual Acuity

  • Often reduced (20/40-20/200 in congenital)
  • May improve at null point

Comprehensive Eye Exam

  • Check for underlying eye conditions:
    • Cataracts
    • Optic nerve abnormalities
    • Retinal dystrophies
    • Albinism (transillumination of iris, foveal hypoplasia)
  • Refraction to correct any refractive error

Neurological Examination

  • Cranial nerves
  • Coordination, gait, balance (cerebellar function)
  • Other neurological signs

Vestibular Testing (If Indicated)

  • Dix-Hallpike maneuver (BPPV testing)
  • Electronystagmography (ENG) or videonystagmography (VNG)
  • Caloric testing

Imaging

Brain MRI

  • Essential for acquired nystagmus
  • Look for:
    • Stroke
    • MS lesions
    • Tumors
    • Chiari malformation
    • Cerebellar atrophy
  • May be done in congenital cases if concern for underlying brain abnormality

Genetic Testing

  • If albinism, specific retinal dystrophy, or familial nystagmus suspected

Electrophysiology (If Needed)

  • Electroretinogram (ERG): Assesses retinal function
  • Visual evoked potentials (VEP): Assesses visual pathway
  • Useful workup with albinism, retinal dystrophies

Treatment

Treat Underlying Cause (If Possible)

  • Medications/toxins: Discontinue or adjust offending agent
  • Vitamin deficiency: Supplement thiamine, B12
  • Vestibular disorders: Specific treatments (canalith repositioning for BPPV, vestibular suppressants for vestibular neuritis)
  • Brain tumor: Surgical removal or radiation
  • MS: Disease-modifying therapy
  • Wernicke encephalopathy: IV thiamine urgently

Optical Correction

  • Glasses or contact lenses: Correct any refractive error; maximizes visual potential
  • Prism glasses: Can shift image to allow use of null point without turning head
  • Contact lenses: Some find contacts dampen nystagmus slightly (debated)

Medications

Limited effectiveness; tried in select cases:

For Acquired Pendular Nystagmus (e.g., MS-Related)

  • Gabapentin or memantine: May reduce nystagmus amplitude, improve oscillopsia
  • Variable success

For Downbeat Nystagmus

  • 4-Aminopyridine (dalfampridine): Potassium channel blocker; may reduce nystagmus
  • Not FDA-approved for this use; off-label

For Vestibular Nystagmus

  • Meclizine, diazepam, or other vestibular suppressants (short-term)

Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injections

  • Injected into eye muscles
  • Weakens muscles to dampen nystagmus
  • Temporary (lasts 3-4 months)
  • Rarely used; limited success; risk of diplopia, ptosis

Surgery

Null Point Surgery (Eye Muscle Surgery)

  • Goal: Move null point to primary gaze position so patient doesn't need head turn
  • Kestenbaum procedure: Weakens/strengthens eye muscles to shift null
  • Candidates: Congenital nystagmus with significant head turn
  • Outcomes:
    • Reduces or eliminates abnormal head posture
    • May improve vision slightly
    • Doesn't eliminate nystagmus, just repositions null
  • Success variable

Tenotomy (Anderson Procedure)

  • Detaches and reattaches all four horizontal muscles
  • Thought to disrupt abnormal proprioceptive feedback
  • May dampen nystagmus amplitude, improve vision
  • Controversial; not universally accepted

Low Vision Aids and Rehabilitation

  • Magnifiers for reading
  • Large-print materials
  • Electronic aids (screen readers, video magnifiers)
  • Occupational therapy for daily living adaptations

Psychosocial Support

  • Counseling for coping with visual impairment
  • Support groups
  • Educational accommodations for children

Living with Nystagmus

Maximizing Vision

  • Wear prescribed glasses/contacts to optimize clarity
  • Use null point (head turn) if helpful
  • Adequate lighting for reading and tasks
  • Minimize glare
  • Take frequent breaks to reduce eye strain

Educational and Occupational Adjustments

  • School: Preferential seating, large-print materials, extra time for exams, assistive technology
  • Work: Screen magnification software, task lighting, ergonomic workspace

Driving

  • Depends on visual acuity and motor vehicle department standards
  • Many with congenital nystagmus can drive if vision adequate
  • Some states allow bioptic telescopes

Sports and Activities

  • Many sports possible; adaptations needed for depth perception challenges
  • Safety considerations (e.g., protective eyewear)

Social and Cosmetic Concerns

  • Visible eye movements can be socially awkward
  • Education and openness help
  • Sunglasses can reduce noticeability

Prognosis

Congenital Nystagmus

  • Stable: Generally non-progressive
  • Vision: Reduced but functional in most; may improve slightly with age as nystagmus dampens
  • Adaptations: Brain and patient adapt well
  • Quality of life: Most lead normal lives with accommodations

Acquired Nystagmus

  • Variable: Depends on underlying cause
  • Reversible causes: May improve with treatment (medication withdrawal, vitamin replacement, vestibular therapy)
  • Structural brain lesions: Often permanent; may partially compensate over time
  • Oscillopsia: Can be very disabling; some improvement with central adaptation

Surgical Outcomes

  • Null point surgery often successfully eliminates abnormal head posture
  • Vision improvement modest
  • Patient satisfaction generally high for cosmetic and neck strain relief

When to See a Doctor

Seek Prompt Evaluation If:

  • New-onset nystagmus in older child or adult (requires neurological workup)
  • Sudden onset with vertigo, imbalance
  • Associated neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, speech changes)
  • Nystagmus with severe headache or vomiting

Acquired nystagmus can signal serious brain or inner ear disease and requires urgent evaluation.

For Congenital Nystagmus

  • Regular eye exams to monitor vision and treat refractive errors
  • Consider surgical consultation if significant head turn
  • Low vision services if vision significantly impaired

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What is causing my/my child's nystagmus?
  • Do I need brain imaging (MRI)?
  • Is it likely to get worse?
  • What treatments are available?
  • Would surgery help?
  • What vision can I expect?
  • Are there support groups or resources?
  • Can I/my child drive?

Bottom Line

Nystagmus is involuntary rhythmic eye movement that can be present from birth (congenital) or develop later (acquired). Congenital nystagmus, often associated with conditions like albinism or optic nerve hypoplasia, is generally stable and patients adapt well, though vision is reduced. Acquired nystagmus in adults is more concerning and requires thorough neurological and vestibular workup for causes like stroke, MS, or inner ear disease. Treatment focuses on correcting underlying causes when possible, optimizing vision with glasses, and in select cases, surgical repositioning of the null point to eliminate abnormal head posture. While there's no cure, most people with nystagmus lead productive lives with appropriate support and accommodations.
