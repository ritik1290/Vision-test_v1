What Is Dry Eye Syndrome?

Dry eye syndrome, also called dry eye disease or keratoconjunctivitis sicca, is a common condition that occurs when your tears aren't able to provide adequate lubrication for your eyes. This can happen either because you don't produce enough tears or because the tears you produce are of poor quality. Dry eyes feel uncomfortable, causing stinging, burning sensations and can affect your vision and quality of life.

Dry eye is one of the most common reasons people visit eye doctors, affecting millions of people worldwide. While it can affect anyone, it becomes more common with age and is particularly prevalent in women, especially after menopause.

Key Facts About Dry Eye Syndrome

  • Very common: Affects 5-30% of people over age 50, with higher rates in women and older adults.
  • Chronic condition: Dry eye is usually a long-term condition that requires ongoing management.
  • Impact on quality of life: Can significantly affect daily activities like reading, computer use, and driving.
  • Two main types: Aqueous deficient (not enough tears) and evaporative (tears evaporate too quickly).
  • Treatable: While usually not curable, symptoms can be well-managed with appropriate treatment.
  • Can damage eyes: Severe untreated dry eye can lead to corneal damage, infections, and vision problems.
  • Often associated with other conditions: Autoimmune diseases, hormonal changes, medications, and environmental factors play a role.

Understanding dry eye and its causes is the first step toward finding relief. Most people with dry eye can find significant improvement with proper treatment and lifestyle modifications.

Understanding the Tear Film

To understand dry eye, it's helpful to know how tears work to keep your eyes healthy and comfortable:

The Three Layers of the Tear Film

Your tear film isn't just water—it's a complex, three-layered structure:

1. Outer Lipid (Oil) Layer

  • Produced by meibomian glands in the eyelids
  • Prevents rapid evaporation of tears
  • Smooths the tear surface for clear vision
  • Problems with this layer cause evaporative dry eye (most common type)

2. Middle Aqueous (Water) Layer

  • Produced by lacrimal glands above each eye
  • Makes up about 90% of tear volume
  • Provides oxygen and nutrients to the cornea
  • Washes away foreign particles and irritants
  • Insufficient production causes aqueous deficient dry eye

3. Inner Mucin Layer

  • Produced by goblet cells in the conjunctiva
  • Helps tears spread evenly across the eye surface
  • Anchors the tear film to the eye
  • Keeps the eye surface smooth

Functions of Tears

Healthy tears serve many critical functions:

  • Lubricate and moisturize the eye surface
  • Provide smooth optical surface for clear vision
  • Wash away debris, allergens, and foreign particles
  • Deliver oxygen and nutrients to the cornea
  • Protect against infection with antibacterial enzymes
  • Promote healing of minor injuries

What Goes Wrong in Dry Eye

Dry eye occurs when this delicate tear system is disrupted through:

  • Insufficient tear production
  • Poor quality tears (lacking oil, water, or mucin)
  • Too-rapid evaporation
  • Inflammation of the eye surface or glands

Types of Dry Eye

Dry eye is classified into two main types based on the underlying problem:

1. Evaporative Dry Eye (Most Common)

Accounts for about 86% of dry eye cases. Tears evaporate too quickly, usually due to problems with the oil layer.

Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)

The most common cause of evaporative dry eye:

  • Meibomian glands in eyelids become blocked or don't produce enough oil
  • Without adequate oil, tears evaporate 4-16 times faster than normal
  • Often associated with rosacea, blepharitis, or aging
  • Can be treated with warm compresses, lid hygiene, and in-office procedures

Other Causes of Evaporative Dry Eye:

  • Low blink rate (computer use, reading, driving)
  • Poor blinking technique (incomplete blinks)
  • Eyelid problems (ectropion, entropion, lagophthalmos)
  • Vitamin A deficiency
  • Preservatives in eye drops
  • Contact lens wear

2. Aqueous Deficient Dry Eye

The lacrimal glands don't produce enough of the watery component of tears.

Sjögren's Syndrome

An autoimmune disease that attacks moisture-producing glands:

  • Can occur alone (primary) or with other autoimmune diseases (secondary)
  • Causes severe dry eyes and dry mouth
  • Affects mostly women (9:1 ratio)
  • Requires treatment from rheumatologist and ophthalmologist

Non-Sjögren's Aqueous Deficiency:

  • Age-related decrease in tear production
  • Lacrimal gland inflammation
  • Congenital lack of lacrimal glands (rare)
  • Damage to lacrimal glands from radiation, surgery, or injury

Mixed Type Dry Eye

Many people have a combination of evaporative and aqueous deficient dry eye, requiring treatment for both aspects.

Causes and Risk Factors

Dry eye has many potential causes and risk factors:

Age-Related Changes

  • Tear production decreases with age
  • Meibomian glands become less functional
  • Risk increases significantly after age 50
  • Changes accelerate in women after menopause

Gender and Hormones

  • Women at higher risk: 2-3 times more common than in men
  • Hormonal changes: Pregnancy, oral contraceptives, menopause
  • Hormone replacement therapy: May increase dry eye risk
  • Androgen deficiency: Affects meibomian gland function

Medical Conditions

  • Autoimmune diseases: Sjögren's syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, scleroderma
  • Diabetes: Can affect tear production and quality
  • Thyroid disorders: Both hyper- and hypothyroidism
  • Rosacea: Often associated with MGD
  • Vitamin A deficiency: Rare in developed countries
  • Parkinson's disease: Reduced blink rate

Medications

Many common medications can cause or worsen dry eye:

  • Antihistamines (allergy medications)
  • Decongestants
  • Antidepressants (especially tricyclics, SSRIs)
  • Blood pressure medications (beta-blockers, diuretics)
  • Anxiety medications (benzodiazepines)
  • Pain medications (opioids)
  • Hormones (birth control, HRT)
  • Acne medications (isotretinoin/Accutane)
  • Glaucoma drops (especially those with preservatives)

Environmental Factors

  • Dry, windy, or smoky environments
  • Air conditioning and heating
  • High altitudes
  • Airplane cabins
  • Smoke exposure (active or passive)
  • Air pollution

Lifestyle and Activities

  • Prolonged screen time: Reduces blink rate by 50-60%
  • Contact lens wear: Disrupts tear film
  • Reading: Decreased blinking
  • Driving: Especially at night with air vents
  • Incomplete blinking: Common with concentration tasks

Eye Conditions and Procedures

  • Blepharitis: Eyelid inflammation
  • LASIK and other refractive surgeries: Temporary or sometimes permanent dry eye
  • Eyelid problems: Incomplete closure (lagophthalmos), ectropion, entropion
  • Corneal nerve damage: From surgery, diabetes, or injury

Recognizing the Symptoms

Dry eye symptoms can range from mild to severe and may fluctuate throughout the day:

Stinging or Burning

Burning, stinging, or scratchy sensation in the eyes, often worsening as the day progresses.

Redness

Eyes appear red or bloodshot, especially toward the end of the day or after screen use.

Excessive Tearing

Paradoxically, dry eyes often produce excessive reflex tears in response to irritation (watery eyes).

Stringy Mucus

Sticky, stringy mucus in or around the eyes, especially upon waking.

Light Sensitivity

Increased sensitivity to bright lights, wind, or air conditioning.

Blurred Vision

Fluctuating or blurred vision that improves temporarily with blinking.

Eye Fatigue

Eyes feel tired, heavy, or difficult to keep open, especially with reading or screen time.

Foreign Body Sensation

Feeling like something is in your eye (gritty, sandy sensation).

Contact Lens Discomfort

Difficulty wearing contact lenses; lenses feel uncomfortable or dry.

When Symptoms Are Worse

Dry eye symptoms often worsen:

  • In the evening after prolonged use of eyes
  • In air-conditioned or heated rooms
  • In windy or dry weather
  • During extended screen time
  • When reading or driving
  • In smoky environments
  • At high altitudes or on airplanes

Why Do Dry Eyes Water?

It seems contradictory, but many people with dry eyes experience excessive tearing. This happens because the dry, irritated eye surface triggers a reflex response, producing a flood of watery tears. However, these reflex tears don't have the right composition to properly lubricate the eye, so the cycle continues.

Diagnosis and Testing

Diagnosing dry eye involves a comprehensive eye examination and specialized tests:

Medical History

Your doctor will ask about:

  • Symptoms and when they occur
  • Medical conditions and medications
  • Work environment and activities
  • Contact lens use
  • Previous eye surgeries

Eye Examination

  • Slit lamp exam: Examines eyelids, cornea, and tear film under magnification
  • Eyelid evaluation: Checks for blepharitis, MGD, and proper eyelid closure
  • Corneal staining: Dye reveals dry spots and damage to the cornea

Specialized Dry Eye Tests

Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT)

  • Measures how long tear film remains stable
  • Normal: >10 seconds
  • Dry eye: < 10 seconds
  • Indicates evaporative dry eye

Schirmer Test

  • Measures tear production
  • Small paper strip placed under lower eyelid for 5 minutes
  • Normal: > 10-15mm wetting
  • Dry eye: < 5-10mm
  • Indicates aqueous deficiency

Tear Osmolarity Test

  • Measures salt concentration in tears
  • High osmolarity indicates dry eye
  • Objective measurement of disease severity

Meibography

  • Imaging of meibomian glands
  • Shows gland dropout and structure
  • Helps diagnose MGD

InflammaDry Test

  • Detects elevated MMP-9 (inflammatory marker)
  • Indicates ocular surface inflammation
  • Helps guide treatment decisions

Severity Classification

Based on symptoms and test results, dry eye is classified as:

  • Mild: Occasional symptoms, minimal signs
  • Moderate: Frequent symptoms, moderate corneal staining
  • Severe: Constant symptoms, corneal damage, vision impact

Treatment Options

Treatment for dry eye is tailored to the type and severity. Most people need a combination of approaches:

Artificial Tears and Lubricants

First-line treatment for mild to moderate dry eye:

Over-the-Counter Eye Drops

  • Preservative-free drops: Best for frequent use (more than 4x/day)
  • Preserved drops: Convenient but may irritate with frequent use
  • Thicker gels and ointments: Last longer, good for nighttime (blur vision temporarily)
  • Lipid-based drops: Help with evaporative dry eye/MGD

Usage tips:

  • Use as often as needed (some people need hourly drops)
  • Apply before activities that worsen symptoms
  • Try different brands to find what works best
  • Use ointments at bedtime

Prescription Medications

Anti-Inflammatory Drops

Cyclosporine (Restasis, Cequa):

  • Reduces inflammation, increases tear production
  • Takes 3-6 months for full effect
  • Used twice daily long-term
  • May cause temporary burning

Lifitegrast (Xiidra):

  • Blocks inflammatory pathways
  • Works faster than cyclosporine (2-4 weeks)
  • Used twice daily
  • May cause taste disturbance

Corticosteroid Drops

  • Short-term use for flare-ups
  • Quickly reduce inflammation
  • Can't be used long-term (risk of increased eye pressure, cataracts)
  • Often combined with cyclosporine or lifitegrast initially

Autologous Serum Eye Drops

  • Made from patient's own blood
  • Contains growth factors and nutrients
  • For severe dry eye unresponsive to other treatments
  • Requires special preparation and refrigeration

Procedures and Devices

Punctal Plugs

  • Tiny plugs inserted into tear drainage ducts
  • Keep tears on eye surface longer
  • Temporary (collagen, dissolve in weeks) or semi-permanent (silicone)
  • Quick, painless office procedure
  • Very effective for aqueous deficiency

In-Office Procedures for MGD

LipiFlow:

  • Thermal pulsation clears blocked meibomian glands
  • Improves oil layer quality
  • Single 12-minute treatment
  • Effects last months to a year

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) Therapy:

  • Light therapy reduces eyelid inflammation
  • Improves meibomian gland function
  • Series of 4-6 treatments
  • Also helps rosacea-related dry eye

Meibomian Gland Expression:

  • Manual expression of blocked glands
  • Performed in office with special instruments
  • Immediate relief for many patients

Amniotic Membrane

  • For severe dry eye with corneal damage
  • Promotes healing, reduces inflammation
  • Placed on eye surface for several days

Scleral Contact Lenses

  • Large lenses create fluid reservoir over cornea
  • Protect and lubricate eye surface
  • Helpful for severe dry eye

Treatment for Underlying Conditions

  • Blepharitis: Lid hygiene, antibiotics if needed
  • Rosacea: Oral antibiotics (doxycycline), skin care
  • Sjögren's syndrome: Systemic immunosuppressants
  • Eyelid problems: Surgery to correct malposition

Home Care and Prevention

Self-care measures are essential for managing dry eye:

Eyelid Hygiene

  • Warm compresses: 5-10 minutes twice daily melts oil in meibomian glands
  • Lid massage: Gentle circular motions after compresses
  • Lid scrubs: Clean eyelid margins with commercial wipes or diluted baby shampoo
  • Use consistently for best results

Environmental Modifications

  • Humidifier: Adds moisture to dry air, especially in winter
  • Avoid direct air: Position vents away from your face
  • Protective eyewear: Wraparound glasses or moisture chamber glasses outdoors
  • Reduce screen time: Or take frequent breaks
  • Lower monitor: Below eye level reduces exposed eye surface

Blink Exercises

  • Consciously blink fully and regularly
  • Use 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds and blink
  • Practice gentle, complete blinks

Dietary Measures

  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) or supplements (1000-2000mg EPA+DHA daily)
  • Stay hydrated: Drink adequate water
  • Reduce omega-6 intake: Limit processed foods

Lifestyle Adjustments

  • Quit smoking: Critical for improvement
  • Limit alcohol and caffeine: Can be dehydrating
  • Take medication breaks: Discuss with doctor if medications are contributing
  • Proper sleep: Ensures adequate overnight tear production

Contact Lens Tips

  • Use daily disposable lenses
  • Scleral lenses may help
  • Limit wearing time
  • Use rewetting drops designed for contacts
  • Consider "glasses days" to give eyes a break

Living with Dry Eye

Dry eye is usually a chronic condition requiring ongoing management. Here's how to cope:

Consistency Is Key

  • Use treatments consistently, even when feeling better
  • Dry eye often requires lifelong management
  • Stopping treatment usually leads to symptom return

Find Your Routine

  • Morning: Warm compress, artificial tears
  • Throughout day: Frequent artificial tears, especially before/during screen time
  • Evening: Lid hygiene routine, prescription drops if used
  • Bedtime: Lubricating ointment

Track Triggers

  • Keep a symptom diary
  • Note when symptoms worsen
  • Identify and avoid triggers when possible

Optimize Your Workspace

  • Position monitor below eye level
  • Use anti-glare screens
  • Adjust lighting to reduce glare
  • Keep artificial tears at your desk
  • Set reminders to blink and take breaks

Be Patient with Treatment

  • Many treatments take weeks to months to work fully
  • Finding the right combination may require trial and error
  • Don't give up—most people find relief with proper management

When to See a Doctor

Seek Medical Attention If You Experience:

  • Severe eye pain
  • Sudden vision loss or significant vision changes
  • Eye discharge (pus or thick mucus)
  • Symptoms of infection (severe redness, swelling, fever)
  • Eye injury or foreign body
  • Symptoms lasting more than a few days despite home treatment

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • Dry eye symptoms interfere with daily activities
  • Over-the-counter treatments aren't providing relief
  • You're using artificial tears more than 4-6 times daily
  • You have persistent redness or irritation
  • You can't wear contact lenses comfortably
  • You have an autoimmune disease

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of dry eye do I have?
  • What's causing my dry eye?
  • What treatments do you recommend?
  • Are any of my medications contributing?
  • Should I use preservative-free drops?
  • Would punctal plugs help me?
  • Do I have meibomian gland dysfunction?
  • Should I be tested for Sjögren's syndrome?
  • How often should I follow up?
