What Is Uveitis?

Uveitis is inflammation of the uvea, the middle layer of the eye that consists of the iris, ciliary body, and choroid. This condition can affect one or all three parts of the uvea and can involve other parts of the eye as well. Uveitis is a serious eye condition that requires prompt medical attention because it can lead to permanent vision loss if left untreated.

While less common than conditions like cataracts or glaucoma, uveitis is responsible for approximately 10-15% of cases of blindness in developed countries. It can affect people of all ages, though it most commonly occurs in working-age adults (20-60 years old). The inflammation can be acute (sudden onset, short duration) or chronic (persistent or recurrent), and the underlying causes range from infections and autoimmune diseases to trauma and, often, unknown factors.

Key Facts About Uveitis

  • Serious condition: Can cause permanent vision loss if untreated; requires prompt medical care.
  • Affects middle-aged adults most: Peak incidence between ages 20-60.
  • Multiple causes: Autoimmune diseases, infections, trauma, or idiopathic (unknown).
  • Various types: Classified by location (anterior, intermediate, posterior, panuveitis).
  • Vision-threatening complications: Can cause cataracts, glaucoma, retinal damage, vision loss.
  • Often recurrent: Many cases are chronic with relapses requiring ongoing management.
  • Treatable: Steroids and immunosuppression can control inflammation and preserve vision.
  • Requires systemic workup: Often associated with systemic diseases requiring evaluation.

Understanding uveitis and recognizing its symptoms early is crucial for preserving vision and preventing serious complications.

Understanding Eye Anatomy and the Uvea

The Uveal Tract

The uvea is the vascular middle layer of the eye, consisting of three parts:

1. Iris

  • Colored part of the eye visible through the cornea
  • Controls pupil size (and therefore light entering the eye)
  • Contains muscles and blood vessels
  • Inflammation here causes "anterior uveitis" or "iritis"

2. Ciliary Body

  • Ring of tissue behind the iris
  • Produces aqueous humor (fluid that fills front of eye)
  • Contains muscles that control lens shape for focusing
  • Inflammation here causes "intermediate uveitis" or "cyclitis"

3. Choroid

  • Layer between retina and sclera (white of eye)
  • Highly vascular; supplies blood to outer retina
  • Contains pigment cells
  • Inflammation here causes "posterior uveitis" or "choroiditis"

Why the Uvea Matters

  • Rich blood supply: Provides nutrients to eye structures
  • Regulates light: Iris controls pupil size
  • Produces aqueous humor: Maintains eye pressure
  • Supports retina: Choroid nourishes photoreceptors

What Happens in Uveitis

  • Inflammation causes blood vessels to become leaky
  • White blood cells and inflammatory proteins enter eye tissues and fluids
  • Swelling and cellular infiltration occur
  • Can damage surrounding structures (retina, lens, optic nerve)
  • Scar tissue may form with chronic inflammation

Types of Uveitis

Uveitis is classified by the primary location of inflammation:

Anterior Uveitis (Iritis/Iridocyclitis)

Most common type (~50-60% of cases):

  • Inflammation of iris and/or ciliary body
  • Also called "iritis" or "iridocyclitis"
  • Symptoms: Eye pain, redness, light sensitivity, blurred vision
  • Onset: Usually sudden
  • Duration: Can be acute (weeks) or chronic (months)
  • Associated conditions: HLA-B27 related diseases (ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease)
  • Prognosis: Generally good with prompt treatment

Intermediate Uveitis (Pars Planitis)

Less common (~10-20% of cases):

  • Inflammation primarily in vitreous cavity and peripheral retina
  • Also called "pars planitis" or "vitritis"
  • Symptoms: Floaters, blurred vision; usually painless
  • Onset: Gradual, insidious
  • Age: Often affects children and young adults
  • Associated conditions: Multiple sclerosis, sarcoidosis; often idiopathic
  • Bilateral: Frequently affects both eyes

Posterior Uveitis (Choroiditis/Chorioretinitis)

Affects back of eye (~15-20% of cases):

  • Inflammation of choroid, retina, or both
  • Also called "choroiditis," "retinitis," or "chorioretinitis"
  • Symptoms: Blurred vision, floaters, visual field defects; usually painless
  • Onset: Variable
  • Causes: Often infectious (toxoplasmosis, CMV, herpes) or autoimmune
  • Vision threat: High risk; can directly damage retina
  • Complications: Retinal scarring, retinal detachment, choroidal neovascularization

Panuveitis (Diffuse Uveitis)

Involves all parts of uvea (~10-15% of cases):

  • Inflammation affects anterior, intermediate, and posterior segments
  • Symptoms: Combination of all symptoms
  • Severe form: Often requires aggressive treatment
  • Causes: Behçet's disease, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease, tuberculosis, syphilis, sarcoidosis
  • Prognosis: More guarded; higher complication risk

Classification by Duration

  • Acute: Sudden onset, limited duration (< 3 months)
  • Recurrent: Repeated episodes with periods of remission
  • Chronic: Persistent inflammation lasting > 3 months

Classification by Cause

  • Infectious: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
  • Non-infectious: Autoimmune, idiopathic
  • Masquerade syndromes: Malignancies or other conditions mimicking uveitis

Causes and Risk Factors

Autoimmune and Inflammatory Diseases (Most Common in Developed Countries)

HLA-B27 Associated Conditions

  • Ankylosing spondylitis: Inflammatory arthritis of spine
  • Reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome): Joint inflammation following infection
  • Psoriatic arthritis: Associated with psoriasis
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis
  • Cause acute anterior uveitis, often recurrent

Other Autoimmune Diseases

  • Behçet's disease: Multisystem inflammatory disease; causes severe panuveitis
  • Sa rcoidosis: Granulomatous disease; any type of uveitis
  • Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) disease: Affects eyes, ears, skin, meninges; bilateral panuveitis
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA): Chronic anterior uveitis in children
  • Multiple sclerosis: Associated with intermediate uveitis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)

Infectious Causes

Viral

  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV): Anterior uveitis
  • Varicella-zoster virus (VZV): Shingles-related
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV): Especially in immunocompromised
  • HIV/AIDS: Increases risk of various infections and uveitis

Bacterial

  • Tuberculosis: Can cause any type of uveitis
  • Syphilis: "Great imitator"; various presentations
  • Lyme disease
  • Cat scratch disease (Bartonella)

Parasitic

  • Toxoplasmosis: Most common infectious cause of posterior uveitis
  • Toxocariasis

Fungal

  • Candida: In immunocompromised or IV drug users
  • Histoplasmosis: Endemic areas (Ohio/Mississippi river valleys)

Other Causes

  • Trauma: Blunt or penetrating injury
  • Post-surgical: Following eye surgery
  • Medications: Rifabutin, bisphosphonates, sulfonamides, others
  • Malignancies: Intraocular lymphoma, leukemia (masquerade syndromes)

Idiopathic Uveitis

  • No identifiable cause found despite thorough evaluation
  • Accounts for ~25-50% of cases
  • Likely autoimmune in many cases

Risk Factors

  • Age: 20-60 years most common
  • Genetics: HLA-B27 gene (present in ~8% population)
  • Autoimmune diseases
  • Infections: Especially immunocompromised
  • Smoking: May increase risk
  • Eye injury or surgery

Recognizing the Symptoms

Symptoms vary by type and location of inflammation:

Anterior Uveitis Symptoms

Eye Pain

Aching, throbbing pain in and around the affected eye, often worsening with eye movement or bright light.

Eye Redness

Red eye, especially around the cornea (circumcorneal injection), creating a "ciliary flush" pattern.

Photophobia

Extreme sensitivity to light; bright light causes severe discomfort.

Blurred Vision

Decreased vision from inflammation, cells in anterior chamber, or pupil constriction.

Small Pupil

Pupil may be constricted (miosis) and sluggish or irregular in shape.

Tearing/Watering

Excessive tearing from irritation and inflammation.

Intermediate/Posterior Uveitis Symptoms

Floaters

Many new floaters (spots, cobwebs, shadows) from inflammatory cells in vitreous.

Blurred Vision

Gradual or sudden vision loss, depending on location and severity of inflammation.

Visual Field Defects

Blind spots or areas of reduced vision if retina affected.

Usually Painless

Unlike anterior uveitis, posterior forms typically don't cause pain or redness.

Progressive Symptoms

  • Sudden onset: Acute anterior uveitis often develops rapidly over hours to days
  • Gradual onset: Posterior/intermediate forms may develop slowly
  • One or both eyes: Can be unilateral or bilateral
  • Worsening: Untreated inflammation progressively damages structures

Red Flags - Seek Immediate Care

Uveitis is a medical emergency. Seek immediate eye care if you experience:

  • Sudden eye pain with redness
  • Severe light sensitivity
  • Sudden vision changes or loss
  • Many new floaters
  • Eye redness that doesn't improve

Delay can result in permanent vision loss.

Diagnosis and Testing

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Visual Acuity

  • Measures current vision
  • Establishes baseline
  • May be reduced in active uveitis

Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy

Detailed examination of eye structures:

  • Conjunctiva/sclera: Type and location of redness
  • Cornea: Precipitates (KP - inflammatory deposits on back of cornea)
  • Anterior chamber: Cells and flare (protein and inflammatory cells in aqueous humor)
  • Iris: Inflammation, nodules, synechiae (adhesions)
  • Pupil: Shape, size, reaction
  • Lens: Clarity, cataract development

Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

  • May be low (from decreased aqueous production)
  • Or elevated (from inflammatory debris blocking drainage)
  • Important for glaucoma risk assessment

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Examine vitreous for cells (vitritis)
  • Inspect retina and choroid for inflammation
  • Check optic nerve
  • Identify specific patterns of inflammation

Ancillary Ophthalmic Tests

  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT): High-resolution retinal imaging; detect macular edema
  • Fluorescein angiography: Dye study showing blood vessel leakage and ischemia
  • Indocyanine green angiography: Evaluate choroid
  • Ultrasound: If view obscured by inflammation
  • Visual field testing: Map areas of vision loss

Laboratory and Systemic Workup

Identify underlying cause:

Initial Screening Tests

  • Complete blood count (CBC)
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) - inflammation markers
  • Chest X-ray (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis)
  • Purified protein derivative (PPD) or Quant if GOLD (tuberculosis screening)
  • Syphilis serology (RPR, FTA-ABS)
  • Lyme disease testing if endemic area

Specific Tests Based on Clinical Picture

  • HLA-B27: If anterior uveitis
  • Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), lysozyme: Sarcoidosis
  • Anti nuclear antibody (ANA): Autoimmune diseases, especially JIA in children
  • Toxoplasmosis serology: If posterior uveitis
  • HIV testing: If risk factors or atypical presentation
  • Herpes virus PCR: From aqueous or vitreous sample

Imaging Studies

  • CT or MRI: Sarcoidosis, CNS involvement, orbital disease
  • Sacroiliac joint X-rays: Ankylosing spondylitis

Specialist Referrals

  • Rheumatology (autoimmune diseases)
  • Infectious disease (complex infections)
  • Oncology (if malignancy suspected)

Note: Extent of workup depends on clinical presentation, laterality, chronicity, and response to treatment.

Treatment

Goals: Control inflammation, preserve vision, prevent complications, treat underlying cause

Corticosteroids (Mainstay of Treatment)

Topical Steroids (Eye Drops)

For anterior uveitis:

  • Prednisolone acetate 1% most common
  • Frequency: Every 1-2 hours initially for severe cases, then taper
  • Duration: Weeks, with gradual tapering
  • Advantages: Direct delivery, minimal systemic effects
  • Limitations: Don't reach posterior segment well

Periocular Steroid Injections

  • Injection around or under the eye
  • For posterior or severe anterior uveitis
  • Triamcinolone most common
  • Provides sustained drug levels

Intravitreal Steroid Injections/Implants

  • Injection directly into vitreous cavity
  • Ozurdex (dexamethasone implant): Sustained release ~3-6 months
  • Retisert, Yutiq (fluocinolone implants): Sustained release up to 3 years
  • For posterior uveitis or macular edema
  • Avoid systemic steroid side effects
  • Risk: Cataracts, elevated eye pressure

Oral Steroids

  • For severe, bilateral, or posterior uveitis
  • Prednisone typical medication
  • High initial dose, gradual taper
  • Side effects: Weight gain, mood changes, elevated blood sugar, bone loss, increased infection risk
  • Long-term use avoided when possible

Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Agents

For anterior uveitis:

  • Dilate pupil and paralyze ciliary muscle
  • Examples: Cyclopentolate, atropine, homatropine
  • Benefits:
    • Reduce pain by relaxing ciliary spasm
    • Prevent/break posterior synechiae (iris adhesions to lens)
  • Used in conjunction with steroids

Immunosuppressive Therapy

For chronic, recurrent, or steroid-resistant uveitis:

Conventional Immunosupp ressants

  • Methotrexate: First-line steroid-sparing agent
  • Azathioprine
  • Mycophenolate mofetil
  • Cyclosporine
  • Allow lower steroid doses or steroid discontinuation
  • Require monitoring for side effects (blood tests)

Biologic Agents

  • Adalimumab (Humira): FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis
  • Infliximab (Remicade): For refractory cases
  • TNF-alpha inhibitors - block specific inflammatory pathway
  • Highly effective for certain types
  • Risk: Infections, including TB reactivation

Antimicrobial Therapy

If infectious cause identified:

  • Antiviral: Acyclovir, valacyclovir (herpesviruses)
  • Antibacterial: Specific to organism (TB, syphilis, Lyme)
  • Antiparasitic: Pyrimethamine, sulfadiazine (toxoplasmosis)
  • Antifungal: Specific antifungals for fungal infections
  • Often combined with steroids (after initiating antimicrobial)

Adjunct Therapies

  • Glaucoma medications: If elevated intraocular pressure
  • Anti-VEGF injections: For choroidal neovascularization

Surgical Treatment

  • Cataract surgery: For steroid-induced or uveitis-related cataracts
  • Glaucoma surgery: If medical management insufficient
  • Vitrectomy: For persistent vitritis, epiretinal membrane, or diagnostic biopsy
  • Usually delayed until inflammation controlled

Potential Complications

Untreated or poorly controlled uveitis can cause serious vision-threatening complications:

Cataracts

  • From chronic inflammation or steroid treatment
  • Very common complication
  • May require surgery

Glaucoma

  • From inflammatory debris blocking drainage (acute)
  • From steroid use (steroid-induced glaucoma)
  • From chronic angle damage
  • Can cause irreversible optic nerve damage

Macular Edema

  • Swelling of central retina (macula)
  • Most common cause of vision loss in uveitis
  • Can be chronic and difficult to treat

Posterior Synechiae

  • Iris adheres to lens
  • Can distort pupil shape
  • May lead to angle-closure glaucoma if 360 degrees (iris bombe)
  • Prevented with cycloplegics

Band Keratopathy

  • Calcium deposition on cornea
  • From chronic anterior uveitis
  • Causes vision impairment

Retinal Damage

  • Epiretinal membrane: Scar tissue on retinal surface
  • Retinal scarring: Permanent damage to photoreceptors
  • Retinal detachment: Rare but serious
  • Choroidal neovascularization: Abnormal blood vessel growth

Vision Loss

  • From any of above complications
  • From optic nerve damage
  • Can be permanent if severe or untreated
  • Uveitis accounts for 10-15% of blindness in developed countries

Prevention: Prompt treatment and close monitoring minimize complication risk.

Living with Uveitis

Long-Term Management

  • Chronic condition: Many cases are recurrent or chronic
  • Ongoing monitoring: Regular eye exams essential
  • Medication compliance: Critical for controlling inflammation
  • Recognizing flare-ups: Know warning signs; seek care promptly

Follow-Up Care

  • Frequency: Initially weekly, then monthly, then every 3-6 months when stable
  • Monitoring: Inflammation control, complication development, medication side effects
  • Coordination: Between ophthalmologist and other specialists

Medication Management

  • Steroid tapers: Never stop abruptly; follow prescribed taper
  • Immunosuppressant monitoring: Regular blood tests for safety
  • Side effect awareness: Report new symptoms
  • Infection precautions: Immunosuppression increases infection risk

Lifestyle Considerations

  • Sunglasses for light sensitivity
  • Avoid smoking (may worsen inflammation)
  • Stress management (stress can trigger flares in some)
  • Healthy diet, regular exercise

Emotional/Psychological Support

  • Chronic disease can be emotionally challenging
  • Vision loss fears are common
  • Support groups, counseling may help
  • Education empowers patients

Prognosis

  • Variable: Depends on type, cause, severity, treatment response
  • Anterior uveitis: Generally good prognosis with treatment
  • Posterior/panuveitis: More guarded; higher complication risk
  • With proper treatment: Many people maintain good vision
  • Recurrence common: Especially in non-infectious cases

When to See a Doctor

Seek IMMEDIATE Eye Care For:

  • Painful red eye, especially with light sensitivity
  • Sudden vision changes or loss
  • Severe eye pain
  • Many new floaters or flashes
  • Eye redness that doesn't improve

Uveitis is a medical emergency. Delay can cause permanent vision loss. Call your ophthalmologist immediately or go to an emergency room.

For Known Uveitis Patients - Contact Doctor If:

  • Symptoms of flare-up (increased redness, pain, light sensitivity, vision changes)
  • New or worsening floaters
  • Medication side effects
  • Any vision changes

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of uveitis do I have?
  • What is the likely cause?
  • What tests do I need?
  • What is my treatment plan?
  • How long will I need treatment?
  • What are the risks and side effects of my medications?
  • What complications should I watch for?
  • How often do I need follow-up appointments?
  • Is this likely to recur?
  • What is my prognosis for vision?
  • Should I see other specialists?
