What Is Iritis (Anterior Uveitis)?

Iritis, also called anterior uveitis, is inflammation of the iris—the colored part of the eye. It's part of the broader category of uveitis (inflammation of the uveal tract: iris, ciliary body, and choroid), specifically affecting the anterior (front) structures. Iritis causes eye pain, redness (especially around the cornea—called ciliary flush or limbal flush), photophobia (light sensitivity), blurred vision, and sometimes tearing. It can occur suddenly (acute iritis) or develop gradually and persist (chronic iritis).

Iritis has many potential causes, including autoimmune diseases (ankylosing spondylitis, inflammatory bowel disease, sarcoidosis), infections (herpes, syphilis, tuberculosis), trauma, or may be idiopathic (no identifiable cause—most common). Diagnosis is made by slit lamp examination showing cells and flare (protein) in the anterior chamber aqueous humor. Treatment primarily involves topical corticosteroid drops to reduce inflammation and cycloplegic drops to dilate the pupil and relieve pain. Most cases of acute iritis respond well to treatment and resolve within weeks, but some patients develop recurrent or chronic iritis requiring ongoing management. Untreated or poorly controlled iritis can lead to complications including glaucoma, cataracts, posterior synechiae (iris adhesions to lens), and vision loss.

Key Facts About Iritis

  • Inflammation of iris (anterior uveitis)
  • Painful red eye: Deep ache, photophobia, blurred vision.
  • Ciliary flush: Redness around cornea (limbus).
  • Causes: autoimmune, infection, trauma, or idiopathic
  • Diagnosis: cells/flare in anterior chamber on slit lamp
  • Treatment: steroid drops + cycloplegic drops
  • Can recur or become chronic.
  • Complications: glaucoma, cataracts, synechiae

Understanding iritis emphasizes the need for prompt diagnosis and treatment to prevent complications and preserve vision.

Causes of Iritis

Idiopathic (Most Common)

  • No identifiable cause found despite workup
  • ~50% of cases

Autoimmune/Systemic Inflammatory Diseases

  • Ankylosing spondylitis: Inflammatory arthritis affecting spine; HLA-B27 positive; anterior uveitis very common (~30-40% develop it)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): Crohn's, ulcerative colitis
  • Reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome)
  • Psoriatic arthritis
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA): Children
  • Sarcoidosis: Granulomatous disease
  • Behçet's disease

Infectious

  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV)
  • Herpes zoster virus (shingles): Herpes zoster ophthalmicus
  • Syphilis
  • Tuberculosis
  • Lyme disease
  • Toxoplasmosis (usually posterior uveitis, but can affect anterior)

Trauma

  • Blunt eye injury
  • Post-surgical (after cataract surgery, etc.)

Other

  • Drug-induced: Rifabutin, cidofovir, bisphosphonates (rare)
  • Masquerade syndromes: Intraocular lymphoma, leukemia (rare)

Recognizing the Symptoms

Eye Pain

Dull, deep ache (not surface irritation); worsens with focusing/accommodation.

Redness (Ciliary Flush)

Redness concentrated around cornea (limbus); violaceous hue; different from conjunctivitis (diffuse redness).

Photophobia (Light Sensitivity)

Severe; bright light very painful; hallmark symptom.

Blurred Vision

From inflammation, cells in aqueous, or pupillary constriction.

Tearing

Watery eye.

Small, Irregular Pupil

Pupil may be constricted (miosis) or irregular from posterior synechiae (adhesions).

Sudden or Gradual Onset

Acute iritis: sudden; chronic: gradual.

Usually Unilateral

One eye typically (but can be bilateral, especially in autoimmune causes).

Diagnosis

Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy - Diagnostic

  • Key findings:
    • Cells in anterior chamber: White blood cells floating in aqueous humor (like "snow" in beam of light)
    • Flare: Protein in aqueous (Tyndall effect—aqueous appears hazy/cloudy in slit lamp beam)
    • Grading: 0 (no cells) to 4+ (severe) based on cell count
    • Posterior synechiae: Iris adhered to lens (irregular pupil shape)
    • Keratic precipitates (KP): Inflammatory cells deposited on corneal endothelium
      • Small KP: non-granulomatous uveitis
      • Large "mutton-fat" KP: granulomatous uveitis (sarcoid, TB, syphilis)
  • Diagnosis primarily clinical based on slit lamp exam

Dilated Fundus Exam

  • Check for posterior uveitis (retinal/choroidal involvement)
  • Rule out panuveitis

Intraocular Pressure (IOP) Measurement

  • IOP may be low (from decreased aqueous production) or high (if inflammatory debris blocks drainage)

Systemic Workup (If Recurrent, Bilateral, or Severe)

  • HLA-B27 test: Check for ankylosing spondylitis association
  • Chest X-ray: Sarcoidosis, tuberculosis
  • ACE level, lysozyme: Sarcoidosis
  • RPR/VDRL, FTA-ABS: Syphilis
  • Lyme serology (if endemic area)
  • TB skin test (PPD) or QuantiFERON
  • Referral to rheumatology or infectious disease if indicated

First Episode, Unilateral, Idiopathic

  • Extensive workup often NOT needed initially
  • Treat, observe; workup if recurrent

Treatment

Topical Corticosteroid Drops (Mainstay)

  • Medications: Prednisolone acetate 1% (most common), dexamethasone
  • Dosing:
    • Severe: Every 1-2 hours initially
    • Taper slowly as inflammation improves (over weeks)
    • Too-rapid taper → rebound inflammation
  • Goal: Reduce inflammation, prevent complications
  • Side effects: IOP elevation (steroid-induced glaucoma), cataract (with chronic use)
  • Requires monitoring

Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Drops

  • Medications: Cyclopentolate, homatropine, atropine
  • Purpose:
    • Dilate pupil → prevent/break posterior synechiae
    • Paralyze ciliary muscle → reduce pain from ciliary spasm
  • Blurs near vision (accommodation paralyzed)

Oral/Systemic Corticosteroids (If Severe or Not Responding)

  • Prednisone orally
  • For severe bilateral or posterior uveitis

Immunosuppressive Agents (Chronic/Recurrent Cases)

  • Methotrexate, mycophenolate, azathioprine, cyclosporine
  • For steroid-dependent or refractory cases
  • Requires collaboration with rheumatology/uveitis specialist

Treat Underlying Cause (If Identified)

  • Antiviral for HSV/VZV (acyclovir, valacyclovir)
  • Antibiotics for syphilis, Lyme, TB
  • Manage autoimmune disease with rheumatologist

Follow-Up

  • Close monitoring initially (every few days)
  • Check IOP (steroid response)
  • Assess inflammation (cells, flare)
  • Taper steroids gradually

Complications

Posterior Synechiae

  • Iris adheres to lens
  • Irregular pupil
  • Can cause pupillary block → angle-closure glaucoma
  • Prevention: Cycloplegic drops to keep pupil dilated

Glaucoma

  • From inflammatory debris blocking drainage (trabeculitis) OR steroid-induced IOP rise
  • Requires additional glaucoma treatment

Cataract

  • From chronic inflammation OR long-term steroid use
  • May need cataract surgery

Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)

  • Fluid accumulation in macula from chronic inflammation
  • Causes vision loss

Band Keratopathy

  • Calcium deposition on cornea from chronic inflammation

Vision Loss

  • From untreated/severe inflammation or complications

Prognosis and Outcomes

Acute Iritis (Single Episode)

  • Good prognosis with prompt treatment
  • Resolves in 4-6 weeks typically
  • Vision usually returns to normal

Recurrent or Chronic Iritis

  • ~50% of patients have recurrence
  • Chronic cases require long-term management
  • Higher risk of complications (glaucoma, cataract)
  • May need immunosuppressive therapy

Associated with Systemic Disease

  • Managing underlying condition reduces recurrence
  • JIA-associated uveitis often chronic, vision-threatening

When to See a Doctor

See Eye Doctor Urgently (Same Day or Next Day) If:

  • Painful red eye with photophobia
  • Eye pain + blurred vision
  • Redness concentrated around cornea (ciliary flush)
  • Do NOT delay—iritis needs prompt treatment

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have iritis?
  • What caused it?
  • Do I need testing for underlying diseases?
  • How often do I use the steroid drops?
  • How long will treatment take?
  • Will it come back?
  • What are signs of complications?

Bottom Line

Iritis (anterior uveitis) is inflammation of the iris causing a painful red eye with photophobia, blurred vision, and ciliary flush (redness around the cornea). It can be caused by autoimmune diseases (ankylosing spondylitis, IBD), infections (herpes, syphilis), trauma, or be idiopathic (~50%). Diagnosis is made by slit lamp exam showing cells and flare in the anterior chamber. Treatment involves topical corticosteroid drops (prednisolone acetate) to reduce inflammation and cycloplegic drops (cyclopentolate, atropine) to dilate the pupil, relieve pain, and prevent synechiae. Most acute cases respond well and resolve in 4-6 weeks, but iritis can recur (~50%) or become chronic, requiring long-term management and sometimes immunosuppressive therapy. Complications include glaucoma, cataracts, posterior synechiae, and vision loss if untreated. If you have a painful red eye with severe photophobia, see an eye doctor urgently for prompt treatment to preserve vision and prevent complications.
