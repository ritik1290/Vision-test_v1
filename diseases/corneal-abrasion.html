What Is a Corneal Abrasion?

A corneal abrasion is a scratch or scrape on the cornea—the clear, dome-shaped front surface of the eye that covers the iris and pupil. The cornea is densely innervated with pain fibers, making even a small abrasion extremely painful and uncomfortable. Corneal abrasions are one of the most common eye injuries, typically resulting from trauma such as a fingernail scratch, contact lens injury, foreign body (dust, sand, metal shaving), or rubbing the eye.

Symptoms include severe eye pain (often described as feeling like "something in the eye"), tearing, redness, light sensitivity (photophobia), and blurred vision. Most corneal abrasions are superficial, affecting only the epithelium (outermost corneal layer), and heal remarkably quickly—typically within 24-48 hours—without permanent damage, as the corneal epithelium regenerates rapidly. Treatment involves pain control, antibiotic eye drops to prevent infection, and sometimes a bandage contact lens for larger abrasions. Complications are rare but include recurrent erosion and infection (if abrasion becomes contaminated), emphasizing the importance of prompt evaluation and appropriate treatment.

Key Facts About Corneal Abrasion

  • Scratch on cornea: Usually from trauma (fingernail, contact lens, foreign body).
  • Extremely painful: Severe eye pain, foreign body sensation.
  • Tearing, redness, photophobia
  • Diagnosed with fluorescein dye: Stains defect bright green under blue light.
  • Usually heals quickly: 24-48 hours for small abrasions.
  • Treatment: antibiotic drops, pain control
  • Avoid contact lenses until fully healed.
  • Rare complications: infection, recurrent erosion

Understanding corneal abrasions emphasizes proper first aid and prompt medical evaluation to ensure healing and prevent complications.

Causes of Corneal Abrasion

Trauma (Most Common)

  • Fingernail scratch: Especially from babies/toddlers, or while rubbing eyes
  • Foreign body:
    • Dust, sand, dirt, wood shavings, metal particles
    • Gets under eyelid, scratches cornea with blinking
  • Contact lens-related:
    • Improper insertion/removal
    • Sleeping in lenses (dry eye → abrasion)
    • Torn or dirty lens
    • Very common cause
  • Eye rubbing: Vigorous rubbing, especially if foreign body present
  • Branch, twig scratch: From plants, trees
  • Paper cut to eye
  • Sports injury (poke to eye)

Other Causes

  • Dry eyes: Severe dryness can cause spontaneous erosion (especially upon waking when eyes dry)
  • Entropion: Inward-turning eyelid → lashes rub cornea
  • Chemical exposure (can cause abrasion + chemical injury)

Recognizing the Symptoms

Severe Eye Pain

Sharp, intense pain; feels like "something in the eye"; worsens with blinking or eye movement.

Foreign Body Sensation

Gritty, scratchy feeling; constant sensation of particle in eye even if removed.

Excessive Tearing

Reflex tearing from irritation.

Redness

Bloodshot eye from irritation.

Photophobia (Light Sensitivity)

Bright light causes pain; squinting.

Blurred Vision

From tear film disruption, swelling, or if abrasion large/central.

Difficulty Opening Eye

Blepharospasm (involuntary eyelid closure) from pain.

Headache

Ipsilateral (same-side) headache common.

Timing

  • Symptoms immediate after injury
  • Or delayed (e.g., wake up in morning with severe pain from contact lens-related abrasion overnight)

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Eye injury? Foreign body? Contact lens use?
  • Time of onset?

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Magnified view of cornea
  • May see obvious defect in corneal epithelium

Fluorescein Staining - Diagnostic

  • Procedure: Fluorescein dye drop instilled in eye
  • Result: Dye stains areas of epithelial defect (abrasion) bright green under cobalt blue light
  • Delineates size and shape of abrasion
  • Classic test; highly sensitive

Evert Eyelid (Rule Out Foreign Body)

  • Check under upper eyelid for retained foreign body
  • Linear vertical abrasions suggest foreign body trapped under lid

Check Visual Acuity

  • Assess vision (may be reduced if large/central abrasion)

Treatment

Remove Foreign Body (If Present)

  • Irrigate eye with saline to flush out loose particles
  • If embedded foreign body, remove with cotton swab or fine needle (by ophthalmologist)

Antibiotic EyeDrops or Ointment

  • Purpose: Prevent bacterial infection while epithelium healing
  • Common: Erythromycin ointment, polymyxin-trimethoprim drops, fluoroquinolone drops
  • Use 3-4 times daily until healed
  • Note: Ointment blurs vision but provides lubrication

Pain Control

  • Oral pain relievers: Ibuprofen, acetaminophen
  • Topical anesthetic drops: Proparacaine, tetracaine
    • Used IN OFFICE for exam comfort
    • NOT prescribed for home use (delays healing, can cause corneal toxicity/ulceration)
  • Cycloplegic drops: Cyclopentolate, homatropine (dilate pupil, relax ciliary muscle → reduce pain from muscle spasm)

Bandage Contact Lens (For Larger Abrasions)

  • Soft contact lens placed on eye to protect abrasion, reduce pain
  • Allows faster healing, more comfort
  • Removed once epithelium healed (24-72 hours)
  • Requires antibiotic coverage to prevent infection

Avoid Contact Lenses (If Abrasion from Contact Lens)

  • Stop wearing lenses until fully healed (usually 1 week minimum)
  • Discard current lenses (may be contaminated)

Eye Patching (Controversial, Not Routinely Recommended)

  • Historically used; now evidence shows no benefit and may slow healing
  • Generally NOT recommended

Follow-Up

  • Small abrasions: re-check in 24-48 hours (or sooner if worsening)
  • Large abrasions or high-risk (contact lens, contaminated injury): daily follow-up until healed

Complications (Rare)

Bacterial Corneal Ulcer (Infection)

  • Abrasion becomes infected
  • Risk factors: Contact lens-related, contaminated injury (organic matter), delayed treatment
  • Symptoms: Worsening pain, white/gray infiltrate on cornea, purulent discharge
  • Serious: Can cause corneal scarring, perforation, vision loss
  • Treatment: Intensive antibiotic drops (fortified antibiotics), close monitoring
  • Prevention: Antibiotic prophylaxis, avoid contact lenses

Recurrent Corneal Erosion Syndrome

  • Abrasion heals, but epithelium doesn't adhere properly to underlying layer
  • Spontaneous re-abrasion occurs (often upon waking when eyes dry)
  • Recurrent episodes of severe pain
  • Treatment: Lubricating ointment at bedtime, hypertonic saline drops, sometimes anterior stromal puncture or laser treatment

Corneal Scarring

  • If abrasion very deep or infected
  • Can cause permanent vision loss if central

Prevention

  • Wear protective eyewear: Safety glasses for work (grinding, sawing, etc.), sports
  • Proper contact lens care:
    • Clean hands before handling lenses
    • Don't sleep in lenses (unless specifically approved)
    • Replace lenses as directed
    • Don't wear if eyes irritated
  • Trim infant/toddler fingernails
  • Be careful with cosmetics, brushes near eyes
  • Avoid rubbing eyes vigorously

When to See a Doctor

See Eye Doctor Same Day If:

  • Eye pain after injury or foreign body
  • Foreign body sensation
  • Vision changes after eye trauma

Urgent/Emergency If:

  • Chemical splash to eye: Irrigate immediately with water for 15+ minutes, then go to ER
  • Penetrating eye injury: ER immediately
  • Severe vision loss

Return If (After Diagnosis/Treatment):

  • Pain worsening despite treatment
  • Not improving by 24-48 hours
  • New discharge (possible infection

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have a corneal abrasion?
  • How large is it?
  • Is there a foreign body?
  • When should it heal?
  • When can I wear contact lenses again?
  • What are signs of infection I should watch for?

Bottom Line

Corneal abrasion is a scratch on the cornea, typically from trauma (fingernail, foreign body, contact lens injury), causing severe eye pain, foreign body sensation, tearing, redness, and photophobia. Diagnosis is made with fluorescein dye staining, which highlights the defect bright green under blue light. Most abrasions are superficial and heal quickly (24-48 hours) with treatment: antibiotic drops to prevent infection, pain control with oral analgesics and cycloplegic drops, and sometimes a bandage contact lens for comfort. Contact lenses must be avoided until fully healed. Complications are rare but include bacterial corneal ulcer (infection, especially with contact lens-related abrasions) requiring intensive antibiotics, and recurrent erosion syndrome causing repeated spontaneous abrasions. Prevention includes protective eyewear and proper contact lens care. If you have eye pain after injury or foreign body, see an eye doctor the same day for evaluation and treatment to ensure proper healing and avoid complications.
