What Is Vitreous Hemorrhage?

Vitreous hemorrhage is bleeding into the vitreous gel—the clear, jelly-like substance that fills the space between the lens and the retina in the back of the eye. Normally, the vitreous is transparent, allowing light to pass through to the retina. When blood leaks into the vitreous from damaged or abnormal retinal blood vessels, it causes the vitreous to become cloudy or opaque, blocking light and causing sudden onset of floaters, shadows, or vision loss ranging from mild to severe depending on the amount of bleeding.

Vitreous hemorrhage is not a disease itself but a sign of an underlying retinal or vascular problem. The most common causes are proliferative diabetic retinopathy (abnormal new blood vessels from diabetes), retinal tear or detachment, and posterior vitreous detachment. Less commonly, it can result from trauma, retinal vein occlusion, or age-related macular degeneration. Management depends on the severity and cause: mild cases may resolve spontaneously as blood clears over weeks to months, while severe cases or those with underlying treatable retinal pathology (retinal tear, detachment) may require vitrectomy surgery to remove the blood and repair the underlying problem.

Key Facts About Vitreous Hemorrhage

  • Blood in vitreous gel: Clouds normally clear gel, blocks vision.
  • Sudden floaters, shadows, vision loss
  • Common causes: diabetic retinopathy, retinal tear, PVD
  • Painless
  • May clear spontaneously over weeks-months (mild cases)
  • Surgery (vitrectomy) if severe or underlying retinal pathology
  • Urgent evaluation needed to rule out retinal detachment.
  • Can recur if underlying cause not treated.

Understanding vitreous hemorrhage emphasizes the need for prompt evaluation to identify and treat the underlying cause.

Causes of Vitreous Hemorrhage

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (Most Common in Adults)

  • Abnormal new blood vessels (neovascularization) grow on retina in response to ischemia from diabetes
  • These vessels fragile, bleed easily into vitreous
  • ~30-50% of vitreous hemorrhages in adults

Retinal Tear or Retinal Detachment

  • Tear in retina can cause bleeding from broken retinal vessels
  • Critical: Must rule out retinal detachment (vision-threatening emergency)

Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD)

  • Vitreous gel separates from retina (common with aging)
  • Can pull on retinal vessels → bleeding
  • Usually mild, self-limited
  • BUT: PVD can also cause retinal tear → must examine carefully

Retinal Vein Occlusion

  • Blocked retinal vein → retinal ischemia → neovascularization → bleeding

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (Wet AMD)

  • Choroidal neovascular membrane can rarely bleed into vitreous

Trauma

  • Blunt or penetrating eye injury
  • Common in younger patients

Other

  • Sickle cell retinopathy
  • Retinal macroaneurysm rupture
  • Bleeding disorders (rare)
  • Terson syndrome (subarachnoid hemorrhage → vitreous hemorrhage)

Recognizing the Symptoms

Sudden Onset of Floaters

Numerous new floaters; "cobwebs," "spots," or "tadpoles" floating in vision; hallmark symptom.

Hazy, Blurred Vision

Vision cloudy, like looking through fog or dirty windshield.

Shadows or Dark Veil

Red or dark shadow obscuring vision.

Vision Loss (Variable)

Mild blur to severe vision loss (light perception only) depending on amount of blood.

Painless

No pain (unlike acute angle-closure glaucoma or uveitis).

Flashes of Light (Sometimes)

If associated PVD or retinal tear—photopsia.

Severity

  • Mild: Few floaters, minimal vision impairment
  • Moderate: Significant floaters, reduced vision
  • Severe (Dense): Vision severely reduced (counting fingers or worse); cannot see retina on exam

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Sudden floaters, vision loss?
  • Diabetes? Prior retinal disease?
  • Trauma?
  • Flashes of light? (suggests retinal tear)

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Look for blood in vitreous (appears as reddish haze or opacity)
  • Key: Examine retina carefully for:
    • Retinal tear or detachment (urgent!)
    • Diabetic retinopathy (neovascularization)
    • Retinal vein occlusion
    • Other pathology
  • If dense hemorrhage, may not be able to see retina

B-Scan Ultrasound (If Retina Not Visible)

  • Ultrasound of eye
  • Shows retinal detachment even if view obscured by blood
  • Critical to rule out detachment

Additional Tests (Once Blood Clears or After Surgery)

  • Fluorescein angiography: Detect ischemia, neovascularization
  • OCT: Assess macula

Treatment

Observation (Mild Cases)

  • If mild hemorrhage, no retinal tear/detachment, vision not severely affected
  • Blood may clear spontaneously over weeks to months as vitreous liquefies and blood settles/absorbs
  • Elevate head of bed (sleep semi-upright) to help blood settle inferiorly away from visual axis
  • Avoid anticoagulants if possible (discuss with doctor)
  • Monitor closely for clearing; if not improving or worsening → surgery

Treat Underlying Cause

Diabetic Retinopathy

  • Once blood clears enough to see retina (or after vitrectomy):
    • Panretinal photocoagulation (PRP) laser: Treat ischemic retina, prevent further neovascularization/bleeding
    • Anti-VEGF injections: Reduce neovascularization
  • Optimize diabetes control

Retinal Tear/Detachment

  • Laser or cryotherapy: Seal retinal tear
  • Scleral buckle, vitrectomy, pneumatic retinopexy: Repair retinal detachment
  • Urgent surgical treatment

Retinal Vein Occlusion

  • Anti-VEGF injections, PRP laser

Vitrectomy Surgery

Indications

  • Severe, dense vitreous hemorrhage not clearing after 2-3 months
  • Bilateral vitreous hemorrhage (both eyes—need at least one eye functional)
  • Retinal detachment or tear (urgent)
  • Neovascular glaucoma (complication from ischemia)
  • Diabetic with dense hemorrhage (earlier surgery often recommended since likely to have persistent neovascularization)

Procedure

  • Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)
  • Remove vitreous gel along with blood
  • Clear view of retina → treat underlying pathology (laser, repair tear/detachment, etc.)
  • Usually outpatient or short hospital stay

Outcomes

  • Clears vision immediately (blood removed)
  • Allows treatment of underlying retinal disease
  • High success rate
  • Visual outcome depends on underlying retinal health

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Appropriate Treatment

  • Mild hemorrhage: Often clears spontaneously; good visual recovery
  • Severe hemorrhage with vitrectomy: Vision improves significantly after surgery if retina healthy
  • Final vision depends on:
    • Underlying cause (diabetic retinopathy, retinal detachment, etc.)
    • Promptness of treatment
    • Extent of retinal damage

Recurrence

  • Can recur if underlying cause (diabetic neovascularization, etc.) not adequately treated
  • Laser/anti-VEGF reduce recurrence risk

Complications If Untreated

  • Persistent severe vision loss
  • Neovascular glaucoma (from ischemia)
  • Retinal detachment (if tear not repaired)

When to See a Doctor

Urgent - See Eye Doctor Within 24 Hours If:

  • Sudden onset of floaters
  • Flashes of light
  • Shadow or veil in peripheral vision
  • Sudden vision loss or blur

These symptoms can indicate vitreous hemorrhage, retinal tear, or retinal detachment.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What caused my vitreous hemorrhage?
  • Do I have a retinal tear or detachment?
  • Will the blood clear on its own, or do I need surgery?
  • How long will it take to clear?
  • What is my prognosis for vision?
  • How can I prevent it from happening again?

Bottom Line

Vitreous hemorrhage is bleeding into the vitreous gel causing sudden onset of floaters, shadows, and vision loss ranging from mild to severe. It's painless and is a sign of underlying retinal or vascular disease, most commonly from proliferative diabetic retinopathy (abnormal fragile blood vessels), retinal tear, or posterior vitreous detachment. Diagnosis requires dilated exam to identify the cause and rule out retinal detachment; B-scan ultrasound is used if blood obscures the retinal view. Mild cases may clear spontaneously over weeks to months with observation, while severe cases or those with retinal tear/detachment require vitrectomy surgery to remove the blood and treat the underlying problem. Treatment of the underlying cause (panretinal laser for diabetic retinopathy, repair of retinal tear) is essential to prevent recurrence. If you experience sudden floaters, flashes, or vision loss, see an eye doctor urgently to rule out retinal detachment and identify the cause.
