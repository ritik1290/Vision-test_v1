What Is a Corneal Ulcer?

A corneal ulcer is an open sore on the cornea—the clear, dome-shaped front surface of the eye. Unlike simple corneal abrasions (scratches), which are superficial, ulcers erode deeper into corneal tissue and are almost always caused by infection. Corneal ulcers are serious eye emergencies that can rapidly progress to vision loss or even loss of the eye if not treated promptly and aggressively.

The cornea plays a critical role in focusing light onto the retina, and it must remain clear and smooth for good vision. When bacteria, fungi, viruses, or parasites infect the cornea—especially if the cornea's protective outer layer (epithelium) has been compromised—they can multiply and create an ulcer. Without immediate treatment with intensive antimicrobial therapy, the infection can penetrate deeper, causing permanent scarring, perforation, or spread to the interior of the eye.

Key Facts About Corneal Ulcers

  • Eye emergency: Requires immediate medical attention to prevent permanent vision loss.
  • Infectious cause: Bacteria, fungi, viruses, or parasites invade cornea.
  • Contact lens-related: Most common risk factor in developed countries.
  • Rapidly progressive: Can worsen within hours to days.
  • Painful: Severe eye pain, foreign body sensation, light sensitivity.
  • Vision-threatening: Can cause permanent scarring, blindness, or eye loss.
  • Intensive treatment needed: Frequent antibiotic drops (hourly initially), sometimes hospitalization.
  • Prevention crucial: Proper contact lens hygiene prevents most cases.

Understanding corneal ulcers and recognizing symptoms early is critical for preserving vision.

Causes and Risk Factors

Infectious Causes

Bacterial Ulcers (Most Common)

  • Organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Streptococcus species
  • Pseudomonas: Particularly aggressive; associated with contact lens use; can perforate cornea in 24-48 hours
  • Appearance: White/yellow infiltrate with surrounding inflammation
  • Treatment: Fortified topical antibiotics

Fungal Ulcers

  • Organisms: Fusarium, Aspergillus, Candida
  • Risk factors: Plant trauma (tree branch scratch), agricultural workers, hot humid climates
  • Appearance: Feathery borders, satellite lesions
  • Slow-growing but serious: Harder to treat than bacterial
  • Treatment: Antifungal drops; may take weeks to months

Viral Ulcers

  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV): Common; dendritic (branching) pattern on fluorescein staining
  • Herpes zoster (shingles): Reactivation of chickenpox virus
  • Treatment: Antiviral medications (oral and/or topical)

Acanthamoeba (Parasitic)

  • Free-living amoeba found in water, soil
  • Risk factor: Contact lens exposure to tap water, swimming with lenses, poor hygiene
  • Severe pain out of proportion to findings
  • Very difficult to treat: Requires prolonged (months) antimicrobial therapy
  • Poor outcomes: Often results in scarring or need for corneal transplant

Major Risk Factors

Contact Lens Wear (Leading Risk Factor)

  • Improper hygiene: Not cleaning lenses, using tap water to rinse
  • Overwear: Sleeping in lenses not approved for overnight wear
  • Contaminated cases: Not replacing lens case regularly
  • Swimming/showering in lenses
  • Contact lens use increases risk 10-15 times

Corneal Trauma

  • Scratch from fingernail, tree branch, foreign body
  • Chemical injury
  • Provides entry point for infection

Dry Eye and Exposure

  • Severe dry eye compromises corneal defense
  • Incomplete eyelid closure (lagophthalmos) causes exposure keratopathy
  • Bell's palsy, stroke, incomplete blinking

Immunocompromise

  • Diabetes
  • HIV/AIDS
  • Chemotherapy, immunosuppressive medications
  • Reduced ability to fight infection

Chronic Eye Conditions

  • Blepharitis (eyelid inflammation)
  • Trichiasis (inward-turning eyelashes scratching cornea)
  • Entropion (eyelid turning inward)

Corticosteroid Use

  • Topical steroid eye drops suppress local immune response
  • Can worsen fungal and herpes infections

Recognizing the Symptoms

Emergency Symptoms - Seek Immediate Care:

  • Severe eye pain
  • Sudden vision loss or blurring
  • Red, inflamed eye
  • White or gray spot on cornea
  • Thick discharge
  • Extreme light sensitivity

Corneal ulcers are medical emergencies. Do not delay—call ophthalmologist immediately or go to emergency room.

Severe Eye Pain

Sharp, intense pain; foreign body sensation; pain with blinking or eye movement.

White or Gray Spot on Eye

Visible opacity on normally clear cornea; may appear as white, gray, or yellowish infiltrate.

Blurred or Decreased Vision

Vision loss proportional to ulcer location and size; vision severely affected if central.

Thick Discharge

Purulent (pus-like) yellow or green discharge; eye may be "stuck shut" in morning.

Severe Redness

Eye markedly bloodshot, especially around cornea (ciliary flush).

Light Sensitivity (Photophobia)

Extreme sensitivity to light; unable to open eye in bright environments.

Excessive Tearing

Reflex tearing from pain and irritation.

Eyelid Swelling

Puffy, swollen eyelids from inflammation.

Progression

  • Initial (hours): Pain, redness developing; may start mildly
  • Early (day 1-2): Increasing pain, visible white spot, discharge
  • Progressive (days 2-7): Ulcer enlarging, deepening; risk of perforation
  • Advanced: Hypopyon (pus in anterior chamber), corneal thinning, perforation risk

Acanthamoeba-Specific Symptoms

  • Pain out of proportion to clinical findings
  • Ring-shaped infiltrate (later stages)
  • Slow progression over weeks

Diagnosis

Emergency Eye Examination

History

  • Contact lens use (type, hygiene, overnight wear)?
  • Recent trauma?
  • Previous corneal problems or surgeries?
  • Immunocompromise, diabetes?
  • Use of steroid drops?

Visual Acuity

  • Measure degree of vision loss

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Magnified view of cornea
  • Appearance: White/gray/yellow infiltrate; surrounding edema
  • Depth assessment: Superficial vs. deep stromal involvement
  • Hypopyon: White layered pus in bottom of anterior chamber (severe infection sign)
  • Size and location: Affects prognosis (central worse than peripheral)

Fluorescein Staining

  • Orange dye highlights epithelial defects
  • Ulcer appears bright green under blue light
  • Reveals size and depth
  • Dendritic pattern: Suggests herpes simplex

Corneal Culture and Scraping (Critical)

Before starting treatment:

  • Procedure: Sterile blade or needle scrapes ulcer edge
  • Samples sent for:
    • Bacterial culture and sensitivity
    • Fungal culture
    • Gram stain, KOH prep
    • Sometimes PCR testing (herpes, Acanthamoeba)
  • Purpose: Identify causative organism, guide targeted antibiotic choice
  • Results: Take 24-72 hours (bacteria) to weeks (fungi, Acanthamoeba)
  • Treatment started empirically while awaiting results

Confocal Microscopy (If Available)

  • High-resolution imaging of corneal layers
  • Can detect Acanthamoeba cysts in vivo
  • Specialized test; not universally available

Treatment

Corneal Ulcers Require Aggressive, Immediate Treatment

Treatment must begin urgently to prevent vision loss. This is NOT a condition that can wait.

Initial Empiric (Broad-Spectrum) Antibiotic Treatment

Started immediately while awaiting culture results:

Fortified Topical Antibiotics

  • Typical regimen: Combination of two antibiotics covering gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria
  • Examples:
    • Fortified cefazolin (50 mg/mL) + fortified tobramycin or gentamicin (14-15 mg/mL)
    • OR fluoroquinolone monotherapy (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin)
  • Dosing: Initially every 30-60 minutes around the clock (yes, waking up at night)
  • Intensive: Not standard bottles from pharmacy; compounded concentrated "fortified" drops

Hospitalization (For Severe Cases)

  • Large central ulcers
  • Perforation risk
  • Need for hourly drops with nursing supervision
  • Immunocompromised patients

Targeted Treatment Based on Culture

Adjust antibiotics once culture identifies organism:

Bacterial Ulcers

  • Continue or adjust antibiotics based on sensitivities
  • Gradually taper frequency as ulcer heals (every hour → every 2 hrs → every 4 hrs → QID → etc.)
  • Treatment duration: Typically 2-4 weeks

Fungal Ulcers

  • Antifungal drops: Natamycin (first-line), voriconazole, amphotericin B
  • Dosing: Hourly initially
  • Oral antifungals: Sometimes added (voriconazole, fluconazole)
  • Duration: Weeks to months (fungi grow slowly)
  • Challenging: Harder to treat than bacterial; higher perforation risk

Herpes Simplex Ulcers

  • Antiviral drops: Trifluridine, ganciclovir gel
  • Oral antivirals: Acyclovir or valacyclovir
  • Debridement: Sometimes epithelium mechanically removed
  • NO STEROIDS initially: Can worsen herpes infection

Acanthamoeba

  • Dual therapy: Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) or chlorhexidine + propamidine or hexamidine
  • Intensive: Hourly for weeks, then tapered
  • Prolonged: Months of treatment
  • Pain management: Often requires oral pain medications
  • Prognosis: Often poor; corneal transplant may be needed

Adjunctive Treatments

Cycloplegic Drops

  • Dilate pupil, paralyze ciliary muscle
  • Reduce pain from ciliary spasm
  • Prevent posterior synechiae (iris adhesions)
  • Examples: Atropine, cyclopentolate

Steroids (Controversial, Used Cautiously)

  • Role: Reduce inflammation and scarring (later in treatment course)
  • Timing: Only after infection controlled; NOT in fungal or herpes ulcers initially
  • Risk: Can worsen infection if started too early
  • Decision made case-by-case by ophthalmologist

Oral Pain Medication

  • Corneal ulcers are very painful
  • NSAIDs, sometimes opioids for severe pain

Surgical Treatment

Corneal Gluing

  • For small perforations or impending perforation
  • Cyanoacrylate glue applied to seal hole
  • Bandage contact lens placed over glue

Corneal Transplant (Keratoplasty)

  • Therapeutic (emergency) transplant: For infection not responding to medical therapy, perforation
  • Remove infected tissue, replace with donor cornea
  • Optical transplant (delayed): After infection resolved, to restore vision if significant scarring

Amniotic Membrane Transplant

  • Promotes healing
  • Reduces inflammation
  • For non-healing ulcers

Monitoring

  • Daily or every-other-day exams initially
  • Assess response to treatment
  • Watch for complications (perforation, endophthalmitis)
  • Adjust medications based on progress

Complications

Corneal Scarring

  • Permanent opacity where ulcer was
  • Central scars: Significantly impair vision
  • Peripheral scars: Less impact
  • May require corneal transplant to restore vision

Corneal Perforation

  • Ulcer erodes completely through cornea
  • Aqueous fluid leaks out
  • Iris can prolapse through hole
  • Emergency: Requires immediate surgical repair or transplant

Endophthalmitis

  • Infection spreads inside eye
  • Devastating; can cause blindness or loss of eye
  • Requires intravitreal antibiotic injections, sometimes vitrectomy

Glaucoma

  • Inflammation or steroids can elevate eye pressure
  • Requires glaucoma medications

Panophthalmitis

  • Infection of entire eye and surrounding tissues
  • Most severe complication
  • May necessitate enucleation (eye removal)

Descemetocele

  • Ulcer erodes to Descemet's membrane (deepest corneal layer)
  • Imminent perforation risk
  • Appears as dark area in center of ulcer

Prevention

Contact Lens Safety (Most Important)

  • Hand hygiene: Wash hands before handling lenses
  • Proper cleaning: Use fresh solution; never tap water
  • Replace case: Every 3 months minimum
  • Don't sleep in lenses: Unless specifically designed and approved
  • Remove if red or irritated
  • Never swim, shower, or expose to water while wearing lenses
  • Replace lenses on schedule: Don't overwear disposables
  • Remove before sleeping

Protective Eyewear

  • Safety glasses for work with flying debris, chemicals
  • Sports goggles
  • Prevents trauma

Treat Dry Eye

  • Lubricant drops if dry eyes
  • Treat underlying causes

Manage Chronic Conditions

  • Control diabetes
  • Treat blepharitis
  • Address eyelid abnormalities (trichiasis, entropion)

Avoid Tap Water with Contact Lenses

  • Acanthamoeba lives in tap water, lakes, hot tubs
  • Use only sterile saline or recommended solution

Prompt Treatment of Minor Injuries

  • Seek eye care for scratches or foreign bodies
  • Prophylactic antibiotic ointment may prevent infection

Prognosis and Outcomes

Factors Affecting Outcomes

Better Prognosis:

  • Small, peripheral ulcer
  • Early treatment
  • Bacterial cause
  • Responds quickly to antibiotics
  • No perforation

Poorer Prognosis:

  • Large, central ulcer
  • Delayed treatment
  • Fungal or Acanthamoeba cause
  • Immunocompromised patient
  • Perforation or endophthalmitis

Visual Outcomes

  • Small peripheral ulcers: Often heal with minimal scarring; good vision
  • Central ulcers: Scarring can significantly impair vision even after healing
  • Scarring treatment: May require corneal transplant for visual rehabilitation

Healing Time

  • Bacterial: Typically 2-4 weeks
  • Fungal: Weeks to months
  • Acanthamoeba: Months

Long-Term

  • Some patients regain excellent vision
  • Others have permanent visual impairment from scarring
  • Regular follow-up to monitor healing and manage complications

When to See a Doctor

SEEK IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY CARE IF:

  • Severe eye pain
  • Red eye with vision loss
  • White spot visible on cornea
  • Thick discharge
  • Extreme light sensitivity
  • Eye trauma followed by pain/redness
  • Contact lens wearer with red, painful eye

Call ophthalmologist immediately or go to emergency room. Corneal ulcers are emergencies—hours matter.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What organism is causing my ulcer?
  • How severe is the ulcer?
  • What is my treatment plan?
  • How often do I need to use drops?
  • Do I need to be hospitalized?
  • What is the risk of vision loss?
  • When will we know if treatment is working?
  • Will I need surgery?
  • How can I prevent this in the future?

Bottom Line

Corneal ulcers are serious eye infections requiring immediate emergency treatment to prevent permanent vision loss or loss of the eye. Most are associated with contact lens use and poor hygiene. Symptoms include severe eye pain, redness, discharge, and a white spot on the eye. Treatment involves intensive antibiotic, antifungal, or antiviral drops—often hourly around the clock initially. Early recognition and aggressive treatment are critical for good outcomes. Prevention through proper contact lens hygiene is far better than treatment—never expose lenses to water, always use fresh solution, and remove lenses immediately if eyes become red or painful.
