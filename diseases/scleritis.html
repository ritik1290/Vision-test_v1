What Is Scleritis?

Scleritis is a serious, painful inflammatory disease affecting the sclera—the tough, white outer coat of the eyeball that provides structural integrity and protection. Unlike episcleritis (inflammation of the superficial episclera layer), which is relatively benign, scleritis is a deep, severe inflammation that can threaten vision and is often associated with underlying systemic autoimmune diseases. The condition causes intense, boring eye pain that may radiate to the face and head, along with redness, light sensitivity, and potential vision loss if structures inside the eye are affected.

Scleritis is relatively uncommon, affecting about 4-6 people per 100,000 annually, with peak incidence in the 40s-50s and a slight female predominance. Approximately 40-50% of scleritis cases are associated with systemic diseases, most commonly rheumatoid arthritis, granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Wegener's), and other vasculitides. Because scleritis can signal serious underlying disease and may lead to vision-threatening complications, prompt recognition and aggressive treatment with immunosuppressive therapy are essential.

Key Facts About Scleritis

  • Deep scleral inflammation: Involves thick white wall of eye, not just surface.
  • Severe, boring pain:Intense, deep pain often waking patient at night; hallmark symptom.
  • Vision-threatening: Can cause complications like uveitis, glaucoma, or scleral thinning/perforation.
  • Associated with systemic disease: ~40-50% have underlying autoimmune or inflammatory condition.
  • Distinct from episcleritis: More serious, painful, and requires aggressive treatment.
  • Requires immunosuppression: Often needs oral steroids or other immunosuppressive drugs.
  • Recurrent: Can flare repeatedly; chronic management often needed.
  • Warrants systemic workup: Underlying disease must be identified and treated.

Understanding scleritis is crucial for early diagnosis, preventing vision loss, and identifying associated systemic diseases.

Types of Scleritis

By Location

Anterior Scleritis (~90%)

  • Involves front part of sclera (visible portion)
  • Sub-divided into diffuse, nodular, and necrotizing types (see below)
  • Most common presentation

Posterior Scleritis (~10%)

  • Involves back part of sclera (behind equator of eye)
  • Not directly visible externally
  • More difficult to diagnose
  • Can cause vision loss from choroidal or retinal involvement
  • Diagnosed by ultrasound or MRI showing thickened sclera

By Severity/Appearance (Anterior Scleritis)

Diffuse Anterior Scleritis (Most Common, ~40-60%)

  • Widespread scleral inflammation
  • Deep, dusky redness (vs. bright red in episcleritis)
  • Painful but usually non-vision-threatening
  • Best prognosis

Nodular Anterior Scleritis (~25-45%)

  • One or more firm, tender nodules on sclera
  • Immobile (fixed to underlying sclera)
  • Severe pain
  • Intermediate prognosis

Necrotizing Anterior Scleritis (Most Severe, ~10-15%)

  • Severe inflammation with scleral tissue destruction
  • Sclera thins, appears bluish/dark (underlying uvea visible)
  • High risk of perforation
  • Two subtypes:
    • With inflammation: Severe pain, very poor prognosis
    • Without inflammation (scleromalacia perforans): Painless, insidious thinning; seen in long-standing rheumatoid arthritis
  • Vision-threatening
  • Highest association with systemic disease (~90%)
  • Worst prognosis; can lead to globe perforation

Causes and Associated Diseases

Association with Systemic Disease (~40-50% of Cases)

Rheumatologic/Autoimmune Diseases (Most Common)

  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Most common association; ~15% of scleritis patients
  • Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Wegener's): Necrotizing vasculitis; very high risk of necrotizing scleritis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Relapsing polychondritis: Inflammation of cartilage
  • Ankylosing spondylitis and other spondyloarthropathies
  • Polyarteritis nodosa: Vasculitis
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis)
  • Sj\u00f6gren's syndrome

Infectious Causes (Rare, ~5-10%)

  • Post-infectious: Herpes zoster, syphilis, tuberculosis, Lyme disease
  • Direct infection of sclera uncommon

Other Associations

  • Gout
  • Sarcoidosis
  • Post-surgical: Following eye surgery (pterygium removal, cataract surgery)

Idiopathic (No Identifiable Cause) ~50%

  • Half of scleritis cases have no associated systemic disease found
  • Still requires systemic workup to rule out occult disease

Why Scleritis Develops

  • Autoimmune attack on scleral tissue
  • Vasculitis (blood vessel inflammation) affecting scleral vessels
  • Immune complex deposition
  • Leads to inflammation, tissue destruction, and potential perforation in severe cases

Recognizing the Symptoms

Severe, Boring Eye Pain

Deep, intense pain radiating to eyebrow, temple, jaw; often worst at night; awakens patient; hallmark of scleritis.

Eye Redness

Deep, dusky, violaceous (purplish-red) color; not bright red like conjunctivitis.

Tenderness to Touch

Eye painful when pressed gently through eyelid.

Light Sensitivity (Photophobia)

Severe photophobia, especially if anterior uveitis present.

Tearing

Excessive tearing from irritation and inflammation.

Blurred Vision

If inflammation spreads to cornea, uvea, or retina; sign of complications.

Pain Characteristics

  • Quality: Deep, boring, aching
  • Severity: Often severe; described as worst eye pain ever experienced
  • Radiation: Spreads to forehead, temple, jaw
  • Worsens with eye movement
  • Nocturnal worsening: Often worse at night, disrupts sleep
  • Not relieved by topical anesthetics (unlike corneal pain)

Scleritis vs. Episcleritis

Critical distinction:

  • Scleritis:
    • Deep, severe pain
    • Dusky, violaceous redness
    • Vessels don't blanch with phenylephrine drops
    • Vision-threatening
    • Associated with systemic disease
    • Requires systemic immunosuppression
  • Episcleritis:
    • Mild discomfort or irritation
    • Bright, localized redness
    • Vessels blanch with phenylephrine
    • Self-limited, benign
    • Rarely associated with systemic disease
    • Topical treatment or observation

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Onset, duration, severity of pain
  • Previous episodes?
  • Recent trauma or surgery?
  • Known systemic diseases (rheumatoid arthritis, vasculitis, etc.)?
  • Systemic symptoms (joint pain, rash, breathing issues, kidney problems)?

Ophthalmic Examination

External Inspection

  • Dusky, deep redness
  • Nodules (if nodular scleritis)
  • Scleral thinning, bluish discoloration (necrotizing scleritis)
  • Tenderness to palpation

Phenylephrine Test

  • Topical vasoconstrictor applied
  • Episcleritis: Superficial vessels blanch (turn white)
  • Scleritis: Deep vessels do NOT blanch; redness persists
  • Helps differentiate scleritis from episcleritis

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Red-free (green) light highlights scleral vessels
  • Deep vascular engorgement
  • Assess for anterior uveitis (cells/flare in anterior chamber)
  • Corneal involvement (keratitis, peripheral ulcerative keratitis)
  • Scleral thinning

Dilated Fundus Exam

  • Look for posterior uveitis, retinal detachment (if posterior scleritis)
  • Choroidal folds

Intraocular Pressure

  • May be elevated (glaucoma) or low (if perforation threatening)

Imaging

B-Scan Ultrasound (For Posterior Scleritis)

  • Shows thickened posterior sclera (T-sign)
  • Fluid in sub-Tenon's space
  • Choroidal thickening

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Assess retinal/choroidal involvement
  • Macular edema

MRI (Occasionally)

  • Can show posterior scleral thickening and orbital inflammation

Systemic Workup (Essential)

Rule out underlying systemic disease:

  • Blood tests:
    • Complete blood count (CBC)
    • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP)
    • Rheumatoid factor (RF), anti-CCP antibodies
    • Antinuclear antibody (ANA), anti-dsDNA (lupus)
    • ANCA (anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody) for vasculitis (Wegener's)
    • Uric acid (gout)
    • ACE level (sarcoidosis)
    • Infectious workup if suspected: RPR/VDRL (syphilis), Lyme, tuberculosis
  • Chest X-ray: Sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, vasculitis
  • Urinalysis: Kidney involvement in vasculitis
  • Referral to rheumatologist if systemic disease suspected

Complications

Vision-Threatening Complications

Anterior Uveitis

  • Inflammation spreads to uvea (iris, ciliary body)
  • Causes photophobia, vision blur
  • Can lead to glaucoma, cataract

Keratitis and Peripheral Ulcerative Keratitis (PUK)

  • Corneal inflammation and melting at corneal edge
  • Progressive, crescent-shaped ulcer
  • Can perforate
  • Associated with necrotizing scleritis and vasculitis
  • Ophthalmologic emergency

Scleral Thinning and Perforation

  • Necrotizing scleritis destroys scleral tissue
  • Sclera becomes thin, bluish (uvea shows through)
  • Risk of globe rupture
  • May require scleral patch graft

Glaucoma

  • From associated uveitis, steroid use, or trabecular meshwork inflammation

Cataract

  • From chronic inflammation or steroid treatment

Retinal Detachment (Posterior Scleritis)

  • Exudative (fluid accumulation under retina)

Vision Loss

  • From complications above, optic nerve involvement, or chronic inflammation
  • Permanent if not treated promptly

Treatment

Scleritis Requires Aggressive Treatment

Unlike episcleritis, scleritis is vision-threatening and usually requires systemic immunosuppression (oral medications), not just topical drops. Treatment aims to control inflammation, prevent complications, and address underlying systemic disease.

First-Line Treatment: Oral NSAIDs

  • Indomethacin or other NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
  • Dose: High dose (e.g., indomethacin 50 mg TID)
  • Effective for: Mild to moderate diffuse or nodular scleritis
  • Response: Should see improvement in 1-2 weeks
  • Limitations: Often insufficient alone for severe or necrotizing scleritis
  • Monitor for GI side effects

Oral Corticosteroids

  • Indication: Severe scleritis, necrotizing scleritis, or inadequate response to NSAIDs
  • Regimen: Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day initially, then taper over weeks to months
  • Effective: Controls inflammation quickly
  • Side effects: Weight gain, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, mood changes, increased infection risk
  • Long-term steroids often not feasible; steroid-sparing agents needed

Immunosuppressive Agents (Steroid-Sparing)

For recurrent, severe, or necrotizing scleritis; allows steroid tapering:

  • Methotrexate: First-line steroid-sparing agent
  • Mycophenolate mofetil (CellCept): Effective, well-tolerated
  • Azathioprine (Imuran)
  • Cyclophosphamide: Reserved for severe, life/vision-threatening cases (Wegener's with PUK)
  • Require monitoring (blood tests for liver, kidney, blood counts)
  • Managed in conjunction with rheumatologist

Biologic Agents

  • Tumor necrosis factor (TNF) inhibitors: Infliximab, adalimumab
  • Rituximab: Anti-CD20 antibody; effective for refractory scleritis
  • Used for severe, refractory cases
  • Expensive; require infusion or injection

Topical Corticosteroids

  • Adjunct only; not sufficient alone
  • Used if anterior uveitis present

Treatment of Underlying Systemic Disease

  • Essential for long-term control
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: DMARDs (methotrexate, biologics)
  • Wegener's: Cyclophosphamide, rituximab

Surgical Treatment

Scleral Patch Graft

  • For severe scleral thinning or impending perforation
  • Donor scleral tissue sutured over thin area
  • Performed after inflammation controlled

Treatment of Complications

  • Cataract surgery if vision impaired
  • Glaucoma medications or surgery

Monitoring During Treatment

  • Frequent ophthalmology follow-up (weekly to monthly initially)
  • Assess response, watch for complications
  • Monitor for medication side effects (blood tests)

Prognosis and Outcomes

Overall Outcomes

  • Variable: Depends on type and severity
  • With treatment: Most achieve control of inflammation
  • Chronic/recurrent: Many have repeated flares requiring ongoing therapy

By Type

  • Diffuse anterior scleritis: Best prognosis; rarely causes vision loss
  • Nodular anterior scleritis: Intermediate; can recur
  • Necrotizing scleritis: Worst prognosis
    • High risk of vision loss (~40%)
    • Risk of perforation
    • Requires aggressive immunosuppression
    • Mortality risk from underlying vasculitis
  • Posterior scleritis: Variable; depends on extent of retinal/choroidal involvement

Visual Outcomes

  • Most retain good vision if treated early
  • Necrotizing scleritis: 25-40% have significant vision loss
  • Vision loss from complications (uveitis, glaucoma, PUK, perforation)

Systemic Implications

  • Scleritis may be first manifestation of serious systemic disease
  • Finding underlying vasculitis important for systemic prognosis and treatment

Recurrence

  • Common, especially if underlying systemic disease
  • Chronic immunosuppression often needed

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Evaluation If:

  • Severe, deep, boring eye pain
  • Eye redness with severe pain (not just irritation)
  • Vision changes
  • Pain radiating to forehead, temple, jaw
  • Pain worsening or awakening you at night

Scleritis is a serious condition requiring prompt ophthalmologic evaluation and systemic treatment to prevent vision loss.

Follow-Up If Diagnosed

  • Close ophthalmology monitoring during treatment
  • Rheumatology consultation for systemic disease management
  • Regular blood work to monitor immunosuppressive medications

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of scleritis do I have?
  • Do I need systemic workup for autoimmune disease?
  • What treatment do you recommend?
  • Will I need long-term immunosuppression?
  • What is my risk of vision loss?
  • What is my risk of recurrence?
  • Should I see a rheumatologist?

Bottom Line

Scleritis is a serious, painful inflammation of the sclera (eye wall) causing severe, boring eye pain and deep eye redness. Unlike benign episcleritis, scleritis is vision-threatening and often associated with underlying autoimmune diseases like rheumatoid arthritis or vasculitis. Necrotizing scleritis is the most severe form, risking scleral perforation and vision loss. Diagnosis requires thorough eye examination plus systemic workup to identify associated diseases. Treatment typically involves oral NSAIDs or corticosteroids, often with immunosuppressive agents for severe cases. If you experience severe, deep eye pain with redness, seek immediate evaluation—early aggressive treatment is essential to prevent permanent vision loss and address potentially serious underlying systemic disease.
