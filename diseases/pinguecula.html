What Is a Pinguecula?

A pinguecula (pronounced "pin-GWEK-you-lah") is a common, benign (non-cancerous) yellowish or white raised bump that forms on the conjunctiva—the clear tissue covering the white part of the eye (sclera). It typically appears on the nasal (inner) side of the eye, near the cornea, though it can also occur on the temporal (outer) side. Pingueculae (plural) are caused by changes in conjunctival tissue due to chronic sun exposure (UV radiation), wind, dust, and aging.

Most pingueculae are asymptomatic and require no treatment. They're extremely common in adults, especially those with significant sun exposure. While cosmetically noticeable, they're harmless and do not affect vision. Occasionally, a pinguecula can become irritated or inflamed (pingueculitis), causing redness, irritation, and foreign body sensation, which can be treated with lubricating drops or anti-inflammatory medications. Very rarely, surgical removal is considered for cosmetic reasons or persistent symptoms.

Key Facts About Pinguecula

  • Benign yellowish bump: Non-cancerous growth on white of eye.
  • Very common: Nearly universal in adults over 40-50.
  • UV-related: Sun exposure primary cause.
  • Usually asymptomatic: Most cause no symptoms.
  • Does NOT grow onto cornea: Stays on conjunctiva (unlike pterygium).
  • Does NOT affect vision: Benign, non-vision-threatening.
  • Treatment rarely needed: Only if inflamed or cosmetically bothersome.
  • Different from pterygium: Pinguecula doesn't invade cornea; pterygium does.

Understanding pingueculae helps reduce unnecessary concern about this benign, age-related finding.

Causes and Risk Factors

Chronic UV Exposure (Primary Cause)

  • Ultraviolet radiation damages conjunctival tissue
  • Leads to degeneration, elastic fiber deposits, yellowing
  • Similar mechanism to sun damage on skin

Environmental Irritants

  • Wind, dust, dry air: Chronic irritation
  • Outdoor exposure: Especially in sunny, windy climates

Aging

  • Cumulative effect of years of exposure
  • Prevalence increases with age

Risk Factors

Age

  • Rare in children
  • Common in middle-aged and elderly (>40 years)
  • Nearly universal over age 70

Geographic Location

  • More common in sunny, equatorial regions
  • High-altitude areas (increased UV)

Outdoor Occupation or Lifestyle

  • Farmers, fishermen, construction workers
  • Prolonged sun exposure without eye protection

Lack of Sunglasses Use

  • No UV protection

Dry Eye Syndrome

  • Chronic dryness may contribute

Symptoms and Appearance

Visual Appearance

  • Color: Yellowish-white or cream-colored
  • Shape: Slightly raised, triangular or oval bump
  • Location: On conjunctiva, typically nasal (inner) side near cornea; can be temporal (outer)
  • Size: Small (1-3 mm usually); can enlarge over years
  • Bilateral: Often affects both eyes
  • Does NOT cross onto cornea: Stays on sclera/conjunctiva (key difference from pterygium)

Symptoms (Usually None)

Asymptomatic (Most Common)

  • Many people unaware of pinguecula
  • Incidental finding during eye exam

If Symptomatic (Pingueculitis - Inflamed Pinguecula)

  • Redness: Conjunctival injection around bump
  • Irritation: Gritty, foreign body sensation
  • Dryness: Feeling of dryness
  • Mild discomfort
  • Triggered by: dry, windy conditions; prolonged screen use; contact lens wear

What It Does NOT Cause

  • Vision loss: Does not affect vision
  • Pain: Usually painless (unless inflamed)
  • Discharge: No pus or mucus

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

  • Based on appearance during eye exam
  • Yellowish bump on conjunctiva diagnostic
  • No testing needed

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Magnified view confirms diagnosis
  • Shows characteristic yellowish, elevated conjunctival lesion
  • Elastic fiber degeneration visible

Differential Diagnosis

  • Pterygium: Grows onto cornea (pinguecula does not)
  • Conjunctival cyst: Clear, fluid-filled
  • Conjunctival tumor (rare): If atypical appearance, biopsy may be considered

Treatment

No Treatment Needed (Most Cases)

  • Benign, asymptomatic pingueculae require no treatment
  • Observation only

Treatment for Symptomatic/Inflamed Pinguecula (Pingueculitis)

Lubricating Eye Drops (Artificial Tears)

  • First-line treatment
  • Frequent use to keep eye moist
  • Reduces irritation, foreign body sensation
  • Preservative-free drops preferred for frequent use

Topical Anti-Inflammatory Drops

  • Topical NSAIDs: Ketorolac, diclofenac
  • Mild topical steroids: Loteprednol, fluorometholone (short-term for acute inflammation)
  • Reduce redness, irritation
  • Used for limited time

Avoid Irritants

  • Protect eyes from wind, dust, smoke
  • Use humidifier if dry environment

Surgical Excision (Rarely Needed)

  • Indications:
    • Cosmetic concerns (patient bothered by appearance)
    • Recurrent inflammation not controlled with drops
    • Interferes with contact lens wear
    • Very large, raised pinguecula
  • Procedure:
    • Outpatient, local anesthesia
    • Excise pinguecula, may use conjunctival graft or glue
    • Quick (15-30 minutes)
  • Risks:
    • Recurrence (~10-20%)
    • Scarring
    • Infection (rare)
  • Not commonly done; most pingueculae don't warrant surgery

Complications

Pingueculitis (Inflamed Pinguecula)

  • Most common "complication"
  • Temporary; treatable with lubricants/anti-inflammatories

Cosmetic Concern

  • Visible yellowish bump
  • Some patients bothered by appearance

Progression to Pterygium (Debated)

  • Some believe pinguecula can evolve into pterygium (growth onto cornea)
  • Others view them as separate entities
  • Both share UV/environmental risk factors

Rarely Interferes with Contact Lenses

  • If large, may affect lens fit

Prevention

UV Protection (Key)

  • Sunglasses: 100% UV-blocking sunglasses
    • Wraparound styles better (block side UV)
    • Wear outdoors, even on cloudy days
  • Wide-brimmed hat: Additional sun protection

Protect from Wind and Dust

  • Protective eyewear in dusty, windy environments
  • Safety glasses for outdoor work

Lubrication

  • Artificial tears if dry eyes
  • Keeps conjunctiva healthy

Avoid Eye Rubbing

  • Mechanical trauma may worsen

When to See a Doctor

Routine Eye Exam

  • Pinguecula often noted during routine exam
  • No urgency

See Doctor If:

  • Redness, irritation (pingueculitis; treatable)
  • Growth seems to be enlarging or changing
  • Vision affected (unlikely from pinguecula; may be other issue)
  • Cosmetic concerns (discuss removal options)
  • Bump appears to grow onto cornea (may be pterygium, not pinguecula)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this a pinguecula or pterygium?
  • Do I need treatment?
  • Will it grow or affect my vision?
  • Should I use lubricating drops?
  • Can it be removed if it bothers me?
  • How can I prevent it from getting worse?

Bottom Line

A pinguecula is a common, benign yellowish bump on the white of the eye caused by chronic UV exposure, wind, and aging. It's extremely common in adults, especially over age 40, and is usually asymptomatic and harmless, requiring no treatment. Pingueculae stay on the conjunctiva (white part) and do NOT grow onto the cornea or affect vision (unlike pterygium). If a pinguecula becomes inflamed (pingueculitis), causing redness and irritation, lubricating eye drops or anti-inflammatory drops can help. Surgical removal is rarely needed and reserved for cosmetic concerns or persistent symptoms. Prevention focuses on UV protection—wear 100% UV-blocking sunglasses outdoors to prevent new pingueculae and slow progression of existing ones.
