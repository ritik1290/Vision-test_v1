What Is Retinal Artery Occlusion?

Retinal artery occlusion (RAO) is a blockage of an artery supplying blood to the retina, causing sudden, painless, severe vision loss—often described as an "eye stroke." Just as a brain stroke results from blocked blood flow to the brain, retinal artery occlusion results from blocked blood flow to the retina, leading to retinal ischemia (oxygen deprivation) and permanent damage if not treated immediately. RAO is an ophthalmologic emergency with an extremely narrow treatment window (minutes to hours) and often results in permanent,devastating vision loss despite treatment attempts.

The most common type is central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO), in which the main central retinal artery is blocked, affecting the entire retina and causing sudden loss of most or all vision in the affected eye. RAO is typically caused by an embolus (blood clot or cholesterol plaque) that travels from the heart or carotid arteries and lodges in the retinal artery. Risk factors mirror those for stroke: carotid artery disease, atrial fibrillation, hypertension, diabetes, and smoking. Unfortunately, treatment options are limited and often ineffective, emphasizing the critical importance of prevention through cardiovascular risk factor management. Patients with RAO require urgent systemic workup to identify and treat the embolic source to prevent future strokes.

Key Facts About Retinal Artery Occlusion

  • Ophthalmologic emergency: "Eye stroke"—sudden severe vision loss.
  • Blocked retinal artery: Usually from embolus (clot or plaque).
  • Painless vision loss: Sudden, profound, one eye.
  • Cherry-red spot on exam: Classic finding in CRAO.
  • Risk factors: carotid disease, atrial fibrillation, hypertension
  • Treatment window: minutes to hours—permanent damage occurs quickly.
  • Poor prognosis: Most have permanent severe vision loss.
  • Warning sign for stroke: High risk of future brain stroke.

Understanding RAO emphasizes the urgency of immediate evaluation and the importance of preventing embolic events.

Types of Retinal Artery Occlusion

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

  • Blockage of main central retinal artery
  • Affects entire retina
  • Vision loss: Sudden, severe (counting fingers to light perception typically)
  • Most devastating type
  • ~90% have permanent vision loss

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)

  • Blockage of tributary branch artery
  • Affects part of retina
  • Vision loss: Sectoral visual field defect
  • Better prognosis than CRAO (since part of retina still perfused)
  • Central vision may be spared if macula unaffected

Cilioretinal Artery Sparing

  • ~15-30% of people have cilioretinal artery (separate arterial supply to macula from choroid)
  • If present in CRAO, macula may be spared → better central vision despite CRAO

Causes and Risk Factors

Mechanism: Embolic Occlusion (Most Common)

  • Embolus (traveling clot/plaque) lodges in retinal artery
  • Sources of emboli:
    • Carotid artery disease: Atherosclerotic plaque from ipsilateral (same-side) carotid artery breaks off
    • Cardiac source: Atrial fibrillation, valvular disease (endocarditis), mural thrombus (post-MI)
    • Cholesterol emboli (Hollenhorst plaques): Visible on exam; yellow, refractile deposits at arterial bifurcations

Other Causes (Less Common)

  • Giant cell arteritis (GCA/Temporal arteritis): Inflammatory vasculitis; medical emergency; can cause CRAO; more common in >50 years; associated symptoms: headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, elevated ESR/CRP
  • Vasospasm
  • Hypercoagulable states (rare)
  • Retinal artery vasculitis

Risk Factors

  • Carotid artery stenosis/atherosclerosis
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking
  • Cardiovascular disease, previous stroke/TIA
  • Age >60

Recognizing the Symptoms

Sudden, Severe, Painless Vision Loss

Abrupt onset; vision goes dark or severely blurred in seconds to minutes; one eye.

"Curtain" or "Shade" Coming Down

Some describe vision loss as dark curtain descending.

No Pain

Completely painless (unlike acute angle-closure glaucoma).

Severity

Vision typically counting fingers, hand motion, or light perception only (CRAO).

Visual Field Defect (BRAO)

Sectoral loss (e.g., superior or inferior field cut) if branch artery.

Amaurosis Fugax (Preceding)

Some patients have prior episodes of transient monocular vision loss (lasting minutes)—warning sign.

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination (EMERGENT)

Visual Acuity

  • Severely reduced (typically counting fingers or worse in CRAO)

Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)

  • Swinging flashlight test: affected pupil dilates when light swung to it (Marcus Gunn pupil)
  • Indicates optic nerve/retinal dysfunction

Dilated Fundus Examination - Diagnostic

  • Classic CRAO findings:
    • Cherry-red spot: Fovea appears bright red against pale, ischemic retina
      • Mechanism: fovea thin, choroidal blood shows through; surrounding ischemic retina pale/whitish
      • Pathognomonic for CRAO
    • Retinal whitening/edema: Diffuse pallor, cloudy retina from ischemia
    • Attenuated (narrowed) retinal arteries
    • Boxcarring: Segmentation of blood column in arteries (stagnant flow)
    • Possible visible embolus (Hollenhorst plaque—cholesterol; or white platelet-fibrin plug)

Imaging

  • Fluorescein angiography: Delayed or absent retinal arterial filling; confirms diagnosis
  • OCT: Shows retinal thickening/edema acutely; later inner retinal atrophy

Urgent Systemic Workup (To Find Embolic Source & Prevent Stroke)

  • ESR, CRP: Rule out giant cell arteritis (GCA) urgently—if elevated, start steroids immediately
  • Carotid ultrasound or CTA: Assess carotid artery stenosis
  • Echocardiogram: Detect cardiac source of emboli (atrial fibrillation, valvular disease, mural thrombus)
  • ECG: Atrial fibrillation?
  • Blood pressure, glucose, lipid panel
  • Hypercoagulability workup (if young or unexplained)
  • Neurology/stroke team consultation often warranted

Emergency Treatment

Critical Time Window

  • Retina tolerates ischemia ~90-100 minutes before irreversible damage
  • Treatment must begin ASAP—ideally within minutes to hours
  • Unfortunately, most patients present too late for effective treatment

Treatment Goals

  • Dislodge embolus, restore blood flow
  • Caveat: No proven consistently effective treatment; most interventions have limited success

Immediate Maneuvers (First Aid, Limited Efficacy)

Ocular Massage

  • Firm, intermittent pressure on closed eyelid (press for 5 sec, release, repeat)
  • Goal: Dislodge embolus by changing IOP
  • May help in some cases if done immediately

IOP Reduction (Anterior Chamber Paracentesis)

  • Remove small amount of aqueous from anterior chamber → lower IOP → improve retinal perfusion pressure
  • Invasive; done by ophthalmologist
  • Variable success

Carbogen Inhalation (95% O₂ + 5% CO₂)

  • Increases retinal oxygenation and vasodilation
  • Not widely available

Medications

  • Sublingual isosorbide dinitrate: Vasodilator
  • IV acetazolamide: Lower IOP
  • Limited proven efficacy

Intra-Arterial Thrombolysis (Investigational)

  • Catheter-directed tPA (clot-busting drug) into ophthalmic artery
  • Requires interventional radiology
  • Must be done within ~6 hours
  • Some case series show benefit, but not standard of care; risks (hemorrhage)

Giant Cell Arteritis Treatment (If Suspected)

  • High-dose IV corticosteroids immediately (methylprednisolone 1 g/day × 3 days)
  • Prevent fellow eye involvement and systemic complications
  • Do NOT delay for biopsy confirmation

Realistic Expectation

  • Despite treatment attempts, most CRAO patients have permanent severe vision loss
  • BRAO has better outcomes (partial vision preservation)

Prognosis and Outcomes

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

  • Very poor visual prognosis
  • ~80-90% have final vision of counting fingers or worse
  • Only ~10-20% regain useful vision (those with cilioretinal artery sparing or very early treatment)
  • Some spontaneous improvement possible if embolus dislodges, but rare

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)

  • Better prognosis than CRAO
  • If macula spared, central vision may be preserved
  • Permanent visual field defect in affected sector

Stroke Risk

  • RAO is warning sign for stroke
  • Patients with RAO (especially embolic) have significantly increased risk of future brain stroke (10-15% within 3 years)
  • Urgent systemic workup and stroke prevention critical

Prevention

Cardiovascular Risk Factor Management

  • Control hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking cessation
  • Treat atrial fibrillation: Anticoagulation if indicated
  • Carotid artery disease: Carotid endarterectomy or stenting if severe stenosis
  • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin, clopidogrel) if atherosclerotic disease

Amaurosis Fugax (Transient Monocular Vision Loss) - Warning Sign

  • If experiencing brief episodes of vision loss (minutes), see doctor urgently
  • Indicates emboli passing through retinal circulation—high risk for permanent RAO or stroke
  • Workup and treatment can prevent RAO/stroke

When to See a Doctor

EMERGENCY - Call 911 or Go to ER Immediately If:

  • Sudden, painless, severe vision loss in one eye
  • Any sudden vision change
  • Time is critical—treat as stroke-equivalent emergency

Urgent Evaluation If:

  • Transient vision loss episodes (amaurosis fugax)—see doctor same day

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have retinal artery occlusion? CRAO or BRAO?
  • What caused it?
  • What is my prognosis for vision?
  • Am I at risk for stroke?
  • What tests do I need (carotid ultrasound, echocardiogram)?
  • Should I be on blood thinners or other medications?

Bottom Line

Retinal artery occlusion is a blockage of the retinal artery causing sudden, painless, severe vision loss—an "eye stroke" and ophthalmologic emergency. Central RAO (CRAO) blocks the main artery affecting the entire retina with devastating vision loss (most end up counting fingers or worse), while branch RAO affects part of the retina with better prognosis. It's usually caused by an embolus from the carotid arteries or heart (atrial fibrillation, valvular disease). The classic finding is a cherry-red spot on fundus exam. The retina tolerates ischemia for only ~90 minutes before irreversible damage, so treatment must be immediate, but unfortunately no consistently effective treatment exists and most patients have permanent severe vision loss despite interventions. RAO is a warning sign for stroke—patients need urgent systemic workup (carotid ultrasound, echocardiogram, ESR/CRP) and stroke prevention (antiplatelet/anticoagulation, carotid surgery). Prevention through cardiovascular risk factor control is critical. If you experience sudden vision loss, call 911 or go to the ER immediately—every minute counts.
