What Is an Epiretinal Membrane?

An epiretinal membrane (ERM), also called macular pucker, cellophane maculopathy, or surface wrinkling retinopathy, is a thin, semi-transparent sheet of scar tissue that forms on the surface of the retina, typically over the macula (central retina). Imagine a piece of cellophane or cling wrap lying on the retina—as it contracts and wrinkles, it causes the underlying retina to wrinkle as well, distorting vision.

ERMs are common in older adults, affecting approximately 7% of the population over age 50 and up to 20% of those over 75. Most cases are mild and cause minimal symptoms ("cellophane maculopathy"), while others progress to more significant visual impairment. Unlike macular holes, ERMs are usually less vision-threatening, but they can significantly affect quality of life by causing distorted or blurred central vision.

Key Facts About Epiretinal Membranes

  • Common in aging: Affects about 7-20% of people over 50.
  • Scar tissue on retina: Thin, transparent membrane contracts and wrinkles retinal surface.
  • Most are mild: Many people have minimal symptoms and don't need treatment.
  • Causes distortion: Wavy, distorted vision is the hallmark symptom.
  • Usually one eye: Unilateral in most cases (can be bilateral)
  • Not immediately vision-threatening: Usually progresses slowly; rarely causes blindness.
  • Surgery effective: Membrane peeling via vitrectomy improves vision in most cases.
  • Often idiopathic: No clear cause in about half of cases.

Understanding ERMs helps patients make informed decisions about monitoring versus treatment based on symptom severity.

Causes and Risk Factors

How ERMs Form

ERMs develop when cells migrate onto the retinal surface and proliferate:

  • Cell types involved: Glial cells, retinal pigment epithelium cells, fibrocytes, macrophages
  • Cells deposit collagen: Form sheet-like scar tissue
  • Membrane contracts: As tissue matures, it shrinks and wrinkles
  • Retina wrinkles: Underlying retina pulled and distorted
  • Vision affected: Wrinkled retina can't process images normally

Idiopathic ERM (Primary/No Obvious Cause)

About 50% of cases:

  • Associated with posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)
  • Vitreous separation may allow cells to access retinal surface
  • Break in internal limiting membrane (retina's inner layer) permits cell migration
  • Age-related changes promote cell proliferation
  • Exact trigger unknown

Secondary ERM (From Other Eye Conditions)

About 50% have identifiable cause:

  • Retinal tears or detachment: Repair process triggers membrane formation
  • Diabetic retinopathy: Abnormal blood vessels stimulate scar tissue
  • Retinal vein occlusion: Vascular blockage
  • Uveitis: Inflammation
  • Eye trauma
  • Previous eye surgery: Cataract, retinal, or laser surgery
  • Retinal tears (even if repaired with laser)

Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases with age, especially over 50
  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)
  • Previous retinal problems: Tears, detachment
  • Diabetic retinopathy
  • Retinal vein occlusion
  • Uveitis or other inflammatory eye diseases
  • Eye trauma or surgery

Demographics

  • More common in women (slight prevalence)
  • Rare before age 40 (unless secondary cause)
  • Both eyes can be affected but usually at different times

Recognizing the Symptoms

Common Symptoms

Distorted Vision (Metamorphopsia)

Most characteristic symptom: straight lines appear wavy, bent, curved, or irregular. Grid patterns distorted.

Blurred Central Vision

Central vision becomes hazy or less clear; difficulty with fine details like reading or recognizing faces.

Image Size Difference (Micropsia)

Affected eye may see objects as smaller than the other eye; images don't match in size between eyes.

Double Vision (Monocular)

Ghost images or doubling in the affected eye alone (not true double vision from eye misalignment).

Reading Difficulty

Letters appear crooked or wavy; lines of text distorted; harder to focus on words.

No Pain

ERMs are painless; no redness, light sensitivity, or discomfort—only vision changes.

Symptom Variations by Severity

  • Mild ERM (cellophane maculopathy):
    • May be asymptomatic or cause minimal symptoms
    • Slight distortion only noticed on Amsler grid testing
    • Vision often 20/40 or better
    • Function not significantly affected
  • Moderate ERM:
    • Noticeable distortion of lines
    • Mild to moderate blur
    • Vision typically 20/50 to 20/100
    • Some functional impairment
  • Severe ERM (macular pucker):
    • Significant distortion and blur
    • Vision worse than 20/100
    • Major impact on reading, driving, daily tasks
    • Retina visibly wrinkled on exam

What ERMs Usually Don't Cause

  • Sudden vision loss (onset is gradual)
  • Complete blindness (peripheral vision remains)
  • Flashes or floaters (unless associated with PVD)
  • Pain or eye redness
  • Curtain or shadow in vision (that's retinal detachment)

Amsler Grid Test

  • Simple grid shows distortion clearly
  • Lines appear wavy, broken, or irregular
  • Useful for monitoring at home
  • Report changes to eye doctor

Diagnosis

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Visual Acuity Testing

  • Measures degree of vision loss
  • Range from 20/20 (mild ERMs) to 20/200+ (severe)
  • Helps decide if treatment needed

Amsler Grid Test

  • Quantifies distortion
  • Patient marks areas of distortion on grid
  • Tracks progression over time

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Direct visualization of retina
  • Mild ERM: Appears as cellophane-like sheen or glistening on macula
  • Moderate ERM: Visible membrane with mild retinal wrinkling
  • Severe ERM: Obvious thick membrane with pronounced retinal folds/striae
  • Assess for any associated conditions (tears, edema)

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Key Test

Essential for diagnosis and treatment planning:

  • Cross-sectional, high-resolution imaging of retinal layers
  • Confirms ERM presence: Membrane visible as hyperreflective line on retinal surface
  • Assesses severity: Degree of retinal thickening and distortion
  • Detects complications: Macular edema, pseudohole, cysts
  • Measures retinal thickness
  • Guides surgery: Helps predict surgical outcomes
  • Non-invasive, quick scan

Fluorescein Angiography (Sometimes)

  • Dye injected to evaluate retinal blood vessels
  • Used if vascular cause suspected (diabetic retinopathy, vein occlusion)
  • Not routinely needed for idiopathic ERM

Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that can appear similar:

  • Macular hole: OCT distinguishes (hole vs. membrane)
  • Vitreomacular traction: Vitreous pulling but no membrane
  • Macular edema: Fluid swelling (can coexist with ERM)
  • Age-related macular degeneration: Drusen, different appearance

Natural History and Progression

Typical Course

  • Initially mild: Often asymptomatic when first detected
  • Slow progression: Symptoms develop gradually over months to years
  • Variable outcomes:
    • Some remain stable with minimal symptoms indefinitely
    • Some progress to more significant distortion and vision loss
    • Rarely progress rapidly
  • Spontaneous improvement rare: Unlike stage 1 macular holes, ERMs rarely resolve on their own

Monitoring

  • Regular eye exams every 6-12 months
  • OCT imaging to track changes
  • Vision testing
  • Amsler grid at home for patient self-monitoring

When Treatment Becomes Necessary

Surgery typically considered if:

  • Vision worse than 20/50 and affecting daily life
  • Significant bothersome distortion
  • Difficulty reading or performing tasks
  • Progressive worsening
  • Patient's quality of life impaired

What If Observation Chosen?

  • Many patients with mild ERMs do well with observation
  • Brain adapts to some distortion over time
  • Surgery always an option if symptoms worsen
  • No harm in waiting if symptoms mild

Treatment Options

Observation (Mild Cases)

  • Best for: Minimal symptoms, good vision (20/40 or better), mild distortion
  • Regular monitoring
  • No medical treatment (eye drops, supplements) proven effective
  • Surgery deferred unless symptoms worsen

Vitrectomy with Membrane Peeling - Standard Surgical Treatment

For symptomatic cases:

When Surgery Recommended

  • Vision 20/50 or worse with symptoms affecting function
  • Significant distortion interfering with daily activities
  • Progressive vision decline
  • Patient desire for improvement

Surgical Procedure

  • Anesthesia: Local with sedation or general
  • Vitrectomy: Remove vitreous gel (relieves any vitreous traction)
  • Membrane peeling:
    • Surgeon grasps edge of ERM with micro-forceps
    • Carefully peels membrane off retinal surface
    • May stain membrane with dye to visualize better
  • Internal limiting membrane (ILM) peeling:
    • Often removed as well (innermost retinal layer)
    • Reduces recurrence risk
  • No gas bubble usually needed (unlike macular hole surgery)
  • Duration: 30-60 minutes
  • Outpatient surgery

Recovery

  • No face-down positioning required (major difference from macular hole surgery)
  • Eye drops (antibiotic, anti-inflammatory) for 2-4 weeks
  • Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous activity for 2 weeks
  • Vision may initially be worse due to surgical swelling
  • Gradual improvement over weeks to months

Alternative/Adjunct Treatment

  • Ocriplasmin injection (Jetrea): Enzyme to release vitreomacular adhesion; limited role for ERM; not standard treatment

Surgery in Detail: What to Expect

Before Surgery

  • Comprehensive eye exam and OCT
  • Medical clearance if needed
  • Stop certain medications (aspirin, blood thinners) as directed
  • Arrange transportation (can't drive day of surgery)

Day of Surgery

  • Outpatient procedure (same-day discharge)
  • Eye numbed with drops or injection
  • Sedation or general anesthesia
  • Procedure typically 30-60 minutes
  • Eye patch or shield placed

Post-Operative Recovery

Immediate (Days 1-7)

  • Eye may be red, irritated, sore
  • Vision blurry initially
  • Use prescribed eye drops
  • Wear eye shield at night
  • Avoid rubbing eye
  • No swimming or hot tubs

Short-Term (Weeks 1-4)

  • Vision gradually improves
  • Redness and irritation resolve
  • Continue eye drops as directed
  • Avoid strenuous exercise

Long-Term (Months 1-6)

  • Continued vision improvement
  • Distortion reduces gradually
  • Full visual recovery may take 3-6 months
  • Final outcomes variable (see Prognosis)

Potential Complications

  • Cataract formation: Very common (50-80%) within 1-2 years, especially if over 60; treatable with cataract surgery
  • Retinal detachment: Uncommon (1-2%)
  • ERM recurrence: 5-10%; may need repeat surgery
  • Macular edema: Swelling; usually temporary
  • Infection (endophthalmitis): Rare (<0.1%)< /li>
  • Bleeding
  • Elevated eye pressure
  • No improvement or worsening vision: Uncommon but possible

Prognosis and Visual Outcomes

Surgical Success Rates

  • Vision improvement: 70-90% of patients experience meaningful improvement
  • Average gain: 2-3 lines on eye chart (e.g., from 20/80 to 20/40)
  • Distortion reduction: Most patients report significant reduction, though some distortion may persist
  • Restoration to normal: Perfect vision uncommon; realistic goal is functional improvement

Factors Affecting Outcomes

Better Prognosis:

  • Shorter duration before surgery (<1 year)
  • Better pre-operative vision
  • Younger age
  • Thinner membrane on OCT
  • No associated macular edema or other pathology
  • Idiopathic (primary) ERM vs. secondary

Poorer Prognosis:

  • Long-standing ERM (>2-3 years)
  • Very poor pre-op vision
  • Severe retinal damage visible on OCT
  • Associated diabetic retinopathy or other disease

Realistic Expectations

  • Goal: Functional improvement for daily activities
  • Most patients satisfied with outcome
  • Some residual distortion common
  • Perfect vision unlikely
  • Quality of life typically improves

If No Surgery

  • Many mild ERMs remain stable for years
  • Some progress slowly
  • Rarely lead to severe vision loss or blindness
  • Observation reasonable if symptoms mild

Living with Epiretinal Membrane

Coping Strategies for Mild ERMs

  • Good lighting: Adequate illumination helps with reading
  • Magnification: Reading glasses, magnifiers, large-print books
  • Contrast enhancement: High-contrast text easier to read
  • Adapt activities: Take breaks, use good eye more
  • Low vision aids: If vision significantly impaired

Monitoring at Home

  • Use Amsler grid regularly (weekly or monthly)
  • Note any increase in distortion or blur
  • Report changes to eye doctor
  • Keep regular eye exam appointments

Driving

  • Legal if vision meets minimum standards (varies by location; typically 20/40 or better)
  • Distortion may affect confidence even if acuity adequate
  • Good eye often sufficient for safe driving
  • Discuss with eye doctor if concerned

When to Consider Surgery

  • Symptoms interfering with desired activities
  • Reading, driving, hobbies significantly impaired
  • Quality of life affected
  • Progressive worsening
  • Personal decision; no urgent rush unless rapidly progressing

When to See a Doctor

Seek Prompt Evaluation If:

  • New distortion or wavy vision
  • Blurred central vision
  • Difficulty reading
  • Lines appear bent or irregular
  • Worsening of existing symptoms

While not a true emergency, these symptoms warrant evaluation within days to weeks to diagnose the cause and plan management.

Regular Monitoring If Diagnosed

  • Follow-up exams every 6-12 months
  • OCT imaging to track progression
  • More frequent visits if symptoms changing

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • How severe is my epiretinal membrane?
  • What is my current vision level?
  • Is there any associated retinal damage?
  • Do I need treatment now, or can we observe?
  • What are the risks and benefits of surgery?
  • What vision improvement can I expect from surgery?
  • How long is the recovery?
  • Will I develop a cataract after surgery?
  • How often should I have follow-up exams?
  • Can this happen in my other eye?

Bottom Line

Epiretinal membranes are common age-related retinal changes that cause distorted and blurred central vision. Many cases are mild and can be observed, but when symptoms significantly affect daily life, vitrectomy with membrane peeling is highly effective (70-90% improve). Unlike macular hole surgery, ERM surgery doesn't require face-down positioning, making recovery easier. If you notice new distortion or wavy vision, seek evaluation—with proper diagnosis and timely treatment when needed, most patients maintain good functional vision.
