What Is Ectropion?

Ectropion is a condition in which the eyelid (usually the lower lid) turns outward, causing the inner surface of the eyelid to be exposed. Normally, the eyelid margins sit flush against the eyeball, protecting the eye and directing tears to the tear drainage system. When the lid everts (turns out), this protective function is lost, leading to excessive tearing, irritation, and potential damage to the cornea and conjunctiva from chronic exposure.

Ectropion most commonly affects the elderly due to age-related weakening of the eyelid tissues and muscles. The lower eyelid is affected far more often than the upper lid. While ectropion can cause significant discomfort and cosmetic concerns, it is highly treatable with surgical repair, which typically has excellent outcomes. Left untreated, chronic ectropion can lead to corneal damage and vision problems.

Key Facts About Ectropion

  • Outward-turning eyelid: Lower eyelid margin rolls away from eye.
  • Common in elderly: Age-related tissue laxity is the most common cause.
  • Excessive tearing (epiphora): Paradoxical—tears can't drain properly, overflow onto cheek.
  • Eye irritation and dryness: Exposed eye surface dries out; conjunctiva becomes red and inflamed.
  • Corneal exposure risk: Can lead to keratitis or corneal ulcer if severe.
  • Surgical correction effective: Eyelid tightening procedures restore normal position.
  • Usually lower lid: Lower eyelid affected >90% of cases.
  • Cosmetically bothersome: Visible deformity; red, drooping lid.

Understanding ectropion helps recognize when surgical correction is needed to prevent complications.

Types and Causes

Involutional (Age-Related) Ectropion - Most Common (~80%)

  • Caused by aging changes:
    • Horizontal laxity of eyelid (loose, saggy)
    • Weakening of lateral canthal tendon (corner of eyelid attachment)
    • Atrophy of orbital fat (eye sinks into orbit, pulling lid)
    • Overriding of preseptal orbicularis muscle
  • Gravity pulls loose lower lid down and out
  • Typically develops in people >60 years old
  • Most common type

Paralytic Ectropion

  • Caused by facial nerve palsy (7th cranial nerve)
  • Bell's palsy: Temporary facial paralysis
  • Stroke, tumor, trauma: Permanent paralysis
  • Orbicularis oculi muscle (eyelid closure muscle) paralyzed
  • Lid tone lost; lower lid sags away from eye
  • Often accompanied by lagophthalmos (incomplete lid closure)
  • Severe corneal exposure risk

Cicatricial Ectropion

  • Scarring of eyelid skin pulls lid margin outward
  • Causes:
    • Chemical or thermal burns
    • Trauma with scar formation
    • Skin cancers (removed, leaving scar)
    • Inflammatory skin diseases (severe eczema, rosacea)
    • Previous eyelid surgery (blepharoplasty complication)
  • Vertical skin shortage
  • Contracture pulls lid down

Mechanical Ectropion

  • Mass or growth weighing down lid
  • Causes:
    • Eyelid tumors (benign or malignant)
    • Edema (severe swelling from allergic reaction, infection, thyroid eye disease)
    • Herniated orbital fat

Congenital Ectropion

  • Present at birth; very rare
  • Associated with genetic syndromes (Down syndrome, blepharophimosis syndrome)
  • Eyelid malformation

Recognizing the Symptoms

Excessive Tearing (Epiphora)

Tears overflow onto cheek; paradoxical—eyelid malposition prevents tear drainage.

Eye Irritation and Redness

Gritty, burning sensation; exposed conjunctiva becomes red, inflamed, thickened.

Dryness

Despite tearing, eye feels dry; tear film not distributed properly; exposure keratitis.

Visible Lid Deformity

Lower eyelid margin visibly turned outward; red inner surface exposed.

Light Sensitivity

Photophobia from exposed, irritated eye surface.

Blurred Vision

If corneal damage develops; mucus discharge can blur vision.

Tearing (Epiphora) Mechanism

  • Punctum (tear drainage opening) normally sits against eyeball
  • Ectropion causes punctum to roll outward, away from eye
  • Tears cannot drain into lacrimal system
  • Tears overflow onto cheek (constant wetness, skin maceration)

Conjunctival Changes

  • Exposed palpebral conjunctiva (inside of eyelid) becomes:
    • Red and inflamed (chronic irritation)
    • Thickened and keratinized (skin-like from chronic exposure)
    • May develop granulation tissue

Corneal Exposure

  • Lower part of cornea exposed due to poor lid apposition
  • Can develop:
    • Exposure keratopathy (corneal dryness and damage)
    • Punctate epithelial erosions
    • Corneal ulcer (if severe/untreated)

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

History

  • Duration of symptoms?
  • Tearing, irritation?
  • Previous trauma, burns, surgery?
  • Facial weakness or paralysis?
  • Skin conditions?

External Inspection

  • Visible outward turning of lower eyelid margin
  • Red, exposed palpebral conjunctiva
  • Eyelid laxity assessment:
    • Snap-back test: Pull lower lid away from eye, release; should snap back immediately (delayed = laxity)
    • Distraction test: Pull lid anteriorly; >6-8mm movement indicates laxity
    • Medial/lateral canthal tendon laxity
  • Assess for masses, scars, skin shortage

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Evaluate cornea for exposure keratopathy:
    • Fluorescein staining shows epithelial defects
    • Lower corneal haze or scarring
  • Conjunctival inflammation, thickening
  • Tear film quality

Facial Nerve Function

  • If paralytic ectropion suspected, test facial nerve:
    • Can patient close eyes tightly?
    • Smile, raise eyebrows?
  • Refer to neurology if acute facial paralysis

Determining Type

  • Involutional: Elderly, horizontal laxity, no other cause
  • Paralytic: Facial weakness, lagophthalmos
  • Cicatricial: History of trauma/burns/surgery; visible scar, skin shortage
  • Mechanical: Mass or swelling visible

Complications

Exposure Keratopathy and Corneal Ulcer

  • Chronic exposure damages cornea
  • Can progress to ulcer and perforation if severe
  • Vision-threatening

Chronic Conjunctivitis

  • Repeated infections
  • Thickened, keratinized conjunctiva

Eyelid Skin Changes

  • Maceration (skin breakdown) from constant wetness
  • Eczematous dermatitis

Cosmetic Disfigurement

  • Red, drooping eyelid
  • Social embarrassment

Treatment

Conservative Management (Temporary/Mild Cases)

Lubricant Eye Drops and Ointments

  • Artificial tears: Frequent during day to protect exposed eye
  • Lubricant ointment: At bedtime; thicker, lasts longer
  • Provides temporary relief but doesn't correct lid position

Taping

  • Surgical tape to pull lower lid upward (temporary measure)
  • Useful for acute paralytic ectropion while awaiting nerve recovery

Treatment of Underlying Cause

  • Paralytic: Treat underlying condition (Bell's palsy management, etc.)
  • Mechanical: Remove tumor or treat edema
  • Cicatricial: Skin grafting may be needed

Definitive Treatment: Surgery

Most effective and long-lasting treatment for symptomatic ectropion.

Surgical Repair

Indications for Surgery

  • Symptomatic ectropion (tearing, irritation)
  • Corneal exposure
  • Cosmetic concerns
  • Failed conservative management

Common Surgical Procedures

Lateral Tarsal Strip Procedure (Most Common for Involutional Ectropion)

  • Technique:
    • Shorten horizontal eyelid length
    • Create "tarsal strip" (piece of lid tissue)
    • Reattach to inside of lateral orbital rim
    • Tightens lid horizontally, pulls lid up against eye
  • Advantages: Effective, reproducible, relatively simple
  • Success rate: >90%

Medial Spindle Procedure

  • For medial (inner corner) ectropion
  • Remove diamond-shaped piece of conjunctiva and lid retractors
  • Tightens medial eyelid

Full-Thickness Skin Graft (For Cicatricial Ectropion)

  • Release scar contracture
  • Graft skin (usually from behind ear or upper eyelid) to replace shortage
  • Allows lid to return to normal position

Quickert Sutures

  • Everting sutures to rotate lid margin inward (also used for entropion)
  • Temporary fix; lower long-term success

Anesthesia

  • Local anesthesia with sedation (most common)
  • General anesthesia for complex cases or patient preference
  • Outpatient procedure

Procedure Details

  • Duration: 30-60 minutes per eyelid
  • Incision: Along lateral eyelid or within natural crease
  • Sutures: Absorbable internal, may have external sutures removed in 1 week

Post-Operative Care

Immediate (Days 1-7)

  • Ice packs to reduce swelling
  • Antibiotic ointment to incision
  • Elevate head when sleeping
  • Avoid strenuous activity, heavy lifting
  • Bruising and swelling expected

Recovery (Weeks 1-4)

  • Swelling, bruising gradually resolve
  • Sutures removed if non-absorbable (1 week)
  • Resume normal activities after 1-2 weeks
  • Final cosmetic result may take weeks to months

Risks and Complications of Surgery

  • Infection: Rare
  • Bleeding, hematoma
  • Scarring
  • Undercorrection: Ectropion persists (may need revision)
  • Overcorrection: Eyelid pulled too tight; entropion (inward turning) may result
  • Recurrence: ~5-10% over years
  • Asymmetry

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Surgical Correction

  • Excellent outcomes: >90% success rate
  • Symptom resolution: Tearing and irritation typically resolve
  • Corneal health: Exposure issues improve
  • Cosmesis: Eyelid appearance normalizes
  • Recurrence rate: Low (5-10%) but can occur with aging

Without Treatment

  • Progressive worsening likely
  • Chronic symptoms (tearing, irritation)
  • Risk of corneal damage
  • Decreased quality of life

Paralytic Ectropion

  • If facial nerve recovers (e.g., Bell's palsy), ectropion may resolve
  • If permanent paralysis, surgery effective

When to See a Doctor

Consult Ophthalmologist or Oculoplastic Surgeon If:

  • Eyelid turning outward
  • Excessive tearing
  • Eye irritation, redness
  • Concern about eye appearance
  • Acute facial weakness (may indicate stroke or Bell's palsy—seek urgent care)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of ectropion do I have?
  • What is causing it?
  • Do I need surgery?
  • What are the risks of surgery?
  • What is the success rate?
  • How long is recovery?
  • Will my insurance cover this?

Bottom Line

Ectropion is an outward turning of the eyelid, most commonly the lower lid, usually caused by age-related tissue laxity in the elderly. It causes excessive tearing (paradoxically, due to poor tear drainage), eye irritation, and exposure-related corneal damage. While lubricating drops provide temporary relief, surgical repair—typically a lateral tarsal strip procedure to tighten the eyelid—is the definitive treatment with excellent outcomes (>90% success rate). If you're experiencing a drooping, outward-turning eyelid with tearing and irritation, see an ophthalmologist for evaluation. Surgery is relatively straightforward, low-risk, and highly effective at restoring comfort and protecting the eye.
