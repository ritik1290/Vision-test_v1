What Is Conjunctivitis?

Conjunctivitis, commonly known as "pink eye," is inflammation or infection of the conjunctiva—the thin, transparent membrane that lines the inside of the eyelid and covers the white part of the eye. When the tiny blood vessels in the conjunctiva become inflamed, they're more visible, giving the eye a pink or reddish appearance, hence the name "pink eye."

Conjunctivitis is one of the most common eye conditions, particularly in children, but it can affect people of all ages. It's usually a minor eye infection or irritation, but it can be uncomfortable and, in some cases, highly contagious. While most cases resolve on their own or with simple treatment, some forms require medical attention to prevent complications.

Key Facts About Conjunctivitis

  • Very common: Millions of cases occur each year in the United States alone.
  • Usually mild: Most cases are self-limiting and resolve within 1-2 weeks.
  • Three main types: Viral, bacterial, and allergic—each requires different treatment.
  • Can be highly contagious: Viral and bacterial types spread easily through contact.
  • Rarely serious: In most healthy people, pink eye doesn't cause long-term problems.
  • Both eyes can be affected: Infection often spreads from one eye to the other.
  • Affects all ages: Especially common in children, daycare settings, and schools.

Understanding the type of conjunctivitis you have is important because treatment varies. While some cases clear up on their own, others may need antibiotics or other medications to prevent complications and reduce spread to others.

Understanding the Conjunctiva

To understand conjunctivitis, it helps to know about the conjunctiva:

What Is the Conjunctiva?

The conjunctiva is a thin, clear mucous membrane with two parts:

  • Palpebral conjunctiva: Lines the inside of the eyelids
  • Bulbar conjunctiva: Covers the white part of the eye (sclera)

Functions of the Conjunctiva

  • Protection: Acts as a barrier against dirt, debris, and microorganisms
  • Lubrication: Produces mucus that helps spread tears evenly
  • Immune defense: Contains cells that fight infection
  • Smooth surface: Allows eyelids to glide smoothly over the eye

What Happens in Conjunctivitis

When the conjunctiva becomes inflamed due to infection, allergy, or irritation:

  • Blood vessels dilate and become more visible (redness)
  • The membrane may swell
  • Increased tear production and discharge occur
  • Discomfort, itching, or irritation develops

Types of Conjunctivitis

Conjunctivitis is classified into several types based on the cause:

1. Viral Conjunctivitis (Most Common)

Accounts for about 80% of all acute conjunctivitis cases.

Characteristics:

  • Cause: Viruses, most commonly adenovirus (same virus family that causes colds)
  • Highly contagious: Spreads easily through contact
  • Watery discharge: Clear, watery secretions (not thick or colored)
  • Usually affects one eye first, then spreads to the other within days
  • Often accompanies upper respiratory infection (cold, sore throat)
  • Self-limiting: Resolves on its own in 1-3 weeks
  • No antibiotics needed: Antibiotics don't work on viruses

Subtypes:

  • Epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (EKC): Highly contagious adenovirus type; can affect cornea
  • Pharyngoconjunctival fever: Adenovirus causing conjunctivitis, sore throat, and fever
  • Herpes simplex conjunctivitis: Caused by herpes virus; can be serious if it spreads to cornea

2. Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Less common than viral, but more likely to need treatment.

Characteristics:

  • Cause: Bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae
  • Thick, yellow-green discharge: Especially noticeable upon waking (eyes "glued shut")
  • Can affect one or both eyes
  • Contagious: But less so than viral
  • Responds to antibiotic drops: Usually clears within 3-5 days with treatment
  • Can resolve without treatment in 2-5 days, but antibiotics speed recovery

Special Cases:

  • Hyperacute bacterial conjunctivitis: Rare, severe form (Neisseria gonorrhoeae); medical emergency
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis: In newborns from birth canal; prevented with eye drops at birth
  • Chlamydial conjunctivitis: Sexually transmitted; needs systemic antibiotics

3. Allergic Conjunctivitis

Immune system reaction to allergens; not contagious.

Characteristics:

  • Cause: Pollen, dust mites, pet dander, mold
  • Intense itching: Hallmark symptom
  • Affects both eyes simultaneously
  • Watery discharge
  • Often seasonal (spring/summer for pollen allergies)
  • Associated with other allergies: Hay fever, asthma, eczema
  • Not contagious

Subtypes:

  • Seasonal allergic conjunctivitis (SAC): Most common; occurs during pollen seasons
  • Perennial allergic conjunctivitis (PAC): Year-round from dust mites, pet dander
  • Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (VKC): Severe form in children; can affect cornea
  • Atopic keratoconjunctivitis (AKC): Associated with eczema; more severe
  • Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC): Related to contact lens wear or eye prosthetics

4. Irritant (Chemical) Conjunctivitis

Reaction to irritants; not infectious or contagious.

Common Causes:

  • Chlorine from swimming pools
  • Air pollution or smoke
  • Chemical splashes
  • Foreign objects in the eye
  • Cosmetics or eye drops

Characteristics:

  • Usually resolves when irritant is removed
  • Watery, irritated eyes
  • Can affect one or both eyes depending on exposure

Recognizing the Symptoms

Symptoms vary by type but often include:

Common Symptoms (All Types)

Pink or Red Eyes

Bloodshot appearance from inflamed blood vessels in the conjunctiva—the hallmark sign.

Increased Tearing

Excessive watery discharge or tearing, especially in viral and allergic types.

Burning or Gritty Feeling

Sensation of sand or foreign body in the eye, discomfort or irritation.

Itching

Intense itching is the primary symptom of allergic conjunctivitis.

Eye Discharge

Type varies: watery (viral/allergic), thick yellow-green (bacterial), or stringy (allergic).

Swollen Eyelids

Puffy eyelids, especially with bacterial or allergic conjunctivitis.

Light Sensitivity

Mild discomfort in bright light (photophobia), more common with viral types.

Crusty Eyelids

Eyelashes and lids crusted together, especially upon waking—common with bacterial type.

How to Tell the Types Apart

Symptom Viral Bacterial Allergic
Discharge Watery, clear Thick, yellow-green, purulent Watery or stringy mucus
Itching Mild Minimal Intense, primary symptom
Eyes Affected One first, then other One or both Both simultaneously
Associated Symptoms Cold, sore throat Ear infection (children) Sneezing, runny nose
Contagious Highly Moderately No
Duration 1-3 weeks Few days with treatment As long as exposed to allergen

When Symptoms Are More Serious

Warning Signs

Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:

  • Severe eye pain (more than mild discomfort)
  • Vision changes or blurred vision that doesn't clear with blinking
  • Sensitivity to light (photophobia) that's moderate to severe
  • Intense redness in one eye
  • Symptoms in a newborn (under 1 month)
  • Weakened immune system (HIV, cancer treatment, etc.)
  • Suspected chemical exposure

Causes and Risk Factors

Infectious Causes

Viruses (Most Common)

  • Adenoviruses: Most common cause (respiratory infections, colds)
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV): Can cause serious complications
  • Varicella-zoster virus (shingles): In people with facial shingles
  • Enteroviruses: Including those causing hand-foot-and-mouth disease
  • COVID-19: Coronavirus can rarely cause conjunctivitis

Bacteria

  • Staphylococcus aureus: Common skin bacteria
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae: Also causes pneumonia, ear infections
  • Haemophilus influenzae: Common in children
  • Neisseria gonorrhoeae: Sexually transmitted; very serious
  • Chlamydia trachomatis: Sexually transmitted

Non-Infectious Causes

Allergens

  • Pollen (trees, grass, weeds)
  • Dust mites
  • Pet dander (cats, dogs)
  • Mold spores

Irritants

  • Chlorine in swimming pools
  • Smoke (cigarette, wildfire)
  • Air pollution
  • Chemical fumes
  • Cosmetics or personal care products
  • Contact lens solutions or improperly cleaned lenses

Risk Factors

For Infectious Conjunctivitis:

  • Close contact with infected person: Schools, daycare, households
  • Poor hand hygiene: Touching eyes with contaminated hands
  • Contact lens wear: Especially with poor lens hygiene
  • Sharing items: Towels, pillowcases, eye makeup, contact lens equipment
  • Swimming in contaminated water
  • Weakened immune system

For Allergic Conjunctivitis:

  • Personal or family history of allergies
  • Asthma or hay fever
  • Eczema or atopic dermatitis
  • Environmental exposures

For Irritant Conjunctivitis:

  • Frequent swimming in chlorinated pools
  • Occupational exposures (chemicals, fumes)
  • Dry, smoky, or dusty environments

Diagnosis

Most cases of conjunctivitis can be diagnosed based on symptoms and appearance, but sometimes tests are needed:

Medical History

Your doctor will ask about:

  • When symptoms started
  • Type of discharge
  • Contact with sick individuals
  • Allergy history
  • Contact lens use
  • Recent exposures (chemicals, pool water)

Physical Examination

  • Visual inspection: Examines eye redness, discharge, swelling
  • Eyelid eversion: Checks inside of eyelids for papillae (bumps) or follicles
  • Vision testing: Ensures vision isn't affected
  • Slit lamp examination: Detailed examination to check for corneal involvement

Laboratory Tests (When Needed)

Not usually necessary but may be done if:

  • Culture of discharge: If bacterial cause suspected and not responding to treatment
  • Viral testing: For severe or unusual cases
  • Chlamydia/gonorrhea testing: If sexually transmitted infection suspected
  • Allergy testing: If chronic or severe allergic conjunctivitis

Fluorescein Staining

Orange dye applied to eye to check for:

  • Corneal involvement (ulcer or keratitis)
  • Foreign bodies
  • Abrasions

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the type of conjunctivitis:

Viral Conjunctivitis Treatment

No specific treatment; focus is on symptom relief and preventing spread:

Supportive Care:

  • Cool or warm compresses: Apply to closed eyes for comfort (5-10 minutes, several times daily)
  • Artificial tears: Lubricating drops soothe irritation (preservative-free if using frequently)
  • Avoid contact lenses until symptoms resolve
  • Pain relief: Over-the-counter pain relievers if needed

What NOT to Do:

  • Antibiotics don't work on viruses
  • Avoid steroid drops unless prescribed (can worsen herpes infections)

Special Cases:

  • Herpes simplex: Antiviral medication (trifluridine, acyclovir)
  • Severe epidemic keratoconjunctivitis: May need steroid drops under close supervision

Duration: Most viral conjunctivitis resolves in 1-3 weeks, though it may get worse in the first 3-5 days.

Bacterial Conjunctivitis Treatment

Antibiotic Eye Drops or Ointments:

Common antibiotics prescribed:

  • Erythromycin ointment: Often first choice, especially for children
  • Polymyxin B/trimethoprim drops (Polytrim)
  • Ciprofloxacin (Ciloxan): Fluoroquinolone, broad-spectrum
  • Ofloxacin (Ocuflox)
  • Moxifloxacin (Vigamox): Once-daily dosing
  • Azithromycin (AzaSite): Twice daily for 2 days, then once daily

Usage Instructions:

  • Apply as directed (typically 3-4 times daily for drops, 2-3 times for ointment)
  • Complete full course even if symptoms improve
  • Symptoms usually improve within 2-3 days
  • Washaway discharge before applying medication

Mild cases may resolve without antibiotics in 2-5 days, but treatment:

  • Speeds recovery
  • Reduces contagiousness faster
  • Prevents complications
  • Prevents spread to others

Allergic Conjunctivitis Treatment

Avoid Allergens:

  • Stay indoors when pollen counts are high
  • Use air conditioning, keep windows closed
  • Remove contact lenses during allergy season
  • Wash hands after petting animals
  • Use allergen-proof bedding covers

Cold Compresses:

  • Soothe itching and reduce swelling
  • Apply for 10-15 minutes several times daily

Medications:

Over-the-Counter Options:

  • Antihistamine eye drops: Ketotifen (Zaditor, Alaway)—reduces itching and redness
  • Decongestant drops: Naphazoline (Clear Eyes)—short-term use only (< 3 days)
  • Artificial tears: Rinse away allergens
  • Oral antihistamines: Cetirizine (Zyrtec), loratadine (Claritin) for systemic allergies

Prescription Options:

  • Antihistamine drops: Azelastine (Optivar), emedastine (Emadine)
  • Mast cell stabilizers: Lodoxamide (Alomide), cromolyn—prevent allergic reaction
  • Dual-action drops: Olopatadine (Patanol), epinastine (Elestat)—antihistamine + mast cell stabilizer
  • NSAID drops: Ketorolac (Acular)—for severe itching
  • Steroid drops: For severe cases only, short-term use under supervision

Immunotherapy:

  • Allergy shots or sublingual tablets
  • For chronic, severe allergic conjunctivitis
  • Gradually desensitizes immune system

Irritant Conjunctivitis Treatment

  • Rinse eyes: Flush with clean water or saline for 15 minutes if chemical exposure
  • Remove irritant: Avoid the triggering substance
  • Artificial tears: Soothe and flush out irritants
  • Usually resolves quickly once irritant is removed

Chemical Exposure

If chemicals splash in your eye, immediately flush with water for at least 15 minutes and seek emergency medical care, especially for acids or alkalis.

Home Care for All Types

  • Don't touch or rub eyes
  • Wash hands frequently
  • Clean discharge gently with warm water and clean washcloth
  • Avoid eye makeup until infection clears
  • Discard old eye makeup and applicators
  • Don't share towels, pillowcases, or eye products
  • Change pillowcases daily

Preventing Conjunctivitis

Preventing Infectious Conjunctivitis

Hand Hygiene (Most Important):

  • Wash hands frequently with soap and water for 20 seconds
  • Use hand sanitizer when soap not available
  • Never touch eyes with unwashed hands
  • Wash hands after touching someone with pink eye

Avoid Sharing:

  • Towels, washcloths, or tissues
  • Eye drops or medications
  • Eye makeup or applicators
  • Contact lens equipment
  • Pillowcases
  • Eyeglasses

Contact Lens Hygiene:

  • Wash hands before handling lenses
  • Follow cleaning and disinfection instructions
  • Replace lenses and cases as recommended
  • Never use tap water or saliva for lenses
  • Don't sleep in lenses unless designed for extended wear
  • Remove lenses immediately if eyes become red or irritated

If You Have Conjunctivitis:

  • Stay home from work or school until no longer contagious (varies by type)
  • Don't swim in pools
  • Change pillowcases and towels daily
  • Disinfect surfaces (doorknobs, counters, phones)
  • Wash hands after touching your eyes

Preventing Allergic Conjunctivitis

  • Identify and avoid allergen triggers
  • Keep windows closed during high pollen season
  • Use air purifiers with HEPA filters
  • Shower and change clothes after being outdoors
  • Wash bedding weekly in hot water
  • Keep pets out of bedroom
  • Use dust mite covers on mattresses and pillows
  • Start allergy medications before season begins

Preventing Irritant Conjunctivitis

  • Wear goggles when swimming
  • Use protective eyewear for chemicals or dusty environments
  • Avoid smoke exposure
  • Choose hypoallergenic cosmetics and personal care products

Is Conjunctivitis Contagious?

Viral Conjunctivitis: HIGHLY Contagious

  • Contagious period: From a few days before symptoms start until symptoms resolve (up to 2 weeks or longer)
  • How it spreads: Direct contact with eye discharge, respiratory droplets, contaminated surfaces
  • When to return: Generally 3-7 days after symptoms start, or as directed by doctor
  • Epidemic keratoconjunctivitis can be contagious for up to 2 weeks after symptoms start

Bacterial Conjunctivitis: Moderately Contagious

  • Contagious period: Until 24-48 hours after starting antibiotic treatment
  • How it spreads: Direct contact with eye discharge or contaminated items
  • When to return: 24 hours after starting antibiotics, no discharge, no fever

Allergic and Irritant Conjunctivitis: NOT Contagious

  • Can attend work or school
  • No isolation needed
  • Cannot spread to others

General Contagion Prevention

  • Wash hands frequently
  • Don't touch or rub eyes
  • Use separate towels and pillowcases
  • Disinfect frequently touched surfaces
  • Dispose of tissues immediately after use
  • Avoid close contact (handshakes, hugs) when infected

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Medical Care For:

  • Newborn with any eye redness or discharge (could be serious infection)
  • Severe eye pain (more than mild discomfort)
  • Vision changes or significant blurring
  • Severe light sensitivity (photophobia)
  • Chemical exposure to the eye
  • Eye injury or foreign body
  • Intense redness in one eye with pain
  • Weakened immune system (HIV, chemotherapy, immunosuppressants)

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • Symptoms don't improve after 2 days of home treatment
  • Thick, yellow-green discharge (suggests bacterial infection)
  • Moderate to severe symptoms
  • You wear contact lenses
  • Recurrent conjunctivitis
  • Symptoms worsen despite treatment
  • Associated vision changes
  • Severe itching affecting daily life (allergic conjunctivitis)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of conjunctivitis do I have?
  • Do I need antibiotic drops?
  • When can I return to work/school?
  • How long will I be contagious?
  • When can I wear contact lenses again?
  • What can I do to feel more comfortable?
  • How can I prevent spreading it to others or my other eye?
  • What symptoms should prompt me to return?
