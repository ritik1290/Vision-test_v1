What Is Strabismus?

Strabismus, commonly called crossed eyes or wandering eye, is a condition where the eyes don't align properly and point in different directions. One eye may turn inward (esotropia), outward (exotropia), upward (hypertropia), or downward (hypotropia) while the other eye looks straight ahead. This misalignment can be constant or intermittent, and can affect one or both eyes.

Strabismus affects approximately 4% of children and adults. While it's often noticed in infancy or early childhood, it can also develop later in life. The condition is more than just a cosmetic concern—it can significantly impact vision development, depth perception, and quality of life if left untreated. Early detection and treatment are crucial, especially in children, to prevent permanent vision loss from amblyopia (lazy eye).

Key Facts About Strabismus

  • Common condition: Affects about 4% of the population (1 in 25 people).
  • Can occur at any age: Most common in infants/children, but can develop in adults.
  • Multiple types: Eyes can turn in, out, up, or down.
  • Threatens vision development: Can cause amblyopia (lazy eye) in children if untreated.
  • Affects depth perception: Interferes with binocular vision and 3D perception.
  • Treatable: Glasses, patching, and surgery can correct alignment.
  • Early treatment essential: Best visual outcomes when treated early in childhood.
  • Not outgrown: Children don't "grow out of it"—requires treatment.

Understanding strabismus and seeking prompt treatment can protect vision, restore eye alignment, and improve quality of life.

How Eye Alignment and Binocular Vision Work

Normal Eye Alignment

In normal vision, both eyes work together as a coordinated team:

  • Six eye muscles per eye: Control eye movement in all directions
  • Brain coordinates: Sends signals to muscles so eyes move together
  • Both eyes point at same target: Focus on the same object simultaneously
  • Binocular vision: Each eye sends slightly different image to brain
  • Brain fuses images: Merges the two images into single 3D perception
  • Depth perception (stereopsis): Ability to judge distances accurately

What Goes Wrong in Strabismus

  • Muscle imbalance: One or more eye muscles are too strong, too weak, or don't respond properly
  • Eyes misaligned: Don't point at the same target
  • Double vision (diplopia): Brain receives two different images
  • Brain adaptation (suppression): Brain learns to ignore image from misaligned eye to avoid double vision
  • Result in children: Suppression leads to amblyopia—permanent vision loss in ignored eye
  • Loss of depth perception: Can't accurately judge distances

The Six Eye Muscles

Each eye has six extraocular muscles:

  • Medial rectus: Moves eye inward (toward nose)
  • Lateral rectus: Moves eye outward (away from nose)
  • Superior rectus: Moves eye upward
  • Inferior rectus: Moves eye downward
  • Superior oblique: Rotates eye downward and inward
  • Inferior oblique: Rotates eye upward and outward

These muscles must work in perfect balance for proper alignment.

Types of Strabismus

By Direction of Misalignment

Esotropia (Eyes Turn Inward)

Most common type:

  • Infantile (congenital) esotropia:
    • Develops before 6 months of age
    • Large, constant inward turn
    • No glasses needed; usually requires surgery
  • Accommodative esotropia:
    • Develops ages 2-4
    • Caused by uncorrected hyperopia (farsightedness)
    • Excessive focusing effort causes eyes to cross
    • Often corrected with glasses alone
  • Acute acquired esotropia:
    • Sudden onset in older children or adults
    • May indicate neurological problem—needs prompt evaluation

Exotropia (Eyes Turn Outward)

  • Intermittent exotropia:
    • Most common exotropia type
    • Eye drifts outward periodically, especially when tired, daydreaming, or looking at distance
    • May start as intermittent, become constant over time
    • Often worsens in bright sunlight (child may close one eye)
  • Constant exotropia:
    • Eye always turned outward
    • Less common than intermittent

Hypertropia/Hypotropia (Vertical Misalignment)

  • One eye higher or lower than the other
  • Less common than horizontal types
  • Often associated with superior oblique muscle weakness
  • Can cause head tilt (compensatory posture to fuse images)

By Constancy

  • Constant strabismus: Eye turn present all the time
  • Intermittent strabismus: Eye turn comes and goes; eyes aligned part of the time

By Which Eye Deviates

  • Unilateral (monocular): Always the same eye that turns
  • Alternating: Either eye can turn; person switches fixation between eyes

Special Types

Pseudostrabismus

  • Appearance of crossed eyes, but eyes actually aligned
  • Common in infants with wide nasal bridge or prominent skin folds (epicanthal folds)
  • Not true strabismus; no treatment needed
  • Appears to improve as face grows
  • Important to have professional evaluation to confirm

Paralytic (Incomitant) Strabismus

  • From paralysis or weakness of one or more eye muscles
  • Misalignment varies with direction of gaze
  • Causes: nerve damage, trauma, stroke, tumors
  • Adults experience double vision

Restrictive Strabismus

  • Eye muscle or connective tissue mechanically restricted
  • Causes: thyroid eye disease, trauma, orbital fractures

Causes and Risk Factors

Childhood Strabismus

Genetics and Family History

  • Strong hereditary component
  • Children with strabismic parents have higher risk
  • Not always inherited; can occur sporadically

Abnormal Development of Binocular Vision

  • Brain's eye coordination centers don't develop properly
  • Exact cause often unknown

Refractive Errors

  • Hyperopia (farsightedness): Main cause of accommodative esotropia
  • Excessive focusing to see clearly causes eye crossing
  • High myopia or asymmetric refractive errors can also contribute

Medical Conditions

  • Cerebral palsy
  • Down syndrome
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Brain tumors
  • Premature birth
  • Low birth weight
  • Developmental delays

Poor Vision in One Eye

  • Cataracts, ptosis (droopy eyelid), significant refractive error
  • Brain favors better-seeing eye; other eye may drift

Adult-Onset Strabismus

  • Stroke: Damages nerves or brain areas controlling eye muscles
  • Head injury/trauma
  • Neurological disorders: Multiple sclerosis, myasthenia gravis
  • Thyroid eye disease (Graves' ophthalmopathy): Muscles become inflamed, restrictive
  • Diabetes: Nerve damage affecting eye muscles
  • Brain tumors
  • Decompensation of childhood strabismus: Childhood eye turn that was controlled becomes manifest
  • Loss of vision in one eye: Eye may drift

Risk Factors

  • Family history of strabismus
  • Premature birth or low birth weight
  • Uncorrected refractive errors (especially hyperopia)
  • Developmental disabilities
  • Certain syndromes or medical conditions

Recognizing the Symptoms

Visible Eye Misalignment

One or both eyes turn inward, outward, upward, or downward. May be constant or intermittent.

Double Vision (Adults)

Seeing two images of a single object, especially in sudden-onset adult strabismus.

Squinting or Closing One Eye

Especially in bright light or when trying to see clearly—compensating for misalignment.

Head Tilting

Turning or tilting head to use eyes together, compensating for vertical misalignment.

Poor Depth Perception

Difficulty judging distances, clumsiness, trouble with stairs or catching objects.

Eye Strain or Fatigue

Eyes tire easily, especially with reading or close work—from effort to align eyes.

Reading Difficulties

Children may struggle with reading, lose place on page, or avoid reading.

Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)

Reduced vision in one eye from brain suppression—serious complication if untreated.

Signs in Infants and Young Children

  • Eyes don't move together or track objects in unison
  • One eye drifts or wanders
  • Frequent squinting or closing one eye
  • Head turn or tilt
  • Poor eye contact

When Misalignment Is Most Noticeable

  • When tired or ill
  • Looking at distance (intermittent exotropia)
  • Doing close work (accommodative esotropia)
  • In bright sunlight (exotropia)
  • After waking up

Important Notes

  • Newborns: Eyes may wander normally in first 3-4 months as vision develops
  • After 4 months: Eyes should be straight and move together; any persistent misalignment needs evaluation
  • Children don't "outgrow" strabismus—it requires treatment
  • Sudden adult strabismus is a medical emergency—may indicate stroke or neurological problem

Diagnosis

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Medical and Family History

  • When misalignment first noticed
  • Constant vs. intermittent
  • Family history of strabismus or amblyopia
  • Birth history (prematurity, complications)
  • Medical conditions or developmental delays
  • Recent trauma, illness, or neurological symptoms

Visual Acuity Testing

  • Tests vision in each eye separately
  • Detects amblyopia
  • Age-appropriate methods (pictures, letters, preferential looking)

Refraction

  • Determines if glasses needed
  • Cycloplegic refraction: Eye drops paralyze focusing (essential in children to find full hyperopia)
  • Uncorrected hyperopia is common cause of esotropia

Alignment and Motility Testing

Cover Test
  • Gold standard for detecting strabismus
  • Cover one eye, observe if other eye moves to fixate
  • Detects even subtle misalignments
Alternate Cover Test
  • Detects latent misalignments (phorias)
Hirschberg Test (Corneal Light Reflex)
  • Light reflection on corneas should be symmetric
  • Quick screening test, especially for infants
Prism and Cover Test
  • Measures degree of misalignment in prism diopters
  • Guides treatment planning
Ductions and Versions
  • Tests eye muscle function in all directions of gaze
  • Identifies specific muscle weaknesses

Binocular Vision Assessment

  • Stereopsis testing: Measures depth perception (e.g., Titmus stereo test)
  • Reduced or absent in strabismus

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Rules out retinoblastoma, cataracts, or retinal problems causing poor vision

Additional Testing (If Indicated)

  • Neuroimaging (MRI/CT): If sudden onset, neurological symptoms, or paralytic strabismus
  • Blood tests: Thyroid function (if thyroid eye disease suspected)
  • Neurological evaluation: If brain or nerve involvement suspected

Treatment Options

Goals: Restore eye alignment, preserve/restore vision, develop binocular vision and depth perception

Eyeglasses

  • Correcting hyperopia: First-line treatment for accommodative esotropia
  • Reduces focusing effort, may fully correct eye crossing
  • Must be worn full-time
  • Some children outgrow need; others require ongoing correction

Patching (Occlusion Therapy)

For amblyopia associated with strabismus:

  • Patch stronger eye to force brain to use weaker eye
  • Duration: Hours per day for weeks to months
  • Critical during visual development period (before age 7-9)
  • Improves vision in amblyopic eye
  • Doesn't fix strabismus itself, but prevents permanent vision loss

Atropine Eye Drops

  • Blur vision in stronger eye (alternative to patching)
  • Forces use of weaker eye
  • For mild to moderate amblyopia
  • Easier compliance than patching for some families

Prism Glasses

  • Special lenses that bend light
  • Help align images on both retinas
  • For small-angle deviations or double vision in adults
  • Not curative; relieve double vision

Vision Therapy (Orthoptics)

  • Eye exercises to improve eye coordination
  • May help: Intermittent exotropia, convergence insufficiency
  • Limited role: Not effective for constant large-angle strabismus
  • Requires commitment to daily exercises

Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injection

  • Injection into overacting eye muscle
  • Temporarily weakens muscle, allows opposite muscle to straighten eye
  • Effect lasts 2-3 months
  • Uses: Acute paralytic strabismus, alternative to surgery in some cases
  • Less commonly used than surgery for childhood strabismus

Eye Muscle Surgery (Strabismus Surgery)

When Surgery Is Recommended

  • Constant or large-angle strabismus
  • Strabismus not corrected by glasses alone
  • To improve alignment and cosmetic appearance
  • To restore binocular vision and depth perception (if done early enough)
  • To eliminate double vision in adults

How It Works

Surgery adjusts position or strength of eye muscles:

  • Recession: Weakens muscle by moving insertion point farther back on eyeball
  • Resection: Strengthens muscle by shortening it
  • Adjustments: Calculated based on degree of misalignment
  • One or multiple muscles may be operated on
  • May involve one or both eyes

The Procedure

  • Anesthesia: General anesthesia (children); local or general (adults)
  • Outpatient: Usually go home same day
  • Duration: 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on complexity
  • Incision: Made in conjunctiva (clear tissue covering white of eye)—no visible scar
  • No cutting of eyeball itself

Recovery

  • Redness: Eye red for 1-2 weeks
  • Discomfort: Mild to moderate; managed with over-the-counter pain relievers
  • Activity: Avoid swimming, contact sports for 2 weeks
  • Eye drops: Antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops for 1-2 weeks
  • Return to school/work: Usually within days
  • Full healing: Several weeks

Success Rates

  • 80-90% achieve good alignment with one surgery
  • Some require additional surgery for fine-tuning
  • Best outcomes when combined with glasses, patching as needed
  • Important: Surgery aligns eyes but doesn't always restore binocular vision or depth perception, especially if done later in life

Risks and Complications

  • Overcorrection or undercorrection (may need reoperation)
  • Double vision (usually temporary)
  • Infection (rare)
  • Bleeding (rare)
  • Scarring
  • Rarely, vision loss or loss of eye (extremely rare)

Adjustable Suture Technique (Adults)

  • Sutures temporarily tied; adjusted after surgery while patient awake
  • Allows fine-tuning of alignment
  • Higher success rate in adults

Potential Complications of Untreated Strabismus

Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)

  • Most serious complication in children
  • Brain suppresses image from misaligned eye to avoid double vision
  • Vision in that eye doesn't develop properly
  • Can result in permanent vision loss
  • Critical period: Must be treated before age 7-9 for best results
  • Leading cause of vision loss in children

Loss of Depth Perception

  • Without both eyes working together, stereoscopic vision doesn't develop
  • Difficulty judging distances
  • Impacts sports, driving, certain occupations

Psychosocial Impact

  • Self-esteem issues from appearance
  • Social difficulties, teasing
  • Studies show strabismus can affect social and professional opportunities

Double Vision (Adults)

  • Sudden adult-onset strabismus causes diplopia
  • Significantly impairs function
  • May require patching, prisms, or surgery

Strabismus in Adults

Types

  • Childhood strabismus that persists: Never treated or incompletely treated
  • Adult-onset strabismus: Develops from medical conditions, trauma, or vision loss
  • Decompensated childhood strabismus: Previously controlled strabismus becomes manifest

Causes of Adult-Onset Strabismus

  • Stroke
  • Thyroid eye disease
  • Myasthenia gravis
  • Diabetes (nerve damage)
  • Head trauma
  • Brain tumor
  • Vision loss in one eye

Key Differences from Childhood

  • Double vision common: Adults have established binocular vision, so misalignment causes diplopia
  • No amblyopia risk: Visual system already mature
  • Depth perception already developed: May lose it if strabismus develops, but can sometimes readapt
  • Underlying causes different: Often medical/neurological

Treatment for Adults

  • Treat underlying cause (thyroid disease, diabetes control)
  • Prism glasses: For small deviations or temporary double vision
  • Patching: One eye to eliminate double vision (temporary measure)
  • Surgery: Effective at any age for alignment and eliminating double vision
  • Vision therapy: Limited role; may help some types

Surgery in Adults

  • Can be performed at any age
  • Success rates similar to children for alignment
  • Often relieves double vision
  • Cosmetic improvement
  • May not restore stereopsis if never had it (long-standing childhood strabismus)
  • Adjustable sutures allow fine-tuning

When to See a Doctor

Seek Immediate Medical Care For:

  • Sudden double vision in adults
  • Sudden eye misalignment at any age
  • Associated neurological symptoms: Headache, weakness, numbness, speech changes, droopy eyelid
  • Eye turn after head injury

These may indicate stroke, aneurysm, brain tumor, or other serious conditions requiring emergency evaluation.

Schedule an Appointment If:

  • Infant over 4 months: Eyes don't move together or appear misaligned
  • Child of any age: Eye turn, squinting one eye, head tilt, closing one eye
  • Reading difficulties or clumsiness that might be vision-related
  • Family history of strabismus: Have child screened
  • Intermittent eye turn: Even if not constant, needs evaluation
  • Any concerns about eye alignment

Regular Vision Screenings

  • Newborns: Checked for eye health
  • 6 months: First comprehensive eye exam recommended
  • Age 3: Vision screening
  • Before kindergarten: Comprehensive eye exam
  • School-age: Regular exams, especially if family history

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of strabismus does my child/I have?
  • What caused it?
  • Is there amblyopia? What's the vision in each eye?
  • What are the treatment options?
  • Will glasses help? Must they be worn all the time?
  • Is patching needed? For how long?
  • Will surgery be necessary? When?
  • What are the chances of restored binocular vision and depth perception?
  • How often should we have follow-up visits?
  • Could this be related to a medical condition?

Bottom Line

Strabismus is a treatable condition, but early intervention is crucial—especially in children—to prevent amblyopia and maximize the chance of developing normal binocular vision and depth perception. If you notice any signs of eye misalignment in yourself or your child, seek prompt evaluation by an eye care professional. Modern treatments, including glasses, patching, and surgery, are highly effective at improving alignment, preserving vision, and enhancing quality of life.
