What Is Ocular Melanoma?

Ocular melanoma, also called uveal melanoma, is a malignant cancer arising from melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) in the uveal tract of the eye—comprising the iris, ciliary body, and choroid. It is the most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults, though still rare, with an incidence of ~5-7 cases per million per year. The majority (~90%) originate in the choroid (the vascular layer beneath the retina), appearing as a pigmented (dark brown/black) or amelanotic (non-pigmented) dome-shaped mass on dilated exam or imaging.

Unlike cutaneous (skin) melanoma, uveal melanoma is not related to sun exposure. Risk factors include light eye color, fair skin, and certain genetic conditions. Many patients are asymptomatic, with tumors discovered incidentally during routine eye exams. Symptoms, if present, include blurred vision, visual field defects, floaters, or flashes from retinal detachment. Diagnosis is primarily clinical (dilated exam, ultrasound) without biopsy in most cases. Treatment depends on tumor size and location: small tumors may be observed, medium tumors treated with plaque brachytherapy (radioactive plaque), and large tumors or those threatening vision may require enucleation (eye removal). A significant concern is metastasis—uveal melanoma has a predilection for spreading to the liver (~50% develop metastasis within 10-15 years), necessitating lifelong surveillance.

Key Facts About Ocular Melanoma

  • Most common primary eye cancer in adults
  • Arises from uveal tract: Mostly choroid (~90%).
  • Not related to sun exposure (unlike skin melanoma).
  • Risk factors: light eyes, fair skin
  • Often asymptomatic initially.
  • Pigmented choroidal mass on exam/ultrasound
  • Treatment: observation, plaque brachytherapy, or enucleation
  • ~50% develop liver metastasis → lifelong surveillance needed.

Understanding ocular melanoma emphasizes the importance of regular eye exams and lifelong metastatic surveillance after diagnosis.

Types and Location

By Location

Choroidal Melanoma (~90%)

  • Most common
  • Arises in choroid (vascular layer beneath retina)
  • Appears as dome-shaped or mushroom-shaped mass
  • May cause overlying retinal detachment

Ciliary Body Melanoma (~6%)

  • Behind iris, difficult to visualize
  • Often larger at presentation (hidden location)

Iris Melanoma (~4%)

  • Visible on anterior segment exam
  • Usually small, slow-growing
  • Best prognosis

By Pigmentation

  • Melanotic (pigmented): Dark brown/black; most common
  • Amelanotic (non-pigmented): Pink/white; can be mistaken for other lesions

Risk Factors

  • Light eye color: Blue or green eyes (higher risk than brown eyes)
  • Fair skin, light hair
  • Caucasian ethnicity: Much more common in whites than other races
  • Age: Median age ~60; rare in children
  • Ocular melanocytosis/nevi: Pre-existing choroidal nevi (freckles) can transform (rare)
  • BAP1 tumor predisposition syndrome: Rare genetic condition; increased melanoma risk
  • NOT related to UV/sun exposure (unlike skin melanoma)

Recognizing the Symptoms

Often Asymptomatic

  • Many patients have no symptoms; tumor found on routine eye exam

Blurred Vision

If tumor involves macula or causes retinal detachment.

Visual Field Defect

Shadow or blind spot from tumor or detached retina.

Floaters and Flashes

From retinal detachment.

Visible Iris Mass

If iris melanoma; pigmented spot on iris.

Gradual Vision Loss

Slow enlargement of tumor.

Painless

Typically painless unless very large causing glaucoma.

Diagnosis

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Appearance: Dome-shaped or mushroom-shaped pigmented (brown/black) or amelanotic mass
    • May have overlying orange pigment (lipofuscin)
    • Subretinal fluid, retinal detachment
  • Experienced ophthalmologist/ocular oncologist can often diagnose clinically

Ocular Ultrasound (B-Scan)

  • Key test: Shows characteristic dome-shaped lesion with low internal reflectivity, choroidal excavation (Lund's sign)
  • Measures tumor thickness (important for staging and treatment planning)

Fluorescein Angiography, Indocyanine Green Angiography

  • Assess tumor vascularity

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Evaluate overlying retina, subretinal fluid

Fine Needle Aspiration Biopsy (Rarely)

  • Usually NOT done (diagnosis primarily clinical)
  • May be performed if:
    • Diagnosis uncertain
    • To obtain tumor genetics (prognostic markers: chromosome 3, 8 status; BAP1 mutation) for metastatic risk stratification

Systemic Staging (To Rule Out Metastasis at Diagnosis)

  • Liver imaging (MRI or CT)
  • Liver function tests
  • Chest imaging

Treatment

Goals: Control primary tumor, preserve eye/vision if possible, minimize metastatic risk.

Observation (Small Tumors)

  • Very small melanomas or suspicious nevi
  • Serial photography, ultrasound to monitor for growth
  • Treat if growth documented

Plaque Brachytherapy (Most Common for Medium Tumors)

  • Procedure: Radioactive plaque (usually iodine-125 or ruthenium-106) sutured to sclera over tumor
    • Left in place 3-7 days
    • Delivers focused radiation to tumor
    • Removed in second surgery
  • Goal: Destroy tumor, preserve eye
  • Success rate: ~85-90% local tumor control
  • Complications: Radiation retinopathy, optic neuropathy, cataract, dry eye (can cause vision loss over time)

Proton Beam Radiotherapy

  • Alternative to plaque brachytherapy
  • External beam radiation with protons
  • Available at specialized centers
  • Similar efficacy and side effects as plaque

Enucleation (Eye Removal)

  • Indications:
    • Large tumors (>10 mm thickness) where radiation may not control tumor
    • Blind, painful eye from tumor
    • Optic nerve invasion
    • Patient preference
  • Curative for primary tumor
  • Does NOT prevent metastasis (metastatic cells may already be in circulation)
  • Prosthetic eye fitted after healing

Local Resection (Rare)

  • Surgical removal of tumor, preserving eye
  • High complication rate; not commonly performed

Laser or Cryotherapy (Very Small Tumors)

  • Focal treatment for tiny lesions

Metastasis and Surveillance

High Metastatic Potential

  • Uveal melanoma metastasizes hematogenously (via bloodstream)
  • Liver: Most common site (~90% of metastases involve liver)
  • Less commonly: lung, bone, skin
  • ~50% of patients develop metastasis within 10-15 years

Prognostic Factors for Metastasis

  • Tumor size: Larger tumors → higher risk
  • Ciliary body involvement
  • Epithelioid cell type (vs spindle cell)
  • Genetic/molecular markers:
    • Monosomy 3 (loss of chromosome 3): high risk
    • Gain of chromosome 8q: high risk
    • BAP1 mutation: high risk
  • Genetic testing (if biopsy performed) can stratify risk

Lifelong Surveillance for Metastasis

  • Essential: All uveal melanoma patients need lifelong monitoring
  • Liver imaging: MRI or CT every 6-12 months initially, then annually
  • Liver function tests
  • Unfortunately, metastatic uveal melanoma is difficult to treat; no curative therapy currently
  • Clinical trials investigating immunotherapy, targeted therapy for metastatic disease

Prognosis and Outcomes

Local Tumor Control

  • Excellent with plaque brachytherapy or enucleation (~85-95% control)

Vision Outcomes

  • Variable; depends on tumor location and treatment side effects
  • Radiation can cause vision loss over time from complications (retinopathy, optic neuropathy)

Metastasis and Survival

  • Overall survival: ~80% at 5 years, ~50-60% at 10-15 years (due to metastasis)
  • Once metastatic disease develops, prognosis poor (median survival ~1 year without treatment; modest improvement with new therapies)
  • Iris melanoma has best prognosis (low metastatic risk)

When to See a Doctor

See Eye Doctor If:

  • New visual symptoms: Blurred vision, floaters, flashes, shadow
  • Pigmented spot noticed on iris
  • Routine eye exam recommended (many tumors asymptomatic)

Regular Eye Exams

  • Especially if risk factors (light eyes, fair skin)
  • Many melanomas detected incidentally

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have uveal melanoma?
  • What is the size and location?
  • What treatment do you recommend?
  • Can the eye and vision be preserved?
  • Should I have genetic testing of the tumor?
  • What is my risk of metastasis?
  • How often do I need surveillance imaging?

Bottom Line

Ocular melanoma (uveal melanoma) is the most common primary eye cancer in adults, arising from melanocytes in the uvea—most commonly the choroid (~90%). It's not related to sun exposure; risk factors include light eye color and fair skin. Many patients are asymptomatic, with tumors discovered on routine eye exams as pigmented choroidal masses. Diagnosis is primarily clinical using dilated exam and ocular ultrasound. Treatment depends on size: small tumors observed, medium tumors treated with plaque brachytherapy (radioactive plaque) with ~85-90% control, and large tumors or those threatening the eye require enucleation. The major concern is metastasis—uveal melanoma spreads hematogenously to the liver in~50% of patients within 10-15 years. Genetic testing (chromosome 3/8, BAP1) can stratify metastatic risk. All patients need lifelong liver surveillance with imaging every 6-12 months. Prognosis for local tumor control is excellent, but overall survival is impacted by metastatic disease, emphasizing the need for vigilant monitoring and ongoing research into effective systemic therapies.
