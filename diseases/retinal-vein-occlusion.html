What Is Retinal Vein Occlusion?

Retinal vein occlusion (RVO) is a blockage of one of the veins that drain blood from the retina, causing blood and fluid to back up into the retinal tissue. This leads to retinal hemorrhages, swelling (macular edema), and ischemia (oxygen deprivation), resulting in sudden, painless vision loss. RVO is often described as an "eye stroke" and is the second most common retinal vascular disease after diabetic retinopathy. It typically affects one eye and occurs more commonly in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors like hypertension, diabetes, and glaucoma.

There are two main types: branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO), in which a tributary branch vein is blocked affecting only part of the retina, and central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO), in which the main central retinal vein is blocked affecting the entire retina (more severe). Treatment focuses on managing complications—primarily macular edema—with anti-VEGF injections, corticosteroid injections, or laser therapy. Visual outcomes depend on the type, severity, and how quickly treatment is initiated. Some cases recover well, while others result in permanent vision impairment, especially if complicated by ischemia or neovascular glaucoma.

Key Facts About Retinal Vein Occlusion

  • Blocked retinal vein: Blood/fluid backs up into retina.
  • Sudden painless vision loss: Blurred or decreased vision, often upon waking.
  • Two types: BRVO (branch) and CRVO (central)
  • Risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, glaucoma, age >50
  • Retinal hemorrhages and swelling on exam
  • Treatment: anti-VEGF injections for macular edema.
  • Complications: chronic edema, neovascularization, glaucoma
  • Second most common retinal vascular disease

Understanding RVO emphasizes the importance of managing cardiovascular risk factors and seeking prompt evaluation for sudden vision changes.

Types of Retinal Vein Occlusion

Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO)

  • Most common type (~75-80% of RVOs)
  • Blockage of tributary branch vein (not main central vein)
  • Affects only part of retina (quadrant or sector)
  • Vision impact:
    • If macula involved: significant vision loss
    • If peripheral retina only: may be asymptomatic or mild
  • Generally better prognosis than CRVO

Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)

  • Blockage of main central retinal vein (at or behind optic nerve)
  • Affects entire retina
  • More severe than BRVO
  • Two subtypes:
    • Non-ischemic (perfused) CRVO: Better prognosis; retina still has blood flow
    • Ischemic CRVO: Severe; large areas of retina with no blood flow; worse outcomes, higher risk of complications (neovascular glaucoma)

Hemi-Retinal Vein Occlusion

  • Occlusion at optic nerve affecting half the retina
  • Behaves similarly to CRVO

Causes and Risk Factors

Mechanism of Occlusion

  • Retinal arteries and veins share common adventitial sheath at arteriovenous (AV) crossing points
  • Arteriosclerotic, rigid artery compresses adjacent vein → turbulent flow → thrombus (clot) forms → vein occlusion
  • Blood/fluid backs up → retinal hemorrhages, edema, ischemia

Major Risk Factors

Hypertension (Most Important)

  • Present in ~70% of RVO patients
  • Causes arteriosclerosis → compression of veins

Age

  • Incidence increases with age; most common >50 years
  • Median age ~60-65

Diabetes Mellitus

  • Vascular damage predisposes to occlusion

Glaucoma or Ocular Hypertension

  • Elevated intraocular pressure compresses veins at optic nerve → CRVO risk

Hyperlipidemia

  • Atherosclerosis

Hypercoagulable States (Blood Clotting Disorders)

  • Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome
  • More common in younger patients with RVO (<50 years)

Smoking

  • Vascular risk factor

Other

  • Obesity, sleep apnea, cardiovascular disease

Recognizing the Symptoms

Sudden Painless Vision Loss

Blurred or decreased vision in one eye; often noticed upon waking (overnight occlusion).

Central or Sectoral Blind Spot

Depending on location of occlusion (central if macula involved, peripheral if branch).

Distorted Vision

From macular edema.

Visual Field Loss

Peripheral vision loss if branch occlusion affects peripheral retina.

No Pain

Painless (unlike acute angle-closure glaucoma).

Severity Varies

Mild blur to severe vision loss (counting fingers or worse).

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Dilated Fundus Examination - Diagnostic

  • Classic findings:
    • Retinal hemorrhages: "Blood and thunder" appearance (CRVO); flame-shaped and dot-blot hemorrhages scattered throughout affected retina
    • Dilated, tortuous veins: Engorged retinal veins
    • Retinal edema: Cloudy, swollen retina
    • Optic disc swelling (especially CRVO)
    • Cotton-wool spots: Ischemic areas
  • Appearance classic for diagnosis

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

  • Cross-sectional retinal imaging
  • Shows: Macular edema quantitatively; retinal thickness
  • Guides treatment and monitors response

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

  • Dye injected IV; retinal photographs taken
  • Shows:
    • Areas of retinal ischemia (non-perfusion)
    • Leakage from damaged vessels
    • Neovascularization (abnormal new vessels)
  • Distinguishes ischemic vs. non-ischemic CRVO
  • Guides laser treatment decisions

Systemic Workup

  • Blood pressure measurement
  • Fasting glucose, HbA1c (diabetes screening)
  • Lipid panel
  • If young patient (<50) or bilateral RVO: Hypercoagulability workup (factor V Leiden, protein C/S, anticardiolipin antibodies, etc.)

Complications

Macular Edema (Most Common)

  • Fluid accumulation in macula
  • Primary cause of vision loss in RVO
  • Treatable with anti-VEGF injections or steroids

Retinal Neovascularization

  • Abnormal new blood vessels grow on retina in response to ischemia
  • Can cause vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment

Neovascular Glaucoma (Severe Complication)

  • New vessels grow on iris/drainage angle (rubeosis iridis)
  • Block aqueous drainage → very high IOP
  • Painful; difficult to treat
  • More common with ischemic CRVO
  • Requires anti-VEGF injections, panretinal photocoagulation, glaucoma medications/surgery

Vitreous Hemorrhage

  • From neovascularization
  • Sudden severe vision loss

Treatment

Treat Underlying Risk Factors

  • Control hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia
  • Optimize cardiovascular health
  • Reduce IOP if glaucoma/ocular hypertension

Observation (Some Cases)

  • If normal vision, no macular edema, peripheral BRVO: may observe
  • Many BRVOs improve spontaneously over months

Anti-VEGF Injections (First-Line for Macular Edema)

  • Medications: Aflibercept (Eylea), ranibizumab (Lucentis), bevacizumab (Avastin off-label)
  • Mechanism: Block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) → reduce vascular leakage and edema
  • Procedure: Intravitreal injection (into vitreous gel) in office
  • Schedule: Monthly initially, then as-needed or extended interval based on response
  • Effectiveness: Very effective; most patients maintain or improve vision
  • Also prevents/treats neovascularization

Corticosteroid Injections or Implants

  • Triamcinolone injection (Triesence) or dexamethasone implant (Ozurdex)
  • Reduce inflammation and edema
  • Longer-acting than anti-VEGF
  • Side effects: IOP elevation, cataract progression
  • Used if anti-VEGF insufficient or as alternative

Laser Photocoagulation

Focal/Grid Laser for Macular Edema (BRVO)

  • Historically used; now mostly replaced by anti-VEGF
  • Can be adjunct in some cases

Panretinal Photocoagulation (PRP) for Ischemia/Neovascularization

  • Scatter laser to ischemic peripheral retina
  • Reduces VEGF production, prevents/treats neovascularization
  • Indicated if ischemic CRVO with neovascularization or high risk

Prognosis and Outcomes

Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO)

  • Generally good prognosis
  • With treatment (anti-VEGF), most maintain or improve vision
  • Some recover spontaneously over 6-12 months
  • Complications less common than CRVO

Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)

  • More variable outcomes
  • Non-ischemic CRVO: Better prognosis; many improve with treatment
  • Ischemic CRVO: Worse prognosis; higher risk of complications (neovascular glaucoma); some have persistent severe vision loss despite treatment
  • ~15-20% of non-ischemic can convert to ischemic

Factors Affecting Prognosis

  • Initial visual acuity (better initial vision → better outcome)
  • Degree of ischemia
  • Promptness of treatment
  • Response to anti-VEGF

Prevention

Control Cardiovascular Risk Factors

  • Blood pressure: Keep well-controlled
  • Diabetes: Optimize glucose control
  • Cholesterol: Manage hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking cessation
  • Healthy weight, exercise

Regular Eye Exams

  • Especially if risk factors present
  • Control glaucoma/ocular hypertension

When to See a Doctor

Emergency - Seek Immediate Care If:

  • Sudden painless vision loss or blurring in one eye
  • Sudden visual field defect
  • See retina specialist or go to ER

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have BRVO or CRVO?
  • Is it ischemic or non-ischemic?
  • Do I need anti-VEGF injections?
  • What is my prognosis for vision?
  • How often will I need follow-up?
  • What are my risks for complications?
  • Should I see a primary care doctor to check for underlying causes?

Bottom Line

Retinal vein occlusion is blockage of a retinal vein causing sudden, painless vision loss from retinal hemorrhages and swelling. Branch RVO (BRVO) affects part of the retina and has a better prognosis, while central RVO (CRVO) affects the entire retina and can be more severe, especially if ischemic. Risk factors include hypertension, diabetes, glaucoma, and age >50. Diagnosis is made by fundus exam showing characteristic "blood and thunder" hemorrhages and dilated veins, confirmed with OCT and fluorescein angiography. The main complication is macular edema causing vision loss, treated with anti-VEGF injections (Eylea, Lucentis, Avastin) which are highly effective. Severe complications include neovascular glaucoma (from ischemic CRVO) requiring panretinal laser and anti-VEGF. Visual outcomes depend on type and severity; many BRVO patients recover well with treatment, while ischemic CRVO has guarded prognosis. If you experience sudden vision loss, see a retina specialist immediately. Prevention focuses on controlling cardiovascular risk factors like hypertension and diabetes.
