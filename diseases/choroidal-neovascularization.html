What Is Choroidal Neovascularization?

Choroidal neovascularization (CNV), also called a choroidal neovascular membrane, is the growth of abnormal new blood vessels from the choroid (the vascular layer beneath the retina) through Bruch's membrane into the subretinal space or retina. These abnormal vessels are fragile and leaky, causing fluid accumulation, bleeding, and scarring under and within the retina, leading to distorted and decreased central vision. CNV is not a disease itself but a complication of several retinal conditions, most commonly wet (exudative/neovascular) age-related macular degeneration (AMD).

In wet AMD CNV develops in response to chronic retinal ischemia and damage, driven by vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Other causes include pathologic myopia (high nearsightedness), ocular histoplasmosis syndrome, trauma, and inflammatory conditions. Symptoms include sudden or gradual central vision loss, metamorphopsia (straight lines appearing wavy/distorted), and central scotoma (blind spot). Diagnosis is made using OCT (showing fluid/bleeding) and fluorescein angiography (showing leaking vessels). Treatment with anti-VEGF injections (Eylea, Lucentis, Avastin) is highly effective, stabilizing or improving vision in most patients by blocking VEGF and causing CNV regression.

Key Facts About Choroidal Neovascularization

  • Abnormal vessels from choroid grow into retina/subretinal space
  • Fragile, leaky vessels cause fluid, bleeding, scarring
  • Most common cause: wet AMD
  • Other causes: pathologic myopia, histoplasmosis, trauma
  • Symptoms: distortion (metamorphopsia), central vision loss
  • Diagnosis: OCT, fluorescein angiography
  • Treatment: anti-VEGF injections (Eylea, Lucentis, Avastin)—highly effective.
  • Requires ongoing treatment to maintain vision.

Understanding CNV emphasizes the importance of prompt treatment with anti-VEGF to preserve central vision.

Causes of Choroidal Neovascularization

Wet Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD) - Most Common

  • ~80-90% of CNV cases
  • Chronic damage to retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) and Bruch's membrane → CNV develops
  • Affects people >50-60 years

Pathologic Myopia (High Nearsightedness)

  • Second most common cause in younger patients
  • Elongated eyeball → retinal/choroidal stretching, breaks in Bruch's membrane → CNV

Ocular Histoplasmosis Syndrome

  • Fungal infection (Histoplasma capsulatum) causes choroidal scars ("histo spots")
  • Scars can develop CNV years later
  • More common in certain geographic regions (Ohio/Mississippi River valleys)

Angioid Streaks

  • Breaks in Bruch's membrane from various causes (pseudoxanthoma elasticum, Paget's disease, sickle cell, trauma)
  • CNV can develop at streaks

Trauma

  • Blunt eye injury → choroidal rupture → CNV

Inflammatory Conditions

  • Posterior uveitis, multifocal choroiditis

Idiopathic (Unknown Cause)

  • Some cases no clear underlying condition

Recognizing the Symptoms

Metamorphopsia (Distortion)

Straight lines appear wavy, bent, or irregular (e.g., door frames, window blinds); hallmark symptom; detected by Amsler grid.

Blurred Central Vision

Difficulty reading, recognizing faces; central vision affected.

Central Scotoma (Blind Spot)

Dark or blurry spot in center of vision.

Rapid or Gradual Onset

Can be sudden (from bleeding) or gradual (fluid accumulation).

Decreased Contrast Sensitivity

Colors seem washed out, dim.

Painless

No pain.

Amsler Grid Test

  • Grid of straight lines
  • CNV causes lines to appear wavy or distorted
  • Useful for home monitoring

Diagnosis

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • May see grayish-green subretinal membrane, hemorrhage, fluid, lipid exudates

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Key Test

  • Cross-sectional retinal imaging
  • Shows:
    • Subretinal or intraretinal fluid
    • CNV as hyperreflective material under retina
    • Retinal thickening
  • Guides treatment, monitors response
  • OCT angiography (OCTA): Non-invasive imaging of CNV blood flow

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

  • Dye injected IV; retinal photographs taken
  • Shows: CNV as area of hyperfluorescence (bright leakage) from leaking vessels
    • Classic CNV: well-defined
    • Occult CNV: poorly defined
  • Confirms diagnosis, characterizes CNV

Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

  • Alternative dye; better for visualizing choroidal vessels
  • Used if FA unclear

Treatment

Anti-VEGF Injections - First-Line, Highly Effective

  • Medications: Aflibercept (Eylea), ranibizumab (Lucentis), bevacizumab (Avastin, off-label)
  • Mechanism: Block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) → CNV vessels regress, fluid/bleeding resolves
  • Procedure: Intravitreal injection (into vitreous gel) in office
    • Topical anesthesia, very quick procedure
  • Schedule:
    • Loading phase: Monthly injections × 3-4 months initially
    • Maintenance: As-needed (PRN) based on OCT, or fixed interval (every 6-12 weeks), or treat-and-extend protocol
    • Ongoing treatment needed; CNV can recur if treatment stopped
  • Effectiveness: ~90% stabilize vision, ~30-40% improve vision significantly
  • Revolutionized CNV treatment

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT) - Less Common Now

  • Verteporfin (Visudyne) dye injected IV, activated by laser to CNV area
  • Causes selective CNV closure
  • Largely replaced by anti-VEGF (more effective, fewer side effects)
  • Sometimes used in combination with anti-VEGF for certain CNV types

Laser Photocoagulation (Thermal Laser) - Rarely Used Now

  • Destroys CNV with laser
  • Creates permanent retinal scar → vision loss at laser site
  • Only for peripheral CNV (not involving fovea)
  • Mostly historical; anti-VEGF preferred

Low Vision Aids (If Advanced)

  • Magnifiers, high-contrast materials if vision severely impaired despite treatment

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Anti-VEGF Treatment

  • Vision stabilization: ~90% maintain or improve vision
  • Vision improvement: ~30-40% gain significant vision (≥3 lines on chart)
  • Earlier treatment → better outcomes
  • Ongoing injections needed (can be lifelong)

Without Treatment

  • Progressive vision loss
  • CNV leads to scarring (disciform scar) → permanent central vision loss

Recurrence

  • CNV can reactivate if treatment stopped
  • Regular monitoring (OCT) essential

When to See a Doctor

See Eye Doctor Urgently (Within Days) If:

  • Straight lines appear wavy or distorted
  • Sudden or gradual central vision loss or blur
  • Central blind spot
  • Any change in vision if you have AMD, pathologic myopia, or other risk factors

Home Monitoring

  • Amsler grid: Check daily (if AMD or at risk)
    • Cover one eye, look at center dot
    • If lines wavy/distorted → see doctor immediately

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have choroidal neovascularization?
  • What caused it?
  • Do I need anti-VEGF injections?
  • How often will I need injections?
  • What is my prognosis for vision?
  • Should I monitor at home with Amsler grid?

Bottom Line

Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) is the growth of abnormal, fragile, leaky blood vessels from the choroid into the retina, causing fluid accumulation, bleeding, distortion, and central vision loss. It's most commonly a complication of wet age-related macular degeneration (AMD), but also occurs in pathologic myopia, ocular histoplasmosis, trauma, and other conditions. The hallmark symptom is metamorphopsia—straight lines appearing wavy—best detected with an Amsler grid. Diagnosis is made with OCT (showing fluid/CNV) and fluorescein angiography (showing leaking vessels). Treatment with anti-VEGF injections (Eylea, Lucentis, Avastin) into the eye is highly effective, stabilizing vision in ~90% and improving it in ~30-40% by blocking VEGF and causing CNV regression. Ongoing injections are needed to maintain results. Without treatment, CNV leads to progressive scarring and permanent central vision loss. If you notice distortion or central vision changes, see a retina specialist urgently—early treatment with anti-VEGF offers the best chance of preserving vision.
