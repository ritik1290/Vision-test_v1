What Is Episcleritis?

Episcleritis is a common, benign inflammatory condition affecting the episclera—the thin layer of tissue between the conjunctiva (clear outer membrane) and the sclera (white wall of the eye). It causes mild to moderate eye redness, typically localized to one area of the eye, along with minor discomfort or irritation. Unlike scleritis (inflammation of the deeper sclera), episcleritis is self-limited, relatively painless, and not associated with vision-threatening complications or serious systemic diseases.

Episcleritis most commonly occurs in young to middle-aged adults and often resolves spontaneously within 1-3 weeks without treatment, though it can recur. While the redness can be cosmetically concerning, the condition is benign and does not cause permanent damage to the eye. Treatment, when needed, consists of lubricating drops or mild anti-inflammatory medications for symptom relief.

Key Facts About Episcleritis

  • Benign inflammation: Superficial, not vision-threatening.
  • Mild symptoms: Slight discomfort, irritation; not severe pain.
  • Localized redness: Sectoral (one area) red patch on white of eye.
  • Self-limited: Resolves on its own in 1-3 weeks.
  • Recurrent: Can come back periodically.
  • Not associated with systemic disease: Usually idiopathic (no underlying cause found).
  • Different from scleritis: Less serious; episcleritis is superficial, painless, benign.
  • Normal vision: Does not affect vision.

Understanding episcleritis helps differentiate it from more serious eye inflammations and provides reassurance about its benign nature.

Types of Episcleritis

Simple Episcleritis (~70-80%)

  • Most common type
  • Diffuse or sectoral (localized) redness
  • Blood vessels dilated, causing red appearance
  • Resolves quickly (1-2 weeks)
  • May recur

Nodular Episcleritis (~20-30%)

  • Raised, tender nodule on episclera
  • Localized inflammation
  • May be slightly more uncomfortable than simple type
  • Takes longer to resolve (may take weeks to months)
  • Also benign, self-limited

Causes and Associations

Idiopathic (Unknown Cause) - Most Common

  • In majority of cases (~70%), no specific cause identified
  • Spontaneous inflammation

Possible Triggers (Occasionally Identified)

  • Viral illness (upper respiratory infection recently)
  • Rosacea (skin condition)
  • Gout (rare)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis—rare association)
  • Lupus or other autoimmune diseases (rare)

NOT Typically Associated with Serious Systemic Disease

  • Unlike scleritis, episcleritis rarely signals underlying systemic disease
  • Extensive systemic workup usually not needed

Recognizing the Symptoms

Eye Redness

Bright or salmon-pink redness; usually localized to one section (sectoral).

Mild Discomfort/Irritation

Slight tenderness, irritation, or foreign body sensation; NOT severe pain.

Watery Eyes

Mild tearing; no thick discharge.

Normal Vision

Vision completely unaffected.

Key Features

  • No severe pain: Distinguishes from scleritis (which causes deep, boring pain)
  • No photophobia: No significant light sensitivity
  • No vision loss
  • No discharge: Unlike conjunctivitis

Episcleritis vs. Scleritis (Critical Distinction)

  • Episcleritis:
    • Superficial episcleral inflammation
    • Mild discomfort or irritation
    • Bright red or pink
    • Vessels blanch (turn white) with phenylephrine drops
    • Self-limited, benign
    • No systemic disease association usually
    • No treatment often needed
  • Scleritis:
    • Deep scleral inflammation
    • Severe, boring pain
    • Dusky, violaceous (purplish-red)
    • Vessels do NOT blanch with phenylephrine
    • Vision-threatening
    • Associated with systemic autoimmune disease (~40-50%)
    • Requires systemic immunosuppression

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

History

  • Onset, duration of redness?
  • Pain level? (Minimal in episcleritis)
  • Previous episodes?
  • Recent illness?
  • Known systemic diseases?

External Inspection

  • Bright, localized redness
  • Sectoral or diffuse episcleral injection
  • Nodule (if nodular type)
  • Minimal tenderness

Phenylephrine Test (Diagnostic)

  • Topical vasoconstrictor (phenylephrine 2.5%) applied
  • Episcleritis: Superficial episcleral vessels blanch (turn white), redness resolves temporarily
  • Scleritis: Deep scleral vessels do NOT blanch, redness persists
  • Key test to differentiate episcleritis from scleritis

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Episcleral vascular congestion
  • No scleral thinning or deep inflammation (would indicate scleritis)
  • Normal cornea, anterior chamber, intraocular pressure

Laboratory/Imaging

  • Usually NOT needed
  • If recurrent or atypical, may consider basic labs (CBC, ESR, CRP, uric acid) to rule out systemic associations
  • Extensive workup not typically warranted

Treatment

Observation (Often Sufficient)

  • Many cases of simple episcleritis resolve without treatment
  • Reassurance that condition is benign
  • Spontaneous resolution in 1-3 weeks

Symptomatic Treatment

Artificial Tears (Lubricating Drops)

  • Frequent use for comfort
  • Reduces irritation
  • First-line symptomatic treatment

Cold Compresses

  • Apply to closed eyelid
  • Soothing, reduces redness temporarily

Anti-Inflammatory Treatment (If Symptoms Bothersome)

Topical NSAIDs

  • Ketorolac, diclofenac eye drops
  • Reduce inflammation, discomfort
  • Often sufficient

Mild Topical Corticosteroids (Short-Term)

  • Loteprednol, fluorometholone
  • For more symptomatic cases or nodular episcleritis
  • Short course (1-2 weeks)
  • Speeds resolution
  • Monitor for steroid side effects (IOP elevation)

Oral NSAIDs

  • Ibuprofen, naproxen
  • Occasionally used for persistent or recurrent cases

Treatment NOT Needed

  • Systemic immunosuppression (unlike scleritis)
  • Antibiotics (not bacterial infection)

Prognosis and Outcomes

Excellent Prognosis

  • Benign, self-limited condition
  • No vision loss
  • No permanent eye damage

Resolution

  • Simple episcleritis: Resolves in 1-2 weeks (often spontaneously)
  • Nodular episcleritis: May take several weeks to months
  • Treatment can shorten duration

Recurrence

  • Common (~30-60% recurrence rate)
  • Recurrent episodes typically benign as well
  • May recur in same or opposite eye
  • If very frequent recurrences, consider underlying trigger (rosacea, etc.)

No Long-Term Sequelae

  • No scar tissue, no vision impairment

When to See a Doctor

See Ophthalmologist If:

  • Eye redness with discomfort (to confirm diagnosis)
  • Severe pain (could be scleritis or other serious condition)
  • Vision changes
  • Light sensitivity
  • Not improving after 2-3 weeks
  • Recurrent episodes (may warrant evaluation for underlying cause)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this episcleritis or scleritis?
  • Do I need treatment, or will it resolve on its own?
  • Do I need any tests for systemic disease?
  • How long will this last?
  • What can I do to prevent recurrences?

Bottom Line

Episcleritis is a common, benign inflammation of the superficial episcleral tissue causing localized eye redness and mild irritation. Unlike scleritis (a serious, painful condition), episcleritis is self-limited, painless or minimally uncomfortable, and not associated with vision loss or systemic disease. It typically resolves spontaneously within 1-3 weeks without treatment. Lubricating eye drops provide symptomatic relief, and topical NSAIDs or mild steroids can speed resolution if symptoms are bothersome. The phenylephrine blanching test helps distinguish episcleritis (vessels blanch) from scleritis (vessels don't blanch). While recurrences are common, each episode is benign with an excellent prognosis. If you have eye redness with severe pain or vision changes, see a doctor immediately to rule out scleritis or other serious conditions.
