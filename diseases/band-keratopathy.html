What Is Band Keratopathy?

Band keratopathy is a corneal disorder characterized by deposition of calcium salts (calcium phosphate and calcium carbonate) in Bowman's layer of the cornea—a layer just beneath the corneal epithelium. The calcium deposits form a grayish-white band across the central cornea, typically in the interpalpebral zone (the area of cornea exposed between the eyelids when eyes are open). The band has a characteristic horizontal orientation and often has a "Swiss cheese" appearance with multiple clear holes or lucencies where corneal nerves penetrate.

Band keratopathy is not a disease itself but rather a sign of underlying chronic eye disease, systemic conditions causing elevated calcium levels (hypercalcemia), or long-term ocular inflammation. Common causes include chronic uveitis (eye inflammation), end-stage glaucoma with long-term use of certain eye drops, systemic hypercalcemia (from hyperparathyroidism, sarcoidosis, vitamin D toxicity, etc.), and chronic corneal disease. The calcium deposits can cause blurred vision, glare, foreign body sensation, and photophobia. Treatment involves removing the calcium deposits with EDTA chelation therapy or superficial keratectomy, along with addressing the underlying cause.

Key Facts About Band Keratopathy

  • Calcium deposition in cornea: Calcium salts deposited in Bowman's layer.
  • Band-shaped appearance: Horizontal grayish-white band across central cornea.
  • Secondary to other conditions: Chronic uveitis, hypercalcemia, chronic eye disease.
  • Symptoms: blurred vision, glare, irritation
  • "Swiss cheese" pattern: Clear holes where corneal nerves penetrate.
  • Treatment: EDTA chelation: Chemical removal of calcium deposits.
  • Can recur: If underlying cause not addressed.
  • More common in chronic eye disease patients

Understanding band keratopathy helps identify underlying systemic or ocular conditions and guides appropriate treatment.

Causes and Risk Factors

Mechanism of Calcium Deposition

  • Calcium salts precipitate in Bowman's layer and anterior stroma
  • Occurs when:
    • Local tissue pH elevated (alkaline)
    • Calcium or phosphate levels in eye elevated
    • Chronic inflammation damages corneal tissue
  • Carbon dioxide evaporates from exposed corneal surface → pH rises → calcium precipitates

Common Causes

Chronic Ocular Inflammation

  • Chronic uveitis: Most common cause
    • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)-associated uveitis
    • Other chronic anterior uveitis
  • Chronic iridocyclitis
  • Inflammation → tissue damage → calcium deposition

Systemic Hypercalcemia

  • Hyperparathyroidism: Parathyroid gland overactivity → elevated serum calcium
  • Sarcoidosis: Granulomatous disease causing hypercalcemia
  • Vitamin D toxicity: Excessive vitamin D supplementation
  • Milk-alkali syndrome: Excessive calcium and alkali intake
  • Hypophosphatasia: Rare metabolic disorder
  • Chronic renal failure: Abnormal calcium/phosphate metabolism
  • Elevated calcium in blood → elevated in eye → deposition

Chronic Glaucoma with Long-Term Eye Drop Use

  • Certain glaucoma drops (especially older formulations with preservatives or phosphate-containing drops)
  • Chronic use → band keratopathy

Chronic Corneal Disease

  • Chronic corneal edema
  • Interstitial keratitis
  • Phthisis bulbi: End-stage, shrunken, non-functional eye

Silicone Oil in Eye

  • After retinal detachment surgery with silicone oil tamponade
  • Prolonged contact with cornea → band keratopathy

Hereditary Causes (Rare)

  • Inherited corneal dystrophies with calcification

Risk Factors

  • Chronic uveitis (especially JIA-associated)
  • Long-standing glaucoma on multiple medications
  • Hypercalcemia from any cause
  • Chronic dry eyes, exposure keratopathy
  • History of intraocular surgery or trauma

Symptoms and Appearance

Visual Appearance (on Examination)

  • Horizontal band: Grayish-white or whitish opacification crossing central cornea
  • Location: Interpalpebral zone (3 o'clock to 9 o'clock, band-shaped)
  • Peripheral clear zones: Usually spares extreme peripheral cornea (covered by lids)
  • "Swiss cheese" pattern: Multiple small clear holes or lucencies within band (where corneal nerves penetrate Bowman's layer)
  • Rough surface: Calcium deposits can elevate epithelium, creating irregular surface
  • Bilateral or unilateral: Can affect one or both eyes depending on cause

Patient Symptoms

Blurred Vision

Calcium deposits scatter light, obstruct vision; central location affects visual axis.

Glare and Photophobia

Light scattering from irregular surface; sensitivity to bright light.

Foreign Body Sensation

Rough, elevated calcium deposits irritate; gritty feeling.

Tearing

Irritation from deposits.

Pain (If Severe)

Epithelial breakdown over deposits can cause pain.

Decreased Contrast

Washed-out vision from light scatter.

Severity

  • Mild: Minimal symptoms, mild haze
  • Moderate: Noticeable vision impairment, irritation
  • Severe: Significant vision loss, chronic pain from epithelial defects

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy

  • Characteristic horizontal band of whitish deposits in Bowman's layer
  • "Swiss cheese" lucencies
  • Rough, irregular corneal surface
  • Diagnostic based on appearance

Investigate Underlying Cause

Ophthalmic History

  • Chronic uveitis?
  • Glaucoma? Eye drop use?
  • Previous eye surgeries (silicone oil, corneal transplant)?

Systemic Workup (If No Obvious Ocular Cause)

  • Serum calcium level: Check for hypercalcemia
  • Serum phosphate
  • Parathyroid hormone (PTH): Rule out hyperparathyroidism
  • Vitamin D levels
  • Renal function tests (BUN, creatinine): Chronic kidney disease?
  • Alkaline phosphatase
  • Consider sarcoidosis workup if clinical suspicion

Differential Diagnosis

  • Other corneal opacities (scars, dystrophies)
  • Salzmann's nodular degeneration
  • Corneal calcific degeneration (different pattern)

Treatment

Address Underlying Cause (Critical)

  • Treat chronic uveitis (steroid drops, immunosuppression)
  • Correct hypercalcemia (treat hyperparathyroidism, stop vitamin D, etc.)
  • Switch glaucoma drops if implicated
  • Prevents recurrence after calcium removal

Calcium Removal (If Symptomatic)

EDTA Chelation Therapy (First-Line)

  • Procedure:
    • Outpatient, in-office or OR
    • Topical anesthesia (numbing drops)
    • Corneal epithelium debrided (removed) in affected area
    • EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) solution applied to exposed Bowman's layer
      • EDTA chelates (binds) calcium, dissolving deposits
      • Typically 0.05-0.2M EDTA, pH neutral
    • Application for several minutes with gentle scrubbing
    • Rinse, repeat if needed
    • Bandage contact lens or pressure patch applied
  • Effectiveness: Removes superficial calcium; improves vision and comfort
  • Healing: Epithelium regrows over days to weeks
  • Disadvantages:
    • May not remove deep deposits completely
    • Can recur if underlying cause not treated

Superficial Keratectomy (Alternative or Adjunct)

  • Surgical removal of affected Bowman's layer with blade or excimer laser
  • More invasive than EDTA alone
  • Used for dense, thick deposits not responding to EDTA
  • Can be combined with EDTA

Phototherapeutic Keratectomy (PTK) with Excimer Laser

  • Laser ablation of superficial cornea including calcium deposits
  • Smooth surface, often combined with EDTA
  • Good outcomes, but more expensive

Symptomatic Treatment

Lubricating Drops

  • Frequent artificial tears for irritation, foreign body sensation

Bandage Contact Lens

  • If epithelial defects, protects while healing

For Minimal Symptoms

  • Observation
  • Lubrication
  • Treat when symptoms worsen or vision significantly affected

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Treatment (EDTA Chelation or Keratectomy)

  • Symptom relief: Improved vision, reduced glare and irritation
  • Visual outcomes: Variable depending on:
    • Severity and depth of deposits
    • Underlying corneal health
    • Presence of other eye disease
  • Many patients achieve significant improvement

Recurrence

  • Common if underlying cause not addressed
  • Calcium can re-deposit over months to years
  • May require repeat EDTA treatment
  • Controlling hypercalcemia or uveitis reduces recurrence

Long-Term

  • Depends on underlying condition
  • Chronic uveitis patients may have recurrent band keratopathy
  • If hypercalcemia corrected, may not recur

When to See a Doctor

See Ophthal mologist or Cornea Specialist If:

  • Whitish band or haze across center of cornea
  • Blurred vision, glare
  • Chronic eye irritation, foreign body sensation
  • Known chronic uveitis or glaucoma with new corneal changes

Systemic Evaluation

  • If band keratopathy diagnosed without obvious ocular cause, see primary care doctor or endocrinologist
  • Check for hypercalcemia

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What is causing my band keratopathy?
  • Do I have high calcium levels?
  • Should I have EDTA chelation?
  • What are the risks and benefits of treatment?
  • Will it come back?
  • How can we prevent recurrence?

Bottom Line

Band keratopathy is deposition of calcium salts in the cornea (Bowman's layer), forming a characteristic horizontal grayish-white band across the central cornea with a "Swiss cheese" pattern of clear holes. It's not a disease itself but a sign of underlying chronic eye inflammation (especially chronic uveitis), systemic hypercalcemia (hyperparathyroidism, sarcoidosis, vitamin D toxicity), chronic glaucoma with long-term eye drops, or other chronic corneal disease. Symptoms include blurred vision, glare, photophobia, and foreign body sensation from the rough, irregular corneal surface. Treatment involves EDTA chelation therapy to chemically dissolve and remove the calcium deposits, often combined with superficial keratectomy or PTK laser ablation for dense deposits. Treating the underlying cause (controlling uveitis, correcting hypercalcemia) is critical to prevent recurrence. If you notice a whitish band across your cornea with vision changes or irritation, see an ophthalmologist for evaluation and appropriate systemic workup to identify the underlying cause.
