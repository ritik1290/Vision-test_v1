What Is Posterior Vitreous Detachment?

Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) is a common, usually benign age-related condition in which the vitreous gel—the clear, jelly-like substance filling the eye—separates from the retina. As we age, the vitreous undergoes liquefaction (syneresis), transitioning from a gel to a more liquid state. This liquefied vitreous can no longer maintain its attachment to the retina, and eventually the posterior hyaloid (the back surface of the vitreous) detaches from the retina and collapses forward. PVD typically occurs suddenly and causes symptoms of floaters (especially a large floater or "cobweb") and flashes of light as the vitreous tugs on the retina during the detachment process.

PVD is extremely common, affecting nearly everyone by age 80-90, and in itself is not vision-threatening. Most cases are uncomplicated and benign—the floaters and flashes gradually diminish over weeks to months as the brain adapts. However, during the detachment, there's a risk (~10-15%) that the vitreous will pull hard enough on the retina to cause a retinal tear or, less commonly, retinal detachment, both of which are serious and require urgent treatment. Therefore, anyone experiencing sudden new floaters and/or flashes should see an eye doctor promptly for a dilated exam to rule out retinal tear.

Key Facts About Posterior Vitreous Detachment

  • Vitreous separates from retina: Normal aging process.
  • Very common: Most people >60 experience PVD.
  • Sudden floaters and flashes: Hallmark symptoms.
  • Usually benign: Not vision-threatening in itself.
  • Risk of retinal tear (~10-15%): Can lead to retinal detachment if untreated.
  • Urgent evaluation needed to rule out tear.
  • No treatment for PVD itself: Observation.
  • Flashes/floaters improve over time in uncomplicated cases.

Understanding PVD emphasizes the importance of prompt evaluation for new floaters/flashes to detect and treat retinal tears early.

Causes and Risk Factors

Age-Related Vitreous Liquefaction (Primary Cause)

  • Vitreous gel liquefies with aging (syneresis)
  • Liquid pockets form within gel
  • Eventually, vitreous can no longer maintain attachment to retina → detaches
  • Normal, universal aging process

Risk Factors for Earlier/Increased Risk of PVD

  • Age >50: Incidence increases sharply with age
  • Myopia (nearsightedness): Myopic eyes have longer axial length → vitreous more liquid, PVD occurs earlier
  • Previous eye surgery or trauma
  • Inflammation (uveitis)
  • Diabetes (can cause earlier vitreous changes)
  • Fellow eye PVD: If one eye has PVD, other eye at higher risk in subsequent years

Recognizing the Symptoms

Sudden Onset of Floaters

New floater(s); often a large, prominent one (cobweb, ring, or "Weiss ring"—circular floater from detached optic nerve head attachment); hallmark symptom.

Flashes of Light (Photopsia)

Brief flashes, often in peripheral vision, especially when moving eyes or in dark; from vitreous tugging on retina.

No Pain

Completely painless.

Usually Normal Vision

Vision typically not affected if uncomplicated; floaters annoying but don't block vision significantly.

Gradual Improvement

Floaters/flashes gradually diminish over weeks to months as brain adapts.

Red Flags (Retinal Tear/Detachment)

Shower of floaters, curtain/shadow in vision, sudden vision loss—urgent!

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Sudden new floaters? Flashes?
  • Age? (>50 typical for PVD)
  • Myopia?

Dilated Fundus Examination (Essential)

  • Purpose: Rule out retinal tear or detachment
  • Findings in uncomplicated PVD:
    • Weiss ring visible (donut-shaped opacity—detached vitreous from optic nerve head)
    • Mobile vitreous opacities
    • No retinal tear, no retinal detachment
  • Examine peripheral retina carefully (360° scleral depressed exam) to look for tears

Additional Testing

  • B-scan ultrasound if view obscured
  • OCT not typically needed for PVD diagnosis

Complications

Retinal Tear (~10-15% of PVD)

  • During PVD, vitreous may pull hard on retina → tear
  • Symptoms: Sudden shower of floaters, increased flashes, possible shadow/curtain in vision
  • Treatment: Urgent laser photocoagulation or cryotherapy to seal tear and prevent retinal detachment

Retinal Detachment (Rare, ~1-2%)

  • If retinal tear untreated, fluid can seep under retina → retinal detachment
  • Symptoms: Curtain or shadow in peripheral or central vision, sudden vision loss
  • Treatment: Urgent surgical repair (scleral buckle, vitrectomy, pneumatic retinopexy)

Vitreous Hemorrhage (~5-10%)

  • PVD can cause small retinal vessels to bleed
  • Symptoms: Sudden increase in floaters, hazy vision
  • Usually mild, clears on its own, but signals need to check for retinal tear

Treatment

Uncomplicated PVD (No Retinal Tear)

  • Observation only
  • No treatment needed for PVD itself
  • Floaters and flashes gradually improve over weeks to months
  • Reassurance that symptoms will diminish
  • Follow-up exam: Repeat dilated exam in 4-6 weeks to recheck for delayed retinal tears (some tears develop weeks after initial PVD)

Floaters (If Bothersome Long-Term)

  • Most adapt to floaters over time; brain learns to ignore them
  • Vitrectomy surgery: For debilitating floaters (rare); removes vitreous and floaters
    • Reserved for severe cases
    • Risks: cataract, retinal detachment
    • Not typically performed for typical PVD floaters
  • YAG laser vitreolysis: Laser to break up large floaters (investigational; limited evidence)

If Retinal Tear Found

  • Laser photocoagulation or cryotherapy: Seal tear urgently to prevent detachment
  • Outpatient procedure; highly effective

If Retinal Detachment Found

  • Urgent surgical repair

Prognosis and Outcomes

Uncomplicated PVD

  • Excellent prognosis
  • No vision loss
  • Floaters and flashes diminish over time (weeks to months)
  • Many people eventually unaware of symptoms

With Retinal Tear (If Treated Promptly)

  • Excellent outcomes with laser/cryo treatment
  • Prevents retinal detachment in ~95%+
  • Vision preserved

With Retinal Detachment

  • Depends on extent and promptness of surgery
  • Better outcomes if macula not yet detached
  • Emphasizes need for urgent evaluation

When to See a Doctor

Urgent - See Eye Doctor Within 24-48 Hours If:

  • Sudden onset of new floaters
  • Flashes of light
  • Need dilated exam to rule out retinal tear

EMERGENCY - See Immediately/Go to ER If:

  • Sudden shower of floaters (many new floaters at once)
  • Curtain or shadow in peripheral or central vision
  • Sudden vision loss
  • Possible retinal tear or detachment

Return for Re-Evaluation If:

  • Significant increase in floaters after initial exam
  • New flashes
  • Shadow/curtain develops

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have posterior vitreous detachment?
  • Did you find any retinal tears?
  • When should I come back for follow-up?
  • What symptoms should prompt me to return sooner?
  • Will the floaters go away?

Bottom Line

Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) is a common age-related condition in which the vitreous gel separates from the retina, causing sudden onset of floaters (especially a large cobweb or ring) and flashes of light. It's a normal aging process affecting most people by their 70s-80s and is usually benign. In itself, PVD is not vision-threatening, and symptoms gradually improve over weeks to months. However, in 10-15% of cases, the vitreous tugs hard enough on the retina to cause a retinal tear, which can lead to retinal detachment if untreated. Therefore, anyone experiencing sudden new floaters or flashes should see an eye doctor within 24-48 hours for a dilated exam to rule out retinal tear. If no tear is found, observation is all that's needed. If a tear is detected, urgent laser or cryotherapy treatment can seal it and prevent retinal detachment with excellent success rates. Warning signs of retinal tear/detachment include a sudden shower of floaters, a curtain/shadow in vision, or sudden vision loss—these require immediate emergency evaluation.
