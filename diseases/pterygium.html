What Is a Pterygium?

A pterygium (pronounced tur-IJ-ee-um, plural: pterygia) is a raised, wedge-shaped growth of tissue on the white part of the eye (conjunctiva) that extends onto the clear front surface (cornea). It typically starts in the corner of the eye near the nose and grows toward the center of the eye. The growth appears as a fleshy, pink or yellowish triangular bump and can vary from small and barely noticeable to large enough to interfere with vision.

Pterygia are benign (non-cancerous) but can cause significant discomfort, redness, and vision problems if left untreated. They're strongly linked to chronic sun exposure, which is why they're sometimes called "surfer's eye"—common in people who spend lots of time outdoors near water, sand, or snow that reflects UV light. While pterygia can be removed surgically, they have a tendency to recur, so prevention through UV protection is crucial.

Key Facts About Pterygium

  • Sun-related growth: Primarily caused by chronic UV exposure.
  • Common in sunny climates: More prevalent near equator, in outdoor workers, surfers, farmers.
  • Benign but bothersome: Not cancer but can cause irritation and vision loss.
  • Grows onto cornea: Can extend across clear part of eye, blocking vision.
  • Usually starts nasally: Begins on inner corner (near nose) in ~90% of cases.
  • Slow progression: Grows gradually over months to years; some remain stable.
  • Can recur after removal: Recurrence rate 5-40% depending on technique.
  • Preventable: UV protection with sunglasses and hats reduces risk.

Understanding pterygia helps people recognize risk factors and take preventive measures, especially in sunny environments.

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Cause: UV Radiation

Chronic ultraviolet light exposure is the main culprit:

  • UV light damages conjunctival cells
  • Triggers abnormal growth and proliferation of tissue
  • Thickened, fibrovascular tissue forms
  • Growth extends from conjunctiva onto cornea
  • Cumulative lifetime exposure determines risk

Additional Environmental Factors

  • Wind: Chronic irritation and drying
  • Dust: Particulate irritation
  • Dry climates: Low humidity exacerbates dryness
  • Reflected UV: Water, sand, snow amplify exposure
  • These factors work synergistically with UV

High-Risk Populations and Locations

  • Geographic: Latitudes between 37° north and south (the "pterygium belt")
  • Outdoor occupations: Farmers, construction workers, fishermen, landscapers
  • Outdoor enthusiasts: Surfers, sailors, skiers, golfers
  • Equatorial regions: Higher UV index
  • High altitude: Increased UV exposure

Demographic Risk Factors

  • Age: More common in adults 20-50; rare in children
  • Gender: Twice as common in men (likely due to greater outdoor occupational exposure)
  • Light skin/eyes: Lower melanin protection from UV
  • Family history: Some genetic predisposition

Other Contributing Factors

  • Chronic dry eye: Reduced tear protection
  • Eye irritation: Allergens, smoke, chemicals
  • Poor UV protection: Not wearing sunglasses

Pinguecula vs. Pterygium

Often confused:

  • Pinguecula:
    • Yellowish raised bump on white of eye
    • Does NOT grow onto cornea
    • Similar causes (UV, irritation)
    • Often precedes pterygium
    • Usually just cosmetic issue
  • Pterygium:
    • Pink, fleshy, triangular growth
    • Grows FROM conjunctiva ONTO cornea
    • Can affect vision
    • More problematic
  • Both related to sun/irritation; pterygium more serious

Recognizing the Symptoms

Visible Growth

Fleshy, pink, triangular bump on white of eye extending toward cornea; most obvious symptom.

Redness & Irritation

Eye appears bloodshot, especially around the growth; feels irritated or inflamed.

Gritty Foreign Body Sensation

Feels like sand or something stuck in eye; persistent discomfort.

Dry Eye Symptoms

Burning, dryness, excessive tearing (reflex tears from irritation).

Blurred Vision

If pterygium grows across cornea, disrupts vision; can induce astigmatism.

Cosmetic Concern

Visible growth can be embarrassing; eye appears abnormal.

Symptom Variability

  • Asymptomatic: Small pterygia may cause no symptoms
  • Intermittent inflammation: Symptoms come and go; worse with sun, wind, dryness
  • Progressive: As pterygium grows, symptoms worsen

Stages of Growth

  • Early/Small:
    • Barely onto cornea edge
    • Minimal symptoms or cosmetic only
    • May remain stable for years
  • Moderate:
    • Encroaching toward pupil area
    • Intermittent redness, irritation
    • May cause astigmatism (irregular corneal shape)
  • Advanced:
    • Extends significantly across cornea
    • Approaching or covering pupil
    • Significant vision impairment
    • Chronic inflammation

When Symptoms Worsen

  • After sun exposure
  • Windy, dusty days
  • Dry environments
  • During periods of growth (may have sudden inflammation)

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Usually diagnosed by appearance alone:

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Magnified view of eye structures
  • Appearance: Triangular, vascularized (blood vessels visible) tissue growing from conjunctiva onto cornea
  • Apex points toward pupil
  • Base at limbus (junction of cornea and conjunctiva)
  • Assess extent of corneal involvement
  • Evaluate inflammation (active/inflamed vs. atrophic/quiet)

Measure Corneal Extent

  • How far onto cornea (distance from limbus)
  • How close to visual axis/pupil
  • Guides treatment decisions

Corneal Topography (If Needed)

  • Maps corneal surface
  • Detects induced astigmatism from pterygium pulling on cornea
  • Useful for pre-surgical planning

Differential Diagnosis

Other conditions that may appear similar:

  • Pinguecula: Doesn't extend onto cornea
  • Conjunctival nevus: Pigmented, doesn't grow onto cornea
  • Conjunctival tumor (rare): More irregular, atypical appearance
  • Pseudopterygium: Scar tissue adhering conjunctiva to cornea (from trauma/chemical burn); can be separated with probe

Biopsy

  • Rarely needed; appearance usually diagnostic
  • Considered if atypical features raise cancer concern
  • Can be done on excised tissue if surgery performed

Potential Complications

Vision Impairment

  • Direct blockage: Pterygium grows across pupil, obstructs vision
  • Induced astigmatism: Pulls on cornea, distorts shape, blurs vision
  • Permanent scarring: Even after removal, corneal scarring may persist if pterygium was large/chronic

Chronic Inflammation

  • Persistent redness and irritation
  • Affects quality of life
  • May require frequent lubricant use or anti-inflammatory drops

Restricted Eye Movement (Rare)

  • Very large pterygium can tether eye muscles
  • Limits motility
  • Uncommon with modern early intervention

Cosmetic Concerns

  • Visible abnormality
  • Self-consciousness
  • Social/professional impact

Recurrence After Surgery

  • Can grow back after removal (see Surgery section)
  • Recurrent pterygia often more aggressive
  • May require repeat surgery with adjunctive treatments

Treatment Options

Observation (Mild, Asymptomatic Cases)

  • Appropriate for: Small pterygium not affecting vision, minimal symptoms
  • Monitor for growth during routine eye exams
  • Emphasize UV protection to prevent progression
  • No treatment needed unless symptoms develop or growth threatens vision

Medical Management (Conservative Treatment)

For symptom relief and inflammation control:

Lubricant Eye Drops (Artificial Tears)

  • Relieve dryness and foreign body sensation
  • Preservative-free formulations best for frequent use
  • Use as needed throughout day
  • Doesn't shrink pterygium but improves comfort

Anti-Inflammatory Eye Drops

  • Corticosteroid drops: For acute inflammation episodes
  • Short-term use (days to weeks) to calm red, angry pterygium
  • Reduces size temporarily in some cases
  • Long-term steroids not recommended (side effects: glaucoma, cataracts)
  • NSAIDs (ketorolac, etc.): Alternative for inflammation

Decongestant Drops ("Get the Red Out")

  • Temporarily reduce redness
  • Not recommended for regular use (rebound redness)
  • Don't address underlying problem

Surgical Removal

Indicated when pterygium:

  • Threatens or affects vision
  • Extends significantly onto cornea (approaching visual axis)
  • Causes persistent irritation not relieved by medical treatment
  • Cosmetic concerns (patient request)
  • Interferes with contact lens wear
  • Progresses despite medical management

Surgical Removal (Pterygium Excision)

When Surgery Is Recommended

  • Encroaching on visual axis (within 3mm of pupil center)
  • Reducing vision
  • Inducing significant astigmatism
  • Chronic, bothersome symptoms
  • Cosmetic request

Basic Excision Procedure

  • Anesthesia: Local injection or topical drops
  • Excision: Pterygium tissue carefully dissected and removed from cornea and conjunctiva
  • Bare sclera: Leaves exposed area on white of eye
  • Issue: High recurrence rate (40-80%) with simple excision alone

Modern Techniques to Reduce Recurrence

Conjunctival Autograft (Most Common, Gold Standard)

  • After removing pterygium, healthy conjunctiva taken from under upper eyelid
  • Graft sutured or glued to bare sclera area
  • Advantage: Reduces recurrence to ~5-10%
  • Disadvantage: More complex, longer surgery time

Amniotic Membrane Graft

  • Preserved amniotic membrane (from placenta) used instead of conjunctival graft
  • Anti-inflammatory, anti-scarring properties
  • Spares healthy conjunctiva
  • Recurrence rates similar to autograft

Adjunctive Treatments to Prevent Recurrence

  • Mitomycin C (MMC):
    • Chemotherapy agent applied topically during surgery
    • Inhibits cell proliferation, reduces scarring/recurrence
    • Very effective but carries risks (scleral thinning, infection if overused)
    • Used selectively, especially for recurrent pterygia
  • Beta radiation (rare): Applied intraoperatively; less common now

Surgical Procedure Details

  • Duration: 30-45 minutes
  • Outpatient: Go home same day
  • Anesthesia: Local; awake but eye numb
  • Discomfort: Mild to moderate during healing

Post-Operative Recovery

Immediate (Days 1-7)

  • Eye red, irritated, foreign body sensation
  • Tearing, light sensitivity
  • Eye patch or shield for 24 hours
  • Antibiotic and steroid drops 4 times daily
  • Lubricant drops frequently
  • Avoid rubbing eye

Short-Term (Weeks 1-4)

  • Redness, irritation gradually improve
  • Graft area may look inflamed initially (normal)
  • Continue eye drops as prescribed
  • Avoid swimming, dusty environments
  • Wear sunglasses outdoors

Long-Term (Months 1-6)

  • Eye appearance normalizes
  • Vision stabilizes (may improve if astigmatism corrected)
  • Graft integrates, becomes less visible
  • Monitor for recurrence

Risks and Complications of Surgery

  • Recurrence: 5-40% depending on technique; most common "complication"
  • Infection: Rare with antibiotics
  • Scarring: Corneal or conjunctival
  • Graft failure/dislodgement: Rare
  • Double vision (diplopia): If eye muscles affected (rare)
  • Persistent redness
  • Scleral thinning: If MMC overused

Recurrent Pterygium

  • Usually occurs within first year after surgery
  • More aggressive growth than original
  • Treatment: Repeat surgery with conjunctival/amniotic graft + MMC
  • Prevention crucial (UV protection)

Prevention

UV Protection - Most Important

  • Sunglasses:
    • 100% UVA and UVB blocking
    • Wraparound style best (blocks side UV)
    • Wear whenever outdoors, even cloudy days
    • Polarized lenses reduce glare
  • Wide-brimmed hat: Provides additional UV protection
  • Avoid peak sun hours: 10am-4pm when UV strongest

Protect Against Wind and Dust

  • Wraparound sunglasses shield from wind
  • Safety goggles for dusty work environments
  • Avoid direct fan or air vent blowing on eyes

Manage Dry Eyes

  • Use artificial tears if eyes feel dry
  • Stay hydrated
  • Humidifier in dry climates

After Pterygium Removal

  • Critical: UV protection even more important to prevent recurrence
  • Lifetime commitment to sunglasses and hats
  • Regular eye exams to monitor for recurrence

Prognosis and Outcomes

Natural History Without Treatment

  • Variable: Some remain small and stable for years; others grow progressively
  • Unpredictable growth rate: May grow slowly or have spurts
  • No spontaneous regression: Doesn't go away on its own
  • If untreated and large: Can significantly impair vision

With Conservative Treatment

  • Symptom relief with lubricants and anti-inflammatory drops
  • Doesn't eliminate pterygium
  • Appropriate for mild cases

With Surgical Treatment

  • Vision improvement: Often improves if astigmatism was present
  • Symptom resolution: Irritation and redness resolve
  • Cosmetic improvement
  • Success rate: 90-95% with modern techniques (conjunctival autograft)
  • Recurrence: 5-10% with autograft; higher with simple excision

Long-Term Outlook

  • With UV protection: Low risk of new pterygium or recurrence
  • Without protection: Ongoing risk, especially in sunny climates
  • Regular monitoring important

When to See a Doctor

Schedule Eye Exam If:

  • Notice growth on white of eye
  • Persistent redness or irritation
  • Foreign body sensation
  • Vision changes
  • Growth enlarging

Regular Monitoring If Pterygium Diagnosed

  • Annual eye exams to assess size and progression
  • More frequent if growing
  • After surgery: Check for recurrence every 6-12 months initially

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • How large is my pterygium?
  • Is it affecting my vision?
  • Should I treat it now or observe?
  • What are my surgical options?
  • What is the recurrence rate with your technique?
  • What can I do to prevent it from growing?
  • How often should I have follow-up exams?

Bottom Line

Pterygium is a common, benign eye growth strongly linked to UV exposure, particularly in sunny, outdoor environments. While often just a cosmetic nuisance or causing mild irritation, larger pterygia can significantly impair vision. Prevention through UV protection (sunglasses, hats) is critical, especially if you spend time outdoors. Small, stable pterygia can be observed, but growing or symptomatic ones can be successfully removed surgically with low recurrence rates using modern techniques. After removal or if you have a pterygium, lifelong UV protection is essential to prevent recurrence or new growth.
