What Is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a serious diabetes complication that affects the blood vessels in the retina—the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. It is the leading cause of new cases of blindness among adults aged 20-74 in developed countries. The condition develops when chronically high blood sugar levels damage the tiny blood vessels in the retina, causing them to leak fluid, bleed, or grow abnormally.

The critical aspect of diabetic retinopathy is that it often develops with no early warning signs. By the time symptoms appear, the disease may already be advanced. However, with proper diabetes management and regular eye exams, diabetic retinopathy can be prevented or its progression significantly slowed.

Key Facts About Diabetic Retinopathy

  • Leading cause of blindness: DR is the #1 cause of new blindness in working-age adults in the United States.
  • Affects all diabetics: Both Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes can lead to diabetic retinopathy.
  • Duration matters: The longer you have diabetes, the higher your risk—nearly all people with Type 1 diabetes and >60% with Type 2 diabetes will develop some degree of retinopathy after 20 years.
  • Often asymptomatic early: You may have diabetic retinopathy without knowing it until vision loss occurs.
  • Preventable: Good blood sugar control can prevent or delay diabetic retinopathy by up to 76%.
  • Treatable: Early detection and timely treatment can prevent 95% of vision loss from diabetic retinopathy.
  • Affects both eyes: Diabetic retinopathy typically affects both eyes.

Understanding diabetic retinopathy and taking proactive steps to protect your vision is crucial for everyone with diabetes. Regular comprehensive dilated eye exams are your best defense against vision loss from this condition.

How Diabetes Affects the Eyes

To understand diabetic retinopathy, it helps to know how diabetes affects your eyes:

The Retina and Blood Vessels

The retina contains a complex network of tiny blood vessels (capillaries) that provide oxygen and nutrients to the light-sensitive retinal tissue. These blood vessels are some of the smallest in your body and are particularly vulnerable to damage from high blood sugar levels.

How High Blood Sugar Causes Damage

When blood sugar levels remain elevated over time, several harmful processes occur:

  • Blood vessel weakening: High glucose levels damage the walls of retinal blood vessels, making them weak and prone to leakage.
  • Protein glycation: Excess sugar attaches to proteins in blood vessel walls, weakening their structure.
  • Inflammation: Chronic high blood sugar triggers inflammatory processes that damage vessel linings.
  • Reduced blood flow: Damaged vessels may close off, depriving areas of the retina of oxygen and nutrients.
  • Abnormal vessel growth: In response to poor oxygen supply, the retina may grow new, abnormal blood vessels that are fragile and leak easily.

Progressive Damage

Diabetic retinopathy progresses through stages, starting with minor blood vessel abnormalities and potentially advancing to sight-threatening complications including:

  • Retinal swelling (macular edema)
  • Bleeding into the vitreous (vitreous hemorrhage)
  • Retinal detachment
  • Glaucoma from abnormal blood vessel growth

Types and Stages of Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is classified into two main types with progressive stages:

Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR)

This is the early stage where retinal blood vessels are damaged but new abnormal vessels haven't formed yet. NPDR has three levels of severity:

Mild NPDR

  • Microaneurysms (tiny bulges in blood vessel walls) appear
  • Usually no symptoms
  • Vision typically unaffected
  • Detected only through eye examination

Moderate NPDR

  • More blood vessels become blocked
  • Blood vessel walls weaken further
  • Retinal swelling may begin (macular edema)
  • Mild vision changes may occur if macula is affected
  • Increases risk of progression to severe stages

Severe NPDR

  • Many more blood vessels are blocked
  • Large areas of retina lose their blood supply
  • Retina sends signals to grow new blood vessels
  • High risk of progressing to proliferative DR
  • Vision may begin to decline if macula is involved

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)

This is the advanced, more dangerous stage where new abnormal blood vessels grow on the retina's surface or into the vitreous gel. PDR can lead to severe vision loss and blindness.

Characteristics of PDR:

  • Neovascularization: New, fragile blood vessels grow in response to poor oxygen supply (ischemia)
  • Vitreous hemorrhage: New vessels are weak and bleed easily into the vitreous, causing sudden vision loss
  • Tractional retinal detachment: Scar tissue from bleeding can pull the retina away from the back of the eye
  • Neovascular glaucoma: New vessels can grow on the iris, blocking fluid drainage and causing dangerous pressure buildup

Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)

DME is a complication that can occur at any stage of diabetic retinopathy. It happens when fluid leaks from damaged blood vessels into the macula (the central part of the retina responsible for sharp vision), causing swelling.

Impact of DME:

  • Can occur in NPDR or PDR
  • Leading cause of vision loss in diabetic retinopathy
  • Causes blurred, distorted central vision
  • May develop slowly or rapidly
  • Treatable with anti-VEGF injections, steroids, or laser

Causes and Risk Factors

The primary cause of diabetic retinopathy is prolonged elevation of blood sugar levels. However, multiple factors influence who develops DR and how quickly it progresses:

Primary Causes

Poor Blood Sugar Control

High blood glucose (hyperglycemia) over time is the main driver. The Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT) showed that intensive blood sugar control reduces diabetic retinopathy risk by 76% and slows progression by 54%.

Duration of Diabetes

The longer you have diabetes, the higher your risk:

  • After 5 years: ~25% of Type 1 diabetics have some DR
  • After 10 years: ~60% of Type 1 diabetics have some DR
  • After 15 years: ~80% of Type 1 diabetics have some DR
  • After 20 years: Nearly 100% of Type 1 and 60% of Type 2 diabetics have some degree of DR

Major Risk Factors

Poor Glycemic Control

  • High HbA1c levels (>7%)
  • Frequent blood sugar fluctuations
  • Poorly managed diabetes

High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

Adds additional stress to already weakened blood vessels. Good blood pressure control (< 140/90 mmHg) reduces DR progression risk by 34%.

High Cholesterol and Lipids

Elevated cholesterol, especially LDL cholesterol and triglycerides, increases risk of hard exudates (lipid deposits) in the retina and macular edema.

Kidney Disease (Diabetic Nephropathy)

People with diabetic kidney disease have a higher risk of diabetic retinopathy, as both are microvascular complications.

Pregnancy

Pregnancy can accelerate diabetic retinopathy, especially if blood sugar control is poor. Pregnant women with diabetes need more frequent eye exams.

Smoking

Smoking damages blood vessels throughout the body, including the eyes, and increases DR risk.

Race and Ethnicity

  • African Americans, Hispanics, and Native Americans have higher rates of diabetic retinopathy
  • May be related to higher diabetes prevalence and access to care

Rapid Improvement in Blood Sugar

Paradoxically, rapidly improving blood sugar control after years of poor control can temporarily worsen retinopathy. This is why gradual improvement is sometimes recommended under medical supervision.

Recognizing the Symptoms

Critical Warning

Early diabetic retinopathy usually has NO symptoms. This is why annual dilated eye exams are absolutely essential for everyone with diabetes, even if vision seems perfect.

Early Stage Symptoms (Often None)

Mild to moderate non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy typically causes no symptoms. The retinal damage is present but hasn't yet affected vision. This is the most important stage to detect through screening, as treatment can prevent progression.

Symptoms of Diabetic Macular Edema

Blurred Central Vision

Gradual or sudden blurring of central vision, making reading and detailed tasks difficult.

Distorted Vision

Straight lines may appear wavy or bent, objects may look distorted.

Altered Color Perception

Colors may appear washed out or less vivid than normal.

Dark or Empty Spots

Blank or dark areas in the center of your vision.

Symptoms of Advanced/Proliferative DR

Floaters

Sudden appearance of dark spots, strings, or cobwebs floating in your vision from bleeding.

Flashes of Light

Brief flashes or streaks of light, often indicating retinal traction.

Fluctuating Vision

Vision that varies throughout the day, often worse in the morning.

Dark Curtain/Veil

A shadow or curtain blocking part of your vision, which may indicate retinal detachment.

Sudden Vision Loss

Sudden, severe vision loss from vitreous hemorrhage or retinal detachment.

Eye Pain (Late Stage)

Severe eye pain may occur with neovascular glaucoma, a serious complication.

Seek Emergency Care For:

  • Sudden appearance of many floaters
  • Sudden vision loss or severe blurring
  • Curtain or shadow over part of your vision
  • Severe eye pain with vision changes

These may indicate vitreous hemorrhage or retinal detachment requiring immediate treatment.

Diagnosis and Screening

Early detection through regular screening is crucial for preventing vision loss from diabetic retinopathy.

Screening Recommendations

Type of Diabetes First Exam Follow-up Frequency
Type 1 Diabetes Within 5 years of diagnosis Annually
Type 2 Diabetes At time of diagnosis Annually (or more frequently if DR present)
Gestational Diabetes Before pregnancy or first trimester Every trimester and 1 year postpartum
With DR Present - Every 3-6 months or as recommended

Diagnostic Tests

1. Comprehensive Dilated Eye Exam

The gold standard for DR detection. Eye drops dilate your pupils, allowing examination of the retina for:

  • Microaneurysms and hemorrhages
  • Blood vessel abnormalities
  • Retinal swelling
  • Fatty deposits (hard exudates)
  • New abnormal blood vessels
  • Retinal detachment

2. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

Non-invasive imaging that creates cross-sectional images of the retina. OCT is essential for:

  • Detecting and measuring macular edema
  • Monitoring DR progression
  • Assessing response to treatment
  • Visualizing retinal layers in detail

3. Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

A dye is injected into your arm and photographs are taken as it passes through retinal blood vessels. FA shows:

  • Leaking blood vessels
  • Areas of poor blood flow (ischemia)
  • Abnormal new blood vessel growth
  • The extent of retinal damage

4. OCT Angiography (OCTA)

A newer, non-invasive alternative to FA that shows blood flow without dye injection.

5. Fundus Photography

Color photographs of the retina create a baseline for tracking changes over time.

6. Visual Acuity Test

Measures how well you can see at various distances to detect vision loss.

7. Tonometry

Measures eye pressure to screen for glaucoma, which can occur with advanced DR.

DR Severity Staging

Based on exam findings, your doctor will classify DR severity:

  • No DR: No abnormalities
  • Mild NPDR: Microaneurysms only
  • Moderate NPDR: More extensive changes
  • Severe NPDR: Extensive hemorrhages and vessel abnormalities
  • PDR: Neovascularization present
  • DME: Noted separately at any stage

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the stage and severity of diabetic retinopathy. The goals are to slow or stop progression and preserve vision.

Medical Management (All Stages)

Blood Sugar Control

The single most important intervention:

  • Target HbA1c < 7% (individualized based on your situation)
  • Regular blood glucose monitoring
  • Medication compliance
  • Dietary management
  • Regular exercise

Blood Pressure Control

  • Target < 140/90 mmHg (< 130/80 for some patients)
  • Reduces DR progression by 34%
  • Antihypertensive medications as needed

Lipid Management

  • Control cholesterol and triglycerides
  • Statins may reduce hard exudates
  • Fenofibrate may slow DR progression

Treatment for Diabetic Macular Edema

Anti-VEGF Injections

The primary treatment for DME. These medications block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), reducing vessel leakage and swelling:

Common anti-VEGF medications:

  • Aflibercept (Eylea): Often first choice, typically every 4-8 weeks
  • Ranibizumab (Lucentis): Monthly or as needed
  • Bevacizumab (Avastin): Off-label, cost-effective option
  • Faricimab (Vabysmo): Newer agent allowing extended dosing

What to expect:

  • Injections performed in office with numbing drops
  • Initial loading phase of monthly injections
  • Maintenance phase with less frequent injections
  • OCT monitoring to guide treatment
  • About 40-50% of patients improve 2+ lines of vision
  • 90% maintain vision

Corticosteroid Injections/Implants

Steroid medications reduce inflammation and vessel leakage:

  • Triamcinolone: Injection lasting 1-3 months
  • Dexamethasone implant (Ozurdex): Lasts 3-6 months
  • Fluocinolone implant (Iluvien, Retisert): Lasts up to 3 years

Considerations:

  • Risk of increased eye pressure (glaucoma)
  • Risk of cataract formation
  • Often used when anti-VEGF doesn't work well
  • May be combined with anti-VEGF

Focal/Grid Laser Photocoagulation

Laser treatment to seal leaking blood vessels in DME:

  • Used less frequently now that injections are available
  • May be combined with anti-VEGF injections
  • Reduces moderate vision loss by 50%
  • Performed in office, usually painless

Treatment for Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy

Panretinal Photocoagulation (PRP)

Scatter laser treatment that shrinks abnormal blood vessels:

  • 1,200-2,000 laser spots applied to peripheral retina
  • Reduces oxygen demand, stopping new vessel growth
  • Usually done in 1-2 sessions
  • May cause some peripheral vision loss (trade-off for saving central vision)
  • Night vision may be affected
  • Prevents severe vision loss in >90% of cases

Anti-VEGF Injections for PDR

Increasingly used as alternative or addition to laser:

  • Can cause rapid regression of new vessels
  • May avoid or reduce need for laser
  • Requires ongoing injections
  • Often combined with PRP

Vitrectomy Surgery

Surgical removal of vitreous gel for complications of PDR:

Indications:

  • Vitreous hemorrhage that doesn't clear
  • Tractional retinal detachment
  • Severe fibrous tissue

Procedure:

  • Performed in operating room under local or general anesthesia
  • Tiny instruments remove blood and scar tissue
  • Laser may be applied during surgery
  • Gas bubble or oil may be placed to hold retina in place
  • Recovery takes several weeks to months

Outcomes:

  • About 70-90% maintain or improve vision
  • Success depends on extent of damage before surgery

Prevention: Your Best Defense

The best approach to diabetic retinopathy is prevention through excellent diabetes management and regular screening:

Blood Sugar Control (Most Important)

Maintaining target blood glucose significantly reduces DR risk:

  • Target HbA1c: < 7% for most adults (individualized targets)
  • DCCT/EDIC Study findings: Intensive glucose control reduces DR by 76% and slows progression by 54%
  • Monitor blood sugar regularly
  • Take medications as prescribed
  • Work with your diabetes care team

Blood Pressure Control

  • Target < 140/90 mmHg (some patients < 130/80)
  • Reduces DR progression by 34%
  • Take BP medications as prescribed
  • Reduce salt intake
  • Maintain healthy weight

Lipid Management

  • Target LDL < 100 mg/dL (< 70 mg/dL if high risk)
  • Fenofibrate may slow DR progression
  • Healthy diet low in saturated fats

Regular Eye Exams

  • Type 1: First exam within 5 years of diagnosis, then annually
  • Type 2: Exam at diagnosis, then annually
  • Pregnancy: Before conception and each trimester
  • More frequent if DR detected

Healthy Lifestyle

Exercise

  • At least 150 minutes of moderate activity weekly
  • Improves blood sugar control
  • Helps manage weight and blood pressure
  • May directly benefit retinal health

Diet

  • Follow a diabetes-friendly eating plan
  • Mediterranean diet may be beneficial
  • Include omega-3 fatty acids (fish)
  • Plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains

Don't Smoke

  • Smoking damages blood vessels
  • Increases all diabetes complications
  • Quitting improves outcomes at any stage

Limit Alcohol

  • Moderate consumption if any
  • Can affect blood sugar control

Manage Other Health Conditions

  • Treat kidney disease (diabetic nephropathy)
  • Manage cardiovascular disease
  • Control sleep apnea if present

Pregnancy Planning

  • Eye exam before conception
  • Optimize blood sugar control before pregnancy
  • More frequent monitoring during pregnancy

Living with Diabetic Retinopathy

A diabetic retinopathy diagnosis can be concerning, but many people maintain good vision with proper treatment and diabetes management:

Follow Your Treatment Plan

  • Attend all scheduled injections and appointments
  • Don't skip treatments even if vision seems good
  • Report any vision changes immediately
  • Continue treatments as recommended

Manage Your Diabetes

  • Make diabetes control your top priority
  • Monitor blood sugar regularly
  • Take all medications as prescribed
  • Attend regular appointments with your diabetes doctor
  • Keep HbA1c, blood pressure, and cholesterol in target ranges

Vision Aids and Adaptive Strategies

If vision loss occurs:

  • Magnifying devices for reading
  • Large-print materials
  • Better lighting throughout your home
  • High-contrast items
  • Screen readers and voice software
  • Consider vision rehabilitation services

Emotional Support

  • Join diabetes or vision loss support groups
  • Talk to a counselor if feeling anxious or depressed
  • Stay connected with family and friends
  • Focus on what you can control

Practical Tips

  • Wear sunglasses to protect eyes from UV rays
  • Control blood sugar before driving
  • Use proper lighting for tasks
  • Organize your environment to reduce eye strain
  • Ask for help when needed

When to See a Doctor

Seek Emergency Care Immediately For:

  • Sudden vision loss
  • Sudden appearance of many floaters
  • Curtain or shadow over part of your vision
  • Severe eye pain with nausea
  • Sudden blurring that doesn't improve

Schedule an Urgent Appointment (Within Days) For:

  • New floaters or flashes of light
  • Sudden worsening of vision
  • New distorted or wavy vision
  • Increased blurriness

Regular Screening Schedule

  • Type 1 diabetes: Annual exams starting 5 years after diagnosis
  • Type 2 diabetes: Annual exams starting at diagnosis
  • With DR: As recommended by your doctor (often every 3-6 months)
  • Pregnancy with diabetes: Each trimester

  • What's my target HbA1c?
