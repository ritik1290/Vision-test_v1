What Is Central Serous Retinopathy?

Central serous retinopathy (CSR), also called central serous chorioretinopathy (CSCR), is a condition in which fluid accumulates under the retina, specifically beneath the macula (the central part of the retina responsible for detailed vision). This fluid leakage causes the retina to detach slightly, creating a small blister-like elevation that distorts vision. CSR typically affects one eye and causes symptoms such as blurred central vision, distortion (straight lines appear wavy or bent), a dark or gray spot in the central vision, and altered color or contrast perception.

CSR is most common in men aged 30-50 and is often associated with stress, Type A personality, and corticosteroid use. The exact cause is not fully understood, but it involves dysfunction of the choroid (vascular layer beneath the retina), leading to fluid leakage through the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). Most cases resolve spontaneously within 3-6 months, but some become chronic or recurrent. Vision typically improves significantly after resolution, though subtle changes may persist.

Key Facts About Central Serous Retinopathy

  • Fluid under macula: Serous (clear fluid) detachment of central retina.
  • Common in middle-aged men: Peak age 30-50; male:female ratio ~6:1.
  • Stress-related: Often occurs during periods of emotional or physical stress.
  • Blurred, distorted vision: Central vision affected; objects appear smaller, distorted.
  • Usually one eye: Unilateral in ~85-90%.
  • Self-limited: ~80-90% resolve spontaneously in 3-6 months.
  • Good visual recovery: Most regain good vision after fluid reabsorbs.
  • Can recur: ~30-50% have recurrence in same or fellow eye.

Understanding CSR helps manage expectations for recovery and identify when treatment is needed.

Causes and Risk Factors

Mechanism (Choroidal Dysfunction)

  • Choroid (vascular layer) becomes hyperper meable (leaky)
  • Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) barrier breaks down
  • Fluid leaks from choroid through RPE defect into subretinal space
  • Creates serous detachment of retina

Strong Risk Factors

Male Sex

  • Men affected ~6 times more than women
  • Reason unclear; possibly hormonal

Age 30-50

  • Peak incidence in middle-aged adults
  • Can occur outside this range

Type A Personality / Stress

  • Classic association: competitive, high-stress individuals
  • Often occurs during stressful life events (job stress, exams, emotional trauma)
  • Mechanism: stress→cortisol elevation→choroidal changes?

Corticosteroid Use

  • Strongest identified risk factor
  • Oral, inhaled, topical, or injected steroids can trigger CSR
  • Even nasal or skin steroid creams
  • Dose and duration related
  • Key: Ask about ALL steroid use

Hypertension

  • Increases risk
  • May worsen choroidal vascular dysfunction

Pregnancy

  • Hormonal changes; elevated cortisol
  • CSR can occur in pregnancy

Sleep Disorders / Sleep Apnea

  • Emerging association

Other Associations

  • H. pylori infection (debated)
  • Autoimmune diseases (rare)

Recognizing the Symptoms

Blurred Central Vision

Central vision becomes hazy, blurred; difficulty reading.

Metamorphopsia (Distortion)

Straight lines (door frames, window blinds) appear wavy, bent, or curved; hallmark symptom.

Central Scotoma (Dark Spot)

Gray or dark spot in center of vision.

Micropsia (Objects Appear Smaller)

Things look smaller or farther away in affected eye.

Altered Color/Contrast

Colors appear washed out, less vivid; reduced contrast sensitivity.

Usually One Eye

Unilateral symptoms; covering good eye makes symptoms more noticeable.

Symptom Characteristics

  • Onset: Sudden or over days
  • Painless: No eye pain
  • No floaters or flashes: (unlike retinal detachment)
  • Vision may fluctuate
  • Reading difficult: Near vision more affected

Amsler Grid Changes

  • Straight grid lines appear wavy or distorted
  • Useful for at-home monitoring

Diagnosis

Clinical History

  • Sudden blurred, distorted central vision
  • Middle-aged man under stress (classic)
  • Steroid use? (critical question)

Visual Acuity Testing

  • Mildly to moderately reduced (20/30 to 20/80 typical)

Dilated Fundus Examination

  • Round or oval area of retinal elevation (serous detachment) at macula
  • May see yellowish subretinal deposits

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Diagnostic

  • Key imaging test
  • Cross-sectional retinal images
  • Shows:
    • Subretinal fluid (dark space beneath retina)
    • Serous retinal detachment
    • RPE detachment sometimes
  • Quantifies fluid amount
  • Tracks resolution over time

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

  • Fluorescent dye injected IV; retinal photographs taken
  • Shows:
    • Leak point (where RPE is defective)
    • Classic patterns:"smokestack" or "inkblot"
  • Useful if considering laser treatment
  • Identifies location of leak

Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

  • Specialized dye; images choroid better than FA
  • Shows choroidal vascular hyperpermeability
  • Useful in chronic or complex cases

Treatment

Observation (First-Line for Acute CSR)

  • Most CSR resolves spontaneously within 3-6 months
  • Initial management: observation, lifestyle modification, monitor with OCT
  • ~80-90% resolve without intervention

Lifestyle Modifications

Stop Corticosteroids

  • If on steroids, discontinue or taper (with prescribing doctor's guidance)
  • Critical step

Stress Reduction

  • Stress management techniques
  • Adequate sleep
  • Address underlying stressors

Control Hypertension

  • Optimize blood pressure

Treat Sleep Apnea

  • If present, CPAP therapy may help

Laser Photocoagulation (If Persistent)

  • Indications:
    • Fluid persists >3-4 months
    • Recurrent CSR in same eye
    • Patient needs faster recovery (e.g., airline pilot, professional driver)
  • Technique:
    • Focal laser applied to leak site (identified on FA)
    • Seals RPE defect
    • Outpatient, office procedure
  • Outcome: Speeds fluid resolution; good visual outcomes
  • Risk: Small scotoma (blind spot) at laser site if near center of fovea; therefore only done if leak is not subfoveal

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

  • For chronic or recurrent CSR
  • Verteporfin dye injected IV, activated with laser
  • Targets abnormal choroidal vessels
  • Reduces choroidal hyperpermeability
  • Effective for chronic cases
  • More expensive; requires special equipment

Medications (Investigational/Limited Evidence)

  • Eplerenone or Spironolactone: Mineralocorticoid antagonists; reduce choroidal fluid?
  • Rifampin: Antibiotic with possible choroidal effects
  • Evidence mixed; not standard

What NOT to Do

  • Anti-VEGF injections generally NOT effective for typical CSR (unlike wet AMD)

Prognosis and Outcomes

Acute CSR (First Episode)

  • Resolution: ~80-90% resolve spontaneously in 3-6 months
  • Visual recovery: Most regain 20/30 or better vision
  • Subtle residual changes:
    • Slight color desaturation
    • Minimal distortion
    • Reduced contrast sensitivity
  • May have small residual RPE changes on exam

Recurrence

  • ~30-50% experience recurrence
  • Can occur in same eye or fellow eye
  • Usually also resolves, but increased risk of chronic changes

Chronic CSR

  • ~10-20% do not resolve or have persistent/recurring fluid
  • Chronic fluid→permanent RPE/photoreceptor damage
  • Visual prognosis worse
  • May develop secondary complications (choroidal neovascularization)
  • Consider PDT or other treatment

Overall Visual Prognosis

  • Good for acute, single-episode CSR
  • Guarded for chronic/recurrent

When to See a Doctor

Seek Prompt Evaluation If:

  • Sudden blurred or distorted central vision
  • Straight lines appear wavy
  • Dark spot in central vision
  • Objects appear smaller in one eye

While CSR is not an emergency like retinal detachment, prompt evaluation ensures correct diagnosis and rules out other conditions.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this central serous retinopathy or something else?
  • Will it resolve on its own?
  • Should I stop my steroid medication?
  • Do I need laser treatment?
  • What is my expected visual recovery?
  • How often do I need follow-up OCT scans?
  • What is my risk of recurrence?

Bottom Line

Central serous retinopathy is a condition causing fluid accumulation under the macula, leading to blurred and distorted central vision. It primarily affects middle-aged men (30-50), often during periods of stress, and is strongly associated with corticosteroid use. Symptoms include blurred vision, wavy distortion of straight lines (metamorphopsia), a dark central spot, and objects appearing smaller. OCT imaging confirms the diagnosis by showing subretinal fluid. Most cases (80-90%) resolve spontaneously within 3-6 months with good visual recovery, so initial treatment is observation with lifestyle modifications (stop steroids, reduce stress). Laser photocoagulation or photodynamic therapy may be needed for persistent or chronic cases. If you experience sudden blurred, distorted central vision, see a retina specialist promptly for evaluation and to rule out other serious retinal conditions.
