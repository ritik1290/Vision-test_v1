What Is a Chalazion?

A chalazion (pronounced kuh-LAY-zee-on) is a firm, painless bump on the eyelid caused by a blocked meibomian gland. Meibomian glands are tiny oil glands along the edge of the eyelids that produce oils to keep tears from evaporating too quickly. When one of these glands becomes blocked, oil builds up inside, creating a pea-sized or larger lump. Unlike a stye (hordeolum), which is an infected gland that's painful and red, a chalazion is usually not infected—it's simply a chronic inflammatory reaction to the trapped oil.

Chalazia are very common and can occur at any age, though they're more frequent in adults ages 30-50. Most chalazia resolve on their own with warm compresses over several weeks, but some persist and require medical treatment with steroid injections or surgical drainage. While generally harmless, a chalazion can be cosmetically bothersome and occasionally affect vision if large enough to press on the eye.

Key Facts About Chalazion

  • Blocked oil gland: Meibomian gland obstruction causes oil to accumulate.
  • Usually painless: Unlike styes, chalazia are typically not painful or tender.
  • Firm bump: Feels like a hard pea under the skin of the eyelid.
  • Can occur on upper or lower lid: More common on upper lid.
  • Not an infection: Inflammatory, not infectious (though can become secondarily infected).
  • Often resolves spontaneously: Many go away with warm compresses in 2-8 weeks.
  • Recurrence common: Can develop repeatedly, especially if underlying risk factors present.
  • Treatable: Steroid injection or minor surgery effective for persistent cases.

Understanding chalazia helps patients recognize when home treatment is sufficient and when professional care is needed.

Understanding Eyelid Anatomy

Meibomian Glands

Key structures involved in chalazion formation:

  • Location: Within the tarsal plate (firm tissue) of upper and lower eyelids
  • Number: About 25-40 glands per upper lid, 20-30 per lower lid
  • Function: Produce meibum (oily secretion) that forms outer lipid layer of tear film
  • Openings: Tiny pores along lid margin just behind lash line
  • Purpose: Prevent tear evaporation, keep tears stable on eye surface

What Happens in a Chalazion

  • Gland opening blocks: Thickened oil, debris, or inflammation clogs the duct
  • Oil accumulates: Meibum secretion continues but can't drain
  • Gland swells: Backed-up oil creates cyst-like swelling
  • Inflammation develops: Body reacts to trapped oil with granulomatous inflammation
  • Firm lump forms: Chronic inflammation creates hard, pea-sized nodule
  • May point inward or outward: Can be visible on skin side or conjunctival (inner) side of lid

Chalazion vs. Stye

Important distinction:

  • Chalazion:
    • Blocked meibomian gland (inside lid)
    • Painless or mildly tender
    • Firm, hard lump
    • Develops slowly over weeks
    • Not infected initially
    • Can be large
  • Stye (Hordeolum):
    • Infected eyelash follicle or gland (margin of lid)
    • Painful, tender
    • Red, swollen
    • Develops quickly over days
    • Bacterial infection
    • Usually smaller; may have pus-filled "head"
  • Can coexist: Sometimes start as stye, become chalazion
  • Treatment overlaps but not identical

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Cause: Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)

  • Abnormally thick or altered meibum that doesn't flow easily
  • Gland openings become plugged
  • Oil accumulates behind blockage
  • Inflammation develops

Contributing Factors

  • Blepharitis: Chronic eyelid inflammation; major risk factor
  • Rosacea: Skin condition affecting face and eyes; associated with MGD
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: Oily skin condition
  • Acne: Overactive oil glands elsewhere often correlate with eyelid issues
  • Poor eyelid hygiene: Not removing makeup, debris
  • Thick or altered meibum: Changes in oil composition or consistency
  • Hormonal changes: Pregnancy, menopause
  • Stress

Medical Conditions Associated with Chalazia

  • Ocular rosacea
  • Chronic blepharitis
  • Meibomian gland dysfunction
  • Seborrheic dermatitis
  • Diabetes (immune factors)
  • High cholesterol or lipid abnormalities (some evidence)

Who Gets Chalazia?

  • Most common in adults 30-50 years old
  • Can occur at any age, including children
  • Equal occurrence in men and women
  • People with oily skin or chronic lid inflammation at higher risk
  • Recurrent chalazia common in susceptible individuals

Recognizing the Symptoms

Firm, Round Bump

Pea-sized (or larger) hard lump on upper or lower eyelid; feels firm to touch.

Painless or Mildly Tender

Usually not painful; may be slightly tender if large or if secondarily inflamed.

Gradual Swelling

Develops slowly over days to weeks; eyelid may appear swollen in area of chalazion.

Visible Lump

May see bump on outer skin of lid or redness on inner (conjunctival) surface when lid everted.

Mild Tearing

Irritation from bump may cause slight watering or foreign body sensation.

Blurred Vision (If Large)

Large chalazion pressing on eyeball can cause astigmatism and blur vision.

Evolution Over Time

  • Early (days 1-7): Small, tender area developing; may feel like something in the lid
  • Established (weeks 1-4): Firm, well-defined lump; minimal pain; cosmetically noticeable
  • Chronic (>4 weeks): Hard, persistent bump; may point to skin or conjunctival surface
  • Spontaneous drainage: Occasionally ruptures and drains on its own (pus or cheesy material)

What Chalazia Usually Don't Cause

  • Severe pain (if very painful, may be stye or infection)
  • Extensive redness of entire eyelid (suggests infection)
  • Fever or systemic illness
  • Vision loss (unless very large and pressing on eye)

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Diagnosis is usually straightforward based on appearance:

History

  • When did the bump appear?
  • Any pain, tenderness, or discharge?
  • Previous chalazia or styes?
  • History of blepharitis, rosacea, or skin conditions?
  • Any treatments tried?

Physical Examination

  • Inspection: Visible lump on upper or lower lid
  • Palpation: Firm, non-tender or mildly tender nodule
  • Evert eyelid: May see redness or pointing on conjunctival side
  • Assess lid margins: Look for blepharitis, clogged glands
  • Check vision: Large chalazion may distort cornea (induce astigmatism)

Differential Diagnosis

Other conditions that can mimic chalazion:

  • Stye (hordeolum): More painful, acute, often has pus
  • Eyelid cyst (dermoid, epidermoid): Congenital; different location
  • Eyelid tumor (rare):
    • Sebaceous cell carcinoma can mimic recurrent chalazion
    • Red flag: Chalazion that recurs in exact same spot, causes lash loss, or doesn't respond to treatment
    • Requires biopsy if suspicious
  • Preseptal cellulitis: Infection of eyelid tissues; entire lid swollen, red, painful

When Biopsy Needed

  • Recurrent chalazion in same location
  • Associated eyelash loss
  • Doesn't respond to treatment
  • Atypical appearance
  • Concern for malignancy (especially in elderly)
  • Tissue sent to pathology to rule out sebaceous gland carcinoma

Treatment Overview

Treatment Goals

  • Promote drainage and resolution
  • Reduce inflammation
  • Restore gland function
  • Prevent recurrence

Treatment Approach by Severity

  • Small, early chalazion: Warm compresses, lid hygiene (often resolves in 2-8 weeks)
  • Persistent (>4-6 weeks): Consider steroid injection
  • Large or long-standing: Surgical drainage
  • Recurrent: Treat underlying blepharitis/MGD; consider long-term lid hygiene

Home Remedies and Self-Care

Warm Compresses - Mainstay of Treatment

Most effective home treatment:

How to Apply

  • Use clean, warm cloth: Soak washcloth in warm (not hot) water
  • Wring out excess water
  • Apply to closed eyelid: Hold gently over chalazion
  • Maintain warmth: Re-warm cloth as it cools; keep warm for entire session
  • Duration: 10-15 minutes per session
  • Frequency: 3-4 times daily
  • Consistency: Continue for several weeks even after chalazion starts shrinking

Why Warm Compresses Work

  • Heat liquefies thickened meibum (oil)
  • Promotes drainage through gland opening
  • Increases blood flow, reduces inflammation
  • Softens and helps resolve the lump

Alternative: Microwavable Eye Masks

  • Commercially available heat masks designed for MGD
  • Maintain consistent temperature longer than washcloth
  • More convenient; improves compliance
  • Examples: Bruder mask, TheraPearl

Gentle Massage (Optional)

  • After warm compress, gently massage area with clean finger
  • Massage toward lid margin to encourage drainage
  • Don't forcefully squeeze or pop the chalazion
  • Can help express blocked oil

Eyelid Hygiene

  • Clean lid margins daily: Use diluted baby shampoo or commercial lid scrub
  • Remove debris: Cotton swab or special lid wipe along lash line
  • Helps prevent recurrence

What NOT to Do

  • Don't squeeze or pop: Can cause infection, spread inflammation
  • Don't wear contact lenses: While chalazion active
  • Don't wear eye makeup: Irritates area, clogs glands further
  • Don't use dirty compresses: Can introduce infection

Over-the-Counter Options

  • Lid wipes: OCuSOFT, Systane, others
  • Omega-3 supplements: May improve meibum quality; helps with MGD/blepharitis
  • Artificial tears: If experiencing dryness

Timeline for Home Treatment

  • First 2 weeks: May see little change; continue warm compresses
  • Weeks 2-6: Chalazion may soften, shrink, or drain spontaneously
  • If no improvement by 4-6 weeks: See doctor for medical treatment
  • Patience essential; can take 2 months to fully resolve with home care

Medical Treatment

Steroid Injection (Intralesional Corticosteroid)

For persistent chalazia not responding to warm compresses:

Procedure

  • Numbing drops applied to eye
  • Small needle injects steroid (triamcinolone or similar) directly into chalazion
  • From skin side or conjunctival side depending on location
  • Quick office procedure; takes minutes
  • Minimal discomfort

Effectiveness

  • 60-80% resolve with one injection
  • Chalazion typically shrinks over 1-2 weeks
  • May need second injection if incomplete response
  • Avoids surgery in many cases

Side Effects/Risks

  • Skin depigmentation (lightening) at injection site—usually temporary, can be permanent in dark-skinned individuals
  • Rarely: elevated eye pressure, small blood vessel rupture (bruising)

Surgical Drainage (Incision and Curettage)

For large, persistent, or recurrent chalazia:

Procedure

  • Local anesthetic injected around chalazion
  • Approach: Usually from conjunctival (inside) surface; avoids skin scar
  • Small incision made over chalazion
  • Contents (thick oil, inflammatory tissue) curetted (scraped) out
  • No stitches needed usually (conjunctival incision heals on its own)
  • Takes 10-20 minutes
  • Office or minor procedure room

Recovery

  • Antibiotic ointment applied
  • Eye patch for few hours or overnight
  • Mild bruising, swelling for days to week
  • Avoid rubbing eye
  • Resume normal activities next day usually

Effectiveness

  • Nearly 100% removal of chalazion
  • Immediate resolution (though swelling takes days to settle)
  • Recurrence possible if underlying MGD not addressed

Risks

  • Infection (rare)
  • Bleeding, bruising
  • Scarring (usually minimal with conjunctival approach)
  • Recurrence at same or different site
  • Rarely: damage to lid structures

Oral Antibiotics

  • Reserved for secondary infection (rare) or associated blepharitis
  • Doxycycline sometimes used for chronic MGD/blepharitis to reduce recurrences
  • Not standard for uncomplicated chalazion

Preventing Recurrence

Lid Hygiene Routine

Key to preventing future chalazia:

  • Daily lid scrubs: Clean lash line with lid wipes or diluted baby shampoo
  • Warm compresses: 1-2 times daily (even when no active chalazion) if prone to recurrence
  • Remove makeup thoroughly: Before bed every night
  • Replace makeup regularly: Especially mascara (every 3 months); old makeup harbors bacteria

Treat Underlying Conditions

  • Blepharitis: Chronic lid inflammation; requires ongoing management
  • Rosacea: Treat with topical or oral medications as prescribed
  • Meibomian gland dysfunction: Regular warm compresses, lid hygiene
  • Seborrheic dermatitis: Manage scalp and facial symptoms

Lifestyle Measures

  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Fish oil or flaxseed supplements may improve meibum quality
  • Stay hydrated
  • Manage stress
  • Avoid touching eyes with unwashed hands

Special Considerations

  • Contact lens wearers: Clean lenses properly; avoid sleeping in lenses
  • Makeup users: Use non-comedogenic products; avoid applying inside lash line

When to See a Doctor

See Eye Doctor If:

  • Chalazion persists >4-6 weeks despite warm compresses
  • Growing larger or affecting vision
  • Becomes painful, red, or signs of infection develop
  • Multiple or recurrent chalazia
  • Entire eyelid swollen (possible pre septal cellulitis)
  • Vision changes
  • Recurs in same spot repeatedly (rule out malignancy)

Urgent Care Needed If:

  • Severe lid swelling extending beyond chalazion
  • Fever
  • Spreading redness
  • Unable to open eye
  • These suggest orbital cellulitis (serious infection requiring immediate treatment)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this definitely a chalazion or could it be something else?
  • Should I continue warm compresses?
  • Do you recommend steroid injection or surgery?
  • What can I do to prevent recurrence?
  • Do I have underlying blepharitis or MGD that needs treatment?
  • Is there any chance this could be a tumor?

Bottom Line

A chalazion is a common, benign eyelid bump caused by a blocked oil gland. While bothersome, most respond well to simple home treatment with warm compresses applied 3-4 times daily for several weeks. If persistent beyond 4-6 weeks, medical treatments like steroid injection or surgical drainage are highly effective. Preventing recurrence involves good lid hygiene and treating underlying conditions like blepharitis or rosacea. While chalazia are generally harmless, recurrent lumps in the same location should be evaluated to rule out rare eyelid tumors.
