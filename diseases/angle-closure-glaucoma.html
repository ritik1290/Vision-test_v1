What Is Angle-Closure Glaucoma?

Angle-closure glaucoma (also called closed-angle glaucoma or narrow-angle glaucoma) is a type of glaucoma in which the drainage angle of the eye—the area where fluid (aqueous humor) exits the eye—becomes blocked, causing a rapid, dangerous increase in intraocular pressure (IOP). Unlike open-angle glaucoma, which develops slowly, acute angle-closure glaucoma is a medical emergency that can cause permanent vision loss within hours if not treated promptly.

The condition occurs when the iris (colored part of the eye) is pushed forward or bulges, physically blocking the trabecular meshwork (drainage system) in the angle between the iris and cornea. This prevents fluid from draining out of the eye, causing pressure to build rapidly—often to dangerously high levels (40-80 mmHg or higher, compared to normal 10-21 mmHg). The elevated pressure damages the optic nerve and can cause permanent vision loss. Acute angle-closure glaucoma is characterized by severe eye pain, blurred vision, halos around lights, nausea, and vomiting.

Key Facts About Angle-Closure Glaucoma

  • Medical emergency: Acute attacks require immediate treatment to prevent permanent blindness.
  • Blocked drainage angle: Iris blocks fluid outflow, causing rapid pressure spike.
  • Severe symptoms: Extreme eye pain, headache, nausea/vomiting, blurred vision, halos.
  • Can cause blindness in hours: Untreated acute attacks damage optic nerve permanently.
  • Anatomical predisposition: Small, crowded eyes; farsightedness; Asian descent.
  • Laser treatment prevents: Laser iridotomy creates drainage hole, prevents attacks.
  • Different from open-angle glaucoma: Sudden onset vs. gradual; emergency vs. chronic.
  • Can be chronic: Some have chronic angle-closure with gradual damage.

Understanding angle-closure glaucoma is critical for recognizing emergency symptoms and seeking immediate treatment.

Types of Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma (Most Dramatic)

  • Sudden, complete blockage of drainage angle
  • Rapid IOP spike (40-80+ mmHg)
  • Severe symptoms: excruciating eye pain, nausea, vomiting, blurred vision
  • Ophthalmologic emergency
  • Requires immediate treatment (within hours)
  • Can cause permanent vision loss if delayed

Subacute (Intermittent) Angle-Closure

  • Partial, temporary angle closure
  • IOP rises moderately, then resolves spontaneously
  • Symptoms: Blurred vision, halos around lights, mild eye discomfort
  • Symptoms may last minutes to hours, then resolve
  • Warning sign for future acute attack
  • Should be treated with laser to prevent acute attack

Chronic Angle-Closure Glaucoma

  • Gradual, progressive angle closure
  • IOP elevation slower, symptoms less dramatic
  • May be asymptomatic like open-angle glaucoma
  • Optic nerve damage develops over time
  • More common in Asian populations

Acute-on-Chronic

  • Chronic angle-closure with acute attack superimposed

Causes and Risk Factors

Anatomical Factors (Primary Cause)

Narrow Drainage Angle

  • Small, crowded anterior chamber (front part of eye)
  • Angle between iris and cornea anatomically narrow
  • Predisposes to blockage

Eye Anatomy Associated with Narrow Angles

  • Hyperopia (farsightedness): Shorter, smaller eyes have crowded anterior chambers
  • Thick lens: Larger crystalline lens pushes iris forward
  • Shallow anterior chamber
  • Small cornea

Triggers for Acute Attack

  • Pupil dilation:
    • Dim lighting (pupil dilates)
    • Dilating eye drops (at eye exam or ER)
    • Emotional stress/excitement
    • Medications causing dilation (anticholinergics, decongestants)
  • When pupil dilates, iris bunches up and can block angle

Risk Factors

Demographics

  • Age: Risk increases after age 40; peak 55-65
  • Sex: Women > men (2-4:1); women have smaller eyes
  • Race: Asian and Inuit populations at higher risk; East Asians especially prone to chronic angle-closure
  • Family history: Genetic component; runs in families

Refractive Error

  • Hyperopia: Farsighted people have smaller eyes, higher risk

Other Eye Conditions

  • Plateau iris syndrome (abnormal iris configuration)
  • Pseudoexfoliation syndrome
  • Mature cataract (swollen lens pushes iris forward)

Medications

  • Anticholinergics (cause pupil dilation)
  • Topiramate (anti-seizure/weight-loss drug; can cause acute angle-closure)
  • SSRIs, antihistamines, decongestants

Secondary Angle-Closure (Less Common)

  • Lens displacement (trauma, Marfan syndrome)
  • Intraocular tumor
  • Inflammation pushing iris forward
  • Neovascular glaucoma (abnormal vessels)

Symptoms of Acute Angle-Closure Attack

MEDICAL EMERGENCY - Seek Immediate Care

Acute angle-closure glaucoma can cause permanent blindness within hours. If you experience these symptoms, go to the emergency room immediately:

Severe Eye Pain

Excruciating, intense pain in affected eye; described as worst eye pain ever experienced.

Headache

Severe headache on same side as affected eye; often frontal or around eye.

Nausea and Vomiting

Often severe; can mimic abdominal illness (patients sometimes misdiagnosed initially).

Blurred, Hazy Vision

Vision severely decreased; cloudy, foggy appearance; corneal edema from high pressure.

Halos Around Lights

Rainbow-colored halos around lights; classic symptom from corneal swelling.

Red Eye

Marked redness, ciliary flush (redness around cornea).

Fixed, Mid-Dilated Pupil

Pupil larger than normal, oval-shaped, does not react to light.

Corneal Haziness

Cloudiness of normally clear cornea (edema from high IOP).

Subacute Attack Symptoms (Milder, Intermittent)

  • Blurred vision episodes
  • Halos around lights (especially at night)
  • Mild eye discomfort or ache
  • Symptoms resolve spontaneously (angle reopens)
  • Warning sign: Should prompt evaluation; laser treatment can prevent acute attack

Chronic Angle-Closure Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Gradual peripheral vision loss (like open-angle glaucoma)
  • May have occasional blurred vision or halos

Diagnosis

Emergency Evaluation (Acute Attack)

Clinical Signs

  • Severe eye pain, nausea, vomiting
  • Markedly elevated IOP (often 40-80+ mmHg)
  • Corneal edema (cloudy, hazy cornea)
  • Shallow anterior chamber
  • Mid-dilated, fixed pupil
  • Closed drainage angle on gonioscopy

Comprehensive Eye Examination

Tonometry (Eye Pressure Measurement)

  • Markedly elevated IOP in acute attack (>40 mmHg, often 60-80 mmHg)
  • Normal IOP is 10-21 mmHg

Gonioscopy (Angle Examination) - Diagnostic Test

  • Special mirrored lens to visualize drainage angle
  • Acute attack: Angle completely closed (iris against cornea)
  • Narrow angle (at risk): Angle open but very narrow
  • Essential for diagnosis and screening at-risk individuals

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Shallow anterior chamber
  • Corneal edema (microcystic changes)
  • Mid-dilated pupil
  • Ciliary injection (redness around cornea)

Optic Nerve Examination

  • Assess for glaucomatous optic neuropathy
  • Cupping of optic disc

Visual Field Testing

  • Check for peripheral vision loss
  • Done after acute attack resolved, to assess damage

Anterior Segment OCT or Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

  • Imaging to visualize angle anatomy
  • Helps assess angle narrowness, plateau iris

Screening At-Risk Individuals

  • Small, crowded-appearing anterior chambers
  • Hyperopia
  • Family history
  • Asian descent
  • Gonioscopy essential to identify narrow angles before acute attack

Treatment

Acute Angle-Closure Attack - Emergency Management

Immediate Treatment Required

Acute angle-closure glaucoma requires emergency treatment to lower eye pressure within hours to prevent permanent vision loss. Treatment involves multiple medications followed by laser procedure.

Medical Management (Lower IOP Emergently)

  • Goal: Reduce IOP as quickly as possible to <20-30 mmHg
  • Medications (combination used):
    • Topical beta-blocker: Timolol (reduces fluid production)
    • Topical alpha-agonist: Apraclonidine or brimonidine (reduces production, increases outflow)
    • Topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitor: Dorzolamide (reduces production)
    • Pilocarpine drops: Constricts pupil, pulls iris away from angle (given AFTER IOP lowered somewhat; ineffective at very high pressure due to ischemic iris)
    • Oral carbonic anhydrase inhibitor: Acetazolamide 500 mg (powerful IOP reduction)
    • IV mannitol or oral glycerol: Hyperosmotic agents (pull fluid out of eye); reserved for very high IOP
    • Topical steroid: Prednisolone (reduce inflammation)
  • IOP usually controlled within 1-3 hours with aggressive medical therapy

Definitive Treatment: Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)

  • When: Once IOP controlled and cornea clears (usually same day or next day)
  • Procedure:
    • Laser creates small hole in peripheral iris
    • Allows aqueous fluid to flow from posterior chamber to anterior chamber, bypassing normal route
    • Relieves iris bowing, opens drainage angle
    • Prevents future attacks
  • Anesthesia: Topical numbing drops; done in clinic
  • Duration: Minutes
  • Success rate: Very high (>90-95%); prevents recurrence

Surgical Iridectomy (If Laser Fails or Not Possible)

  • Surgically create opening in iris in operating room
  • Rarely needed

Fellow Eye Treatment

  • Critical: If one eye has acute angle-closure, the other eye is at high risk
  • Prophylactic laser iridotomy recommended for fellow eye to prevent attack

Treatment of Chronic Angle-Closure

  • Laser peripheral iridotomy (first-line)
  • IOP-lowering medications if needed after laser
  • Cataract surgery (removes lens, deepens anterior chamber, very effective)

Long-Term Management After Acute Attack

  • Monitor IOP and optic nerve regularly
  • Some may need ongoing IOP-lowering drops even after laser
  • Visual field testing to monitor for glaucoma progression

Prevention

Identifying At-Risk Individuals

  • Comprehensive eye exams including gonioscopy
  • Screen those with:
    • Hyperopia (farsightedness)
    • Family history of angle-closure
    • Asian descent
    • Age >40, especially women

Prophylactic Laser Peripheral Iridotomy

  • For individuals with anatomically narrow angles (even without symptoms)
  • Prevents acute attacks
  • Simple, low-risk outpatient procedure
  • Significantly reduces risk of acute angle-closure

Medication Precautions

  • Avoid anticholinergic medications if possible (or use with caution)
  • Alert physicians/pharmacists if diagnosed with narrow angles
  • Read medication warnings (some list "narrow-angle glaucoma" as contraindication)

Early Cataract Surgery

  • Removing cataract and replacing with thinner intraocular lens deepens anterior chamber
  • Opens angle, reduces risk
  • Increasingly used as preventive strategy in high-risk individuals

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Prompt Treatment (Acute Attack)

  • If treated within hours: Good outcomes; vision often preserved
  • Laser iridotomy prevents recurrence in >90%
  • Some residual optic nerve damage possible if IOP was very high or prolonged

Delayed Treatment

  • Optic nerve damage from prolonged high IOP
  • Permanent peripheral or central vision loss
  • Blindness if untreated >24 hours

After Laser Iridotomy

  • Excellent prevention of future attacks
  • Some patients may still develop chronic angle-closure or require IOP-lowering medications
  • Regular monitoring essential

Visual Outcomes

  • Depends on severity/duration of attack and baseline optic nerve health
  • Early treatment: best outcomes
  • May have residual peripheral vision defects even with treatment

When to See a Doctor

GO TO EMERGENCY ROOM IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Severe, sudden eye pain
  • Severe headache with eye pain
  • Nausea and vomiting with eye symptoms
  • Blurred vision with halos around lights
  • Red, painful eye with cloudy cornea

THIS IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Do not wait—permanent vision loss can occur within hours. Call 911 or go to the nearest ER.

Routine Eye Exams for Prevention

  • If you are farsighted, >40 years old, Asian, or have family history, get comprehensive eye exams with gonioscopy
  • Identify narrow angles before acute attack occurs
  • Consider prophylactic laser treatment

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Do I have narrow drainage angles?
  • Am I at risk for angle-closure glaucoma?
  • Should I have a preventive laser iridotomy?
  • What medications should I avoid?
  • What are warning signs of an attack?

Bottom Line

Acute angle-closure glaucoma is an ophthalmologic emergency caused by sudden blockage of the eye's drainage angle, leading to a rapid, dangerous spike in eye pressure. It presents with severe eye pain, headache, nausea/vomiting, blurred vision, and halos around lights. Without immediate treatment (aggressive IOP-lowering medications followed by laser iridotomy), permanent blindness can occur within hours. Individuals at risk—those who are farsighted, over 40, female, Asian, or with family history—should have gonioscopy screening to identify narrow angles. Prophylactic laser peripheral iridotomy is a simple, safe procedure that creates a drainage hole in the iris and prevents acute attacks. If you experience sudden severe eye pain with nausea and blurred vision, seek emergency care immediately—this is a vision-threatening emergency requiring urgent treatment.
