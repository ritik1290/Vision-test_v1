What Is Entropion?

Entropion is a condition in which the eyelid (usually the lower lid) turns inward toward the eye, causing the eyelashes and skin to rub against the cornea and conjunctiva. This constant mechanical irritation leads to significant discomfort, tearing, redness, and potential corneal damage. Unlike ectropion (where the lid turns out), entropion creates direct trauma to the eye surface from abrasive lash contact, which can result in corneal erosions, scarring, and vision impairment if left untreated.

Entropion is most common in the elderly due to age-related changes in eyelid tissues and muscles, though it can also result from scarring (cicatricial entropion), spasm of the eyelid muscle, or congen ital abnormalities. The condition causes a vicious cycle: irritation from lashes rubbing the eye causes the patient to squeeze eyelids tightly (blepharospasm), which further inverts the lid. Surgical correction is highly effective and is the definitive treatment for most cases of entropion.

Key Facts About Entropion

  • Inward-turning eyelid: Lower eyelid margin rotates inward toward eyeball.
  • Lashes rub cornea: Constant mechanical irritation; painful, damaging.
  • Common in elderly: Age-related tissue changes most common cause.
  • Severe irritation: Foreign body sensation, pain, tearing, light sensitivity.
  • Vision-threatening: Can cause corneal abrasions, scarring, ulcers.
  • Surgical correction effective: Eyelid repositioning procedures highly successful.
  • Usually lower lid: Lower eyelid affected more than upper.
  • Requires prompt treatment: Prevent permanent corneal damage.

Understanding entropion helps recognize when surgical intervention is needed to protect the eye.

Types and Causes

Involutional (Age-Related) Entropion - Most Common

  • Caused by aging changes:
    • Horizontal laxity of lower eyelid
    • Weakening of eyelid retractors (muscles that pull lid down)
    • Overriding of preseptal orbicularis muscle (muscle slips over tarsal plate edge)
    • Atrophy of orbital fat
  • Lower lid margin rotates inward
  • Lashes contact cornea and conjunctiva
  • Typically develops in people >60 years old
  • Most common type

Spastic Entropion

  • Acute, temporary inward turning triggered by:
    • Ocular irritation (dry eye, foreign body)
    • Inflammation (blepharitis, conjunctivitis)
    • Recent eye surgery
  • Orbicularis muscle spasm causes lid to roll in
  • Can become chronic if not addressed

Cicatricial Entropion

  • Scarring of palpebral conjunctiva (inside of lid) contracts and pulls lid margin inward
  • Causes:
    • Trachoma: Chronic chlamydial infection leading cause worldwide (developing countries)
    • Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid: Autoimmune blistering disease causing conjunctival scarring
    • Stevens-Johnson syndrome/Toxic epidermal necrolysis: Severe drug reactions
    • Chemical burns
    • Chronic inflammatory conjunctivitis
    • Trauma
  • More common in upper lid (unlike involutional)
  • Progressive condition

Congenital Entropion

  • Present at birth; rare
  • Lower lid most common
  • Often resolves spontaneously in first year of life
  • Caused by maldevelopment of lid structures

Mechanical Entropion

  • Eyelid tumor or mass distorting lid position

Recognizing the Symptoms

Foreign Body Sensation

Constant feeling of something in the eye; gritty, scratchy; due to lashes rubbing cornea.

Eye Pain and Irritation

Severe discomfort; worsens with blinking as lashes scrape across cornea.

Excessive Tearing (Epiphora)

Reflex tearing from corneal irritation; tears overflow onto cheek.

Redness

Eye markedly bloodshot from chronic irritation and inflammation.

Light Sensitivity (Photophobia)

Difficult to tolerate bright light; corneal abrasions cause photophobia.

Blurred Vision

If corneal abrasions or scarring develop; mucus discharge can blur vision.

Visible Lid Turning Inward

Lower eyelid margin rotated inward; lashes point toward eye (vs. outward normally).

Chronic Squinting

Squeezing eyelids shut (blepharospasm) in response to irritation; paradoxically worsens entropion.

Vicious Cycle

  • Lashes rub cornea → Irritation
  • Irritation → Reflex squeezing/blinking
  • Squeezing → Further inward rotation of lid
  • Worsening entropion → More lash contact

Corneal Damage

  • Punctate epithelial erosions (small superficial abrasions)
  • Linear corneal abrasions (vertical scratches from lashes)
  • Corneal scarring (if chronic)
  • Corneal ulcer (if severe/infected)

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

History

  • Duration and severity of symptoms?
  • Previous episodes?
  • History of trachoma, chemical burns, autoimmune disease?
  • Recent ocular surgery or inflammation?

External Inspection

  • Visible inward turning of lower eyelid margin
  • Lashes pointing toward eye, contacting cornea/conjunctiva
  • Redness, irritation
  • Assess eyelid laxity (snap-back test, distraction test)
  • Dynamic examination: Ask patient to squeeze eyes shut then open—entropion may become more pronounced or only visible with squeezing (spastic component)

Slit Lamp Examination

  • Evaluate cornea with fluorescein staining:
    • Punctate epithelial erosions
    • Linear vertical abrasions from lashes
    • Scarring (white opacities)
    • Ulcer (if present)
  • Assess conjunctiva for scarring (cicatricial causes)
  • Lash position

Determining Type

  • Involutional: Elderly, horizontal laxity, no scarring
  • Spastic: Recent onset, associated with irritation/surgery, improves when not squeezing
  • Cicatricial: Conjunctival scarring visible, history of trachoma/burns/autoimmune disease
  • Congenital: Infant, present from birth

Complications

Corneal Abrasion and Erosion

  • Superficial scratches from lashes
  • Painful; risk of infection

Corneal Scarring

  • Chronic rubbing causes permanent opacities
  • Impairs vision

Corneal Ulcer

  • Deep infection from chronic epithelial breakdown
  • Vision-threatening; can perforate

Chronic Conjunctivitis

  • Persistent inflammation
  • Discharge, discomfort

Vision Loss

  • From corneal scarring or ulcer
  • Permanent if not treated

Treatment

Conservative Management (Temporary/Mild Cases)

Lubricant Eye Drops and Ointments

  • Protect cornea from lash trauma
  • Artificial tears frequently during day
  • Thick ointment at night
  • Provides symptomatic relief but doesn't correct lid position

Taping (Temporary)

  • Surgical tape applied to pull lower lid down/out
  • Temporary measure while awaiting surgery
  • Useful for acute spastic entropion

Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injection

  • Injected into orbicularis muscle to weaken it
  • Reduces spasm component
  • Temporary effect (3-4 months)
  • Sometimes used for acute spastic entropion or in patients unfit for surgery

Treat Underlying Cause

  • Dry eye treatment, blepharitis management to reduce irritation/spasm

Definitive Treatment: Surgery

Most effective and long-lasting treatment for symptomatic entropion.

Surgical Repair

Indications for Surgery

  • Symptomatic entropion (pain, irritation, tearing)
  • Corneal abrasions or damage
  • Failed conservative management
  • Chronic or recurrent entropion

Common Surgical Procedures

Everting Sutures (Quickert Sutures)

  • Technique:
    • Sutures passed through lower lid to rotate margin outward
    • Pull lid margin away from eye
  • Advantages: Quick, simple, can be done in office
  • Disadvantages: Lower long-term success (~50-70%); recurrence common
  • Often used for acute/spastic entropion or temporary fix

Lateral Tarsal Strip with Lower Lid Retractor Reinsertion (More Definitive for Involutional)

  • Technique:
    • Tighten horizontal eyelid (lateral tarsal strip)
    • Reattach/advance lower lid retractors (pull lid down and stabilize)
    • Remove/reposition orbicularis muscle if overriding
  • Advantages: Addresses multiple causes; higher success rate (>90%)
  • Most common definitive procedure

Transverse Blepharotomy with Retractor Advancement (Wies Procedure)

  • Horizontal incision splits lid; retractors advanced
  • Effective for involutional entropion

Cicatricial Entropion Surgery

  • More complex; address conjunctival scarring
  • Techniques:
    • Tarsal fracture (break tarsal plate to rotate margin out)
    • Mucous membrane grafting (replace scarred conjunctiva)
    • Eyelid margin rotation procedures
  • Often requires staged procedures

Anesthesia

  • Local anesthesia with sedation (most common)
  • General anesthesia if needed
  • Outpatient procedure

Procedure Details

  • Duration: 30-60 minutes
  • Sutures: Absorbable internal; may have external sutures removed in 1 week

Post-Operative Care

  • Ice packs, head elevation
  • Antibiotic ointment
  • Avoid rubbing, strenuous activity
  • Swelling/bruising resolves over 1-2 weeks

Risks and Complications of Surgery

  • Infection, bleeding (rare)
  • Scarring
  • Undercorrection (entropion persists)
  • Overcorrection (ectropion—lid turns outward)
  • Recurrence (~5-15% depending on technique)
  • Asymmetry

Prognosis and Outcomes

With Surgical Correction

  • Excellent outcomes: 85-95% success rate with definitive procedures
  • Symptom resolution: Irritation, tearing, pain resolve
  • Corneal healing: Abrasions heal; no further lash trauma
  • Recurrence: Low with comprehensive repair

Without Treatment

  • Progressive worsening likely
  • Ongoing corneal damage
  • Risk of ulcer, scarring, vision loss
  • Chronic discomfort

Cicatricial Entropion

  • More challenging to treat
  • May require multiple surgeries
  • Progressive disease (e.g., ocular cicatricial pemphigoid) may recur despite surgery
  • Requires ongoing management of underlying condition

When to See a Doctor

Seek Evaluation If:

  • Eyelid turning inward
  • Constant foreign body sensation
  • Eye pain, redness, tearing
  • Lashes rubbing eye
  • Vision changes

Entropion can cause corneal damage if left untreated. Prompt evaluation and treatment protect vision.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of entropion do I have?
  • Is there corneal damage?
  • Do I need surgery?
  • What procedure do you recommend?
  • What is the success rate?
  • How long is recovery?
  • What is my risk of recurrence?

Bottom Line

Entropion is an inward turning of the eyelid causing eyelashes to rub against the cornea, resulting in severe irritation, pain, tearing, and potential corneal damage. Most common in the elderly due to age-related tissue laxity, entropion can also result from scarring (cicatricial) or muscle spasm (spastic). The constant mechanical trauma from lashes scraping the cornea can cause abrasions, scarring, and vision loss if untreated. While lubricating drops and taping provide temporary relief, surgical correction—typically involving eyelid tightening and retractor advancement—is the definitive treatment with excellent success rates (85-95%). If you experience an inward-turning eyelid with foreign body sensation and irritation, seek prompt evaluation—surgery is highly effective at resolving symptoms and preventing permanent corneal damage.
